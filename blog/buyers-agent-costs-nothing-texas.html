Your Buyer Agent Costs You Nothing — And Why Going Direct to the Listing Agent Is a Mistake

By Chris Drawdy · April 28, 2026 · 10 min read

Most San Antonio home buyers don't realize that having their own real estate agent typically costs them absolutely nothing out of pocket — and that skipping one to "go direct" to the listing agent can cost them thousands. In this post, you'll learn exactly how buyer agent compensation works in Texas after the 2024 NAR settlement, what legal protections you lose without your own agent, and why the listing agent's job is to serve the seller, not you.

How Buyer Agent Compensation Actually Works in Texas Today

If you've been house-hunting in San Antonio and wondering "do I have to pay a buyer's agent?", you're not alone. The rules changed in 2024 following the National Association of Realtors settlement, and there's been a lot of confusion — and a lot of bad information floating around social media.

Here's the straightforward truth: buyer agent compensation is now negotiated directly in the purchase contract or offered by the seller as part of the deal. It is no longer automatically shared through the MLS the way it once was. But in practice, across most of San Antonio's active market — from Stone Oak to Converse to Helotes — sellers are still routinely offering to cover the buyer's agent fee to attract more buyers.

That means for the vast majority of buyers working with me right now, their representation costs them zero dollars at closing. You get a licensed professional in your corner, someone who knows the difference between a fair price in Alamo Ranch versus Schertz, and you pay nothing extra for that expertise.

The Buyer Representation Agreement: What You're Actually Signing

Under Texas law and post-settlement rules, agents are required to have a signed Buyer Representation Agreement before showing you homes. This document spells out exactly what compensation the agent is seeking and under what terms.

Think of it like a seed packet — it tells you exactly what you're getting before you plant anything. There are no hidden fees buried in fine print. If a seller is not offering to cover the buyer agent fee, the agreement tells you upfront what that number is and gives you the chance to negotiate it into your offer.

The agreement also locks in your agent's loyalty to you. Without it, an agent technically has no formal duty to represent your interests. With it, you're protected — legally and practically — at every step of the transaction.

The Listing Agent Works for the Seller — Full Stop

This is the single most misunderstood fact in real estate, and it trips up buyers every single day. When you call the number on a yard sign in Cibolo or tap "contact agent" on Zillow, you are reaching out to someone whose job — by law and by contract — is to get the best possible outcome for the person selling that house.

Texas real estate law is clear: the listing agent has a fiduciary duty to the seller. That means they are legally obligated to negotiate the highest price, the most favorable terms, and the fewest contingencies on the seller's behalf. They are not neutral. They are not your advisor. They are the other team's coach.

That doesn't make listing agents bad people — they're doing exactly what they're paid and legally required to do. But walking into that relationship without your own representation is like going to court without a lawyer because the opposing counsel seemed friendly in the hallway.

What Information Can the Listing Agent Share With You?

Here's something that surprises most buyers: there are things a listing agent legally cannot tell you, even if they wanted to. They cannot disclose the seller's bottom line. They cannot tell you the seller has had the home on the market for 90 days and is desperate to sell. They cannot advise you to offer less.

In neighborhoods like The Dominion or in hot subdivisions like Kallison Ranch in Helotes, knowing whether a seller has flexibility can mean the difference between paying list price and saving $15,000. Your buyer's agent can research days on market, price reductions, comparable sales, and neighborhood trends — and use all of that to advise you strategically.

The listing agent can only respond to what you offer. Your buyer's agent helps you decide what that offer should be in the first place.

What Dual Agency Means in Texas — And Why It Should Give You Pause

When a buyer contacts the listing agent directly and that same agent agrees to represent both the buyer and seller in the same transaction, it's called dual agency — or in Texas, it's referred to as an "intermediary relationship." It's legal here, but it comes with real limitations that most buyers don't fully understand before they agree to it.

In an intermediary relationship, the agent can no longer give undivided loyalty to either party. They can't advise the seller to drop the price, and they can't advise the buyer to offer less than asking. They become a facilitator, not an advocate. Gardeners know you can't give one plant all the water and also give the plant next to it all the water — someone ends up dry.

For buyers in competitive situations — say, bidding on a new-build in Alamo Ranch or a resale in the highly sought-after NEISD school zone near Stone Oak — losing that strategic counsel at the exact moment you need it most is a real disadvantage. Having an independent agent who is 100% on your side costs you nothing but gives you everything.

Local San Antonio Details That a Buyer's Agent Actually Knows

Good buyer representation isn't just about contract law — it's about hyper-local knowledge that protects you from expensive surprises. San Antonio's market is enormously varied, and a few miles can mean very different things for your daily life, your property taxes, and your resale value.

For example, homes in Bexar County near Loop 1604 and US-281 give you relatively quick access to the South Texas Medical Center and the airport corridor along US-410. But buyers in Cibolo or Schertz — just past I-35 — are in Guadalupe County, which has a different tax rate structure. That matters for your monthly payment calculation, and it's the kind of thing your buyer's agent flags before you fall in love with a house.

School district boundaries are another critical factor. NEISD, NISD, SAISD, Comal ISD, and Judson ISD all carve through different pockets of the metro. A house on one side of a street can feed into a different school than the house directly across — and your buyer's agent will verify that boundary with the district, not just trust the listing data.

Down Payment Assistance and Local Lending Programs

San Antonio buyers also have access to programs that can further reduce their upfront costs. The City of San Antonio's Homeownership Incentive Program (HIP) offers down payment and closing cost assistance for income-qualifying buyers purchasing within city limits. The Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers programs available statewide, including options for teachers, veterans, and first responders.

Local lenders like Generations Federal Credit Union and Randolph-Brooks Federal Credit Union (RBFCU) offer competitive first-time homebuyer products and are familiar with the San Antonio market's appraisal patterns. Your buyer's agent can help connect you with lenders who understand how to structure offers competitively in this market — not just who has the lowest advertised rate online.

None of this guidance is something the listing agent can provide. They're focused on getting the seller's home sold, not on making sure your financing strategy is optimized for your long-term benefit.

The Negotiation Advantage You're Giving Up

Let's put real numbers on this. The median San Antonio home price in early 2026 is hovering around $299,000. Experienced buyer's agents regularly negotiate reductions, seller-paid closing costs, repairs, or credits that add up to thousands of dollars. A $5,000 closing cost concession, a $3,000 repair credit, or a price reduction after inspection — these are outcomes that happen through skilled representation, not by going it alone.

Your buyer's agent will pull a comparative market analysis (CMA) before you write an offer. They'll review the seller's disclosure notice — the TREC-required document that lists known defects — with a trained eye. They'll recommend a qualified inspector (critical in older Alamo Heights bungalows and in newer construction in Bulverde where foundation and drainage issues can lurk beneath a fresh coat of paint).

Think of it like this: a garden doesn't thrive just because the soil is good. It needs consistent tending, the right conditions, and someone paying attention to the details. A real estate transaction is the same — the right professional watching over your interests at every stage makes the difference between a deal that blooms and one that wilts at the closing table.

After the NAR Settlement: What Changed and What Didn't

Since the NAR settlement took effect in August 2024, the biggest practical change for buyers in Texas is the requirement to sign a Buyer Representation Agreement before touring homes. This was already common practice among professional agents in San Antonio, but it's now a formal requirement across the board.

What did NOT change: sellers can still offer compensation to buyer's agents, and most are still doing so because it opens their listing to the broadest pool of represented buyers. What also didn't change is the fundamental value of having someone legally and contractually obligated to represent your interests — that remains as important as ever.

Some buyers interpreted the settlement news as meaning agents were "no longer getting paid" or that buyers should "just go direct" to save money. That interpretation is incorrect and, frankly, can be financially harmful. The settlement changed how compensation is disclosed and negotiated — it didn't change whose side each agent is on.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a buyer agent cost money in Texas after the NAR settlement?

In most transactions in the San Antonio market, the buyer's agent fee is still covered by the seller as part of the negotiated deal, meaning buyers pay nothing out of pocket for representation. Where a seller does not offer compensation, the fee can often be negotiated into the purchase contract as a seller concession, rolled into closing costs, or addressed through down payment assistance programs. The key difference post-settlement is that the compensation arrangement must be transparently documented in the Buyer Representation Agreement before you tour homes, so there are no surprises. Buyers should always ask their agent upfront how compensation will be handled in their specific situation — a good agent will walk you through it clearly before you sign anything.

Can the listing agent legally represent me as a buyer in Texas?

Yes, it's legally permitted under an arrangement called an intermediary relationship in Texas, but it comes with significant restrictions that limit the agent's ability to advocate for you. In an intermediary situation, the agent cannot advise either party on pricing strategy, cannot share confidential motivations, and essentially becomes a transaction facilitator rather than your advocate. The listing agent also already has an established relationship with the seller and detailed knowledge of the seller's priorities — an information asymmetry that puts you at a disadvantage even if the agent acts with complete integrity. For a transaction as large and legally complex as buying a home, having your own fully dedicated representative is almost always the better choice.

What does a buyer's agent actually do for me when buying a home in San Antonio?

Beyond the obvious tasks of scheduling showings and writing offers, a skilled buyer's agent in San Antonio researches comparable sales to make sure you don't overpay, reviews the seller's disclosure notice for red flags, coordinates with inspectors and lenders, monitors contract deadlines, and negotiates repairs or credits after the inspection. They also bring local knowledge that's genuinely hard to replicate — knowing which streets in Converse back up to TxDOT right-of-way, which new subdivisions in Kyle and New Braunfels have HOA restrictions that affect short-term rental, or which flood zones near Leon Creek require additional insurance. The process from offer to close in Texas involves TREC-promulgated contracts with multiple addenda, option periods, and earnest money mechanics — having a professional manage that timeline protects you from costly mistakes.

Is it a good idea to use the seller's agent when buying a new construction home in San Antonio?

This is one of the most common situations where buyers assume they don't need their own agent — and one of the most important situations where they do. New construction builders' onsite agents work exclusively for the builder; their job is to sell you the home at full price with the builder's preferred lender and minimal concessions. A buyer's agent can review the builder's contract (which is NOT the standard TREC form and is written entirely to protect the builder), negotiate upgrades or closing cost contributions, and recommend an independent inspector before you take possession. Builders in San Antonio's fast-growing northwest corridor — including developments near Culebra Road in Helotes and along US-281 in Bulverde — routinely work with buyer's agents and factor their compensation into the sale price, so you're not adding cost by bringing representation.

How do I know if a buyer's agent in San Antonio is actually looking out for me?

The clearest signal is transparency: a trustworthy buyer's agent will show you the Buyer Representation Agreement, explain the compensation structure in plain language, and answer every question without pressure. They should be willing to walk you through a CMA before you offer, explain their reasoning on price, and tell you honestly when a home is overpriced or has issues — even if that means you don't buy. Ask them how many buyers they've represented in the past year, what neighborhoods they know best, and whether they have relationships with inspectors, lenders, and title companies in San Antonio specifically. An agent who gives you honest, sometimes inconvenient information is worth far more than one who tells you what you want to hear to get to closing.
