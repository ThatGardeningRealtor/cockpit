How to Buy a New Home Before Selling Your Current One — Bridge Loans and Contingencies in Texas

By Chris Drawdy · June 3, 2026 · 9 min read

Timing is everything in real estate—and sometimes you need to move forward before you can let go. Whether you're eyeing a new build in the booming North Central neighborhoods or a charming fixer-upper in Alamo Heights, buying before you sell is entirely possible in Texas. We'll walk you through bridge loans, contingency offers, and the Texas-specific tools that make this transition smooth.

The Core Challenge: Timing Your Move

Most homebuyers face the classic chicken-and-egg dilemma: you've found your dream home in The Dominion or Windcrest, but you haven't sold your current place yet. The market won't wait, and carrying two mortgages isn't always realistic. This timing gap is exactly where bridge loans and contingency strategies step in.

Texas real estate law gives you two main pathways to solve this problem. The first is a bridge loan—short-term financing that lets you buy now and pay it back once your old home sells. The second is a contingency offer, which makes your purchase dependent on your current home selling within a set timeline. Both tools have real costs and real benefits, and choosing the right one depends on your market position and financial comfort.

Think of it like planting in stages: you don't wait for one crop to fully mature before you prepare the soil for the next. With the right strategy, you can overlap both harvests seamlessly.

Understanding Bridge Loans in Texas

A bridge loan is short-term financing that "bridges" the gap between buying a new home and selling your current one. You borrow against the equity in your existing home to fund the down payment and closing costs on the new property. Once your old home sells, the bridge loan is paid off with those proceeds.

Bridge loans are particularly popular in strong seller's markets—like many San Antonio neighborhoods have experienced over the past few years. In competitive situations, sellers often see bridge-funded offers as nearly equivalent to cash offers because the buyer isn't contingent on financing approval.

How Bridge Loans Work in Practice

Let's walk through a real scenario. You own a home in Northolmes worth $350,000 (with about $200,000 in equity). You're ready to buy a $425,000 home in Terrell Hills, but you won't see your current home's sale proceeds for 30–90 days.

A bridge lender advances you funds based on that equity—typically 80% of your home's appraised value. You use that money to close on the Terrell Hills home without waiting for your Northolmes sale to complete. You now own both properties briefly, covering payments on both through that bridge period. Once Northolmes sells, the proceeds pay off the bridge loan, and you're left with just your new mortgage.

The math looks clean on paper, but the costs add up quickly. Bridge loans typically charge 7–12% interest rates (compared to 6–7% on traditional mortgages) plus origination fees of 1–2%. If your bridge period stretches to 6 months, you could pay $8,000–$15,000 in interest alone on a $200,000 bridge loan.

Bridge Loan Costs Broken Down

Interest rates: Most Texas bridge lenders charge 0.5–2% above the current mortgage rate. In early 2026, expect 7.5–10% depending on loan-to-value and your credit profile.

Origination fees: Typically 1–2% of the loan amount. On a $200,000 bridge, that's $2,000–$4,000 upfront.

Appraisal and inspection: Bridge lenders require current appraisals of both your old and new homes ($400–$600 total).

Processing and underwriting: Often $1,000–$2,500 in additional fees. These aren't always disclosed clearly, so ask your lender for a full Loan Estimate.

Property taxes and insurance: You'll pay these on both homes while the bridge is active. For a six-month bridge on a $425,000 home in the NEISD or North East ISD area, expect an extra $1,500–$2,500.

Real example: A $200,000 bridge at 9% for six months costs roughly $9,000 in interest, plus $3,000 in fees, plus $2,000 in taxes and insurance overlap. That's $14,000 to avoid waiting 60 days—which is meaningful but sometimes worth it if the new home won't be available later.

Where to Find Bridge Lenders in San Antonio

Not every lender offers bridge products, and those who do have different risk appetites. Local and national options include PennyMac Home Loan, Pave (San Antonio-based fintech), CrossCountry Mortgage, and some credit unions like Randolph-Brooks Federal Credit Union.

I recommend getting pre-qualified with at least two bridge lenders before you make an offer. You'll learn your true borrowing capacity and lock in rates before the process heats up. Many bridge lenders also have minimum down payment requirements (often 20% on the new home), so confirm your cash position upfront.

Contingency Offers: A Lower-Cost Alternative

If bridge loans feel too expensive, a contingency offer lets you make a non-bridge offer on a new home that's *conditional* on you selling your current home within a specified timeframe. It's a built-in safety net, but it's also less attractive to sellers because it introduces uncertainty.

In Texas real estate contracts, this is typically handled using the Texas Real Estate Commission's (TREC) "Addendum for Property Subject to Sale of Other Property." This form clearly lays out the timeline, what happens if your home doesn't sell, and whether the seller can continue marketing the property you're trying to buy.

Contingency offers cost you nothing upfront—there are no bridge loan fees. But they come with a real risk: if your current home doesn't sell by the contingency deadline, you either lose the new home or have to scramble to bridge suddenly, often at worse terms.

How the Texas Sale of Home Addendum Works

The TREC Sale of Home Addendum is a one-page form that attaches to your purchase contract. It specifies three key things: (1) the property you must sell, (2) the deadline by which it must be sold, and (3) what happens if it isn't.

In San Antonio and Texas generally, the standard timeline is 60–90 days, though competitive markets sometimes see 30–45 day contingencies. The longer your timeline, the more attractive your offer appears—but the more risk the seller takes on.

The addendum also addresses a crucial point: can the seller continue showing the property to other buyers while your contingency is active? In most San Antonio offers, the answer is yes—the seller can accept a backup offer. This means if another buyer comes along with a non-contingent offer, the seller might take it, and you're given 48 hours to remove your contingency or walk away.

Key clause: Many contingencies include language that allows the seller to "cancel and remove" your contract if you don't remove the contingency within 48 hours of notice. This protects the seller but puts pressure on you to either sell your current home or increase your down payment (bridge financing) very quickly.

When Contingencies Work Best

Contingency offers make sense in slower markets or when you have time. Right now in San Antonio, if you're selling a home in a transitional neighborhood (outer Loop 1604, far North Stone Oak) or a home that needs work, a contingency might be your only realistic option—sellers simply won't take non-contingent offers in less competitive areas.

Contingencies also work well if you're upsizing gradually. For example, you're selling a townhome in Alamo Heights for $310,000 and buying a 4-bedroom in North Central for $385,000. The gap is small, and your home is in a strong, liquid market. A 60-day contingency here is often accepted without much pushback.

Conversely, contingencies are nearly impossible to negotiate in competitive zip codes like 78209 (Monte Vista), 78212 (Alamo Heights), and 78209 (Mahncke Park). In these neighborhoods, inventory is tight, and sellers receive multiple non-contingent offers. Adding a contingency here is an uphill battle.

Combining Strategies: The Contingency with Bridge Option

Here's a sophisticated approach many savvy San Antonio buyers use: offer contingent, but have a bridge loan pre-approved and ready. If the seller pushes back on the contingency, you can pivot fast and convert to a non-contingent bridge offer within 24 hours.

This dual-ready approach signals credibility to sellers. You're saying, "I'm flexible and serious—if you need me non-contingent, I can be." Most sellers respect this, and it often leads to negotiation rather than outright rejection.

The cost of having a bridge pre-approval standing by is minimal (maybe $500–$1,000 in appraisal fees), but it buys you enormous negotiating power. This strategy is especially effective in neighborhoods like Windcrest, Stone Oak, and The Dominion, where the market is active but not absolutely frenetic.

Timing Strategies: The Golden Window

Real estate timing isn't mystical—it's mathematical. Here's how to think about it strategically.

Spring and Early Summer (March–June) in San Antonio

This is peak season. Inventory is high, buyer competition is fierce, and homes move fast. If you're selling, this is your golden window—your current home will likely sell quickly, reducing bridge loan duration. If you're buying, contingencies are harder to negotiate, but bridge windows are shorter.

Strategy: If you're buying in this window, seriously consider a bridge loan. Your sale will likely close in 45–75 days, keeping bridge costs manageable. Lock in bridge pre-approval by mid-March.

Fall (September–November)

Sales activity drops slightly, but inventory is still reasonable. Schools have started, so family moves are less common. This is often a sweet spot: less competition, but homes still sell within 60–90 days in established neighborhoods.

Strategy: Contingency offers have a better chance in fall. If you're selling a home in a stable neighborhood (Alamo Heights, Monte Vista, Northeast ISD areas), a 75-day contingency is negotiable here.

Winter and Early Spring (December–February)

This is the slowest season. Homes take longer to sell, and many buyers don't move during the holidays. Contingencies are more acceptable here because sellers have fewer competing offers. But your bridge loan—if needed—will run longer and cost more.

Strategy: If you must close on a new home in winter but haven't sold your current place, this is actually a better time to negotiate contingencies. However, if you go the bridge route, budget for 5–7 months of bridge interest.

Real-World San Antonio Scenarios

Scenario 1: The Alamo Heights to Terrell Hills Move

You're selling a $340,000 home in Alamo Heights (strong market, quick sale expected) and buying a $420,000 home in Terrell Hills that has an urgent closing date.

Best approach: Bridge loan. Your Alamo Heights home will likely sell in 30–60 days given the neighborhood's strength (median days on market: 25–35 days). The bridge costs ($10,000–$12,000 for 60 days) are worth the certainty and speed. You close on Terrell Hills immediately, bridge for 6–8 weeks, and pay it off when Alamo Heights closes.

Scenario 2: The Windcrest to North Central Expansion

You're selling a $280,000 townhome in Windcrest and buying a $350,000 home in North Central (Zilker Hill area). The new home is in-demand territory, but not desperately urgent.

Best approach: Contingency offer with bridge pre-approval backup. Windcrest is liquid (25–45 days on market), so a 60-day contingency is credible. Make the offer contingent, but mention your bridge pre-approval in your cover letter. Most North Central sellers will respect this. If they counter with a 45-day contingency, you're comfortable because Windcrest homes move fast. If they push for non-contingent, activate your bridge within 24 hours.

Scenario 3: The Fixer-Upper Flip (Stone Oak to Downtown Living)

You're selling a fixer-upper in Stone Oak that needs work (harder sale, likely 80–120 days on market) and buying a $475,000 urban condo near downtown.

Best approach: Strong contingency with transparent communication. Stone Oak is further out and less liquid than central neighborhoods, so sellers understand longer sales timelines. Use a 120-day contingency and be honest in your offer letter: "We're committed to selling our Stone Oak property within 120 days and are already marketing it aggressively." Some sellers will negotiate; others won't. Don't bridge here—the carrying costs over 4 months would exceed $18,000. Better to wait or negotiate a delayed closing date.

Key Legal and Financial Considerations in Texas

Your Appraisal Must Support the Bridge

Bridge lenders won't advance funds based on your *hoped-for* home sale price. They appraise both your current home and your new purchase. If your current home appraises lower than expected, your available bridge amount shrinks—sometimes by tens of thousands.

Before pursuing a bridge, get a pre-appraisal estimate of your current home's value from a local appraiser. In San Antonio, this costs $300–$400 and takes 3–5 days. A few hundred dollars upfront beats discovering mid-transaction that your bridge doesn't work.

Your Credit and Debt-to-Income Ratio Matter

During the bridge period, you're technically liable for two mortgages. Lenders calculate your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) using both properties. If you're at the edge of approval already, adding a bridge payment can push you over the limit.

Most lenders require DTI under 43–50% during the bridge period. Talk to your lender about your exact capacity before making an offer. Some bridge lenders are more flexible than traditional mortgage banks—Pave and Blend, for example, sometimes go higher on DTI for qualified borrowers.

Texas Law and Your Sale of Home Addendum

The TREC addendum is a legal document. Once both you and the seller sign it, it's binding. If you miss your contingency deadline (e.g., you said you'd sell your home by July 31, and you didn't), the seller can enforce the contract and force you to close on the new home—or sue you for damages.

Always use the official TREC form (not a custom template), and always have a real estate attorney review contingencies in Texas before signing. I work with several excellent real estate attorneys here in San Antonio who specialize in contingency negotiations—I'm happy to refer you.

Homeowner's Insurance and Property Taxes

During the bridge period, you'll have two insurance policies and two tax bills. Make sure your insurance covers both properties and that you've budgeted for the overlap. In Bexar County, property taxes for a $400,000 home run roughly $4,800–$5,400 annually; for six months, expect $2,400–$2,700. Factor this into your bridge cost analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a bridge loan with less than 20% equity in my current home?

Yes, but with limitations. Most bridge lenders will advance up to 80% of your home's appraised value, regardless of your remaining mortgage. However, if your home is worth $300,000 and you owe $280,000, you only have $20,000 in equity—so your bridge would be capped at $24,000 (80% of $300k minus your $280k mortgage). Some specialty lenders go higher, but expect stricter requirements and higher rates. Before assuming a bridge is viable, have a lender review your equity position on your current home.

What happens if my current home doesn't sell by the contingency deadline in Texas?

Your contingency expires, and you have 48 hours to remove it or lose the contract. If you remove it, you're legally bound to close on the new home without the sale of your current property—meaning you'll need to bridge, find alternative financing, or come up with additional cash. If you don't remove it within 48 hours, the seller can cancel the contract and keep any earnest money deposit (typically 1–2% of purchase price). To protect yourself, set your contingency deadline conservatively—give yourself 75–90 days even if you think your home might sell in 60.

Are there any San Antonio-specific bridge loan programs or grants?

Not directly. However, some Texas credit unions and community lenders offer slightly lower bridge rates for members. Randolph-Brooks Federal Credit Union (headquartered in San Antonio) sometimes offers bridge loans to members at 0.5% below market rates. Additionally, some first-time homebuyer programs through COSA (City of San Antonio) or non-profits like SAISD's down payment assistance programs don't directly cover bridges, but they can free up cash for a bridge down payment. Ask your lender about any local programs; your real estate agent should also know about community-specific tools.

If I use a contingency offer, will the seller keep showing the home to other buyers?

Yes, almost always. Your TREC contingency addendum allows the seller to market the property and accept backup offers. This is actually standard and protects the seller from tying up their property. What it means for you: if a second buyer comes in with a non-contingent offer, you'll get a 48-hour notice to remove your contingency or walk away. This is why having a bridge pre-approval ready is smart—if a competing offer appears, you can quickly pivot to non-contingent status and stay in the game. Don't view the seller accepting backup offers as a red flag; it's just good business on their part.

How do I avoid paying bridge loan interest if my home sells quickly?

Most bridge loans charge interest only on the days the funds are drawn. If you close the bridge after 30 days instead of 90, you pay 30 days of interest, not 90. That said, bridges are typically structured with a minimum interest charge (e.g., 60 days' interest regardless) or a prepayment penalty. Always ask your lender about prepayment terms upfront. Some lenders waive minimum interest if your current home closes early; others charge the full term. A lender comparison here can save you thousands. For example, one lender might charge 60-day minimum interest ($4,500), while another charges interest-only with no minimum (potentially $2,000 if you close in 30 days).
