San Antonio Property Taxes Explained: What Every Buyer and Homeowner Needs to Know

By Chris Drawdy · June 18, 2025 · 11 min read

Texas has no state income tax — but property taxes are how the state funds almost everything, and in San Antonio they can catch buyers off guard if nobody walks them through the math first. This guide breaks down exactly how Bexar County property taxes work, what the homestead exemption saves you, how to protest an unfair appraisal, and what to realistically budget neighborhood by neighborhood. Think of it as planting the right knowledge before you put down roots.

How Texas Property Taxes Actually Work — The Big Picture

Texas funds local government almost entirely through property taxes. There is no state property tax; instead, every taxing entity in your area — your school district, city, county, hospital district, community college — sets its own rate, and those rates stack on top of each other to form your total bill.

In San Antonio and Bexar County, that typically means you're paying into the City of San Antonio, Bexar County, a school district (NISD, NEISD, SAISD, EISD, FISD, HISD, or others depending on where you buy), San Antonio River Authority, University Health System, and Alamo Colleges. Each entity votes on its own tax rate annually, usually expressed in dollars per $100 of taxable value.

The Bexar Appraisal District — known locally as BCAD — is the independent office that determines what your home is worth for tax purposes. They appraise; the taxing units set rates; the Bexar County Tax Assessor-Collector sends the bill. These are three separate functions, and understanding the difference matters when something goes wrong.

What "Appraised Value" vs. "Market Value" Means

BCAD is required by Texas law to appraise your home at 100% of market value as of January 1st each year. In a rising market, your appraised value can jump dramatically even if you haven't done a single thing to the property — and that's exactly what triggered a wave of protests across Bexar County in 2022 and 2023.

The good news is that once you file a homestead exemption, a 10% annual cap kicks in. BCAD cannot raise the taxable value of your home by more than 10% per year, no matter how hot the market gets. That cap is one of the most valuable protections Texas homeowners have — and it's one reason buying and staying put makes long-term financial sense here.

Breaking Down Your Total Tax Rate by San Antonio Neighborhood

The single biggest variable in your property tax bill isn't your home's value — it's which school district sits beneath it. School district levies typically make up 40–55% of your total rate, so a $350,000 home in Northside ISD (NISD) will have a noticeably different annual bill than the same home just a few miles away in North East ISD (NEISD) or Judson ISD.

As a rough guide for 2024 tax year rates: homes in NISD territory (think Helotes, Leon Valley, parts of the Medical Center corridor) see combined rates around $2.10–$2.25 per $100. NEISD areas like Alamo Heights adjacent neighborhoods and parts of Stone Oak run similar ranges. Communities in the Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD (SCUCISD) or Judson ISD corridor off I-35 near Converse and Live Oak can see rates closer to $2.25–$2.55 depending on the city layer added.

Municipalities also matter. The City of San Antonio's 2024 rate was approximately $0.3417 per $100. But if you're buying in a smaller incorporated city like Windcrest, Converse, Selma, or Leon Valley, you'll have that city's rate stacked on instead — sometimes higher, sometimes lower. Unincorporated Bexar County areas skip the city layer entirely, which can meaningfully reduce your bill.

A Real-World Example

Let's say you're buying a $375,000 home in the Sonterra area near Stone Oak, sitting in NEISD and the City of San Antonio. After your $100,000 homestead exemption, your taxable value drops to $275,000. At a combined rate of roughly $2.20 per $100, you're looking at approximately $6,050 per year — about $504 per month escrowed.

That same price point in an unincorporated part of western Bexar County near Helotes, in NISD, might come in around $5,200–$5,500 annually once the city layer disappears. Over a 30-year mortgage, that difference is real money. I always run these numbers for my buyers before we ever write an offer.

The Texas Homestead Exemption: How to File and What You Save

The homestead exemption is the single most important tax step you can take as a new homeowner in Texas, and it's completely free to apply for. As of 2023, the state increased the standard homestead exemption to $100,000 off your home's appraised value for school district taxes — a significant jump from the prior $40,000 exemption that was in place for decades.

To qualify, the home must be your primary residence as of January 1st of the tax year. You file once with BCAD — not annually — and the exemption stays as long as you live there. You can file online at bcad.org or submit a paper form. There's no income threshold; any homeowner using the property as their primary residence qualifies.

On a $375,000 appraised home, that $100,000 exemption saves you roughly $1,750–$2,000 per year depending on your school district rate. Think of it like the first crop of savings that just keeps coming back every season — you plant it once and it keeps producing.

Additional Exemptions Worth Knowing

If you're 65 or older, you get an additional $10,000 off the county tax and, more importantly, your school district taxes are frozen at the level they were when you first qualified. That freeze is transferable if you move to another Texas home — a fact many long-time homeowners don't realize.

Veterans with a 100% VA disability rating pay zero property taxes in Texas on their primary residence — a powerful benefit that affects a significant portion of San Antonio's homeowner population given the presence of Joint Base San Antonio. Partial disability ratings from 10–90% receive graduated exemptions. The City of San Antonio also offers a local optional homestead exemption of 20% off appraised value, stacked on top of the state exemption.

How to Protest Your Property Tax Appraisal in Bexar County

Every year in late March or early April, BCAD mails appraisal notices. If your value jumped and it doesn't feel right, you have the right to protest — and tens of thousands of Bexar County homeowners do it successfully every year. The deadline is May 15th, or 30 days from the date on your notice, whichever is later.

The fastest path is the online informal protest through BCAD's iFile system at bcad.org. Many homeowners settle at the informal stage without ever going to a formal Appraisal Review Board (ARB) hearing. BCAD appraisers review comparable sales you submit and often agree to a reduction when the evidence is solid.

If you don't settle informally, you move to an ARB hearing — a formal panel of citizens (not BCAD employees) who hear your case. You'll present comparable sales, photos of condition issues, or a professional appraisal if you have one. It's less intimidating than it sounds, and the process is designed for regular homeowners to navigate without an attorney.

Building Your Evidence: What Actually Works

The strongest evidence is recent closed sales of comparable homes in your immediate neighborhood — same general square footage, age, and condition — that sold for less than your appraised value. Pull these from HAR.com, Zillow's sold listings, or ask your agent to run a quick CMA (comparative market analysis) using MLS data. MLS comps carry more weight than public-facing sites.

Condition is the second strongest argument. If your home has deferred maintenance, an aging roof, foundation issues, or outdated systems that the neighbors don't have, document that with photos and repair estimates. BCAD appraises at the neighborhood level in bulk — they don't always know your specific home's quirks. That's your opening.

Third-party protest firms are also an option and fairly common in San Antonio. Companies like O'Connor & Associates or Ownwell work on contingency — they take a percentage of your first-year savings, nothing if they don't reduce your value. For investment properties or busy homeowners, it's a reasonable trade. For primary residences, the informal process is usually simple enough to handle yourself.

After a Successful Protest

If BCAD agrees to reduce your value, that new number becomes the baseline for next year's 10% cap calculation — another reason to protest early in homeownership, when the cap clock is still ticking in your favor. Keep a copy of your protest settlement; you'll want it for reference the following year.

What New Buyers Should Budget for Property Taxes at Closing

One of the most common surprises I see with buyers — especially those relocating from states with lower property taxes — is the escrow setup at closing. Your lender will typically collect 2–3 months of estimated property taxes upfront into escrow, plus the prorated portion of the current year.

Here's where it gets tricky: in the year you purchase, BCAD may still have the previous owner's tax bill on record — possibly lower if they had a senior freeze or if the home was undervalued. Your lender estimates based on current assessed value, but your first full-year bill after you file your homestead exemption may look different. Budget for a potential escrow shortfall in year two.

For a $350,000 home in San Antonio with a combined rate around $2.20, expect to see $600–$700 per month in your PITI payment just for taxes and insurance combined. At a $450,000 price point, that escrow component can reach $800–$950/month. I always share a full payment breakdown — not just principal and interest — when we're evaluating whether a home fits your budget.

Texas Property Tax Relief Programs You Might Be Missing

Beyond exemptions, Texas offers a few lesser-known relief mechanisms. The Tax Deferral for Seniors and Disabled Persons program allows qualifying homeowners to defer all property taxes — no limit — until the property is sold or transferred. Interest accrues at 5% annually, but no penalties apply. This is a genuine lifeline for retirees on fixed incomes who are house-rich but cash-constrained.

The Residence Homestead Tax Ceiling also applies to heirs who inherit and occupy a homesteaded property where the deceased owner was 65 or older. The tax ceiling transfers, preserving that frozen school tax rate for the inheriting family member. San Antonio's large multigenerational family culture makes this provision more relevant here than in many other Texas metros.

Bexar County also participates in the State's Truth in Taxation transparency website (texastransparency.org), where you can see exactly how each taxing unit's rate has changed year over year and compare them to neighboring counties. It's dry reading, but empowering — like checking the soil before you plant. Knowing your ground is never wasted effort.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the homestead exemption deadline in Texas, and what happens if I miss it?

Texas law allows you to file a homestead exemption application late — up to two years after the delinquency date for the tax year you're claiming — so missing the initial window isn't catastrophic. However, you'll pay full taxes in the interim and won't have the 10% appraisal cap protection during those uncovered years, which in a rising market can cost you real money. Filing promptly after closing is strongly advisable; BCAD's online portal at bcad.org makes it straightforward. If you bought in late 2024, you can still file for the 2025 tax year as long as the home was your primary residence on January 1, 2025. Some title companies remind you at closing, but many don't — so put it on your calendar.

Do property taxes go up automatically every year in San Antonio?

Not automatically — but in practice, yes, for most homeowners during a rising market. BCAD reappraises all properties annually at market value, and in competitive San Antonio neighborhoods like Alamo Ranch, Stone Oak, Timberwood Park, and Schertz, values have risen significantly over the past several years. The 10% annual cap on homesteaded properties provides a real ceiling, but even a 10% annual increase compounding over five years is substantial. Taxing entity rates can also fluctuate — school districts, the city, and the county each vote on their rates annually, and while Truth in Taxation laws require a rollback election if the effective revenue increase exceeds 3.5%, rates still move. Staying engaged with your annual BCAD notice and your local entities' budget hearings is the best long-term protection.

Is it worth hiring a property tax protest company in Bexar County?

For investment properties, rental homes, or homeowners who genuinely don't have time to gather comps and attend a hearing, protest services can absolutely pay for themselves — especially on higher-value properties where even a modest percentage reduction saves significant dollars. Most contingency-based firms charge 25–50% of the first year's tax savings, with no fee if unsuccessful. For a primary residence valued under $400,000, the informal online protest process through BCAD is usually manageable on your own with two hours of prep work and a handful of comparable sales. The critical thing either way is not to let an unreasonable appraisal go uncontested — BCAD's bulk appraisal model isn't perfect, and unchallenged high values become the new baseline for future caps. Even a small reduction in year one compounds favorably over the life of your homeownership.

How do San Antonio property taxes compare to Austin or Houston?

Bexar County's effective property tax rates are generally comparable to Travis County (Austin) and Harris County (Houston), all hovering in the 2.0–2.5% range on market value — though Austin's dramatically higher home prices mean dollar-for-dollar annual bills there tend to be larger despite similar percentage rates. San Antonio's relative affordability actually makes the tax burden more manageable in absolute terms; a $350,000 San Antonio home and a $700,000 Austin home at similar effective rates produce very different annual bills. All three metros benefit from Texas's 2023 homestead exemption increase and the 10% appraisal cap. One meaningful San Antonio-specific advantage: a sizable portion of the metro falls outside city limits in unincorporated Bexar County, eliminating the city tax layer entirely — something less common in Austin's more densely incorporated geography.

What happens to property taxes when I sell my San Antonio home?

Texas property taxes are paid in arrears — your 2025 tax bill isn't due until January 31, 2026. When you sell, the closing settlement statement includes a proration where you credit the buyer for the portion of that year's taxes attributable to your ownership period, even though the bill hasn't arrived yet. The proration is typically calculated using the prior year's final tax bill as a baseline, sometimes with a negotiated percentage adjustment if values are expected to change. Your homestead exemption and any senior or disability exemptions do not transfer to the buyer — they expire when you sell. The buyer must file their own exemptions, which is something I make a point of reminding every buyer I close with, because that first year without the exemption filed can produce an unexpectedly large escrow shortfall.
