What Happens When the Appraisal Comes In Low — Your 4 Options as a Buyer or Seller in Texas

By Chris Drawdy · March 6, 2026 · 10 min read

A low appraisal can feel like a late frost right before harvest — you've done everything right, and then something outside your control threatens the whole deal. But a low appraisal doesn't have to kill your transaction. In this guide, you'll learn exactly what a low appraisal means in Texas, what your four real options are as a buyer or seller, and how to protect yourself before you ever get to that conversation.

Why Low Appraisals Happen in San Antonio's Market

San Antonio's real estate market moves fast — especially in corridors like the Loop 1604 expansion zone, the 151 corridor toward Alamo Ranch, and established neighborhoods inside Loop 410 like Woodlake, Olmos Park, and Castle Hills. When buyer demand outpaces the pace at which closed sales become public record, appraisers are sometimes working with comparable sales (comps) that don't yet reflect current prices.

Appraisers are required to use arm's-length closed sales, typically within the last 90 days and within a one-mile radius when possible. In fast-appreciating zip codes like 78245, 78253, or 78260, that backward-looking methodology can produce a number that feels out of step with what buyers are actually paying right now.

Low appraisals also happen because of legitimate issues — a seller who overpriced, a buyer who overbid in a heated multiple-offer situation, or a home with unusual characteristics that make it hard to comp accurately. Understanding the root cause matters, because it shapes which of your four options is most likely to work.

Understanding the Texas Contract Framework Before You React

In Texas, the standard TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract addresses appraisal through Paragraph 2B — the "Loan Assumption" or financing contingency section, and separately through the buyer's right to terminate under the Third Party Financing Addendum. If you're using a conventional, FHA, or VA loan, this addendum gives you protection when the appraised value comes in below the purchase price.

Specifically, the Third Party Financing Addendum states that if the property doesn't appraise at or above the sales price, the buyer can terminate and receive their earnest money back — but only if that addendum is properly executed and the buyer acts within the defined notice period. Missing that window can cost you your earnest money, which in San Antonio typically runs 1% of the purchase price, or $2,500–$5,000+ on a median-priced home.

Cash buyers don't have a lender-driven appraisal requirement, but many still order independent appraisals for protection. If you're a cash buyer and waived any appraisal contingency, you're in a different — and more vulnerable — position. Always read what you signed before assuming you have an exit.

What the Gap Actually Looks Like in Numbers

Say you're under contract on a home in Cibolo near Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD for $385,000. The appraiser comes back at $370,000. That's a $15,000 gap. Your lender will now only lend based on $370,000, meaning you'd need to bring an extra $15,000 to closing — on top of your down payment — to make the deal work at the original price.

On a 5% down conventional loan, you were already planning to bring roughly $19,250. Now you'd need $34,250 in cash at closing. For most buyers, that's a deal-changer. That's exactly why knowing your options before this moment — not during it — makes all the difference.

Option 1 — Renegotiate the Purchase Price

The most common resolution to a low appraisal in Texas is a straightforward price reduction. The seller agrees to drop the purchase price to the appraised value, the loan proceeds as normal, and both parties move forward. This is the cleanest outcome — but it requires a seller who is motivated, realistic, and understands the market.

In San Antonio's more balanced neighborhoods — think Converse, Universal City, or parts of the South Side near Palo Alto College — sellers are often more willing to negotiate because days-on-market are longer and they have fewer backup offers waiting. In tight-inventory areas like Stone Oak or Helotes, sellers may push back harder, knowing another buyer could be right behind you.

Your agent's job here is to present the appraisal clearly, make the case calmly, and help the seller understand that a new buyer will likely face the same appraisal result. A different buyer doesn't mean a higher appraisal — the market is the market.

Option 2 — Pay the Appraisal Gap Out of Pocket

If you love the house and have the cash reserves, you can simply cover the difference between the appraised value and the purchase price yourself. You're not overpaying in your own mind — you're choosing to pay what the home is worth to you, even if it's more than what the appraiser determined.

This is where appraisal gap coverage clauses come in. In competitive San Antonio markets — particularly when bidding on newer construction in communities like Kallison Ranch, Sonterra, or Alamo Ranch — some buyers include an appraisal gap clause in their initial offer. This clause states that the buyer agrees to cover up to a specified dollar amount if the appraisal falls short, giving the seller confidence the deal won't fall apart.

An appraisal gap clause might read: "Buyer agrees to pay up to $10,000 above appraised value." This strengthens your offer significantly in a multiple-offer situation without completely waiving your appraisal protection. It's a tool worth discussing with your lender first — you need to confirm you actually have those reserves and that they won't affect your debt-to-income or loan qualification.

Option 3 — Challenge the Appraisal with a Formal Reconsideration of Value

Appraisers are professionals, but they're working with limited time and sometimes limited local knowledge. If you believe the appraiser missed relevant comps, made a factual error, or failed to account for significant upgrades, you have the right to request a Reconsideration of Value (ROV). This is a formal process — not just an email saying "I think it's worth more."

A strong ROV submission includes 3–5 comparable sales the appraiser didn't use, a clear explanation of why those comps are more relevant (similar square footage, lot size, school district, year built, and proximity), and documentation of any improvements the appraiser may have undervalued — a newer roof, updated HVAC, a recently renovated kitchen, or an outdoor living space with a pergola and built-in kitchen. That last one matters more than people think in San Antonio's outdoor-living culture.

Your lender handles the formal submission to the appraiser — you can't contact the appraiser directly under federal HVCC rules. The turnaround is typically 5–10 business days. ROVs don't always succeed, but in cases where the appraiser genuinely missed a recent comp or made a square footage error, they can and do result in a revised value.

What Makes a Strong ROV in the San Antonio Market

Local nuance matters in an ROV. If the appraiser pulled comps from a neighborhood across a major arterial like Culebra Road or Bandera Road, but your subject property is in a higher-demand pocket, your agent can make that case in writing. School district boundaries are powerful here — a home zoned to Northside ISD versus a neighboring district can carry a measurable premium.

Factual errors are the easiest wins. A miscalculated gross living area, a missing garage conversion, or a failure to note a pool can all justify a revision. Your agent should pull the county appraisal district records through the Bexar County Appraisal District (BCAD) at bcad.org to cross-check square footage and feature data against what the appraiser reported.

Option 4 — Walk Away and Protect Your Earnest Money

Sometimes the right move is to let the deal go. If the appraisal gap is too large to bridge, the seller won't negotiate, and your ROV didn't succeed, walking away — with your earnest money intact — is a legitimate and sometimes wise choice. The key word is "intact." This only applies if you followed the contract terms precisely and exercised your termination rights within the required timeframe.

In Texas, if your Third Party Financing Addendum is properly in place and you provide written notice of termination within the deadline (often tied to the closing date or a specific contingency period), your earnest money is returned. Waiting too long, or not following the exact notice procedures, can mean forfeiting those funds to the seller.

Don't let the sunk cost of inspections, appraisals, and time pressure you into a deal that doesn't make financial sense. Think of it like a gardener who pulls a struggling plant before it drains resources from the whole bed — it's not failure, it's good judgment. The right home at the right price is worth waiting for.

What Sellers Can Do When the Appraisal Comes In Low

Sellers aren't powerless in a low appraisal situation — they just have fewer direct levers to pull. The most productive first step is to ask your agent to review the appraisal report carefully. If the appraiser used comps from a different subdivision, ignored recent upgrades, or made a factual error, your agent can work with the buyer's lender to support a Reconsideration of Value with better data.

If the appraisal appears accurate, sellers have a choice: reduce the price, offer a partial concession (splitting the gap with the buyer), or hold firm and risk the buyer walking. In San Antonio's current market, holding firm makes sense in highly competitive areas with active demand — Timberwood Park, Shavano Park, and parts of Leon Valley have seen consistent buyer competition. In softer submarkets, flexibility is the smarter path.

Sellers can also take a proactive step before listing: order a pre-listing appraisal. At $450–$700, it's a modest investment that tells you exactly where the market values your home before you go under contract. It can also serve as supporting documentation if a buyer's appraisal comes in lower — providing your own independent third-party value is a legitimate tool in a dispute.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a seller refuse to lower the price after a low appraisal in Texas?

Yes, a seller is under no legal obligation to reduce the price just because an appraisal came in low — the Texas TREC contract doesn't force them to negotiate. However, if the buyer has a properly executed Third Party Financing Addendum and provides timely written notice of termination, they are entitled to their earnest money back. The seller's realistic choice is then between renegotiating or returning to the market, knowing that any new buyer using financing will likely face the same appraised value. In practice, sellers who refuse to negotiate in a situation with an accurate low appraisal often find themselves relisting and accepting a lower price anyway. It's rarely a winning strategy unless the seller has strong evidence the appraisal was wrong.

How does an appraisal gap clause work in Texas and when should I use one?

An appraisal gap clause is language added to your offer — typically in the Special Provisions section of the TREC contract or as a separate addendum — stating that you'll cover a specified dollar amount above the appraised value if there's a shortfall. It does not waive your right to terminate entirely; it defines a ceiling on how much extra you're willing to bring. You should consider using one in high-demand San Antonio neighborhoods where sellers are fielding multiple offers and need assurance that financing won't kill the deal. The amount you include should be directly tied to what you've confirmed with your lender that you can bring to closing without jeopardizing your loan qualification. Your lender should review any gap clause language before you submit your offer.

How long does a Reconsideration of Value take in Texas?

A formal Reconsideration of Value (ROV) typically takes 5–10 business days from the date your lender submits it to the appraiser, though timelines vary by lender and appraiser workload. During that time, your contract's financing or closing deadlines continue to tick, so your agent should communicate with the seller or listing agent about a possible extension if needed — most reasonable sellers will agree to a short extension while an ROV is pending. It's worth noting that even a successful ROV rarely bridges a large gap completely; increases of $5,000–$15,000 are more common than a full revision to purchase price. If the original appraisal was in the right ballpark but missed one key comp, an ROV has a reasonable chance of succeeding. If the appraiser's methodology was sound and the gap reflects a genuine pricing issue, the ROV is unlikely to change the outcome significantly.

Does FHA or VA appraisal work differently than conventional when it comes to a low appraisal?

Yes — FHA and VA appraisals have additional layers that conventional appraisals don't. FHA appraisals include a property condition component; the appraiser can flag required repairs that must be completed before the loan closes, separate from the value issue. VA appraisals are conducted by VA-assigned appraisers and also include Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs) that must be met. Importantly, VA loans have a specific rule called the VA Escape Clause (also called the amendatory clause), which gives VA buyers the right to walk away without penalty if the appraised value is below the purchase price — regardless of other contract terms. For FHA and VA buyers in San Antonio, the seller's willingness to address condition items and negotiate price may be even more critical than it is for conventional buyers, and listing agents should advise their sellers accordingly when reviewing offer types.

What happens to earnest money if a deal falls through due to a low appraisal in Texas?

If the buyer properly invokes their termination rights under the Third Party Financing Addendum — specifically citing the appraisal shortfall and doing so within the required timeframe — the earnest money is returned to the buyer and released by the title company, typically within a few business days. The key risk is a missed deadline: if the buyer fails to send written notice within the contract's specified period, the earnest money may be forfeited, and the seller could potentially pursue it. In San Antonio, earnest money is typically held by a title company like Chicago Title, Alamo Title, or Independence Title, and requires a signed release form from both parties before disbursement. If there's a dispute, neither party can simply demand the funds — both must agree or a court must decide. This is why having an agent who tracks your contract deadlines carefully is not optional; it's essential protection for one of the largest assets you'll move in a transaction.
