The Broken Mentorship System in Real Estate — And Why So Many Good Agents Quit

By Chris Drawdy · December 29, 2025 · 9 min read

You've probably heard the statistic: 87% of real estate agents quit within five years. But here's what brokerages won't tell you — most of those agents weren't weak or unmotivated. They were handed a license, pointed toward the market, and left to figure it out alone. This post breaks down why the mentorship system is broken, what real support actually looks like, and why the agents who leave aren't failures — they're casualties of a system that promises growth but delivers isolation.

The License Promise vs. The Reality

Most real estate brokerages in San Antonio market their training programs as comprehensive and supportive. You hear promises about "full onboarding," "ongoing coaching," and "a team environment where everyone wins together." Then you get your license, and the door closes quietly behind you.

The truth is, passing your real estate exam is the easiest part of becoming a successful agent. Your broker will give you access to the MLS, a desk, and maybe a 30-minute orientation on how to fill out contracts. But they won't teach you how to generate leads, negotiate offers, manage client relationships, or survive the psychological weight of rejection—which is constant in real estate.

This isn't intentional cruelty. Brokerages operate on a simple model: hire many agents, keep whoever sticks, and move on. The assumption is that "good salespeople" will figure it out. But real estate isn't just sales—it's relationships, local knowledge, market timing, and emotional resilience all at once.

Why Brokerages Don't Invest in Real Mentorship

Here's the uncomfortable economics of the brokerage business: mentorship requires time, and time is money. A broker paying an experienced agent to mentor newcomers is a direct cost with no guaranteed return.

In San Antonio's real estate market, brokerages thrive when they have volume—lots of agents chasing deals in neighborhoods like Alamo Heights, Stone Oak, and the North Loop. If one agent quits and takes their sphere of influence with them, that's unfortunate, but the next batch of licensing candidates is already on their way. The math works when you're dealing with high turnover: recruit 10 agents, six quit, but those four who stick generate enough commission to justify the losses.

Brokerages also face a perverse incentive: if you invest heavily in mentoring an agent and they become successful, they might leave for another brokerage offering a slightly better commission split. So why invest at all?

The result is a system where new agents are expected to succeed in spite of their brokerage, not because of it.

What Good Agents Experience That Bad Systems Don't Provide

Agents who thrive in real estate typically have one of three advantages: they inherited a client list, they came from a family legacy in the business, or they found a mentor who cared enough to invest in their growth. The third group is rarest—and that's the problem.

A real mentor in real estate does things that sound obvious but are almost never institutionalized. They sit with you while you make your first cold calls. They roleplay difficult client conversations so you're not caught off guard. They introduce you to lenders, home inspectors, and contractors you can actually trust—not just anyone with a business card.

They also normalize failure. New agents often assume that losing a deal means they're bad at sales. A good mentor says, "That deal fell apart because the appraisal came in low, and here's how you handle that conversation the next time." They provide context that saves years of painful trial and error.

The San Antonio-Specific Problem

San Antonio's real estate market is booming, which makes the mentorship gap worse. With inventory moving fast and buyer competition fierce in neighborhoods like Dominion, Legacy, and Churchill High School's district, new agents feel like they should be closing deals immediately. When they're not, they blame themselves instead of blaming the system that set them up to fail.

The average home price in San Antonio is now around $325,000, up from $298,000 just two years ago. That means transactions are more complex, financing is tighter, and one misstep can cost buyers tens of thousands of dollars. New agents are making these mistakes because no one taught them the local lender landscape, the appraisal quirks of specific neighborhoods, or how to navigate VA loan complications—which is especially important in San Antonio, given our military presence.

Our city also has quirks that require local knowledge. A house in Terrell Hills carries different expectations than one in Southton. The commute from Stone Oak to downtown is measurably different depending on traffic patterns. These are things you only learn from someone who's already failed on them.

The Emotional Cost of Isolation

People often talk about real estate burnout in terms of long hours and rejection. Those are real, but they're not the core problem. The core problem is doing it alone.

New agents typically work in isolation for months. They cold-call, leave voicemails that go unanswered, send emails that get deleted. They sit at their desk watching other agents close deals, wondering what they're doing wrong. Meanwhile, no one is checking in, asking how they're doing, or reminding them that slow starts are normal.

The psychological impact is severe. Real estate is one of the few professions where your income is entirely based on your own hustle, and you're competing directly with the people sitting next to you. That's a recipe for isolation, even when you're surrounded by colleagues.

Good mentorship isn't just about teaching—it's about validation. It's someone saying, "I've been here, I survived, and you will too." Without that voice, many good people simply decide the emotional cost isn't worth it.

What Real Mentorship Looks Like

Real mentorship in real estate requires structure, time, and genuine care. It's not a one-time thing; it's an ongoing relationship that evolves as the agent grows.

Month 1–3: Foundation

A mentor should help new agents build foundational systems before they're expected to close deals. This means teaching them how to set up their CRM, how to organize client files, how to track leads, and how to manage their calendar. It sounds boring, but without systems, everything else fails.

Month 3–6: Client Acquisition

Now it's time to help them build their sphere of influence. This is where many agents get stuck—they don't know how to ask for referrals, how to network in San Antonio's neighborhoods, or how to create a reputation locally. A mentor can introduce them to their existing sphere, show them how to stay in touch with past clients, and help them build relationships with the service providers who send referrals (inspectors, contractors, lenders).

Month 6+: Deal Execution

By now, deals are coming. A mentor should review contracts, help with negotiations, prepare the agent for closing, and help them learn from every transaction. They're also checking in on the agent's mental health—how are they handling the wins and losses? Are they burning out?

Why the Best Agents Often Create Their Own Mentorship

Many successful real estate agents didn't wait for their brokerage to mentor them. They found mentors outside the system—other agents, industry coaches, mastermind groups. They paid for training, joined expensive coaching programs, and invested in their own education because their brokerage wouldn't.

This creates an unfair advantage based on money and initiative. If you have the means to hire a $500/month coach while making $16,000 in your first year, great. If you don't, you're already behind. That's not a meritocracy—it's a system that rewards people who can afford to compensate for what their brokerage doesn't provide.

The agents who quit aren't the ones who lacked talent. They're often the ones who didn't have the financial runway, the family support, or the luck of stumbling into someone willing to help.

What Change Would Actually Look Like

Real reform in real estate mentorship would require brokerages to accept lower turnover in exchange for better retention and production. It means assigning experienced agents to mentor newcomers, paying them fairly for that time, and measuring success not by the number of agents recruited, but by the number who stay and thrive.

It also means brokerages owning the narrative. Instead of pretending that all agents can succeed with minimal support, they could say: "We're investing in our agents' growth because it pays off for everyone." This builds loyalty, reputation, and actual talent development.

Some brokerages in San Antonio are starting to get this right, but they're the exception. The bigger firms still operate on the old model: high recruit, high churn, extract value, repeat.

How to Identify a Brokerage That Actually Mentors

If you're considering getting your real estate license, here's what to look for in a brokerage that actually invests in its agents:

1. Ask about their mentorship program specifically. Not "training"—mentorship. These are different. Training is a class. Mentorship is an ongoing relationship with someone who has skin in the game.

2. Ask to talk to a new agent (not someone chosen by the broker). What's their experience been like in their first six months? How often do they talk to a mentor or manager? Are they being coached through deals or just told to figure it out?

3. Look at retention rates. If a brokerage recruits 20 agents per month but only 3 stay past their first year, that's not a brokerage issue—that's a system designed to fail people.

4. Ask about ongoing education. Real brokerages should offer continuing education beyond the license renewal minimum. They should teach market analysis, negotiation, technology, and client service as ongoing skills.

5. Check if they have a real estate attorney or closing coordinator on staff. New agents will mess up transactions if they don't have expert oversight. If the brokerage doesn't provide this, you're on your own.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do real estate agents make in their first year in San Antonio?

Most new agents in San Antonio earn between $16,000 and $25,000 in their first year, according to industry data. Some earn nothing if they don't close a deal. However, this varies wildly based on their starting sphere of influence and the local market. An agent with wealthy family connections might close multiple deals quickly, while one starting from zero might not make their first sale until month 8 or 9. These early earnings need to cover licensing costs, continuing education, technology, marketing, and personal living expenses—which is why many agents can't survive the first year financially.

What percentage of new real estate agents actually quit before five years?

Approximately 87% of new real estate agents leave the industry within five years. The attrition is heaviest in years one and two, with about 50% quitting before the end of their first year. This statistic has remained relatively consistent for decades, which suggests the problem is systemic rather than cyclical. When an industry loses nearly nine out of every ten new entrants, you can't blame the individuals—you have to look at the system that's failing them.

What's the difference between a brokerage and an independent mentor for new agents?

A brokerage is responsible for compliance, licensing, and handling transaction paperwork, but they have a financial incentive to recruit more agents and tolerate high turnover. An independent mentor (often a successful agent or paid coach) has no financial stake in keeping you at their brokerage and can give you honest feedback without corporate pressure. Many successful agents hire independent coaches because brokerages are optimized for recruiting, not developing. The ideal scenario is working at a brokerage with a strong mentorship culture, but if that's not available, hiring an outside mentor is often worth the investment.

Is it harder to succeed as a new real estate agent in a hot market like San Antonio?

Counterintuitively, yes. In a hot market with lots of inventory turnover, new agents feel pressure to close deals immediately, but they lack the foundation to do it well. They rush through due diligence, miss inspection issues, or get surprised by appraisal gaps. In slower markets, agents are forced to slow down and build relationships, which often leads to better long-term success. San Antonio's current market (median home price around $325,000 and rising) means transactions are higher stakes and mistakes are more costly. Without good mentorship, new agents are under pressure to perform quickly with limited knowledge.

How do I know if I should quit being a real estate agent vs. stick it out?

The decision depends on whether you're struggling because of the system or because you're genuinely not suited for the work. If you've been cold-calling for three months with no leads, that's likely a system failure (you don't have a lead generation strategy). If you've closed deals but hate the work, that's genuine misalignment. Before quitting, spend 30 days focused on one specific area that's holding you back—whether that's lead generation, negotiations, or deal management. If you have support from a mentor or coach and you're still not seeing progress after six months, then it might be time to leave. But if you're struggling in isolation, get support first before deciding you're not cut out for it.
