Buying Land in South Texas: Utilities, Access, Structures, and Financing — What Nobody Tells You

By Chris Drawdy · June 5, 2026 · 11 min read

Buying land in South Texas feels like standing in front of a blank garden bed — full of potential, but the work is in the preparation you do before you ever plant a seed. Most buyers fall in love with the acreage and the sunsets, then get blindsided by a $40,000 well, a lender who won't touch raw land, or a "road" that's really just a neighbor's good intentions. This guide covers every layer of the land-buying process — utilities, access, structures, financing, and due diligence — with the real numbers and local specifics that most agents skip over.

Why South Texas Land Is Different From Anywhere Else in the State

South Texas isn't the Hill Country, and it isn't the Piney Woods. The geology, aquifer access, caliche soil, and land-use history here create a very specific set of challenges and opportunities that buyers relocating from other parts of Texas — or from out of state — consistently underestimate.

The Edwards Aquifer recharge zone cuts through counties like Medina and Bexar, which means certain parcels carry strict restrictions on what you can build, how you can handle wastewater, and whether impervious cover is limited. Before you get attached to a piece of ground, you need to know whether it sits on the recharge zone, the contributing zone, or completely outside it — because that one fact shapes everything from your septic options to your land value resale.

Atascosa, Wilson, and McMullen counties farther south tend to have deeper water tables, thornier brush, and a more agricultural or recreational character. These are deer lease country, dove hunting territory, and increasingly, homesteads for people commuting up US-281 or I-37 into San Antonio. Each county has its own permit office, its own soil conditions, and its own quirks — and that's exactly why local knowledge matters more here than almost anywhere.

The Utility Reality: Water, Power, and Waste in Rural South Texas

Think of utilities as the root system of your land purchase. You can't see them from the road, but everything else depends on them. Getting them wrong — or not budgeting for them — is the single most common reason land buyers end up underwater before they ever break ground.

Water: Wells, Hauling, and Rural Water Systems

Municipal water simply doesn't exist on most rural parcels in South Texas. Your options are a private water well, a rural water supply corporation (RWSC), or hauling — and each comes with a very different price tag and reliability profile.

Drilling a water well in South Texas can cost anywhere from $15,000 to over $45,000 depending on how deep you have to go. In Medina County, you might hit water at 200 feet; in parts of Atascosa, drillers are going 600–800 feet and coming up with brackish water that requires a treatment system on top of the well cost. Always ask for well logs from neighboring properties before you buy — any reputable driller or the Texas Water Development Board's well database can pull historical records.

Rural water supply corporations like the Medina County WCID or the Atascosa-McMullen RWS provide treated water to rural customers, but they don't serve every parcel and there are often waiting lists and connection fees ranging from $2,000 to $8,000 just to tap in. Confirm with the RWSC directly — don't take the seller's word for it that a connection is available.

Electricity: GVEC, Bandera Electric, and the Cost of Running Line

If a power line doesn't already run to the parcel, you're looking at paying for line extension — and GVEC (Guadalupe Valley Electric Cooperative), Bandera Electric, or Medina Electric will charge you per foot of extension beyond a certain baseline, often $8–$15 per linear foot after the first 300–500 feet. On a remote 20-acre parcel, that could easily run $10,000–$30,000 before you've touched the ground.

Solar-plus-battery systems are increasingly viable for off-grid builds in South Texas given the sun exposure, but they add $20,000–$60,000 to your build budget. Factor that in honestly rather than treating it as a fallback plan.

Wastewater: Septic Systems and the OSSF Permit Process

Every county in the region handles On-Site Sewage Facility (OSSF) permits differently. Bexar County's Environmental Services department is stricter than Wilson or Medina, especially near the recharge zone where aerobic systems are often required instead of conventional septic — and aerobic systems cost $12,000–$20,000 versus $8,000–$12,000 for conventional. You'll need a site evaluation and soil analysis before the county will even issue a permit.

Medina County is generally more permissive, which is one reason it's popular for homesteaders. But "more permissive" doesn't mean "no rules" — the state's Chapter 285 rules still apply, and a failed perc test can kill your ability to build on a lot entirely. Order the perc test as a contract contingency, not an afterthought.

Road Access and Easements: The Part That Causes the Most Lawsuits

A parcel can look completely accessible on a satellite map and be legally landlocked. This happens more often in South Texas than buyers expect, because land has been subdivided, inherited, and traded informally for generations — and the paperwork doesn't always reflect how people actually get around.

Legal access means a recorded easement or direct frontage on a public road — not a handshake with the neighboring landowner, not a caliche trail that's been there "forever," and not a county road that was never formally accepted into the county's maintenance system. Before closing, your title company should pull a title commitment that specifically identifies how legal access is established. If it can't be confirmed, that is a title defect and the seller needs to cure it.

Private roads and shared easements add another layer of complexity. Who maintains the road? Who pays for caliche when it washes out? Is there a road maintenance agreement recorded in the deed records? These are not small questions — I've seen buyers inherit a road situation that costs $15,000 to fix the first year after purchase because no one asked. If the parcel requires crossing a neighbor's land to reach the county road, you want that easement to be appurtenant (attached to the land, not personal to one individual) and recorded at the county clerk's office.

Existing Structures: What to Watch For and What to Be Grateful For

A barn, a water storage tank, a mobile home, an old ranch house — existing structures on raw land can be a gift or a liability, sometimes both at once. The key is knowing which you're dealing with before you close.

Older structures on rural land often have no permits on file because permits weren't required when they were built, or because the county simply didn't track rural construction the way municipalities do. That's usually fine for a barn or a storage building. It becomes a problem when you want to finance the land (lenders will require the structure to meet habitability standards if you're getting a construction loan) or when the structure has unpermitted electrical that creates an insurance or safety issue.

Manufactured homes are a special category. If a mobile or manufactured home sits on the property, you need to know whether its title has been retired (converted to real property and recorded in deed records) or whether it's still titled as personal property with the Texas DMV. An unretired title on a manufactured home is a title defect that will prevent conventional financing and can complicate closing significantly. Check with your title company early.

Agricultural Exemptions: Don't Let Them Lapse

Many rural parcels in South Texas carry an agricultural or wildlife tax exemption (ag exemption) that dramatically reduces the property tax bill — sometimes by 80–90%. These exemptions run with the land use, not automatically with ownership. If you purchase land with an active ag exemption and change the use — or simply fail to maintain qualifying activity — you can trigger a "rollback tax" of up to five years of back taxes at the non-exempt rate, plus interest.

Qualifying activities include cattle grazing, hay production, beekeeping, and wildlife management — the last of which has become popular because it's flexible. The Texas Parks and Wildlife Department has an approved wildlife management plan template that many Bexar, Medina, and Bandera county landowners use. Talk to a local ag extension agent at the Texas A&M AgriLife Extension office in Castroville or Pleasanton before you assume the exemption transfers seamlessly.

Raw Land Financing in Texas: How It Actually Works

Financing raw land is nothing like financing a house. Most national banks won't touch it. The lenders who will tend to move slower, charge higher rates, and want larger down payments. Knowing the landscape ahead of time keeps you from wasting weeks with lenders who ultimately can't help you.

Farm Credit and AgTexas: Your Best Local Options

AgTexas Farm Credit and Capital Farm Credit are the two most borrower-friendly options for raw land in South Texas. They understand agricultural land, they have offices in the region (AgTexas has a San Antonio office off I-10 West; Capital Farm Credit covers much of the Hill Country fringe), and they'll lend on parcels that conventional banks won't touch. Down payments typically run 20–35% for land with some improvements, higher for truly raw ground.

Rates through Farm Credit are competitive but not as low as a 30-year mortgage — expect them to float closer to commercial loan rates, and terms are usually 10–20 years. The upside is that they understand things like easement access, ag exemptions, and caliche roads. You won't have to explain why there's no municipal water.

Texas Veterans Land Board: A Huge Advantage If You Qualify

The Texas Veterans Land Board (VLB) land loan program is one of the most underutilized benefits available to Texas veterans. Eligible veterans can borrow up to $150,000 for land purchases at below-market interest rates with as little as 5% down — and the program specifically allows raw, rural land. The land must be at least one acre and in Texas, which covers essentially every parcel in our area.

San Antonio has one of the largest veteran populations in the country, and yet I consistently meet buyers who didn't know this program existed. If you've served, contact the VLB directly at 1-800-252-8387 or check GLO.Texas.gov before you approach any other lender — the savings over the life of the loan can be substantial.

Owner Financing: More Common Than You Think

Many South Texas landowners — particularly older families who've held land for decades — are open to owner financing, especially on smaller tracts under 50 acres. Terms vary widely but typically involve 10–30% down, a 5–10 year balloon, and an interest rate negotiated between buyer and seller. Owner financing bypasses lender appraisal requirements and can close much faster, but you absolutely need a real estate attorney to draft or review the contract — not just a standard TREC form.

Due Diligence: The Checklist You Can't Skip

Buying land is like preparing a garden bed — skip the soil test, and everything you plant will struggle. Due diligence on a rural parcel isn't optional; it's the difference between a great investment and a very expensive lesson.

At minimum, your due diligence period should cover: a boundary survey (not just the GIS map), a title commitment reviewed by your attorney, confirmation of legal road access, a perc test if you plan to build, verification of the water source and any RWSC connections, confirmation of utility availability and extension costs, and a review of any deed restrictions, HOA covenants, or pipeline easements recorded against the property. Pipeline easements are especially common in South Texas — they run across thousands of acres and can restrict where you build and what you do with a significant strip of your land.

Flood zone status matters too, even on land that looks dry. FEMA maps for Medina Creek, the Atascosa River corridor, and various unnamed arroyos in the region can put seemingly high-and-dry land in a special flood hazard area. Pull the FEMA FIRM map for the parcel and verify — flood insurance on raw land is uncommon, but flood zone status affects your future build permit and your lender's requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to build a house on raw land in South Texas after purchase?

Beyond the land price itself, buyers should budget $80,000–$200,000 in infrastructure costs before a single foundation board is poured — covering well drilling, septic installation, electrical line extension, a road base or driveway, and site clearing. Custom home construction in the San Antonio metro fringe runs $150–$250 per square foot for stick-built homes as of mid-2026, not counting the site work. Manufactured and modular homes are a more affordable entry point, but lender requirements and county permits still apply. Getting competitive bids from contractors in the specific county — not just San Antonio-based builders — usually saves 10–20% because local crews understand local conditions. Always get a full project cost estimate before finalizing your land budget so you're not land-rich and cash-poor.

Can I use a VA loan to buy land in Texas?

Standard VA home loans cannot be used to purchase raw land on its own — the VA loan program requires that the property include a home the veteran will occupy as a primary residence. However, if you're simultaneously purchasing land and constructing a home with a VA construction-to-permanent loan, that is allowed, though finding a lender who offers VA construction loans in Texas can be challenging. The Texas Veterans Land Board program is entirely separate from VA loans and is specifically designed for land-only purchases, making it the better tool for veterans who want to buy now and build later. Some veterans use both programs in sequence — VLB for the land, then a VA construction loan once they're ready to build. A lender who specializes in veteran financing in San Antonio, like USAA's mortgage division or Security Service Federal Credit Union, can map out which path makes the most sense for your situation.

What is the difference between an ag exemption and a wildlife exemption in Texas, and which is easier to maintain?

An agricultural exemption requires active qualifying use — livestock grazing, farming, timber, beekeeping, or similar activities — and the land must meet minimum acreage and intensity standards set by the county appraisal district. A wildlife management exemption (sometimes called a wildlife valuation) allows landowners to shift from traditional ag use to wildlife conservation practices like habitat management, supplemental water sources, predator control, and census counts. The wildlife exemption has become popular in South Texas because it doesn't require livestock and fits well with smaller tracts of 10–200 acres where running cattle isn't economically practical. Both valuations must be applied for and approved by your county appraisal district — the Central Appraisal District of Bexar County, Medina CAD, Wilson CAD, or Atascosa CAD depending on where your land sits. The wildlife exemption generally requires an approved management plan but is considered lower-maintenance by many small landowners.

How do I find out if land in South Texas has mineral rights included?

In Texas, mineral rights and surface rights can be — and very often have been — severed, meaning a previous owner sold the surface but retained the oil, gas, and mineral interests beneath it. To determine what's being conveyed with a land purchase, you need a title search that includes a mineral chain of title, not just a surface ownership chain. Your title company can run this search, though not all do it automatically — you may need to request it specifically and pay a small additional fee. If the minerals have been severed and there are active oil and gas leases, that can mean surface use agreements, pipeline easements, and potentially drilling activity on your land that you have very limited ability to stop. South Texas sits in or near the Eagle Ford Shale play, making mineral rights a very real consideration in counties like Karnes, Atascosa, and McMullen especially.

What recreational land near San Antonio is best for hunting and outdoor use?

For deer and dove hunting combined with reasonable proximity to San Antonio, Medina, Bandera, and Real counties offer the classic Hill Country blend of live oak mottes, senderos, and managed brush — with commute distances of 45–90 minutes to the city. Atascosa and McMullen counties farther south are known for trophy whitetail and thick South Texas brush country habitat, favored by serious deer hunters, but they're a 90-minute-plus drive from downtown SA. Wilson County offers a sweet spot of affordability and accessibility along the US-87 corridor toward Floresville and Stockdale, with a growing number of small recreational tracts in the 10–50 acre range. Bandera County land near Medina Lake or the Sabinal River corridor adds water access to the outdoor recreation equation, which drives prices higher but also rental income potential if you're considering a hunting lease to offset costs. Always verify that your intended recreational use — including hunting blinds, food plots, and ATV trails — is compatible with any recorded deed restrictions on the parcel before you make an offer.
