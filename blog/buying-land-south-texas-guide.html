Buying Land in South Texas: Utilities, Access, Structures, and Financing — What Nobody Tells You

By Chris Drawdy · May 16, 2026 · 11 min read

Buying raw land in South Texas feels a little like starting a garden from scratch — the potential is real, but the prep work nobody warns you about can make or break the whole project. From hauling water to a dry Atascosa County tract to navigating a lender who's never touched a raw land deal, the hidden obstacles are what trip up most first-time land buyers. In this guide, I'm breaking down exactly what you need to know about utilities, road access, existing structures, and financing before you put a single dollar on the table.

Why South Texas Land Buying Is a Completely Different Animal

Most people who've bought a house in San Antonio assume land works the same way. It doesn't. When you're shopping tracts in Wilson County near Floresville, or recreational land in Frio County outside Pearsall, you're operating in a market with its own rules, its own risks, and its own vocabulary.

Unlike a home purchase where the infrastructure is already in place, raw land is a blank canvas — and a blank canvas still has a price tag for every brushstroke. The well, the septic, the road, the power line — none of that is guaranteed, and all of it costs real money before you ever park a camper or break ground on a house.

South Texas specifically throws in a few extra curveballs: caliche soil that can complicate septic systems, brush-heavy terrain that requires clearing, and rural water districts with long waitlists. Going in with clear expectations isn't just smart — it's the difference between a great investment and a very expensive lesson.

The Real Cost of Utilities on Raw Land

The listing says "electricity nearby." That phrase can mean the power line is fifty feet away or half a mile away. Before you ever make an offer, call the serving utility — CPS Energy handles much of Bexar County, but once you move into Atascosa, Medina, or Wilson counties, you're often in Bandera Electric Cooperative, Karnes Electric, or Guadalupe Valley Electric (GVEC) territory. Each co-op has its own cost structure for extensions.

Electric Line Extensions

Expect to pay between $10 and $30 per linear foot to bring power to your meter base, depending on terrain and the co-op's current material costs. A half-mile extension can run $26,000–$79,000. Some co-ops will credit a portion back if you're a permanent residence, but recreational land buyers often get no credit at all.

Solar plus battery storage is increasingly viable for remote South Texas tracts, especially south of Hwy 97 where sun exposure is exceptional. A properly sized off-grid system can run $20,000–$40,000 but eliminates the extension cost entirely and adds a compelling selling feature down the road.

Water: Wells, Rural Water, and Hauling

Groundwater in South Texas comes from the Carrizo-Wilcox Aquifer in the eastern counties and the Edwards and Trinity aquifers further west. Drilling depth — and therefore cost — varies dramatically by location. In parts of Atascosa County you might hit water at 200 feet; in Medina County you could be drilling 600+ feet. Always ask for neighbor well logs before committing.

Rural water districts like the Bexar Metropolitan Water District (BexarMet) and Atascosa Rural Water Supply serve parts of the region, but availability isn't universal and connection fees can exceed $3,000–$6,000. Some buyers haul water for years while they save up for a well — a 1,500-gallon cistern system runs about $2,000–$4,000 installed and keeps you functional short-term.

Septic Systems and Soil Testing

The Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) requires a site evaluation before you can permit a septic system. In South Texas, caliche and clay-heavy soils often fail perc tests, pushing buyers toward more expensive aerobic treatment units (ATUs) instead of conventional systems. Budget $8,000–$15,000 for a standard system and up to $20,000 for an ATU in challenging soil.

Bexar County has its own Environmental Services department that oversees septic permitting for unincorporated areas — their process is more streamlined than in some rural counties, but still requires a licensed installer and inspector approval. Don't assume a prior owner's old septic meets current code; many older systems on South Texas land are non-compliant and will need replacement.

Road Access: The Overlooked Deal-Killer

I've watched buyers fall hard for a piece of land only to discover they couldn't legally access it. Legal access and physical access are two very different things. A caliche road on the aerial photo doesn't mean you have a deeded easement to use it.

Deeded Easements vs. Prescriptive Access

Before making an offer on any South Texas tract, pull the title commitment and look specifically for recorded access easements. If the property is landlocked — meaning it has no direct road frontage and no recorded easement — you're looking at a potential legal battle or a costly negotiated easement purchase just to get in and out.

Prescriptive easements (essentially claiming a right of way through long use) are possible under Texas law but require litigation to establish. That process is expensive and uncertain. If a seller says "we've always just used that road," that's a red flag worth investigating before you're emotionally invested.

County Road Maintenance and Private Road Agreements

Many rural tracts in Atascosa, Frio, and Medina counties sit off county roads that are only intermittently maintained. After a significant rain event on the Balcones Escarpment, those caliche roads can become impassable. If access depends on a private road shared with neighbors, get a written road maintenance agreement before closing — verbal agreements don't hold up when relationships sour.

TxDOT maintains state highways and the counties handle their own road networks, but county budgets are stretched thin in rural South Texas. Drive the access route yourself in wet conditions if you can, and ask neighbors honestly about seasonal accessibility. This is especially important for recreational land in Frio County that you're counting on reaching during deer season.

Existing Structures: Value or Liability?

A lot of South Texas land listings come with "improvements" — a word that covers everything from a solid deer blind to a condemned hunting cabin with knob-and-tube wiring. Understanding what an existing structure actually represents legally and financially changes how you negotiate the deal.

Unpermitted Structures and Code Issues

In unincorporated county areas, building permits are not always required — but that doesn't mean structures are necessarily safe or insurable. An older barn or cabin that predates county codes might be fine structurally, or it might have serious issues a home inspector would flag. Always hire a licensed inspector to evaluate any structure, even a small one, before closing.

If a structure was built inside a floodplain without proper elevation certificates, you may be inheriting a FEMA compliance problem. FEMA flood map data for South Texas rural areas has been updated repeatedly — check the current FIRM maps through the FEMA Map Service Center and cross-reference with the county floodplain administrator before assuming you're in the clear.

Manufactured Homes and HUD Titles

Many rural South Texas properties have older manufactured homes on them. These homes have separate HUD titles (like a vehicle title) in addition to the real property deed. If the manufactured home hasn't been properly "titled to real property" through a process called affixation, your lender may not count it as real estate — which complicates financing significantly.

Confirming that a manufactured home has been affixed to the land and that the HUD title was properly retired with the Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs (TDHCA) is a non-negotiable step before financing. I've seen deals fall apart at the closing table because this wasn't resolved early. Your title company should catch it, but don't wait — ask the question on day one.

Financing Raw Land in Texas: Lender Reality vs. What You've Heard

Here's the honest truth: most conventional mortgage lenders don't know how to do raw land loans, and the ones who try often make it painful. Land financing in Texas is a specialty product, and going to the right lender from the start saves months of wasted effort.

Farm Credit Lenders and AgTexas

AgTexas Farm Credit and Capital Farm Credit are the two dominant agricultural lenders in South Texas and they genuinely understand rural land. They serve counties from Bexar all the way down to the border and routinely finance tracts that conventional banks won't touch. Their rates are competitive and their appraisers actually know what land in Atascosa or Wilson County is worth.

Capital Farm Credit, headquartered in Bryan but with offices in San Antonio, will typically finance recreational land with as little as 20–25% down if the property has some agricultural use or hunting value. AgTexas has a strong presence in the Laredo and Cotulla corridors and handles a lot of the Frio County hunting ranch market.

Texas Veterans Land Board (VLB) Land Loans

If you're a Texas veteran, the Texas Veterans Land Board offers one of the best-kept secrets in land financing: land loans up to $150,000 at below-market interest rates with as little as 5% down. The property must be at least one acre and must be in Texas, but there's no requirement that it be your primary residence or have a home on it.

The VLB also offers home improvement loans and home loans, which can work in combination if you plan to eventually build. Their San Antonio regional office is accessible and responsive — this program is genuinely underutilized by the veteran community here. If you served, this is the first call you should make.

Seller Financing and What to Watch For

Seller financing is common in rural South Texas land sales, particularly on smaller tracts under 20 acres. It can be a legitimate path forward, but buyer protections matter enormously. Always use a title company, always record the deed of trust, and always have an attorney review the contract terms.

Balloon payments — where a large lump sum comes due after 3–5 years — are standard in seller-financed land deals. Make sure you have a clear plan for refinancing or paying that balloon before it's due. Buyers who don't plan ahead end up in a difficult renegotiation with a seller who has all the leverage.

Due Diligence Checklist Before You Close

Think of due diligence on land like preparing soil before planting — skip the prep and you're setting yourself up for failure no matter how good the seed is. Here's what every South Texas land buyer should verify before releasing their option period.

Start with a current survey. Many rural tracts haven't been surveyed in decades, and fences don't always follow property lines. A new survey from a licensed Texas surveyor confirms your actual boundaries and identifies any encroachments. Budget $1,500–$4,000 depending on acreage and terrain.

Check for mineral rights conveyance. South Texas sits over active oil and gas formations, and many properties have severed mineral estates — meaning someone else owns what's underground. That's not always a dealbreaker, but you should know it going in and understand the surface use agreement protections available to you under Texas law.

Verify the property's ag exemption status with the county appraisal district. Atascosa CAD, Wilson CAD, and Medina CAD all handle this locally. Inheriting an ag exemption dramatically lowers your annual property taxes — losing one (or never having one) can mean a tax bill 10–20x higher than expected. Confirm the requirements to maintain or establish the exemption for your intended use.

Recreational Land, Hunting Leases, and Income Potential

South Texas is one of the most prized whitetail deer hunting regions in North America. Tracts in Frio, LaSalle, Webb, and Zavala counties consistently produce trophy-class deer, and that hunting value is a real, quantifiable part of the property's worth. Understanding how to leverage it — or protect it — is part of owning land intelligently here.

Hunting lease income can offset your carrying costs significantly. A well-managed 200-acre Frio County property might generate $8,000–$20,000 per season in lease income, depending on deer herd quality and improvements like senderos and water features. That income doesn't happen by accident — it requires active management, but it's a real return while you hold the asset.

If you're buying south of San Antonio along the IH-35 corridor — say, south of Cotulla toward Laredo — also keep an eye on infrastructure growth and energy sector activity. The Eagle Ford Shale play has changed land valuations in LaSalle and Webb counties, and proximity to major transportation routes adds long-term upside beyond the recreational value alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a loan to buy raw land in Texas with no house on it?

Yes, but you'll need to work with lenders who specialize in it rather than standard mortgage companies. Agricultural lenders like Capital Farm Credit and AgTexas are your strongest options in South Texas, offering competitive rates for both recreational and agricultural raw land. The Texas Veterans Land Board is another excellent resource for qualifying veterans, with down payments as low as 5%. Expect lenders to require 20–50% down on raw land, higher interest rates than residential mortgages, and shorter loan terms — typically 15–25 years rather than 30. Lining up your financing before you start shopping seriously will save you from falling for a property you can't actually fund.

How do I know if a South Texas property has water rights or a good well location?

Water rights in Texas are governed by the doctrine of "rule of capture" for groundwater, meaning landowners generally have the right to pump from beneath their property — but this is subject to Groundwater Conservation District rules, which vary by county. In South Texas, check with the Evergreen Underground Water Conservation District (serves parts of Atascosa, Frio, La Salle, and McMullen) or the applicable local GCD for any pumping restrictions or permit requirements. Before drilling, hire a licensed water well driller to pull neighbor well logs from the Texas Water Development Board's online database — these free records show nearby well depths, yields, and water quality indicators. A professional hydrogeological assessment ($500–$1,500) is money well spent on any tract where water is uncertain.

What is an agricultural exemption and how do I keep it on land I buy in Texas?

An agricultural exemption (technically an agricultural valuation) means the county appraises your land based on its agricultural productivity rather than its market value — the tax savings can be dramatic, often reducing your taxable value by 80–95%. To qualify, the land must be used for an agricultural purpose that meets the minimum intensity standards for your county: cattle grazing, hay production, beekeeping, and wildlife management are all common qualifying uses in South Texas. Most counties require 5–7 years of consistent ag use to establish the exemption from scratch, though if the current owner has it, you can inherit it without that waiting period. If you change the land use — say, by subdividing or building a commercial structure — you may trigger a "rollback tax" covering the past five years of tax savings plus interest. Confirm the current status and requirements with the relevant county appraisal district before closing.

What should I look for when buying land near San Antonio for future development or homesteading?

For homesteading or future building within commuting distance of San Antonio, the sweet spot is generally along the Highway 97/87 corridor toward Floresville in Wilson County, or south on Highway 16 into Atascosa County near Pleasanton — both offer reasonable commute times to Loop 410 while keeping land prices significantly below Bexar County. Check ETJ (Extraterritorial Jurisdiction) boundaries carefully — land inside a municipality's ETJ is subject to their subdivision platting rules even if it's not yet annexed, which affects how you can divide or develop it. Soil type matters enormously for a homestead: have a perc test done and review the NRCS Web Soil Survey before committing, especially if you're planning a large garden, food forest, or livestock operation. Also verify that the tract's deed restrictions (if any) and any applicable CCRs don't prohibit the structures or animals you're planning — some rural subdivisions have surprisingly restrictive covenants.

Are there any Texas state programs that help with buying rural or recreational land?

Beyond the Texas Veterans Land Board discussed earlier, the Texas Department of Agriculture administers several programs supporting beginning farmers and ranchers that can include loan assistance and technical support, though these are production-focused rather than pure purchase programs. The USDA Farm Service Agency (FSA) offers the Direct Farm Ownership loan program for beginning farmers, with loans up to $600,000 and down payments as low as 5% — the San Antonio FSA office on NW Military Highway serves Bexar and surrounding counties. The USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) also offers cost-share programs like EQIP (Environmental Quality Incentives Program) that can fund brush management, water development, and wildlife habitat improvements once you own the land. Texas A&M AgriLife Extension, with offices in most South Texas counties, provides free consultations on ag management plans, wildlife management valuations, and soil health — an underused resource that can help you maximize both the productivity and tax efficiency of your tract.
