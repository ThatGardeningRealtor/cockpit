What to Expect From a Home Inspection in San Antonio: A Buyer's Complete Guide

By Chris Drawdy · July 7, 2025 · 11 min read

A home inspection is one of the most important steps in buying a San Antonio home — and one of the most misunderstood. In this guide, you'll learn exactly what Texas inspectors examine, which local issues show up most often in Bexar County homes, how to read your report without panicking, and how to use it to negotiate repairs or credits like a pro.

Why the Home Inspection Step Matters More in San Antonio Than You Might Think

San Antonio's unique geography, climate, and housing stock create a specific set of challenges that buyers from other parts of the country often don't anticipate. We have expansive clay soils that swell and shrink with rain cycles, extreme summer heat that punishes roofs and HVAC systems, and a housing inventory that ranges from post-WWII bungalows in Mahncke Park to brand-new construction in Converse and Cibolo.

The inspection contingency in your Texas purchase contract is your built-in protection during the option period — the negotiated window of time (typically 5–10 days in today's SA market) when you can walk away for any reason. Think of it like the germination stage in gardening: this is when you dig in, examine what's really beneath the surface before you commit to the full growing season.

Skipping or rushing the inspection to appear more competitive is a mistake I've seen cost buyers tens of thousands of dollars. A few hundred dollars and a few hours of your time can reveal problems that would otherwise be invisible until they're very expensive.

What Does a Texas Home Inspector Actually Check?

Texas home inspectors are licensed and regulated by TREC, and they follow a standardized scope that's more comprehensive than many states require. Your inspector will evaluate the home's major systems and components — not just give the place a quick walk-through.

Structural and Foundation Systems

Foundation inspection is arguably the most critical item in San Antonio. Inspectors look for cracks in slab foundations, uneven floors, sticking doors and windows (classic signs of shifting), and separation at wall-to-ceiling joints. They'll also evaluate the grading around the home — water that pools near the foundation accelerates the clay soil movement that causes so many issues in Bexar County.

If the inspector flags foundation concerns, they'll typically recommend a follow-up evaluation by a licensed structural engineer, which costs an additional $300–$600 but is absolutely worth it. Many San Antonio neighborhoods — including parts of Leon Valley, Helotes, and older sections of Alamo Heights — have homes built on highly active Expansive clays that require ongoing management.

Roofing, Attic, and Insulation

San Antonio roofs take a beating. Inspectors examine shingles for granule loss, curling, and storm damage; flashing around chimneys, vents, and skylights; and the condition of gutters and fascia boards. Our hailstorm season — particularly active in spring — means many roofs have insurance-claim history that's worth investigating.

Attic access allows the inspector to check insulation levels, ventilation, and any signs of past or active roof leaks. In our climate, adequate attic ventilation is crucial — a poorly ventilated attic in a Texas July can reach 160°F, accelerating shingle deterioration and dramatically increasing cooling costs.

HVAC Systems

Heating and cooling systems get a thorough review: the inspector will check the age of the equipment (most SA HVAC units last 12–18 years before efficiency drops significantly), look for proper airflow and refrigerant signs, examine ductwork for leaks, and test the thermostat response. Given that San Antonio cooling season runs roughly April through October, a failing AC unit is not a minor issue.

Inspectors also check the furnace heat exchanger for cracks — a safety concern, not just a mechanical one. If your home has an older system, budget $4,000–$8,000 for replacement as part of your financial planning even if the inspector says it's currently functional.

Plumbing, Electrical, and Everything Else

Inspectors test all accessible plumbing fixtures for flow and drainage, look for signs of leaks under sinks and around water heaters, and check the water heater age and condition. In older San Antonio neighborhoods like Southtown, King William, and Monte Vista, cast iron drain lines are common and can be prone to root intrusion or deterioration — a sewer scope (a separate, optional test) is often worth adding.

The electrical panel is examined for proper breaker sizing, double-tapping, and safety concerns like Federal Pacific or Zinsco panels — older brands known for fire risk that still show up in homes built before the 1990s. Inspectors also test a representative sample of outlets, check GFCI protection in kitchens and bathrooms, and look for visible wiring issues.

Beyond these major systems, inspectors evaluate windows and doors, garage doors and openers, visible insulation, exterior siding and trim, decks and patios, fireplaces, and built-in appliances. It's genuinely comprehensive — which is why it takes several hours and produces a report that can run 50–100 pages with photos.

Common Issues Specific to San Antonio and Bexar County Homes

After working with buyers across neighborhoods from Stone Oak and Shavano Park to Lackland Terrace and Floresville, I've seen certain issues come up again and again in this market. Knowing what to expect helps you stay calm when the report arrives — and helps you focus on what actually matters.

Foundation Movement

This is the big one. San Antonio sits on a mix of soil types, with significant Expansive (shrink-swell) clay in many areas. Virtually every older home in SA shows some degree of foundation movement — the key question the inspector (and potentially a structural engineer) answers is whether that movement is historical and stable, or active and progressing. Minor cosmetic cracking is extremely common; significant structural compromise is not, but it does happen.

Pier-and-beam foundations, more common in historic neighborhoods like Highland Hills and some areas near UTSA's original campus, have their own set of concerns: wood rot, inadequate ventilation in the crawl space, and shifting piers. These are fixable problems, but they need to be priced into your offer.

Roof Age and Storm Damage

San Antonio gets its share of severe weather. Inspectors frequently flag roofs that are past their serviceable life or show impact damage that should have triggered an insurance claim but didn't. A roof replacement in San Antonio currently runs $8,000–$18,000 depending on size and materials — significant enough that you want to know about it upfront.

One thing many buyers don't know: if a roof has hail damage, the seller's homeowner's insurance may still be able to file a claim even at the time of sale, potentially covering the replacement. Your inspector's findings and photos can support that process.

Plumbing and Sewer Line Issues

Older San Antonio homes — especially those built before 1980 in neighborhoods like Beacon Hill, Monticello Park, and the area around Fort Sam Houston — often have original cast iron or galvanized steel pipes. These can develop corrosion, root intrusion, or bellying (low spots that collect waste) over decades. A basic inspection won't catch everything underground, which is why a sewer scope for $150–$250 is often one of the best investments a buyer can make.

HVAC Wear and Undersized Systems

With summers routinely hitting 100°F and above, an aging or undersized HVAC system is both a comfort issue and a utility bill issue. Inspectors note the age and apparent condition, but a full HVAC assessment by an HVAC contractor (often $75–$150) can give you a clearer picture of remaining life expectancy and efficiency ratings if the unit is older than 10 years.

How to Read Your Inspection Report Without Losing Your Mind

A 75-page report full of red flags and photos can feel overwhelming — like trying to read a seed catalog in a foreign language. The key is understanding that inspectors are required to note every deficiency they observe, regardless of severity. Not everything in bold is a crisis.

Most TREC-compliant reports use a consistent system: items are flagged as Deficient, or noted as observations that may require monitoring. Walk through the report with your Realtor and ask one simple question for each flagged item: "Is this a safety issue, a major system failure, or cosmetic?" That mental sorting helps you quickly identify the handful of items that truly warrant negotiation versus the long list of minor maintenance notes.

Pay close attention to anything flagged in the structural, roof, electrical, plumbing, and HVAC sections — these are your big-ticket categories. A cracked outlet cover is on the same report as a failing heat exchanger, but they are absolutely not the same thing, and treating them equally will lead you astray.

Recommended Follow-Up Inspections

Your general inspector isn't a specialist in every system. Based on what they find, they may recommend additional evaluations — and you should take those recommendations seriously. Common follow-up inspections in SA include: a structural engineer for foundation concerns ($300–$600), a sewer scope for older homes ($150–$250), a pest inspection for wood-destroying insects like termites ($75–$150), a pool inspection if the home has one ($150–$300), and a chimney inspection if there's a wood-burning fireplace ($150–$250).

Budgeting $500–$1,000 for additional inspections on the right house is smart money. I like to think of it the same way I think about soil testing before planting a new garden bed — a little investment in knowledge up front saves you from expensive problems later.

Using the Inspection Report to Negotiate Repairs or Credits

In Texas, buyers have two main options after an inspection: request specific repairs be completed before closing, or request a reduction in the sales price (or a closing cost credit) to compensate for the issues found. Each approach has advantages depending on the situation and the market.

Requesting repairs works best for clear safety items — a non-functioning GFCI outlet, a missing smoke detector, an active plumbing leak. The challenge is that you don't always get to choose who the seller hires to do the work, and a quick fix before closing isn't always a quality fix. A price reduction or credit often gives you more control over how the issue is ultimately addressed.

In San Antonio's current market, most sellers expect some negotiation after inspection — but savvy buyers focus their asks on the high-dollar, high-priority items rather than submitting a laundry list of every minor thing on the report. Sellers are far more likely to negotiate meaningfully on a leaking roof or a failing HVAC system than on a handful of cosmetic touch-ups. Your goal is to arrive at a fair deal, not to win every line item.

What Sellers Are and Aren't Likely to Budge On

In a competitive seller's market — which much of the San Antonio metro, including the Northside ISD and NEISD zones, has experienced — sellers have more leverage and may push back on extensive repair requests. Focusing on safety concerns and major system deficiencies gives you the strongest ground to stand on. Cosmetic issues, deferred maintenance items, and "normal for the age of the home" observations are generally not strong negotiating points.

If you're buying in a slower segment of the market — say, a higher price point in the far northeast suburbs like Converse or Universal City — sellers may be more willing to engage on a broader list. Your Realtor's knowledge of current market dynamics in that specific neighborhood is invaluable here.

How to Find a Good Home Inspector in San Antonio

All Texas home inspectors must be licensed by TREC — you can verify any inspector's license and complaint history at trec.texas.gov. Beyond that baseline, look for inspectors who have been working in San Antonio specifically for several years, since local knowledge of Bexar County soil types, common construction eras, and neighborhood-specific issues genuinely matters.

InterNACHI and ASHI certifications indicate an inspector who has pursued additional education beyond state minimums. Ask whether they'll allow you to attend the inspection — any reputable inspector will say yes, and walking through the home with them is one of the best education opportunities a buyer can get. You'll learn more about your future home in three hours than you ever would from reading the report alone.

San Antonio home inspection costs typically run $350–$450 for a home under 2,000 square feet, $450–$550 for 2,000–3,500 square feet, and $550+ for larger properties or homes with pools, detached structures, and other add-ons. Don't shop purely on price — the difference between a $299 inspector and a $450 inspector is often the difference between a thorough report and a missed foundation issue.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a home inspection cost in San Antonio, TX?

Most San Antonio buyers pay between $350 and $550 for a standard general inspection, with the final cost depending on square footage, age of the home, and any add-on services like pool inspection or sewer scope. Specialty inspections — structural engineering evaluations, chimney inspections, pest inspections — are separate fees typically ranging from $75 to $600 depending on the service. When budgeting for your home purchase, I recommend setting aside $600–$1,200 total for inspection-related costs, especially if you're buying an older home in a historic SA neighborhood. That may sound like a lot, but it's a small fraction of what a missed issue could cost you after closing. The peace of mind — and the negotiating information — is genuinely worth every dollar.

Can a seller refuse to make repairs after a home inspection in Texas?

Yes — in Texas, sellers are not legally obligated to make any repairs after a home inspection. The inspection period gives buyers the right to walk away (exercising the option period), but it doesn't compel the seller to fix anything. Negotiation is exactly that — a negotiation, and the outcome depends on what the contract says, current market conditions, and how motivated both parties are to complete the sale. If a seller refuses to address significant safety concerns or major deficiencies, buyers have to decide whether the home still makes sense at the agreed-upon price or whether to terminate the contract and continue their search. This is where having an experienced local Realtor in your corner makes a real difference — knowing when to push, when to accept a credit, and when to walk is as much art as it is data.

What are the most common problems found in San Antonio home inspections?

Beyond the foundation and roof issues covered earlier in this guide, inspectors in Bexar County frequently flag improper attic ventilation (a major issue in our climate), water heater age and improper pressure relief valve installation, missing or non-functional GFCI outlets especially in older homes, wood rot on exterior trim and fence posts from alternating wet and dry seasons, and improper grading that directs water toward the foundation. Pest issues — specifically termites and wood-destroying insects — show up with enough regularity that a separate WDI (Wood-Destroying Insect) inspection is smart for any home with mature trees close to the structure. San Antonio's tree canopy is one of its great beauties, but those root systems can also find their way into sewer lines and lift concrete over time.

Should I attend my home inspection in person?

Absolutely, if at all possible — and I tell every buyer I work with the same thing. Attending the inspection gives you the opportunity to ask the inspector questions in real time, see issues firsthand rather than just in photos, and get a gut sense of the home's overall condition that no report can fully convey. Most inspectors will walk you through their findings at the end, flagging what they consider the most significant items and giving you context for what's typical versus what's genuinely concerning. Plan to arrive about an hour before the inspection wraps up so you have time for that walkthrough without disrupting the inspector's process during the detailed examination phase. Bring a notepad, your questions, and comfortable shoes — you'll be covering a lot of ground.

Does a new construction home in San Antonio still need an inspection?

New construction absolutely needs an inspection — possibly more than a resale home does, because construction quality control varies widely between builders and even between phases of the same development. Brand-new homes in fast-growing SA suburbs like Cibolo, Schertz, and the areas along TX-1604 and US-281 have been built rapidly over the past several years, and inspectors regularly find issues including improper grading, incomplete caulking, HVAC installation errors, and missing insulation in walls that have already been closed up. For new construction, many buyers opt for a phase inspection — separate evaluations at the foundation pour, framing, and pre-drywall stages — in addition to a final pre-closing inspection. Builders generally provide a one-year warranty on workmanship, but you want to catch issues before you move in, not after the warranty claim process has begun.
