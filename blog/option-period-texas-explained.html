The Option Period in Texas: What It Is, What It Costs, and How to Use It Strategically

By Chris Drawdy · January 13, 2026 · 10 min read

If you're buying or selling a home in San Antonio, the option period is one of the most powerful — and most misunderstood — tools in the entire transaction. In this guide, you'll learn exactly how the Texas option period works, what the fee covers, and how smart buyers and sellers use it to their advantage in today's market.

What the Option Period Actually Is — and Why Texas Is Unique

The option period is a defined window of time — written directly into the TREC (Texas Real Estate Commission) contract — during which a buyer has the unrestricted right to terminate the agreement for any reason whatsoever. No justification needed. No negotiation required. Just written notice to the seller, and the buyer walks away.

Texas is one of a relatively small number of states that builds this kind of explicit "free look" period into its standard residential contract. In many other states, buyers have to rely on contingency clauses — financing contingencies, inspection contingencies — each of which requires a specific triggering event to exit the deal. In Texas, the option period stands on its own, separate from those contingencies.

Think of it like preparing a garden bed before you plant. You don't just throw seeds in the ground and hope for the best — you take time to test the soil, check the drainage, and make sure conditions are right. The option period is that due-diligence window. It's protected time to make sure the home you're about to commit to is actually the one you want.

How It Differs from Contingencies

Financing and inspection contingencies still exist in Texas contracts and serve their own purposes. But those contingencies typically require documented reasons to be invoked — a lender denial letter, a specific repair issue that wasn't resolved. The option period requires nothing of the sort.

During the option period, a buyer could terminate simply because they changed their mind, found another property, or just had a bad feeling about the neighborhood. That's a meaningful protection that buyers — especially first-timers buying in fast-moving San Antonio submarkets like Alamo Ranch, Stone Oak, or the Hill Country Village area — should fully understand before they go under contract.

The Option Fee: What You Pay, Where It Goes, and When

To activate the option period, the buyer pays an option fee directly to the seller. This is not earnest money — it's a separate payment. In San Antonio's current market, typical option fees range from about $100 on the low end for more modestly priced homes up to $500 or more on higher-value properties or in competitive multiple-offer situations.

The option fee must be delivered to the seller — not the title company — within three days of the contract execution date. That's a tight window, and missing it is a serious mistake. If the fee isn't delivered on time, the buyer technically has no option period, even if it's written into the contract. Your agent or title company should flag this deadline immediately after signatures are collected.

Here's the good news: if the deal closes, the option fee is credited toward the buyer's purchase price at closing. So if you paid a $300 option fee and the home was under contract at $375,000, your effective purchase price becomes $374,700. You're not throwing money away — you're paying for time and certainty, and you get it back if you proceed.

What Happens to the Fee If You Terminate?

If a buyer terminates during the option period, the option fee stays with the seller. That's the trade — the seller takes their home off the market and gives you that protected window, and in exchange they keep the fee if you walk. It's a modest but meaningful compensation for the seller's risk.

Earnest money, by contrast, is typically held at the title company — places like Alamo Title, Texas National Title, or Stewart Title, all common in San Antonio transactions — and is returned to the buyer upon a valid option period termination. The two amounts are separate, and it's important not to confuse them.

How the Option Period Length Is Negotiated

Nothing about the option period is fixed by law. Both the length and the fee are fully negotiable between buyer and seller, and both are written into the contract before signatures. In practice, most San Antonio transactions land somewhere between five and ten days, though I've seen everything from three-day option periods in very hot markets to fourteen-day windows on properties with complex issues.

In a competitive multiple-offer scenario — like we've seen periodically in neighborhoods like Helotes, Boerne, or New Braunfels just up I-35 — buyers sometimes shorten their option period or increase the option fee to make their offer look more attractive to sellers. A seller reviewing five offers will notice a buyer willing to pay $500 and limit themselves to five days versus a buyer asking for ten days at $100.

On the flip side, if a home has been sitting on the market for a while, or if you have reason to believe there may be complicated issues to investigate — an older home in Olmos Park, say, or a property with a large detached structure or well and septic — it's worth negotiating for more time upfront rather than scrambling to extend it later.

Can the Option Period Be Extended?

Yes, but only with mutual written agreement. If your inspector finds something that needs further evaluation — a structural engineer, an HVAC specialist, a plumber scoping the sewer line — you can ask the seller for an extension. Many sellers will agree, especially if the alternative is losing the buyer entirely. But the extension has to be documented in writing, and the seller has no obligation to grant it.

How Buyers Use the Option Period Strategically

The most important thing to accomplish during your option period is the home inspection. A qualified Texas-licensed inspector — I always recommend getting referrals from your agent or checking the Texas Real Estate Inspection Association directory — will evaluate the structure, roof, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, foundation, and more. In San Antonio, foundation inspections deserve particular attention given our expansive clay soils, especially in older neighborhoods like Lackland Terrace, Woodlawn Hills, or parts of the South Side near Loop 410.

Beyond the inspection, the option period is also the right time to get your appraisal ordered, finalize your loan application with your lender, review the HOA documents if the property is in a community like Alamo Ranch or Sonterra, and walk the property a second or third time if something didn't sit right the first visit. Think of it as your due diligence sprint — everything that helps you confirm you're making the right decision.

Some buyers also use this window to get contractor estimates if the inspection reveals needed repairs. If the roof is aging, getting two or three roofing quotes during the option period arms you with real numbers before you sit down to negotiate repairs or a price reduction with the seller. That's not just smart — it's the difference between a guess and a position.

Negotiating Repairs After the Option Period — A Note of Caution

A common misconception is that you can only negotiate repairs during the option period. That's not exactly true — you can submit a TREC Amendment requesting repairs or a price reduction at any point before closing. But your leverage is greatest while you still hold the unrestricted right to terminate. Once the option period expires, you've signaled commitment, and the seller knows it.

This is why timing matters. If your inspector delivers a report on day six of a seven-day option period, you have very little runway to get contractor bids, request repairs, and receive a response before your window closes. Scheduling the inspection on day one or two — not day five — gives you room to breathe and negotiate from a position of strength.

How Sellers Should Think About the Option Period

Sellers sometimes view the option period nervously, and understandably so. Your home is off the market, you've told your neighbors and your family, and the buyer can still walk away for no reason. That uncertainty is real. But it's also the cost of doing business in Texas, and buyers need that window to feel confident enough to move forward.

What sellers can do is protect themselves in how they negotiate the terms. In a strong seller's market — which parts of San Antonio, particularly in the Northside ISD and North East ISD footprints, have experienced periodically — sellers have genuine leverage to push for shorter option periods, higher option fees, or both. A five-day window with a $400 fee is meaningfully different from a ten-day window with $100.

Sellers should also be responsive during the option period. If a buyer submits a repair amendment on day four and the seller takes until day eight to respond, the buyer may feel stonewalled and terminate — not because the issue was unresolvable, but because communication broke down. Prompt, good-faith responses keep deals alive. Like a garden, a transaction needs consistent attention during that early growth period — neglect it, and things wither.

What If a Seller Receives a Better Offer During the Option Period?

This is a scenario sellers ask about often. Once you're under contract and the buyer is in their option period, you can continue to receive and even negotiate backup offers, but you cannot enter into another primary contract. You can accept a backup offer with a contingency that makes it effective only if the first contract terminates. Working with a knowledgeable agent during this phase is essential to staying on the right side of your contractual obligations.

Local San Antonio Nuances Worth Knowing

San Antonio's real estate market spans dramatically different submarkets, and the norms around option periods can shift depending on where you're buying. In higher-demand areas like Alamo Heights, Terrell Hills, and the 78209 zip code, option periods tend to be shorter and fees higher because sellers have more options. In slower-moving areas or on higher-days-on-market listings, buyers have more room to negotiate favorable terms.

If you're using a city or county down payment assistance program — like the San Antonio Homebuyer Incentive Program, the Bexar County DPAP, or TSAHC's Home Sweet Texas loan — factor in that these programs sometimes have additional timeline requirements that interact with your option period. Coordinating with your lender early, whether you're working with Randolph-Brooks Federal Credit Union, Generations Federal Credit Union, or a mortgage broker familiar with these programs, prevents surprises.

Military buyers purchasing near JBSA-Lackland, JBSA-Fort Sam Houston, or JBSA-Randolph using a VA loan should also be aware that VA appraisals can take longer to schedule and complete than conventional appraisals. Building in a slightly longer option period — or at minimum getting the appraisal ordered on day one — helps ensure you have the information you need before your termination window closes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a seller keep my earnest money if I terminate during the option period in Texas?

No — if you terminate properly during the option period, your earnest money must be returned to you. The option period grants you an unrestricted right to terminate, meaning the seller has no grounds to claim the earnest money held at the title company. However, you must deliver written notice of termination to the seller before the option period expires, typically using the TREC Notice of Buyer's Termination of Contract form. What the seller does keep is the option fee — that's the separate, smaller amount paid directly to them at the outset. This is precisely why the two amounts serve different purposes and should never be confused with each other.

What happens if I forget to pay the option fee on time in Texas?

Failing to deliver the option fee within three days of contract execution is one of the most consequential procedural mistakes a buyer can make. Without a valid option fee payment, you technically have no option period — even if the number of days is written into the contract — because the option was never properly exercised. This means you would not have the unrestricted right to terminate and could potentially forfeit your earnest money if you walk away. Some title companies and attorneys have argued that late delivery can be cured in certain circumstances, but you should never rely on that possibility. Always confirm with your agent and title company exactly when and how the fee must be delivered — in San Antonio, many agents arrange for the fee to be hand-delivered or wired the same day contracts are executed.

Is the option period the same as a home inspection contingency in Texas?

They serve overlapping purposes but are legally distinct protections. An inspection contingency in other states typically requires the buyer to cite specific defects and give the seller an opportunity to cure them before the buyer can exit — it's conditional. The Texas option period is unconditional: you can terminate for any reason or no reason at all, regardless of whether an inspection was even performed. That said, most Texas buyers use the option period to conduct their inspection, review findings, and negotiate repairs, because it provides the cleanest legal protection. If an inspection issue arises after the option period ends, you'd need to rely on other contract provisions — like the financing contingency or a negotiated amendment — which carry less unilateral power to exit without consequences.

How much option fee is normal for San Antonio homes in 2026?

There's no legally mandated amount — the fee is entirely negotiable — but current norms in San Antonio generally fall between $100 and $500 for most residential transactions, with higher-end or luxury properties sometimes seeing fees of $1,000 or more. The fee amount is often influenced by how competitive the market is for that specific property: a well-priced home in a top-rated NEISD school zone may attract offers with elevated option fees as a signal of buyer seriousness. In a less competitive situation, a buyer has more room to keep the fee modest. Keep in mind that whatever you pay will be credited toward your purchase price at closing if the deal proceeds, so the economic impact is minimal unless you terminate — making this a relatively low-cost protection for the certainty it provides.

Can I still negotiate repairs after the option period expires in Texas?

Yes, repair negotiations can happen at any point before closing — there is no contractual deadline that prohibits submitting a TREC Amendment after the option period ends. However, your negotiating position shifts significantly once you no longer hold the unrestricted termination right, because the seller knows exiting the deal now would cost you your earnest money and potentially expose you to other consequences. Sellers are generally less motivated to offer significant concessions once they know you're committed. Additionally, any repairs agreed upon after the option period must still be documented in writing via an amendment signed by both parties — verbal agreements carry no weight in a Texas real estate contract. The takeaway: if your inspection uncovers major issues, it's almost always better to negotiate while you're still inside your option window.
