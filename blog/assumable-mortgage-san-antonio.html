What Is an Assumable Mortgage — and Why San Antonio Is Ground Zero for Them

An assumable mortgage lets a home buyer step into the seller's existing loan — same balance, same interest rate, same terms — rather than taking out a brand-new mortgage at today's rates. The lender must approve the new borrower, but the rate itself is locked in from when the original loan was originated.

Here's why San Antonio is one of the best cities in the country for this strategy: Joint Base San Antonio is the largest military installation complex in the United States, encompassing Lackland AFB, Fort Sam Houston, and Randolph AFB. Military families move on PCS orders every two to three years, and a huge percentage of them used VA loans to buy homes in 2020, 2021, and 2022 — when rates were historically low.

That means neighborhoods ringing JBSA — Converse, Universal City, Schertz, Helotes, Lackland Terrace, and even parts of Stone Oak — have a steady pipeline of VA-financed homes coming onto the market from relocating service members. It's like a seed bank of low-rate loans, just waiting for the right buyer.

The Two Loan Types That Allow Assumption

VA loans are assumable by both veterans and non-veterans, though non-veterans assuming a VA loan don't restore the selling veteran's VA entitlement — an important detail we'll cover in the negotiating section. The VA charges a funding fee of 0.5% of the loan balance for assumptions.

FHA loans are also fully assumable after lender qualification. FHA assumptions are processed through the original servicer and typically close faster than VA assumptions. San Antonio has a solid inventory of FHA-financed homes in neighborhoods like Southside, Pecan Valley, and along the Highway 90 corridor.

Conventional loans — Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac — are almost never assumable. They contain a "due-on-sale" clause that requires the full loan balance to be paid when the property transfers. Don't let anyone tell you otherwise.

The Math That Actually Changes Your Life: 3% vs. 7% on a Real San Antonio Home

Let's ground this in real numbers, because the difference is staggering and deserves to be seen clearly.

Imagine a home in Converse — a three-bedroom, two-bath near Judson ISD — listed at $310,000. The seller bought it in early 2021 with a VA loan at 2.875%. After five years of payments, the remaining loan balance is approximately $285,000.

Here's how the monthly principal and interest payment compares depending on how you finance it:

Side-by-Side Payment Comparison

Assumed VA loan at 2.875% on $285,000 balance: ~$1,183/month (P&I). Over 25 remaining years, total interest paid: ~$69,900.

New conventional loan at 7.0% on $248,000 (20% down on $310K): ~$1,651/month (P&I). Over 30 years, total interest paid: ~$346,360. That's not a typo.

The monthly savings of roughly $468 on P&I alone adds up to $5,616 per year. But the total interest savings over the life of the loan exceed $270,000. That's a college education, a retirement account, a lot of raised garden beds. The assumption does require a down payment that covers the gap between the sale price and the remaining loan balance — in this example, $25,000 — which is something buyers need to plan for and we'll address head-on.

Finding Assumable Mortgages in San Antonio: Where to Look

Most buyers don't know where to start, and that's actually an advantage — the competition for assumable loans is still relatively low compared to the opportunity. The trick is knowing how to identify them before they're widely marketed.

Search Strategies That Work Right Now

Assumption platforms: Sites like Roam and AssumeList aggregate MLS data and tag VA and FHA listings. They're not perfect — they won't catch every eligible home — but they're a solid starting point for San Antonio neighborhoods like Universal City, Schertz, and the areas around Loop 1604 and Highway 35.

Direct MLS filtering: A good buyer's agent (that's me) can filter MLS searches specifically for VA and FHA listings in the zip codes you're targeting. Homes in 78109 (Converse), 78154 (Schertz), 78245 (near Lackland), and 78247 (Stone Oak area) consistently surface eligible inventory due to high military homeownership rates.

Ask directly: When you find a VA or FHA home you like, have your agent ask the listing agent whether the seller is open to assumption. Many sellers don't know it's an option — and when they learn it can help their home sell faster, they're often receptive. Think of it like finding heirloom seeds at a swap: sometimes you just have to ask.

Working With San Antonio Lenders Who Know the Process

Assumption processing happens through the original loan servicer — not a new lender you choose. But you'll still want a local mortgage professional in your corner who understands the timeline and documentation requirements. In San Antonio, USAA, Navy Federal, and Security Service Federal Credit Union are major servicers of military VA loans and have dedicated assumption departments.

A local HUD-approved housing counselor through Alamo Area Council of Governments (AACOG) or the San Antonio Housing Authority (SAHA) can also help buyers who need guidance on bridging the equity gap with a secondary loan.

The Equity Gap Problem — and How to Solve It

Here's the challenge nobody talks about enough: the price of the home is almost always higher than the remaining loan balance. That gap is money you have to bring to the table, either in cash or through a second loan. In a market where San Antonio home values have appreciated significantly since 2020, that gap can be substantial.

Using our Converse example: the home sells for $310,000, the remaining VA balance is $285,000, so you need $25,000 at closing plus closing costs. That's manageable for many buyers. But a home in Stone Oak or Alamo Ranch that sold for $400,000 in 2021 and is now listed at $460,000 with a $350,000 remaining balance? That's a $110,000 gap — real money that requires planning.

Options for Covering the Gap

Cash: The simplest solution. If you have savings or gift funds, bringing cash to cover the gap avoids the complexity of a second loan.

Second mortgage / piggyback loan: Some lenders will write a second mortgage to cover the equity gap. The rate will be higher than the assumed first mortgage, but your blended rate can still beat a full conventional loan at 7%. Down Payment Assistance programs through the Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) and the San Antonio Housing Authority may also be layerable in some cases — worth exploring with a HUD counselor.

Seller concessions or seller carryback: In some negotiations, a motivated seller (especially a relocating service member on a tight PCS timeline) will carry a small second note themselves to make the deal work. This requires more legal structuring but it happens.

Negotiating an Assumable Mortgage: What Sellers Need and What You Can Offer

Negotiating an assumption is different from a standard real estate transaction. You're not just agreeing on a price — you're solving a structural puzzle together, and the seller has legitimate concerns you need to address.

The VA Entitlement Issue (Critical for Military Sellers)

When a non-veteran assumes a veteran's VA loan, the selling veteran's VA entitlement remains tied up in that loan until it's paid off. This means they may not have full entitlement available for their next VA purchase — a real problem for an active-duty service member headed to a new duty station.

The cleanest solution: if you're a qualifying veteran yourself, assuming the loan and substituting your own VA entitlement releases the seller's. This is a major selling point when you're a vet-to-vet transaction, and it should be the first thing you put on the table. In a city like San Antonio, many buyers searching near JBSA are themselves veterans or active duty — this alignment happens more than you'd think.

If you're not a veteran, you can still assume a VA loan, but you'll need to acknowledge the entitlement situation honestly and potentially offer other incentives — a faster close, a stronger earnest money deposit, or flexibility on the possession date — to compensate the seller for their entitlement risk.

Timeline Leverage and PCS Pressure

VA assumptions currently take 45 to 90 days to process through the servicer. That's longer than a conventional close, which creates stress for sellers on PCS orders with hard report dates. If you can offer flexibility — renting the home back to the seller for a month or agreeing to a leaseback — you become a much more attractive buyer even at the same price.

Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD (SCUCISD) and East Central ISD neighborhoods near Randolph AFB are full of families navigating exactly this scenario every summer PCS season. Understanding their timeline is part of negotiating with real empathy, not just tactics.

The Assumption Process Step by Step: What to Expect

The assumption process is more paperwork-intensive than a standard purchase, but it's not complicated if you know the sequence. Think of it like transplanting a mature tree — more labor than planting a seedling, but the payoff is immediate.

Step 1 — Identify and make an offer: Find the eligible listing, confirm the loan type and balance with the listing agent, and write your purchase agreement with an assumption contingency. Your offer should specify that the purchase is contingent on lender approval of the assumption.

Step 2 — Contact the servicer: Once you're under contract, you or your agent contacts the original loan servicer directly to request assumption packages. For VA loans, the servicer submits to the VA Regional Loan Center (the San Antonio RLC handles Texas). For FHA, it stays with the servicer.

Step 3 — Qualification and underwriting: You'll submit a full loan application to the servicer — income documentation, credit pull, assets. VA requires a minimum 620 credit score for most servicers; FHA assumptions typically follow similar guidelines. This is real underwriting, not a rubber stamp.

Step 4 — Approval and closing: Once approved, you close like a standard real estate transaction. VA assumption fees are 0.5% of the loan balance. FHA charges up to $500 plus a small ongoing MIP. Title is transferred and the loan is now yours — at that beautiful original rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a non-veteran assume a VA loan in San Antonio?

Yes — VA loans are assumable by anyone who meets the lender's credit and income qualifications, not just veterans. However, when a non-veteran assumes the loan, the original veteran-seller's VA entitlement stays tied to that property until the loan is fully paid off or refinanced. This can prevent the seller from using their full VA benefit on a future home purchase, which is why non-veteran buyers should acknowledge this issue openly and may need to offer compensating value in the negotiation. If entitlement restoration is critical to the seller, a veteran buyer who can substitute their own entitlement is always the more attractive option. Talk to the VA Regional Loan Center or a knowledgeable local agent before assuming you'll be automatically accepted.

How long does a VA loan assumption take to close in Texas?

VA loan assumptions in Texas typically take between 45 and 90 days from the time the servicer receives a complete application package — longer than the 21–30 days of a standard conventional close. The timeline depends heavily on which servicer holds the loan: USAA and Navy Federal tend to have dedicated assumption teams that move efficiently, while some larger national servicers are slower. Buyers should build this timeline into their purchase agreement and avoid locking rate commitments or lease-end dates that conflict with it. FHA assumptions through the same servicers generally close in 30 to 60 days. Planning ahead is everything — rushing the servicer rarely works.

What happens if I don't have enough cash to cover the difference between the sale price and the loan balance?

The equity gap — the difference between what you're paying and what remains on the loan — is the biggest practical hurdle in assumption transactions, and you have real options for bridging it. Some buyers use a second mortgage from a portfolio lender or credit union to cover the gap; Security Service Federal Credit Union and local community banks in San Antonio have offered these products. Programs through TSAHC (Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation) offer down payment and closing cost assistance that may be applicable in some scenarios — a HUD-approved housing counselor can review eligibility. In motivated-seller situations, particularly with relocating military families, seller-financed seconds or closing cost concessions are also negotiable. The key is running all the numbers including the blended rate on both loans to confirm you're still beating a conventional mortgage.

Are there assumable mortgages available near good San Antonio school districts?

Yes, and this is one of the most compelling opportunities in the current market. Neighborhoods in Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD — consistently ranked among the top districts in the greater San Antonio metro — have high concentrations of VA-financed homes due to proximity to Randolph AFB. Similarly, Judson ISD communities in Converse and Live Oak, and Northwest ISD areas near Helotes, see regular VA loan turnover from military relocations. Northeast ISD pockets near the 1604/281 corridor also have FHA inventory worth searching. The combination of strong schools and potentially locked-in 2021-era rates makes these zip codes worth prioritizing in your search strategy.

Does assuming a mortgage affect the seller's credit or financial obligations?

Once a VA or FHA loan assumption is fully approved and closed by the servicer, the seller is released from personal liability on the debt — this is called a "release of liability" and it's a formal step in the VA assumption process that must be explicitly requested and granted. Without this release, the original borrower technically remains on the hook if the new owner defaults, even though they no longer own the property. This is an important protection for sellers that buyers should understand and commit to pursuing as part of the transaction. For FHA loans, the same principle applies and is handled through the servicer. Never let a seller assume (pun intended) that the loan is off their record just because closing happened — confirm the release of liability in writing.
