What Agent Attraction Actually Is (And Isn't)

Agent attraction at eXp Realty is the term for a hybrid business model where agents earn commission on their own transactions and also receive revenue share from agents they bring into the company. It's not a multi-level marketing scheme, though I'll be honest — the language around it can sound confusing if you're new to real estate.

Think of it like planting a garden bed and tending to it over seasons. You don't just throw seeds down and expect a harvest; you plant, water, prune, and cultivate the right conditions for growth. Agent attraction works the same way — it's about creating an environment where agents want to stay and thrive, not just signing them up and moving on.

At its core, eXp's model says: if you help other agents succeed at eXp, you share in the company's revenue generated by those agents. But — and this is critical — that sharing only kicks in when your team hits meaningful production thresholds, usually $100K or more in gross commission income annually.

How Revenue Share Actually Works at eXp Realty

Let's talk numbers, because this is where the real value lives or dies. Revenue share at eXp is tiered, meaning your percentage increases as your team's collective production grows.

The Revenue Share Tiers

At the entry level, there's no revenue share until you hit $100K in team GCI. Once you're there, you typically earn 2–5% of your team's gross commission income. As your team grows and you hit higher production milestones ($250K, $500K, $1M+), your percentage climbs toward the maximum of 20%.

Here's what that means in real money. If your team produces $200K in GCI (which is entirely doable in San Antonio — that's roughly 8–12 sides for a small team), and you're at the 5% tier, you're looking at $10,000 in additional revenue that year. That's real income, but it also requires building a legitimate team of producing agents.

Many agents get attracted to the revenue share potential and never ask the harder question: what does it take to get there? The answer is patience, mentorship, and genuinely caring whether the agents you bring on actually succeed.

Why Most Agent Attraction Efforts Fail

Here's where I'll be direct. A lot of agents jump into eXp thinking they can recruit their way to success without doing the real work. They sign up 10–15 people in year one, feel excited, and by year two, most of those agents have either left eXp or are inactive. The problem? Those agents were never set up to win.

Recruitment without cultivation is like planting seeds in depleted soil and never watering them. You'll get some sprouting, but most won't take root. The agents that fail aren't failures because the model doesn't work — they fail because no one invested in their success.

Sustainable revenue share comes from attracting agents who fit your culture and your market, then providing them with systems, training, and genuine support. That's harder than posting "Join my team!" on social media, but it's the only way that actually builds something lasting.

Building a Team That Grows vs. Building a Team That Leaves

The difference between a thriving eXp team and a revolving door comes down to one thing: whether your recruitment strategy is based on value or pressure.

A value-based approach asks: "What can I teach this agent? What market gaps can we fill together? How can they build a sustainable business here?" A pressure-based approach asks: "How many agents can I sign up before the quarter ends?" One builds a business. The other builds a résumé line.

What Makes an Agent Stick

Agents stay where they feel supported, where they see other agents succeeding, and where they're not treated like recruitment numbers. In San Antonio specifically, we have a strong community of residential real estate professionals. Agents know each other, and word spreads fast about which brokers actually invest in their people.

At eXp, agents also get access to cloud-based tools, national training resources, and a virtual office environment that appeals to agents who want flexibility. But those perks only matter if someone on your team is showing them how to use those tools to actually close more deals.

I've seen agents build teams of 5–8 producing agents by focusing on neighborhoods and niches. One agent I know specialized in homes under $350K in areas like Castle Hills and Universal City — solid markets with strong school districts and first-time buyer appeal. She attracted agents interested in that same segment, built systems around it, and created real team production.

The Reality of San Antonio's Real Estate Market

San Antonio's median home price sits around $275K, and our market has been growing steadily. The Austin commute corridor (toward places like New Braunfels and Boerne) is increasingly populated. The North Star area near Loop 1604 is booming with residential development, and neighborhoods like Windcrest, Shavano Park, and Stone Oak remain competitive.

This diversity is actually your advantage if you're thinking about building a team. You can attract specialists — someone focused on the first-time buyer market, someone who knows ranch properties in the Hill Country, someone who dominates townhome communities. That's a team with real depth, not just depth of numbers.

The Right Way to Attract Agents: Through Value, Not Pressure

Let me be clear about what works and what doesn't, based on what I've seen across San Antonio's real estate community.

Transparent Communication About the Model

If you're attracting agents, tell them the truth: revenue share isn't automatic. It requires real team production. It takes 2–3 years to see meaningful passive income from your team's efforts. The primary income still comes from your own transactions.

Agents respect honesty. When you're upfront about the work required and the timeline involved, the people who join your team are signing up for the right reasons. They understand it's a partnership, not a lottery ticket.

Provide Real Training and Systems

Build an onboarding process for new agents. Share your market knowledge. Create listing presentations and buyer consultation frameworks they can use. Show them where deals come from in your market — whether that's sphere of influence, past client relationships, online lead generation, or sphere farming in specific neighborhoods.

In San Antonio, agents who know how to work the North Star corridor, or who specialize in new construction around the Loop 1604 expansion, or who understand the rental investor market near downtown — those agents have an edge. Your job is to transfer that knowledge.

Be Selective About Who Joins

This goes against the "recruit everyone" mentality, but it's critical. An agent with poor work ethic who joins your team will drag down your numbers and your culture, regardless of what commission split you offer.

Look for agents with existing client bases, a growth mindset, and willingness to follow systems. Not everyone is cut out for eXp's virtual environment or independent contractor model. The agents who thrive are self-motivated and don't need someone hovering over them to work.

I'd rather see you bring on 2–3 solid agents over 18 months than 10 marginal ones in six months. The revenue share you actually see will be more sustainable.

Revenue Share vs. Traditional Brokerage Splits: The Math

Let's talk about what you give up and what you gain when comparing eXp's model to traditional brokerages.

eXp Typical Structure

At eXp, you typically cap out around 70–90% commission split as a regular agent (depending on your production). That's competitive or better than many traditional brokerages. Where eXp differs is that you don't have a desk fee, and you have access to revenue share from your team's production once it reaches scale.

So if you do $400K in personal production and have a team doing another $300K, you might keep 85% of your personal transactions (roughly $340K) plus 8% of team GCI ($24K) = $364K gross before expenses. Compare that to a traditional brokerage where you might keep 80% of personal transactions ($320K) with no team revenue at all.

Traditional Brokerage Typical Structure

Traditional brokerages often offer higher splits at certain production thresholds, but they charge desk fees, transaction fees, or other back-end costs. You don't have access to a revenue-sharing model; your income is purely transaction-based.

For agents building a team, traditional brokerages often have more overhead (the brokerage keeps a percentage of everything your team does, but you get no sharing mechanism). eXp inverts that — you share in revenue, but you also share some of the responsibility for technology and compliance.

Is Agent Attraction Right for You? Questions to Ask Yourself

Not every agent should focus on building a team. This model works if you meet certain criteria.

Do you genuinely enjoy mentoring? If you don't like teaching other agents or you see them as competition, agent attraction will feel miserable. You need to get energy from helping people succeed.

Are you willing to invest time with no immediate return? It takes 18–24 months to build a small team that's actually producing. If you need income next quarter, focus on your own business first.

Can you tolerate some turnover? Not every agent you attract will stay. Some will go back to traditional brokerages, some will leave real estate entirely. You need resilience and a long-term view.

Do you have a market specialization or niche? The strongest teams attract agents interested in similar markets or property types. Generic "join my team" recruiting rarely creates lasting communities.

If you answered yes to most of these, agent attraction might be worth exploring. If not, there's absolutely nothing wrong with staying focused on your own transactions and letting eXp's tools and flexibility do the heavy lifting.

Building Community in San Antonio's Real Estate Market

Here's what I've learned from working in San Antonio: real estate is ultimately about community. The agents, the clients, the neighborhoods themselves — they're all interconnected.

When you approach agent attraction with a community-first mindset (which, honestly, mirrors how I think about gardening), you're asking different questions. How can we serve the Northside better? How can we specialize in properties that appeal to remote workers moving into the area? How can we become the go-to team for multi-generational homes in family-oriented neighborhoods?

That approach — focused on community value rather than recruitment targets — naturally attracts the right agents. They see what you're building. They want to be part of it.

The revenue share becomes a nice benefit, not the main attraction. And that's when it actually works.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can you really make from revenue share at eXp Realty?

Revenue share income depends entirely on your team's production and tier level. A small team producing $300K in GCI at a 5% tier generates $15K annually. A larger, more mature team at $1M+ production and a 15% tier could generate $150K+. However, most agents see $0–$15K in their first 2–3 years because building that production takes time. The key is that this is bonus income on top of your personal transaction revenue, not a replacement for it.

What's the difference between downline and team at eXp?

Your "downline" is everyone directly and indirectly recruited under you — a hierarchical structure. Your "team" is typically the agents you directly sponsor and work with daily. eXp's revenue share structure rewards downline production, which means agents under your recruits' recruits still contribute to your revenue share, but you have less direct control over their success. This can create perverse incentives if not managed carefully — focusing on recruitment depth rather than agent quality and support.

Is eXp Realty's agent attraction model a pyramid scheme?

No. eXp Realty is a legitimate brokerage regulated by state real estate commissions, and revenue share is earned from actual real estate transactions, not from recruitment itself. However, the model can *behave* like a pyramid scheme if agents focus on recruitment instead of actual real estate production. The FTC's test is whether primary income comes from recruiting or from product sales — at eXp, it should come from real estate transactions. If it doesn't, you're in the wrong crowd.

How do you know if an agent is right for your eXp team in San Antonio?

Look for agents with existing client bases, market familiarity in specific San Antonio neighborhoods, and comfort with technology (since eXp is cloud-based). They should have a growth mindset and be willing to follow your systems rather than reinventing the wheel. Avoid agents who are desperate or treating eXp as their last resort — desperation leads to burnout and turnover. Interview them like you're hiring for a role that matters, because you are.

What happens to your revenue share if agents on your team leave?

Their future production no longer counts toward your revenue share, and you drop down to whatever tier your remaining team production supports. If your team was producing $500K (15% tier) and half your agents leave, dropping you to $250K production, you fall to a lower tier with a smaller percentage. This is why focusing on agent retention and satisfaction matters more than constant recruitment — losing agents hurts your income more than gaining new ones helps it.
