Pre-Qualified vs. Pre-Approved: The Difference That Can Cost You the House You Want

By Chris Drawdy · April 10, 2026 · 10 min read

In San Antonio's competitive market, showing up with the wrong piece of paper isn't just a small mistake — it can mean losing a home in Alamo Ranch or Stone Oak to a buyer who simply did their homework first. This guide breaks down exactly what separates a pre-qualification from a real pre-approval, why sellers and their agents can spot the difference immediately, and what you need to do right now to make your offer count.

Why Pre-Qualification Is Almost Meaningless in Today's Market

A pre-qualification is essentially a lender taking your word for it. You tell them your income, your debts, and a rough sense of your credit score — and they hand back a number based on nothing verified. No tax returns, no pay stubs, no hard credit pull.

Think of it like planning a garden based on what you think the soil might be like. Until you actually dig down and test it, you're just guessing. Pre-qualification is the guess. It tells a seller almost nothing they can rely on.

In a market like San Antonio right now — where well-priced homes in neighborhoods like Helotes, Cibolo, and the Northside ISD zone routinely see multiple offers within the first weekend — a pre-qual letter might as well be invisible. Listing agents advise their sellers to treat pre-quals skeptically, and most will recommend waiting for a stronger offer rather than accepting one backed only by an unverified estimate.

What Pre-Qualification Actually Involves

Most pre-qualifications are completed through an online form or a quick phone call. The lender inputs the figures you provide into a debt-to-income calculator and generates a letter. There is no verification of employment, no review of bank statements, and no underwriter involvement of any kind.

The letter will often include language like "based on information provided" — which is lender-speak for "we haven't checked any of this." Savvy seller's agents in San Antonio read those letters carefully, and that phrase is a yellow flag at best.

What a Real Pre-Approval Actually Requires

A genuine pre-approval requires you to submit a full mortgage application and back it up with documentation. That means W-2s or 1099s for the past two years, recent pay stubs, two to three months of bank statements, and authorization for the lender to pull your credit — a hard inquiry that shows up on your report.

The loan officer reviews all of this, confirms your income and employment, and calculates your debt-to-income ratio using real numbers. They also verify that your down payment funds are properly sourced — something that matters a lot if you're using gift funds or a Texas down payment assistance program like TSAHC's Homes for Texas Heroes.

What comes out the other end is a conditional commitment: the lender is willing to make this loan provided the property appraises and nothing material changes in your financial picture. That's a completely different document than a pre-qual, and experienced listing agents in San Antonio know it the moment they read it.

Documents You'll Typically Need to Gather

For most conventional and FHA loans, you'll need two years of federal tax returns (all pages), your two most recent pay stubs, two to three months of bank and investment account statements, a government-issued ID, and your Social Security number for the credit pull. If you're self-employed — which covers a lot of San Antonio's contractor and military-adjacent workforce — expect to also provide a profit and loss statement and potentially a CPA letter.

VA loan borrowers will also need their Certificate of Eligibility, which a lender can often pull directly through the VA portal. San Antonio has one of the highest concentrations of active-duty and veteran homebuyers in the country, and local lenders like Security Service Federal Credit Union and Randolph-Brooks FCU are well-practiced at handling VA documentation efficiently.

Full Underwriting vs. Conditional Pre-Approval: The Next Level Up

There's actually a third tier that most buyers don't know exists: a fully underwritten pre-approval, sometimes called a credit approval or TBD approval. This is where a real underwriter — not just a loan officer — reviews your entire file before you've even found a house.

The only thing missing is the specific property. Once you go under contract, the underwriter only needs to review the appraisal and title work rather than starting your file from scratch. In competitive situations, a fully underwritten approval is as close to a cash offer as a financed buyer can get.

Lenders like USAA (hugely relevant in San Antonio given Joint Base San Antonio), Cornerstone Home Lending, and movement mortgage branches in the metro area all offer some version of this product. It takes longer — typically three to seven business days — but the competitive advantage in neighborhoods like Shavano Park, Timberwood Park, or any Comal ISD community where inventory moves fast is enormous.

Why the Distinction Matters When You're Competing

When a seller in Converse or Universal City reviews two offers on the same afternoon, the listing agent is going to walk them through the strength of each financing package. A conditional pre-approval from a known local lender will almost always outrank a standard pre-qual from an online lender — even if the pre-qual buyer offered slightly more money.

Sellers worry about deals falling through. A deal that falls through costs them time, market momentum, and sometimes money if rates shift. The stronger your financing documentation, the less risk they're absorbing when they accept your offer.

San Antonio-Specific Programs That Require Pre-Approval First

Several of the most valuable homebuyer assistance programs available in the San Antonio area have a hard requirement: you must have a real pre-approval in place before you can access the funds. This is not a formality — it's a gate that exists because these programs don't want to commit dollars to buyers who haven't verified their ability to close.

The Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers down payment assistance of up to 5% of the loan amount for eligible buyers, including its Homes for Texas Heroes program for teachers, police officers, firefighters, and veterans. The Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs (TDHCA) runs a similar program called My First Texas Home. Both require a participating lender and a completed pre-approval application.

The San Antonio Housing Authority (SAHA) administers its own homeownership assistance programs for qualifying income levels. The City of San Antonio's Neighborhood and Housing Services Department also periodically opens its Homebuyer Incentive Program (HIP), which has provided forgivable loans of up to $30,000 in certain city council districts. None of these close for a buyer who walks in with only a pre-qual in hand.

Credit Score Minimums and Income Limits to Know

TSAHC programs generally require a minimum 620 credit score for government loans (FHA, VA, USDA) and 640 for conventional. Income limits vary by county and household size — in Bexar County, the limit for most programs in 2026 runs somewhere in the $90,000–$110,000 range for a household of two, though you should verify current figures directly with a participating lender since they update annually.

USDA rural development loans — which apply to areas like portions of Helotes, Lytle, Floresville, and communities along the 1604/281 corridor edge — require no down payment at all but cap household income relative to area median. A proper pre-approval will confirm whether you qualify for USDA eligibility based on both income and property location.

How Sellers and Their Agents Actually Read Your Pre-Approval Letter

Most buyers assume their offer letter does all the talking. In reality, the listing agent often calls the lender on the pre-approval letter directly — especially in a multiple-offer situation. They want to confirm the letter is real, that the loan officer knows the buyer's file, and that the lender has capacity to close on your timeline.

This is why using a local or regionally known lender matters. A loan officer at a San Antonio branch of a recognized institution picks up the phone and can speak to your file immediately. An anonymous online lender with a 1-800 number does not provide the same confidence — even if the rate they quoted you was slightly lower.

The listing agent is also reading the letter for specifics: the loan type (conventional, FHA, VA), the loan amount, the expiration date of the pre-approval, and whether it references any unusual conditions. A letter that's vague, expired, or missing the loan type raises questions that work against your offer before you've even had a chance to negotiate.

Steps to Get Pre-Approved in San Antonio Right Now

Start by pulling your own credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com so you're not surprised by anything a lender sees. Dispute any errors before you apply — even a 10-point swing in your credit score can move you into a better rate tier and save thousands over the life of a 30-year loan.

Then gather your documentation before you contact a lender: two years of tax returns, recent pay stubs, bank statements, and your ID. Going into the conversation organized tells the loan officer immediately that you're a serious buyer, and it speeds up the process significantly — some lenders can return a pre-approval decision within 24 hours when a buyer comes in fully prepared.

Interview at least two lenders. Rates vary, but so does service quality, communication speed, and experience with the specific loan type you need. If you're a veteran buying near JBSA-Lackland, JBSA-Fort Sam Houston, or JBSA-Randolph, make sure your lender has processed VA loans specifically in Bexar County — the appraisal and MPR process has local nuances that experienced VA lenders navigate much more smoothly.

Timeline: When to Get Pre-Approved Before You Start Looking

The answer is before you fall in love with a house, not after. In San Antonio neighborhoods with tight inventory — think Alamo Heights ISD territory, the 78209 and 78212 zip codes, or anything in the Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD zone — you may have 48 hours or less to put together an offer once you find the right home.

Getting pre-approved four to six weeks before you start seriously touring gives you time to address anything that comes up — a collections account you forgot about, a gap in employment documentation, or a credit utilization issue that's easy to fix but takes a billing cycle to correct. Gardeners know you can't rush a seed planted in dry ground. Give your financial prep the same patience you'd give a seedbed: start early, condition the soil, and be ready when the season opens.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does getting pre-approved hurt my credit score in Texas?

Yes, a pre-approval requires a hard credit inquiry, which typically drops your score by about five points temporarily — but the impact is minor and short-lived, usually recovering within a few months. More importantly, if you're shopping multiple lenders, credit bureaus typically treat mortgage-related hard inquiries made within a 14–45 day window as a single inquiry for scoring purposes under FICO's rate-shopping rules. So getting two or three pre-approvals in quick succession to compare rates will not compound the damage. The long-term benefit of a better interest rate secured through a competitive lender comparison far outweighs the brief score dip.

How long is a mortgage pre-approval valid in Texas?

Most pre-approval letters in Texas are valid for 60 to 90 days, after which the lender will need to re-pull your credit and verify that your financial situation hasn't changed materially. If your home search extends beyond that window — which happens frequently in lower-inventory San Antonio markets like Boerne, New Braunfels, or Alamo Heights — you'll need a refresh. This is not a big deal as long as you haven't taken on new debt or changed jobs. However, if your pre-approval expires mid-search and you've recently opened a new credit card or leased a car, your qualification numbers may shift, so avoid major financial moves between pre-approval and closing.

Can I get pre-approved with a low credit score in San Antonio?

FHA loans allow credit scores as low as 580 with a 3.5% down payment, making them one of the most accessible paths for buyers rebuilding credit in San Antonio. Scores between 500 and 579 may still qualify for FHA with a 10% down payment, though fewer lenders choose to work at that tier. VA loans technically have no minimum score set by the VA itself, though individual lenders typically impose overlays starting at 580–620. If your score is below these thresholds, many local HUD-approved housing counselors in San Antonio — including those affiliated with the nonprofit Alamo Area Council of Governments — offer free credit counseling specifically designed to help buyers reach qualification benchmarks within six to twelve months.

What's the difference between a pre-approval and a commitment letter in Texas?

A pre-approval is issued before a specific property is identified and is conditional on the home appraising and no material changes occurring in your financial picture. A loan commitment letter is issued after underwriting has reviewed both your file and the specific property — meaning the appraisal has come in, title is clear, and the underwriter is satisfied. The commitment letter is the final green light before closing and is far stronger than any pre-approval. In Texas real estate transactions, the third-party financing addendum in the TREC contract references the loan commitment date as a key deadline, making it a critical milestone that your real estate agent and lender should both be tracking closely.

Should I use a local San Antonio lender or a national online lender?

Online lenders can sometimes offer marginally lower rates, but the tradeoffs in a competitive Texas market are real and worth understanding. Local lenders have established relationships with title companies, appraisers, and listing agents in Bexar and surrounding counties — relationships that smooth out the inevitable friction points of a transaction. When a listing agent in Stone Oak calls your loan officer and gets a voicemail that routes to a national call center, that creates doubt; when they reach someone who knows the local market and can speak to your file personally, it builds confidence. Many San Antonio buyers find that the peace of mind and smoother closing process with a local lender is worth a small rate difference — and sometimes the rates are competitive anyway, especially at institutions like Randolph-Brooks FCU or USAA that have strong local roots in this market.
