Buying New Construction in San Antonio 2026: What the Builder Won't Tell You

By Chris Drawdy · March 6, 2026 · 10 min read

New construction feels like the dream — fresh finishes, no bidding wars, a home nobody else has lived in. But builders have full-time sales teams whose job is to protect the builder's bottom line, not yours. In this guide, you'll learn exactly how to negotiate builder incentives, avoid costly upgrade traps, understand your warranty rights, and walk into a San Antonio new build with your eyes wide open.

Why the San Antonio New Construction Market Looks the Way It Does Right Now

San Antonio has been one of the most active new construction markets in the entire country for the past several years, and 2026 is no different. Builders like D.R. Horton, Lennar, Perry Homes, Pulte, and Taylor Morrison are actively developing communities across Bexar County and into Comal, Guadalupe, and Kendall counties.

The growth corridors tell the story clearly. Highway 281 North toward Bulverde and Spring Branch is packed with new subdivisions targeting buyers who want Hill Country proximity. Loop 1604 West near Helotes and Culebra Road is drawing families chasing Northside ISD and NISD schools. The FM 1518 and Loop 1604 East corridor through Converse and Schertz feeds Joint Base San Antonio (JBSA) buyers and commuters heading downtown via I-35.

Inventory of existing homes in San Antonio remains tight below $350,000, which has pushed many first-time buyers and move-up buyers toward new construction as a genuinely competitive option — not just a fallback. Understanding how the process works before you set foot in a model home will save you thousands of dollars and a lot of stress.

The Builder's Sales Rep Is Not Your Agent — Here's What That Means

When you walk into a builder's model home, the person greeting you is friendly, knowledgeable, and genuinely helpful in many ways. But they are employed by the builder. Their fiduciary duty runs to the company that signs their paycheck — not to you.

That's not cynicism; it's just contract law. The builder's rep can explain floor plans, walk you through the design center, and process your purchase agreement. What they won't do is advise you to negotiate harder, flag a clause in the contract that limits your legal options, or tell you that a competing builder two miles away has a better incentive package this month.

Bringing your own buyer's agent costs you absolutely nothing on a new construction purchase. Builders budget the commission into their cost of sales, and that budget doesn't disappear if you show up unrepresented — it just stays with the builder. Having an experienced advocate in your corner at zero cost to you is one of the clearest wins in real estate, and it's one most buyers leave on the table simply because they didn't know.

Register Your Agent on Your First Visit

Most builders require your agent to be present or registered on your very first visit to the community in order to receive commission — and in order to represent you throughout the transaction. If you visit a model home solo and then try to bring an agent later, many builders will deny the registration.

Before you ever drive to a model home, contact your agent first. A quick five-minute phone call protects your representation for the entire build process, which can stretch 6–12 months on a semi-custom or spec home in San Antonio's current build-time environment.

Builder Incentives in San Antonio: What's Real and What's a Distraction

In 2026, most major builders in San Antonio are offering incentive packages in the range of $10,000–$30,000 — sometimes more on slower-moving inventory. These incentives typically come in one of three forms: closing cost assistance, mortgage rate buydowns through the builder's preferred lender, or structural upgrade credits applied at the design center.

The rate buydown incentive deserves special attention right now. With 30-year fixed rates still hovering in the mid-to-upper 6% range, a permanent 1-point buydown can meaningfully reduce your monthly payment for the life of the loan. A $400,000 home at 6.75% carries a principal and interest payment around $2,594/month; dropping that to 5.75% brings it to roughly $2,334/month — a $260/month difference that compounds to over $93,000 in savings across a 30-year loan.

Here's the catch: most of these buydowns are only available if you use the builder's preferred lender. That lender may or may not offer you the best overall terms. Before you commit, have an independent lender — local options like Frost Bank, Broadway Bank, or a credit union like RBFCU (Randolph-Brooks) — run a competing quote. The math sometimes still favors the builder's lender after the buydown, but you need to verify that for yourself rather than assume it.

When Incentives Are Negotiable

Builders are more flexible than they appear. At the end of a fiscal quarter, on a lot that's been sitting unsold, or in a community where they need to move spec homes to trigger the next phase of development, incentives can expand significantly. An experienced agent who works new construction regularly will know which communities are under pressure and when to push.

Think of it like knowing when to harvest. A tomato pulled too early is hard and flavorless; at the right moment, everything just gives. Builder negotiations work the same way — timing and local knowledge matter enormously.

Design Center Upgrades vs. Closing Cost Credits: Where Your Money Goes Further

Every new construction buyer in San Antonio eventually sits down at the builder's design center — a carefully curated showroom where every beautiful thing costs more than you expect. Upgraded flooring, extended tile, quartz countertops, a gas cooktop, a covered patio extension — these are genuinely nice things, and the builder knows exactly how to present them.

The uncomfortable truth is that design center upgrades carry the highest markup in the entire transaction. A builder may charge $8,000–$12,000 for a kitchen countertop upgrade that a licensed contractor could install post-closing for $3,500–$5,000. Flooring, lighting packages, and cabinet hardware are similarly inflated. The design center is where builders make a significant portion of their profit margin.

The smarter play is to take closing cost credits or rate buydowns when offered as an alternative to design center dollars, then use your savings after closing to hire local contractors for cosmetic upgrades on your terms. San Antonio has a strong network of tile, flooring, and remodeling contractors — many of them small local businesses that will do excellent work at fair prices. Spend builder money on structural or hard-to-change items: additional windows, an extended foundation for a larger covered patio, or a third-car garage if the lot allows it.

What's Worth Upgrading at the Design Center

Some upgrades genuinely make sense to do through the builder because adding them later is structurally difficult or disproportionately expensive. These include: rough-in plumbing for a future outdoor kitchen or water softener loop, an upgraded electrical panel or pre-wiring for solar, insulation upgrades beyond code minimum, and any structural option that requires foundation work.

Think of the design center like soil amendments — you put in the foundational elements before you plant, because going back to change the ground later disrupts everything above it. Surface-level cosmetics, though, can be added season by season after you're settled in.

Understanding Your New Home Warranty — Before You Sign

Texas has specific protections for new home buyers, and most builders operating in San Antonio follow the standard industry warranty structure: 1 year on workmanship and materials, 2 years on mechanical systems (HVAC, plumbing, electrical), and 10 years on structural defects. These timelines are not negotiable, but the process for filing claims absolutely is something you need to understand before you close.

Many builder contracts in Texas include a binding arbitration clause, meaning if you have a dispute with the builder, you waive your right to a jury trial and must resolve the issue through a private arbitration process. This isn't automatically bad, but it's something you should know about going in. Have a real estate attorney review your purchase agreement — a one-time legal consultation in San Antonio typically runs $200–$400 and is worth every cent on a $350,000+ transaction.

Document everything from day one. Walk your home at every stage of construction if the builder allows access — slab pour, framing, pre-drywall, and final walkthrough. Photograph every wall, every connection point, every area that will be covered up. This documentation is invaluable if a warranty claim ever becomes a dispute. And hire an independent inspector for your pre-drywall and final walkthroughs — don't rely solely on the builder's quality control team, which works for the builder.

Schools, Commutes, and Community Feel: What the Brochure Doesn't Show You

Builders sell lifestyle. What they don't always make clear is which school district you'll actually be zoned to, whether that district is at capacity, and how long your daily commute will realistically be once the community is fully built out and traffic patterns shift.

In the Converse and Schertz area, buyers often assume SCUC ISD (Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City) but may find themselves in Judson ISD depending on exact lot location — always verify zoning on the specific lot, not just the general community. Up in Bulverde and Spring Branch, Comal ISD is a significant draw for families, with Johnson Ranch, Kinder Ranch, and Copper Canyon all feeding into schools ranked among the best in the region. On the far northwest side near Helotes, NISD and Northside ISD share territory along Culebra Road — again, lot-specific verification matters.

Commute reality is worth a test drive — literally. Before you commit to a community on the FM 3009 corridor in Cibolo or out on US-281 near Canyon Lake, drive the route to your workplace on a Tuesday morning at 7:30 AM. New development often outpaces road infrastructure in Bexar County and surrounding counties, and that beautiful quiet neighborhood can feel very different during rush hour eighteen months after buildout when the next phase adds another 400 homes.

The Closing Process on a New Build: What's Different From Resale

Closing on a new construction home in San Antonio involves a few important differences from buying a resale property. First, your timeline is largely controlled by the builder — not by mutual agreement. Build timelines in 2026 are running 6–10 months for semi-custom builds and 60–120 days for spec (already-under-construction) homes, but weather delays, supply chain issues, and trade scheduling can extend either of these.

Your mortgage rate lock is a critical issue. Most conventional lenders will lock a rate for 30–60 days. If your build runs long, you may need to extend your lock — which costs money — or float to market rates, which carries risk. Some of the builder's preferred lenders offer extended lock programs specifically designed for new construction timelines; this is one area where their in-house lender may genuinely have an advantage. Discuss rate lock strategy with any lender you work with before you sign your purchase agreement.

Title companies and closing costs work similarly to resale, though builders often have a preferred title company. You are generally entitled to choose your own title company in Texas, but some builders offer incentives for using theirs. Compare the fees — title insurance rates in Texas are promulgated (set by the state), so the difference usually comes down to ancillary fees and convenience rather than the base premium.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I negotiate the price on a new construction home in San Antonio?

Yes, though it works differently than negotiating a resale. Builders rarely reduce the base price on paper because it affects the appraised value of all comparable homes in the community — which impacts their future sales. Instead, they negotiate through incentives: additional closing cost credits, upgraded lot premiums waived, design center allowances, or mortgage rate buydowns. On spec homes (completed or nearly completed inventory), builders are often more willing to move because carrying costs on finished homes add up quickly. An agent who actively works new construction communities across San Antonio will know which builders are under end-of-quarter pressure and how to structure a request that gets results.

Do I need a home inspection on a brand-new construction home in San Antonio?

Absolutely — and this surprises many first-time new construction buyers who assume a new home is automatically defect-free. Municipal inspectors in Bexar County and surrounding counties check for code compliance, but they are not conducting the same thorough inspection a licensed professional inspector performs. Common issues found in new San Antonio builds include improper HVAC duct sealing, grading problems that direct water toward the foundation, incomplete insulation, and minor plumbing irregularities that are cheap to fix before drywall but expensive after. Budget $400–$600 for a pre-drywall inspection and another $400–$550 for a final walkthrough inspection — these are the two most valuable dollars-per-outcome expenditures in the entire new construction process.

What happens if the builder raises prices after I sign my contract?

In Texas, once you have a fully executed purchase agreement with a fixed price, the builder is generally contractually obligated to honor that price — this is one of the advantages of new construction over resale in a rising market. However, read your contract carefully for any escalation clauses tied to material costs, and confirm that your agreed price is clearly stated as fixed. If the builder attempts to add costs mid-build through change orders you didn't authorize, document everything in writing. Texas law provides buyers with recourse through the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) and, if necessary, the arbitration process outlined in your contract. This is another reason why having a real estate attorney review your purchase agreement before you sign is genuinely important on a new build.

Are there any first-time homebuyer programs that work with new construction in San Antonio?

Yes — several, though the eligibility rules vary. The Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers down payment assistance programs, including the Home Sweet Texas program, that can be layered with new construction purchases as long as the home meets price limits (currently up to $404,800 in Bexar County for most programs as of 2026). The City of San Antonio's Homeownership Incentive Program (HIP) provides down payment and closing cost assistance for buyers in certain income brackets purchasing within city limits — some new construction communities on the inner Loop qualify. Additionally, the Texas Veterans Land Board (VLB) offers below-market interest rate loans specifically for Texas veterans that are compatible with new construction timelines. Combining one of these programs with a builder's preferred lender incentive requires careful coordination, which is exactly the kind of scenario where having an experienced agent and a knowledgeable lender working together makes a real difference.

What should I look for during a new construction final walkthrough in San Antonio?

The final walkthrough — your "blue tape" walk — is your last opportunity to document deficiencies before closing, and it's worth treating with real seriousness. Bring your independent inspector if the builder allows it. Test every outlet, every door latch, every window seal, and every faucet. In San Antonio's climate specifically, check that all exterior caulking is complete and that attic ventilation looks correct — improper attic ventilation in a South Texas summer significantly shortens roof life and drives up energy costs. Verify that all grading around the foundation slopes away from the home (critical given San Antonio's expansive clay soils), that the HVAC filter is clean and the system blows at proper temperature on all vents, and that the builder has provided all documentation: warranty certificates, appliance manuals, and a copy of the completed municipal inspection records. A punch list item that isn't documented before closing is an item the builder has little incentive to fix promptly afterward.
