Getting Into Real Estate for the Right Reasons — And Building the Patience to Last

By Chris Drawdy · February 17, 2026 · 9 min read

Real estate attracts people for all kinds of reasons — and the ones who thrive are rarely the ones chasing quick commissions or easy money. In this guide, I'll show you what entering real estate for the right reasons actually looks like, what the early years really demand, and why patience isn't just helpful — it's everything.

The Wrong Reasons to Enter Real Estate (And Why They Fail)

Let me be direct: if you're getting into real estate because you saw someone else make money, or because you think the hours are flexible and the income is automatic, you're already standing on shaky ground.

I see it happen every few months in San Antonio. Someone gets their license, talks about the freedom, mentions they'll work for themselves, and by month seven they're gone. The difference between these people and the ones who build sustainable careers almost always comes down to motivation.

The wrong reasons include:

  • Money as the primary driver. Real estate can be lucrative, but it's never the fastest path to a paycheck. If you need income in 30 days, this isn't the business for you.
  • Perceived flexibility. Yes, you set your own schedule — but that freedom means you work 50-hour weeks for your first 18 months, often unpaid. There's no "off" button when you're building a client base.
  • Escape from a job you hate. Fear-based career changes rarely stick. You need to be pulled toward something, not just running away from something else.
  • Status or ego. Real estate looks good at dinner parties until people realize you're still figuring out title insurance in month six.

The honest truth: these motivations evaporate around month four, right when things actually get hard. And that's when most people quit.

What the Right Reasons Actually Look Like

I got into real estate because I love helping people make one of the biggest decisions of their lives. I stayed in it because watching a family close on their first home in Stone Oak or seeing someone downsize into a condo in Southtown never gets old.

The right reasons are quieter. They're less about what real estate will do for you and more about what you want to do for the people in your community.

You genuinely enjoy learning about homes and neighborhoods

This might sound simple, but it matters. Do you actually enjoy walking through a home and imagining who might live there? Do you find yourself noticing architectural details, thinking about school commutes to places like Churchill High School or South San, considering how sunlight hits a property at different times of day?

If you're bored by homes themselves, real estate will bore you to tears. You'll spend more hours in houses and talking about square footage than almost any other task in the job.

You want to build trust over time

Real estate rewards patience and consistency in ways that very few businesses do. Your best clients five years from now aren't your first clients — they're people who've watched you work, referred friends, and come back because they trust you.

That kind of trust doesn't happen in month three. It happens when someone calls you at 10 p.m. with a concern and you call them back within the hour. It happens when you remember that they have a teenage daughter with soccer tournaments and help them find a home with good schools and proximity to the fields. It happens when you actually know your San Antonio communities — not just the ones that make you the most commission.

You're committed to educating, not just transacting

The agents who last are the ones who genuinely believe their clients should be informed. They explain why a home in the Medical Center might appreciate differently than one near the Pearl District. They talk honestly about property taxes (which in San Antonio vary significantly by zip code and school district). They don't push you toward the highest offer if a lower offer comes from someone who'll actually steward the home well.

This mindset is the opposite of quick money. But it's also the reason these agents still have clients 10 years in.

The Reality of the Early Years

Let's talk about what "getting into real estate" actually requires, because the honest version is harder than the recruiting version.

You will not make real money for 18–24 months

Most new agents close their first home around month four to six. After brokerage fees, MLS fees, education costs, and broker split, your commission on that first $350,000 home might net you $1,500–$2,500. You'll feel accomplished and broke at the same time.

Your expenses are immediate: licensing ($150–$200), continuing education ($500–$1,000 annually), marketing and cards ($1,000+), phone and tech ($300–$500/month), and broker fees (varies by company, but plan on 40–50% of your commission). You're spending money from day one while waiting months for the first paycheck to arrive.

Many successful San Antonio agents I know worked another job or had savings for their first year. There's no shame in that — it's honesty.

You will have to build your sphere from zero

You won't inherit clients. No one owes you their business. Even if you know 200 people in San Antonio, maybe five will actually consider working with you on a home purchase — the biggest financial decision of their lives. The rest are waiting to see if you're serious, if you know your stuff, and if you'll actually show up.

For the first 12 months, most of your prospecting will feel like rejection. You'll call people who are "not ready right now." You'll send emails that don't get opened. You'll attend community events with zero immediate payoff. The agents who make it past month 18 are the ones who stopped counting the rejections and started counting the relationships.

Your broker will probably try to recruit you with lies

Not all brokers, but enough of them. They'll show you earnings statements of top 5% agents. They'll say you can make six figures in year one (you cannot). They'll promise training and support that never materializes. They'll position their high split percentage as an "advantage" for experienced agents when really they're banking on churn.

Do your homework on San Antonio brokerages. Talk to actual agents, not just the recruiting team. Ask to see their average agent income — not the top earners, the median. Find out what "support" actually means (often: here's the MLS password).

Why Patience Is Your Real Competitive Edge

Think of building a real estate career like building a garden. You don't plant seeds on Monday and expect harvests by Friday. You prepare soil, you plant thoughtfully, you tend consistently, and somewhere around year two or three you start genuinely harvesting.

The agents who thrive are the ones who understand this deeply. They're not chasing transactions — they're building something meant to last.

Patience builds systems that compound

Every client interaction, referral, and transaction makes the next one slightly easier. Your first client took 100 hours of work spread over four months. Your 20th client takes 30 hours because you've streamlined your process, people refer their friends to you, and you actually know your market.

By year three, your best business comes with no effort beyond showing up because your reputation precedes you. But you only get there if you survive years one and two without expecting it.

Local knowledge becomes an actual moat

I can tell you which neighborhoods have the best commute to Tech Port, where young families are moving in San Antonio, which school districts are improving, why Windcrest has different property tax implications than Terrell Hills. That knowledge took years to build. No algorithm can replicate it. A new agent with a better tech platform can't out-compete me because they don't know that the Alamo Heights school district is in the North East ISD, or that properties near the airport see different appreciation curves.

That moat — that deep local knowledge — is only valuable if you stay around long enough to build it.

Questions to Ask Yourself Before You Get Licensed

Before you sign up for that licensing course, sit with these questions honestly. This is between you and you — no one's judging.

Do I have financial runway? Can you cover your living expenses for 12 months without real estate income? If not, this isn't the right time, and that's okay. A part-time role, a partner's income, or savings matters.

Am I running toward something or away from something? Real estate is hard enough when you want to be there. It's impossible when you're just avoiding somewhere else. Be honest about which category you're in.

Do I actually like people and homes, or do I like the idea of real estate? The day-to-day work is showing homes, listening to what people need, and solving problems. If that doesn't sound appealing, this job will drain you.

Can I handle months of "no" before I get to "yes"? Most of your prospecting will be rejection. Most of your leads won't close. Most of your hours won't directly produce income. If you need immediate validation, this will hurt.

Do I want to serve a community, or do I want to get rich quick? These aren't mutually exclusive long-term, but short-term they are. Choose your priority honestly.

How to Start Right (If You Decide to Go All In)

If you've asked yourself those questions and you're still here, here's how to start in a way that sets you up for the long game.

Choose a broker aligned with your values, not their earnings model

In San Antonio, you have options: independent brokers, regional companies like eXp Realty (which I'm with), big franchises like Keller Williams, and boutique agencies. Don't choose based on commission split. Choose based on whether the broker's philosophy matches yours.

Ask about training, whether they support agents for longer than 90 days, and what happens when you're in month six with no closings. The brokers who take care of their agents in that period are the ones building real careers.

Invest in deep market knowledge, not flashy marketing

Your first priority isn't a fancy website or a billboard. It's knowing San Antonio neighborhoods inside-out. Walk through Boerne, Bulverde, the North Loop, Southtown, the Medical Center. Understand property tax rates, school boundaries, median prices, appreciation trends. Talk to 100 locals about where they love living and why.

That knowledge is your actual product. Everything else is secondary.

Build relationships before you need sales

Join the Greater San Antonio Home Builders Association. Volunteer with local nonprofits. Attend neighborhood association meetings. Build real relationships with other agents, lenders, and inspectors. These people become your professional family and your referral source.

You're not "networking for leads." You're building a community. That's how you actually survive.

Be transparent about what you don't know yet

New agents don't need to pretend to be experts. Your clients need you to be honest, quick to find answers, and committed to learning. "I don't know, but I'll find out by tomorrow" is infinitely better than confidently giving bad advice.

The Patience Advantage Over Time

Here's what most people don't talk about: by year three or four, if you've stuck it out, you'll be at a massive advantage to anyone just starting. While they're struggling through their own early years, you'll have built a reputation, a referral network, systems that work, and actual knowledge.

The San Antonio real estate market moves fast, but relationships move slower. If you can hold the line through the hard years, you'll have built something durable. You'll know which lenders actually close on time (crucial in San Antonio's competitive market). You'll have referral partners. You'll have repeat clients.

Most importantly, you'll have earned the trust of your community. And that's worth infinitely more than a quick commission.

Real Estate Isn't Wrong for You — But It Might Be Wrong for Right Now

If you're feeling pulled toward real estate but unsure about timing or approach, that's actually wisdom, not hesitation. The best agents I know took their time deciding. They talked to people in the business. They tested their assumptions.

Real estate will be here. The market in San Antonio isn't going anywhere. If this is genuinely the right move for you, getting in six months later with better preparation is infinitely better than getting in tomorrow and burning out in month five.

The agents who make it are the ones who understand that real estate isn't a get-rich-quick scheme — it's a craft. Like gardening, it rewards patience, consistency, and genuine care. If you're willing to show up with that mindset, you'll build something meaningful.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the realistic first-year income for a new real estate agent in San Antonio?

Most agents who stay active close 2–4 homes in their first year, netting roughly $3,000–$8,000 per transaction after broker fees and expenses. That's $6,000–$32,000 total, but many close zero homes and make nothing. Your actual income depends entirely on your effort, market conditions, and whether you have an existing network. San Antonio's median home price of $340,000 means an average commission of $10,200 (3%), split between you and your broker, which often means $2,500–$5,000 per transaction before taxes, licensing, and marketing costs.

How long does it actually take to become a licensed real estate agent in Texas?

Texas requires 4 hours of pre-licensing coursework and a passing score on the state exam, which can typically be completed in 1–2 weeks if you're moving quickly. However, passing the exam isn't the same as being ready to sell homes—that knowledge gap is why so many new agents struggle. After licensing, you'll need to choose a broker, complete any broker-specific training, and set up your MLS access, which adds another 1–3 weeks. The real timeline to your first client interaction is more like 4–8 weeks if you're organized.

Should I get into real estate part-time while keeping my job?

Part-time real estate can work if you have a flexible job and realistic expectations—but "part-time" in real estate means 25–35 hours per week, not evenings and weekends. Clients don't coordinate their home searches around your day job. That said, starting part-time with income from another source is actually safer than going all-in without financial runway. The agents who succeed part-time are usually ones who can be responsive during key hours (mornings, early evenings, weekends) and who treat it like a real business, not a side hustle.

What are the biggest reasons new agents fail in San Antonio?

The top reasons I've seen: expecting income before they've built relationships (usually by month 3–4), not investing in local market knowledge, choosing the wrong broker, and not having financial runway to survive the early months. San Antonio's market is competitive and growing, so agents without a clear understanding of neighborhoods like Dominion, Alamo Ranch, and the North Loop, or who can't articulate why a client should work with them specifically, get filtered out quickly. Additionally, many new agents treat it like a job instead of a business—they work when they feel like it, and that sporadic effort shows in their results.

Is now a good time to get into real estate in San Antonio?

San Antonio's market is stable and growing, with consistent demand and new development in areas like Boerne and New Braunfels, so the market opportunity is real. However, "good time" for you personally depends entirely on your financial situation, motivation, and readiness for the early grind—not on market conditions. Even in slow markets, patient agents with deep roots and real client relationships do well. Even in hot markets, agents who chase commissions and cut corners fail. Focus less on "is now good" and more on "am I ready?"
