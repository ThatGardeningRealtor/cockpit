How to Buy a Home in San Antonio With No Down Payment: 5 Real Options in 2026

By Chris Drawdy · June 5, 2026 · 10 min read

A lot of San Antonio buyers assume they need tens of thousands of dollars saved before they can even start looking — but that's simply not true in 2026. Between VA loans, USDA eligibility zones, city-funded assistance programs, gift funds, and smart negotiation tactics, there are real, legitimate paths to closing day with little or nothing out of your own pocket. This guide walks you through all five options in plain language, with local details that actually apply to this city.

Option 1: VA Loans — The Gold Standard for Military Families in San Antonio

San Antonio is one of the most military-connected cities in the country, home to Joint Base San Antonio-Lackland, Fort Sam Houston, Camp Bullis, and Randolph Air Force Base. That means a significant portion of buyers here have access to what is genuinely the best zero-down loan product available anywhere — the VA loan.

VA loans require no down payment, no private mortgage insurance (PMI), and offer competitive interest rates backed by the Department of Veterans Affairs. In a city where even a modest home in Converse or Universal City runs $230,000–$270,000, eliminating a 3–5% down payment saves you $7,000 to $13,000 right out of the gate.

Eligibility extends to active-duty service members, veterans, National Guard and Reserve members who meet minimum service requirements, and surviving spouses. If you've served and haven't used your VA benefit, it's worth a conversation with a lender before you assume you don't qualify.

What VA Buyers Should Know in the SA Market

One misconception is that sellers don't like VA offers — that hasn't been our experience in the current San Antonio market. With inventory more balanced in 2026 than it was in 2021–2022, VA buyers are competing more effectively, especially in neighborhoods like Schertz, Selma, and the northeast side near Randolph.

You will still pay a VA funding fee — typically 2.15% of the loan amount for first-time use with no down payment — but this can be rolled into your loan rather than paid at closing. Veterans with a service-connected disability rating of 10% or higher are exempt from this fee entirely.

Local lenders with strong VA experience include USAA (headquartered here in San Antonio), Generations Federal Credit Union, and Security Service Federal Credit Union. Working with a lender who processes VA loans daily makes a real difference in timeline and seller confidence.

Option 2: USDA Loans — More San Antonio Zip Codes Qualify Than You'd Think

USDA loans are often dismissed as "rural only," but that undersells them significantly in the San Antonio metro. Parts of the city's outer edges and many surrounding communities still fall within USDA-eligible zones as of 2026 — and that designation changes periodically, so checking now matters.

Areas like Von Ormy, Natalia, Lytle, Pleasanton, Floresville, and Seguin all have strong USDA eligibility. Even some sections on the far western and southern outskirts of San Antonio proper — communities along Highway 90 and beyond Loop 1604 — may qualify depending on updated census designations.

Like VA loans, USDA loans offer 100% financing with no down payment required. They do carry a small upfront guarantee fee (currently 1% of the loan) and an annual fee of 0.35% — but there's no PMI, and rates are typically very competitive for buyers who meet income guidelines.

Income Limits and What They Mean for Local Buyers

For Bexar County, the USDA income limit for a household of 1–4 is approximately $110,650 in 2026. Larger households get higher limits — a household of 5–8 can earn up to roughly $146,050. These are gross household income figures, not just the borrower's income.

The tradeoff with USDA is location. If your heart is set on the Alamo Heights school district, Castle Hills, or the Stone Oak corridor, USDA likely won't apply. But if you're open to the Floresville ISD area or Medina Valley ISD — both of which have solid schools and easy commutes down Highway 97 or US-90 — USDA opens real doors.

Think of USDA eligibility like good soil — it doesn't exist everywhere, but where it does, things grow beautifully. Getting pre-approved specifically for USDA with a lender familiar with the program is the fastest way to know what's possible for your situation.

Option 3: Down Payment Assistance Programs — Stacking Your Way to Zero

This is where buyers leave the most money on the table, simply because they don't know these programs exist. San Antonio and the state of Texas both fund meaningful down payment assistance, and in many cases you can layer multiple programs together — a strategy sometimes called "DPA stacking."

The City of San Antonio's Homeownership Incentive Program (HIP) offers forgivable grants of up to $30,000 for income-eligible buyers purchasing within city limits. Income limits vary by household size, but a family of four can earn up to roughly 80% of the Area Median Income — approximately $68,000 in 2026 — and still qualify.

The Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers the Homes for Texas Heroes and Home Sweet Texas programs, which provide 3–5% of the loan amount as either a grant or a deferred second lien. Heroes programs specifically benefit teachers (NISD, SAISD, NEISD employees, take note), police officers, firefighters, veterans, and healthcare workers.

How DPA Stacking Actually Works

Stacking means combining a first mortgage — often an FHA loan at 3.5% down — with a DPA grant that covers that 3.5% and potentially your closing costs too. Done correctly, this genuinely gets you to zero out of pocket at closing, or very close to it.

The Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs (TDHCA) My First Texas Home program pairs a 30-year fixed mortgage with up to 5% in down payment and closing cost assistance. TDHCA also allows that assistance to be combined with federal Mortgage Credit Certificates (MCCs), which reduce your federal tax liability by up to $2,000 per year for the life of the loan.

The complexity here is real — DPA programs have layering rules, lender eligibility requirements, and income caps that interact with each other. That's why working with a lender who specializes in assistance programs (like Cornerstone Home Lending or CrossCountry Mortgage, both active in San Antonio) matters as much as working with the right realtor.

Option 4: Gift Funds — Using Family Support the Right Way

Many buyers receive financial help from family members and aren't sure whether that's allowed — the answer is yes, with proper documentation. Gift funds are a legitimate and commonly used source of down payment money across FHA, VA, USDA, and even conventional loans.

For FHA loans, 100% of your down payment can come from a gift, as long as it's from an eligible donor (parent, grandparent, sibling, employer, or certain charitable organizations). The donor must provide a signed gift letter stating the funds are not a loan and will not need to be repaid.

Conventional loans have slightly more nuanced rules — if you're putting less than 20% down, at least some of the funds may need to come from your own account depending on the loan type. Your lender will walk you through exactly what's required for your specific situation.

Avoiding Common Gift Fund Mistakes

The most common mistake is depositing gift money into your account without telling your lender first. Underwriters track "large deposits" — typically anything over 50% of your monthly income — and will require a paper trail explaining where it came from.

The cleanest approach is to coordinate with your lender before the funds move. In many cases, the donor can wire the gift directly to the title company at closing, which simplifies documentation considerably. Local title companies like Alamo Title or Capital Title of Texas handle this regularly and can guide you through the process.

Gift funds work especially well when paired with a DPA grant — the grant covers your down payment, and a family gift covers moving costs, first-year maintenance, or that fence you've been dreaming about for the backyard.

Option 5: Seller Concessions — Negotiating Closing Costs Out of the Equation

Even if your loan requires a small down payment, closing costs — which typically run 2–4% of the purchase price in San Antonio — can catch buyers off guard. Seller concessions are a negotiated agreement where the seller pays a portion of your closing costs, reducing or eliminating what you bring to the table.

On a $270,000 home, 3% in seller concessions equals $8,100 — enough to cover the bulk of most buyers' closing costs, including lender fees, title insurance, prepaid taxes, and homeowner's insurance escrow. This isn't asking the seller for a handout; it's a standard negotiation tool, especially when homes have been sitting on the market or when you have competing factors to offer, like flexibility on closing date.

Conventional loans allow up to 3% in seller concessions when the buyer puts less than 10% down, and up to 6% for buyers putting down more. FHA allows up to 6%. VA loans cap at 4% for traditional concessions but allow sellers to pay all loan-related closing costs separately — making them especially powerful for zeroing out costs.

Using Concessions Strategically in Today's SA Market

In neighborhoods where inventory has softened — places like the far northwest side near Helotes, portions of the South Side near Brooks City Base, or outer suburbs like Cibolo and Converse — sellers are more willing to negotiate concessions than they were two or three years ago.

The key is structuring the offer correctly. Asking for concessions without understanding how they affect the seller's net proceeds can kill a deal before it starts. A well-written offer that accounts for seller motivation, pricing the home fairly, and requesting concessions as part of a complete package is an art form — and it's something an experienced local agent genuinely earns their commission on.

Concessions can also be combined with DPA programs and gift funds. Think of it like companion planting in the garden — each element supports the others, and the overall outcome is stronger than any single piece working alone.

Putting It All Together: What Zero Down Really Looks Like in San Antonio

Let's make this concrete. A veteran buying a $265,000 home in Converse uses a VA loan — zero down payment, zero PMI, and asks the seller for 3% in concessions to cover closing costs. Total out of pocket: close to zero, minus the VA funding fee rolled into the loan.

A schoolteacher at a Northside ISD campus uses the TSAHC Homes for Texas Heroes program for 5% assistance on a $240,000 FHA loan. That covers the 3.5% FHA down payment and leaves cash for closing costs, with a family gift filling any remaining gap. She walks into closing without draining her savings account.

A young couple buying in Floresville uses a USDA loan — no down payment — and negotiates $6,000 in seller concessions on a home that's been listed for 40 days. They pay for their appraisal and inspection out of pocket (roughly $600–$900 total) and finalize their first home with their emergency fund intact. These aren't hypotheticals — these are the kinds of paths San Antonio buyers are walking right now.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I buy a home in San Antonio with bad credit and no down payment?

It's harder but not impossible. VA and USDA loans don't set a strict minimum credit score at the federal level, but most lenders require at least a 580–620 for VA and 640 for USDA. FHA loans accept scores as low as 580 with 3.5% down, and some DPA programs accept 620. If your credit is below those thresholds, spending 6–12 months paying down revolving balances, disputing errors, and keeping utilization below 30% can move your score meaningfully. A HUD-approved housing counselor — SAHA (San Antonio Housing Authority) offers free counseling — can give you a personalized roadmap without any cost or obligation.

What are the income limits for the City of San Antonio down payment assistance program in 2026?

The City of San Antonio's HIP program ties limits to Area Median Income (AMI) as published annually by HUD. For 2026, a single buyer earning up to approximately $53,600, or a household of four earning up to roughly $76,600, falls within the 80% AMI threshold that most tiers require. Some program tiers serve buyers up to 120% AMI with a smaller benefit amount. These numbers shift annually, and the program has funding cycles that can open and close throughout the year — so timing your application matters. The City's Neighborhood and Housing Services Department (NHSD) maintains the current figures and processes applications directly.

How long does it take to close on a home using down payment assistance in San Antonio?

DPA transactions typically add 7–14 days to a standard closing timeline because additional layers of approval, documentation, and funding requests are involved. Most DPA-backed closings in San Antonio close in 35–50 days from executed contract, compared to 21–30 days for a straightforward conventional loan. The biggest variables are lender experience with the specific DPA product and how quickly the buyer provides required documentation. Choosing a lender who processes TSAHC or TDHCA loans regularly — rather than one learning the process on your file — can keep things moving on schedule and avoid seller frustration with extended timelines.

Are there no-down-payment options for buying a home in San Antonio as a first-time buyer with no military service?

Absolutely — non-military first-time buyers have several strong paths. USDA loans are the cleanest zero-down option if you're buying in an eligible area. The TSAHC Home Sweet Texas program provides down payment assistance as a grant (no repayment required) that can effectively bring your FHA down payment to zero. TDHCA's My First Texas Home adds closing cost assistance on top. If you're a teacher, nurse, first responder, or work in another qualifying profession, the Heroes programs apply even without military service. The combination of an FHA loan at 3.5% with a 5% TSAHC grant frequently results in buyers receiving a small amount back at closing — legally structured as a credit.

Do USDA loan eligible areas near San Antonio include good school districts?

Several USDA-eligible communities surrounding San Antonio feed into school districts with solid reputations. Floresville ISD has seen significant investment in facilities and consistently strong community involvement. Medina Valley ISD, serving parts of Castroville and Lytle, is smaller and tight-knit with competitive extracurricular programs. Pleasanton ISD in Atascosa County is another well-regarded option for families prioritizing a smaller school environment. If you're comparing school performance data, GreatSchools.org and the Texas Education Agency's accountability ratings are the most reliable current sources. The commute from these areas to major San Antonio employment centers — downtown, the South Texas Medical Center, or Lackland — typically runs 30–45 minutes via US-281, US-87, or US-90 depending on the exact address.
