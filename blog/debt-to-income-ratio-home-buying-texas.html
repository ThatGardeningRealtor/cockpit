What Is Debt-to-Income Ratio and Why It Actually Matters

Your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is a percentage that tells lenders how much of your monthly gross income goes toward debt payments. It's the lens through which banks decide whether you can handle a mortgage payment without becoming house-poor. Think of it like a garden's soil-to-water ratio—get it right, and everything thrives; get it wrong, and your growth is stunted.

Banks use DTI because monthly income is the most reliable predictor of repayment ability. If you earn $5,000 a month and owe $2,000 on existing debts, your DTI is already 40%—before adding a mortgage payment. Lenders won't approve a home that pushes you over their threshold, no matter how much you love that corner lot in Alamo Heights or the newer construction in The Dominion.

The higher your DTI, the less home you can afford, even if your credit score is perfect and you have savings for a substantial down payment. This number is often the deal-breaker or deal-maker in San Antonio's market, where multiple offers and quick closings reward pre-approved buyers with clean financial profiles.

Front-End vs. Back-End DTI: Which One Matters More

Lenders calculate two separate DTI numbers, and both must pass their approval thresholds. Understanding the difference is crucial—many borrowers optimize for one and overlook the other.

Front-End DTI (Housing Ratio)

Front-end DTI measures only your housing costs against your gross monthly income. These costs include your mortgage principal, interest, property taxes, homeowners insurance, and (if applicable) HOA fees and mortgage insurance (PMI or MIP).

The formula is simple: (Housing Payment ÷ Gross Monthly Income) × 100 = Front-End DTI. Most lenders cap front-end DTI at 28–31%, depending on loan type and credit profile.

For example, if you earn $6,000 gross per month and your total housing payment would be $1,650, your front-end DTI is 27.5%—well within conventional limits. San Antonio's property taxes run around 0.98% of assessed value (lower than Texas average), which helps keep housing costs reasonable in neighborhoods like Stone Oak and Windcrest.

Back-End DTI (Total Debt Ratio)

Back-end DTI includes all debt: housing payment, car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, child support, personal loans, and any other monthly obligations. This is the number that trips up most buyers, because it's much harder to control.

The formula is: (Total Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income) × 100 = Back-End DTI. Conventional loans typically max out at 43%, FHA loans at 50%, and VA loans have no official cap (though lenders may apply their own limits).

If that same $6,000/month earner has a $450 car payment and $200 in student loan payments, their total debt jumps to $2,300—a back-end DTI of 38.3%. Now they have only about 5–12% of breathing room before hitting lender limits.

How Lenders Calculate Your DTI: The Real Numbers That Count

Not every payment counts equally, and lenders have strict rules about what gets included. This is where many borrowers misunderstand their actual DTI.

What Counts as Debt

Always counted: Car loans, auto leases, student loans (all payments), credit card minimum payments (not full balance—just the minimum), personal loans, medical collections, past-due child support or alimony, lines of credit, and any accounts in collections that are active.

Counted for new mortgage: The new mortgage payment itself, including principal, interest, property taxes, homeowners insurance (HO insurance in San Antonio averages $1,100–$1,400 annually), PMI or MIP, and HOA dues.

Not counted: Utilities, phone bills, insurance (car/health/life), groceries, Netflix subscriptions, or any expense not related to debt obligation. Rent doesn't count if you're moving out, but current rent can count if you're keeping a rental property.

Credit cards—the gray zone: If you're carrying a balance, lenders count the monthly minimum payment (typically 1–3% of balance). If the card has no balance but is open, most lenders count 3–5% of the credit limit as a phantom payment. This is why closing old credit cards before applying can actually hurt your DTI.

Income That Counts

Lenders are conservative with income. You must verify everything with tax returns, pay stubs, W-2s, or other documentation. Your gross income counts—before taxes, not after.

For W-2 employees, use your current annual salary divided by 12. For self-employed or side-income earners, lenders average your last two years of tax returns. If you've been self-employed less than two years, they may count only last year—or deny that income entirely.

Bonuses, commissions, and overtime typically require 2+ years of documented history. Veterans' disability payments, Social Security, alimony, and child support count as verifiable income, but each has specific documentation requirements.

DTI Requirements by Loan Type: FHA, VA, and Conventional

Different loan programs have different DTI tolerances. What's approvable on an FHA loan might be rejected on conventional, and vice versa. Understanding these boundaries is critical for San Antonio buyers working with local lenders like USAA, Comerica, or Southwest Bancorp of Texas.

Conventional Loans (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac)

Conventional loans are the strictest: 28% front-end and 43% back-end for most borrowers. With excellent credit (760+), significant reserves, or substantial down payment (20%+), some lenders may push to 50% back-end, but this is rare.

Conventional loans don't require mortgage insurance if you put down 20%, which saves money over the life of the loan—but they demand tighter DTI ratios upfront. In San Antonio's neighborhoods like North Star, The Dominion, and Alamo Heights, many homes are priced to attract conventional buyers with strong finances.

FHA Loans (Federal Housing Administration)

FHA loans allow up to 31% front-end and 50% back-end DTI for well-qualified borrowers. This flexibility makes FHA ideal for first-time buyers or those with higher existing debt loads.

The trade-off: FHA requires mortgage insurance (MIP), both upfront (1.75% of loan amount) and annually (0.55–0.80%). A $300,000 FHA mortgage costs about $5,250 upfront plus roughly $138–$200 monthly in MIP—but the higher DTI allowance can make the monthly payment affordable.

Many San Antonio buyers in growing areas like Windcrest, Live Oak, and New Braunfels use FHA to get into the market faster, then refinance to conventional later when they've built equity and paid down other debt.

VA Loans (Veterans Affairs)

VA loans have no official DTI cap—each lender sets their own, but many VA specialists approve up to 50% or even 60% back-end for veterans with strong payment history. This flexibility reflects the VA's mission to support military homeownership.

VA loans require no down payment, no mortgage insurance, and offer competitive interest rates. The funding fee (1.25–2.3% of loan amount) is often rolled into the loan balance. For San Antonio's military community (home to Lackland Air Force Base and Fort Sam Houston), VA loans are often the most affordable path to homeownership.

However, a high DTI on a VA loan doesn't mean you should max it out—a 55% DTI still means over half your income goes to debt. Just because you can doesn't mean you should.

How to Calculate Your Own DTI in Five Minutes

You don't need a lender's approval to know your DTI. Pull out a calculator and be brutally honest about every monthly debt obligation.

Step 1: Calculate Gross Monthly Income

Add up all sources: salary, bonuses (if consistent and documented), self-employment income (average of last 2 years), pensions, disability payments, rental income, or alimony received. Use gross (before taxes).

If you earn $75,000 annually, your gross monthly income is $6,250. If you have a side business earning $15,000 annually but only over the last 18 months, lenders may not count it yet (the 2-year rule is strict).

Step 2: List All Monthly Debt Payments

Write down every payment:

• Car loan: $450
• Student loans: $200
• Credit card minimum (if carrying balance): $50
• Personal loan: $150
• Child support: $300
Total existing debt: $1,150

Step 3: Estimate Your Potential Mortgage Payment

Use an online mortgage calculator (plug in a loan amount, current rates, your down payment, property taxes, and insurance). For a $300,000 home in San Antonio with 5% down, 6.8% interest, 30-year term, plus property tax and insurance, expect roughly $1,950/month (rates and taxes change daily).

Step 4: Calculate Back-End DTI

($1,150 existing + $1,950 mortgage) ÷ $6,250 = $3,100 ÷ $6,250 = 0.496 = 49.6% back-end DTI

This borrower can get an FHA loan but is outside conventional limits. They'd need to either increase income, lower debt, or reduce the home price.

Step 5: Calculate Front-End DTI

$1,950 (housing only) ÷ $6,250 = 0.312 = 31.2% front-end DTI

Front-end is fine, but back-end is the problem—a common scenario for borrowers with student loans or car debt.

Seven Fastest Ways to Lower Your DTI Before Applying

You can't grow a garden overnight, but you can prepare the soil in 30–60 days. Same with DTI improvement. Here are the most effective strategies local San Antonio lenders actually see work:

1. Pay Down Revolving Credit (Credit Cards) Aggressively

This is the fastest win. Lenders count credit card minimums, but if you pay down balances, those minimums shrink. Paying a $5,000 card from 50% to 10% utilization can cut your counted payment from $150 to $30 immediately—without affecting your credit much.

Don't close the account after paying it down; closing cards lowers your total available credit and can hurt your score. Just let it sit at zero balance.

2. Pay Off a Car Loan or Personal Loan Completely

If you have $3,000–$5,000 sitting in savings or can get a bonus/refund, paying off a smaller loan eliminates that entire monthly payment from DTI. A $250/month car payment represents $250 ÷ $6,250 = 4% of DTI—sometimes the difference between approval and denial.

3. Increase Your Income (Documented)

This takes longer but is powerful. If you ask for a raise, change jobs to higher pay, or have consistent side income documented over 2 years, you can increase gross income without adding debt. A $500/month income bump (from $6,250 to $6,750) lowers your DTI ratio across the board.

Self-employed buyers: ensure you're filing taxes consistently; unpaid income doesn't count until it's on a return.

4. Reduce the Home Price You're Targeting

Obvious but effective. Lowering your offer from $350,000 to $320,000 reduces the mortgage payment (and DTI) by several percentage points. In San Antonio's diverse market—from affordable Southside homes to premium North Star properties—there's flexibility at every budget level.

5. Increase Your Down Payment

A larger down payment means a smaller loan, which means a smaller monthly payment and lower DTI. Going from 3% to 10% down on a $300,000 home drops the mortgage payment by about $170/month, reducing back-end DTI by nearly 3 percentage points.

6. Don't Open New Accounts or Take on New Debt

This is passive but critical. Even a small new credit card account can trigger a hard inquiry, and opening new debt before mortgage approval can disqualify you. Dealerships pushing end-of-year car purchases? Wait. Furniture store financing? Wait. Everything can wait until after closing.

7. Consolidate Student Loans (Strategic Timing)

Income-driven repayment plans can lower your counted student loan payment significantly. If you're on Standard repayment ($400/month) but could shift to Income-Based Repayment ($150/month), that cuts $250 from your DTI. However, consolidation can temporarily lower your credit score, so do this 60+ days before applying.

San Antonio-Specific Considerations: Taxes, HOAs, and Growth Neighborhoods

San Antonio's real estate landscape is unique, and DTI planning should account for local factors most guides miss.

Property taxes and appraisals: Bexar County property taxes are moderate (0.98% of assessed value), but appraisals can surprise you. Homes in hot neighborhoods like The Dominion, North Star, and Stone Oak appreciate quickly; a $300,000 purchase may appraise higher, pushing your mortgage insurance or rates. Budget conservatively.

HOA fees: Many San Antonio communities have HOAs (The Dominion: ~$380/month; Windcrest: ~$200–$350). These count toward housing costs and front-end DTI. When calculating affordability, add HOA to your mortgage estimate.

Growth and inflation: San Antonio's population is growing (Toyota's North Texas plant decision, local tech expansion), which means rising home prices and property taxes. A 3% annual tax increase compounds; factor this into long-term affordability, not just DTI today.

Military-connected buyers: San Antonio's military presence (Lackland AFB, Fort Sam Houston) creates VA loan demand. If you're military-connected, prioritize VA lenders like USAA or Military.com partners who understand your unique DTI flexibility.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if my DTI is 45% but I have 20% down and perfect credit?

Credit score and down payment help, but they don't override DTI. A 45% back-end DTI is above conventional limits (43%) and most lenders' comfort zones, regardless of how strong your credit is. You'd need FHA (which allows 50%) or a VA loan, or you'd need to lower debt or increase income. Some portfolio lenders or credit unions have flexibility, but you'd pay for it with higher rates. The reality is that DTI reflects actual repayment capacity, not just creditworthiness—a lender can't approve you to become house-poor, even if you've never missed a payment.

Do I count my spouse's income and debt if we're married but file taxes separately?

Only if both of you are applying for the mortgage. If only you're on the loan, only your income and debt count, even if you're married. If both are applying, both incomes and all debts (even those in one spouse's name) count together. This can hurt if one spouse has high debt; some couples avoid joint applications, but then that spouse can't be on the deed. Talk to your lender about community property rules in Texas—they don't override DTI calculation, but they affect liability. In most cases, having both incomes on the application improves your approval odds, even with higher combined debt.

Can I exclude my student loans from DTI if I'm on income-driven repayment?

No. Lenders count whatever your documented payment is, even on IBR or PAYE plans. If your payment is $100/month under income-driven repayment, that $100 counts—not the standard repayment amount. However, switching to an income-driven plan before applying can reduce your counted payment (and lower your DTI immediately). Just do this 60+ days before applying, so the change shows on your credit report and loan servicer statements. The earlier you switch, the more stable the change looks to lenders.

Why do lenders count credit card minimums on cards with zero balance?

Lenders apply a phantom payment because they assume you'll use the card again. Most count 3–5% of available credit limit; a $10,000 card with zero balance counts as $300–$500 in monthly debt, even though you're not carrying a balance. This is why paying off high-limit cards is effective (removes the phantom payment) but closing them is bad (removes the credit line and lowers your available credit ratio). Instead, pay them off and leave them open. The system seems backward, but it protects lenders from you maxing out cards right after mortgage approval.

If I'm self-employed with variable income, how do lenders handle my DTI?

Lenders average your last two years of tax returns (or sometimes use a lower of the two years). If you earned $60,000 year one and $80,000 year two, they might count $70,000 or even just $60,000, depending on the lender. They'll also look at a profit-and-loss statement for this year; a strong YTD average can help. If you're newly self-employed (under 2 years), most conventional lenders won't count that income at all. FHA is more flexible and may count documented income from 1–2 years. Bonus: keep meticulous records and file taxes on time; inconsistencies kill self-employed buyer approvals.
