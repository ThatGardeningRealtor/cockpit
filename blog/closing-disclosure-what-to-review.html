The Closing Disclosure: What to Review Before You Sign and Numbers That Can Surprise You

By Chris Drawdy · July 24, 2025 · 9 min read

The Closing Disclosure (CD) is your final financial roadmap before you get the keys to your San Antonio home. In this guide, you'll learn exactly what to check line by line, how to spot common errors, and why understanding prepaid items versus closing costs can save you thousands—or at least spare you an unpleasant surprise at the closing table.

What Is the Closing Disclosure and When Do You Get It?

The Closing Disclosure is a standardized federal form (TRID — Total Return Information Disclosure) that lists every financial detail of your real estate transaction in one place. It's essentially your final bill before you sign.

By federal law, your lender must deliver the CD to you at least three business days before your closing date. This is non-negotiable, even in a fast-closing scenario, so don't let anyone pressure you to skip this review window.

Think of it like a garden inspection before planting season: you wouldn't skip checking the soil, pH, and drainage just to get started faster. The CD is that inspection for your home purchase.

The Five Sections of the Closing Disclosure You Must Know

The CD is divided into clear sections, and knowing where to focus your energy saves time and catches errors.

Section 1: Loan Terms

This section shows your loan amount, interest rate, loan type (fixed, ARM, etc.), and monthly payment. Verify that the interest rate matches your Loan Estimate and lock agreement—even a 0.125% difference can add thousands in interest over 30 years.

Check that the loan amount is correct. If you were approved for $350,000 and agreed to a 20% down payment, your loan should reflect that exactly. Errors here cascade through the entire CD.

Section 2: Property and Transaction Details

Here's where your property address, sale price, and closing date live. Confirm the address is spelled correctly and matches your purchase agreement—especially important in established neighborhoods like Alamo Heights or Stone Oak where similar street names can cause mix-ups.

Verify the sale price and any adjustments. In San Antonio's competitive market, make sure any contingencies or price adjustments are reflected accurately.

Section 3: Closing Costs Breakdown

This is the heavy lifting section. Here you'll see loan-related fees, title services, transfer taxes, insurance, and HOA charges broken into "paid by borrower" and "paid by seller" columns.

We'll dive deeper into this below, but the key is line-by-line verification. A single typo can cost hundreds.

Section 4: Calculating Cash to Close

This section shows the actual cash you need to bring to closing—not just closing costs, but a calculation that includes your earnest money credit, down payment, and all fees.

This number should match what your loan officer told you to expect. If it's different by more than $50–$100, ask questions immediately.

Section 5: Summary and Authorization

The final section recaps your total fees and serves as your authorization page. Don't skip reading it just because you're ready to be done.

Line-by-Line Closing Cost Review: The Real Details That Matter

Closing costs are where surprises live. Let's break down the most common line items you'll see on a San Antonio transaction.

Origination Charges (Lender Fees)

These are fees your lender charges to process and underwrite your loan. Common items include origination fees (typically 0.5–1% of loan amount), underwriting fees ($400–$600), and processing fees ($300–$500).

Compare these directly to your initial Loan Estimate. They should match within a narrow margin. If your lender quoted 0.75% origination and the CD shows 1.25%, call immediately.

Appraisal and Title Services

The appraisal fee (typically $500–$700 in San Antonio) covers the property valuation. Title insurance and title search fees ($1,000–$2,000) protect you against ownership disputes.

Title fees vary by title company. Shop these early—you have the right to choose your title company in Texas, and comparing quotes between companies like Stewart Title, Chicago Title, and local firms can save $200–$400.

Property Taxes and Insurance Reserves

Your lender will likely require a property tax and homeowners insurance escrow. These are prepaid amounts held in reserve to pay your taxes and insurance annually.

The amount depends on your annual tax and insurance estimates. San Antonio property taxes average 0.75–0.85% of home value annually—higher than many Texas cities due to education funding formulas. Verify these calculations are based on realistic estimates for your neighborhood (Windcrest taxes differ significantly from Lincoln Heights, for example).

HOA Fees and Transfer Taxes

If your new home is in an HOA community (Stone Oak, Dominion, Alamo Ranch), you may see HOA transfer fees ($100–$500), HOA membership fees, or homeowner association insurance requirements.

In Texas, transfer taxes are generally low or nonexistent depending on your county and city. Bexar County doesn't charge transfer tax, but some cities within it may. Verify you're not being charged for a tax that shouldn't apply.

Home Inspection and Pest Control

While these sometimes appear on the CD, they're often paid outside of closing. Make sure any pest inspection or termite report fees aren't duplicated—once paid to the inspector directly and again through escrow.

Prepaid Items vs. Closing Costs: The Critical Difference

This is where many buyers get confused—and it matters for your cash-to-close calculation.

Prepaid items are costs for services you haven't used yet: homeowners insurance premiums for the first year, property taxes for the remainder of the month, HOA dues, or utility deposits.

Closing costs are one-time fees for the transaction itself: title insurance, appraisal, origination fees, and agent commissions. These don't come back to you.

Here's why this matters: prepaid items are often negotiable between buyer and seller. In a buyer's market, you might ask the seller to pay your first month's HOA dues or property tax reserves. In a seller's market, they likely won't.

On your CD, prepaid items are separated from closing costs so you can see exactly what you're paying now versus what's being held in escrow. Understanding this distinction helps you negotiate effectively and understand your actual out-of-pocket cost at closing.

The "Cash to Close" Box: Your Real Bottom Line

After all the calculations, there's one number that matters most: cash to close. This is the total amount of money you need to bring to the closing table—or have wired beforehand.

Cash to close includes: your down payment, closing costs you're paying, prepaid items, minus any earnest money you've already deposited and any seller concessions or credits.

Formula: Down Payment + Closing Costs + Prepaid Items - Earnest Money Deposited - Seller Credits = Cash to Close

Example: You're buying a $450,000 home in Windcrest. You're putting 15% down ($67,500), closing costs are $12,000, prepaid taxes and insurance are $3,500, you've deposited $5,000 earnest money, and the seller is crediting you $2,000 for repairs.

$67,500 + $12,000 + $3,500 - $5,000 - $2,000 = $76,000 cash to close

This should be crystal clear on your CD. If you're confused about where this number came from, don't sign until it's explained line by line.

Common Errors and Red Flags to Catch Before Signing

After years of helping buyers navigate closing, I've seen the same mistakes appear repeatedly. Here's what to watch for:

Duplicate Charges

Title search fees and title insurance sometimes appear twice—once in the title section and again as an agent fee. Or appraisal fees might be listed under "appraisal" and again under "processing."

Scan the entire CD for repeated line items with similar amounts. If you see two $500 charges for anything, ask why before signing.

Inflated Property Tax Reserves

Lenders estimate your annual property tax and divide it by 12, holding two months' worth at closing. If your lender estimates your tax at 1.2% annually but your home's actual assessed rate is 0.8%, you'll overpay.

Get your property's actual tax rate from the Bexar County Appraisal District and verify it's been used in the calculation. Overpaying here means cash sitting in escrow that could've been invested.

Incorrect Loan Amount

If your loan amount on the CD doesn't match your purchase agreement minus down payment, there's an error. Even $500 off suggests something went wrong in underwriting.

Mismatched Interest Rates or Terms

Your rate lock agreement should match your CD exactly. If you locked at 6.5% and the CD shows 6.75%, you have a rate lock violation claim—notify your lender immediately.

Seller Concession Errors

If the seller is paying your closing costs or contributing to your down payment, verify this credit appears on the CD. Missing credits directly increase your cash to close.

HOA or Transfer Tax Issues

In Stone Oak or Dominion properties, HOA fees can be substantial. Verify the exact amount and that it's been quoted correctly by your title company. Transfer taxes should be zero in Bexar County—if you see them, question it.

Your Three-Day Review Checklist Before Signing

You have three business days after receiving the CD to review it carefully. Here's your actionable checklist:

Day One: Initial Review — Print the CD and sit down for 30–45 minutes. Check: loan amount, interest rate, property address, sale price, and cash to close amount against what you were quoted verbally.

Day Two: Line-Item Audit — Go through each fee category. Compare it to your Loan Estimate. Highlight any discrepancies and compile a list of questions for your lender.

Day Three: Final Verification — Contact your lender with questions. Get written confirmation of any corrections before closing. Call your real estate agent to discuss any seller concessions or credits that might be missing.

This isn't paranoia—it's due diligence. And I've seen it catch five-figure errors more than once.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I negotiate closing costs after I receive the Closing Disclosure?

Technically, yes—but it's complicated. Lender fees are largely non-negotiable at this stage since underwriting is complete. However, title insurance, homeowners insurance premiums, and HOA fees sometimes have wiggle room. More importantly, if there are errors on the CD, the lender must correct them at no charge. In Texas, you can also shop title companies up to three days before closing, so if you notice high title fees, you can switch providers and potentially save $200–$400 even after receiving the CD.

What happens if I find an error on the Closing Disclosure the day before closing?

Contact your lender and title company immediately—don't wait. Most errors (wrong address, duplicate fees, math mistakes) can be corrected within hours, and closings are delayed regularly for this reason. Your real estate agent can facilitate communication with all parties. Never close on a CD you don't understand or that contains errors you've identified. A one-day delay is far better than discovering a $5,000 mistake after you've signed.

Why is my cash to close amount higher than the closing costs I was quoted?

Because "closing costs" and "cash to close" are different calculations. Closing costs are the fees for the transaction. Cash to close includes closing costs, your down payment, prepaid taxes and insurance, minus earnest money you've already paid and any seller credits. For a $400,000 home purchase with 10% down ($40,000), $8,000 in closing costs, $2,000 prepaid items, and $3,000 earnest money deposited, your cash to close would be approximately $47,000—not $8,000. Review the CD's calculation section to see exactly where each number comes from.

Are property tax reserves on the Closing Disclosure refundable if I pay off my mortgage early?

Yes, generally. Prepaid property taxes and insurance held in escrow are returned to you if you pay off your loan or refinance, since the lender no longer needs to hold those reserves. However, the exact process depends on your loan terms and your lender's policies. Some lenders deduct a small admin fee; others don't. When reviewing your CD, ask your lender explicitly about their process for returning prepaid escrow amounts if circumstances change.

What if the seller is supposed to pay for a repair credit but it's not showing on my Closing Disclosure?

This is a critical issue and must be resolved before closing. Seller credits are binding contractual obligations, and they absolutely should appear on the CD. Contact your real estate agent immediately—this is typically a title company or lender communication oversight, not an intentional omission. The credit will be added to a final amendment to the CD (often issued 24 hours before closing). Never proceed to closing without seeing your seller concessions reflected in writing.
