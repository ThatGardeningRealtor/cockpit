Buying a Short Sale or Foreclosure in San Antonio — Realistic Expectations and How the Process Works

By Chris Drawdy · October 2, 2025 · 9 min read

Short sales and foreclosures can offer real value in San Antonio's market — but only if you understand the actual timeline, inspection realities, and what "as-is" truly means. After years of working REO properties for Priority Bank and navigating dozens of distressed sales across the North Star District, Stone Oak, and Southside neighborhoods, I've learned what separates smart buyers from disappointed ones.

What's the Real Difference Between a Short Sale and a Foreclosure?

A short sale happens when a homeowner owes more on their mortgage than the home is worth, and the lender agrees to accept less than what's owed. The seller is still in control — they list the property, accept an offer, and must get bank approval for any sale price below their loan balance.

A foreclosure is when the lender takes back the property because the owner stopped paying. The bank now owns it outright, and it's marketed as "bank-owned" or REO (Real Estate Owned). No seller approval needed — the bank sets the terms.

Here's the gardening parallel: a short sale is like negotiating with a nursery about selling plants at a loss because the season's ending. A foreclosure is like the nursery shutting down and selling off inventory at clearance prices just to liquidate fast.

Understanding the Real Timeline: Why It Takes Months, Not Weeks

This is where most buyers get frustrated. If you're dreaming of closing in 30 days on a short sale, let's reset expectations right now.

Short Sale Timeline (4–8 months is realistic)

Weeks 1–2: Offer & Negotiation. You submit an offer. The seller and their agent review it. Unlike a standard sale, this isn't the finish line — it's barely the starting line.

Weeks 2–4: Seller Prepares Bank Package. The seller's agent gathers documents: hardship letter, proof of financial difficulty, comparative market analysis (CMA), and a detailed offer. This isn't rushed; many sellers are overwhelmed and working with limited resources.

Weeks 4–12: Bank Review (The Real Waiting Game). The lender reviews everything. They have no deadline — they're evaluating whether accepting less money makes business sense. Some banks take 30 days; others take 90. I've seen Frost Bank-held properties move faster than some out-of-state servicers.

Weeks 12–16: Inspection (If Approved). If the bank approves your offer, you finally get inspection access. But here's the catch: short sales typically allow ONE or TWO inspections, not unlimited access. Plan ahead.

Weeks 16–32: Title Work, Appraisal, Final Approval. Your lender orders an appraisal. If the home appraises below your offer price, your loan may not fund — and the deal collapses. Title issues (especially common in San Antonio's older neighborhoods like Southtown and the Pearl) can add weeks.

Foreclosure Timeline (2–4 months is faster, but still not quick)

Days 1–7: You Find the Property. Banks list REO homes on MLS, and sometimes directly through their loss mitigation departments. Priority Bank typically posts their San Antonio foreclosures through local listing services and their REO portal.

Weeks 1–4: Bank Inspection & Appraisal. The bank orders their own inspection and appraisal before reviewing your offer. They know what they're working with.

Weeks 4–8: Offer Review & Acceptance. You submit an offer. The bank's REO team reviews it within 5–30 days, depending on their backlog. There's no negotiation — you either meet their price or you don't.

Weeks 8–12: Title, Final Walkthrough, Closing. Once accepted, things move faster. Most foreclosures close in 30–45 days after acceptance.

Inspection Limitations: What You Can and Cannot Do

Here's where the as-is reality hits hardest. Short sales and foreclosures are not standard transactions.

Short Sale Inspection Reality

The seller's lender controls access. You typically get ONE inspection appointment, sometimes two if you're lucky. No re-inspections after appraisal. No "inspection period" to negotiate repairs. You cannot demand the seller fix the roof or the foundation.

Bring your inspector — and I mean a thorough one. This is not the time to skip the structural engineer or skip checking HVAC mechanicals. In San Antonio's older neighborhoods (like Alamo Heights or Terrell Hills), foundation settling is common. In North Star or Stone Oak, you're looking at newer builds, but previous owner deferred maintenance can hide problems.

Ask the listing agent for the seller's disclosures upfront. Yes, the seller must disclose known issues, even in a short sale. Most won't have much — they may not have lived there long or may not know the property well — but you have a legal right to ask.

Foreclosure (REO) Inspection Reality

Banks are more generous with inspection access since they own the property outright. You typically get 3–5 days of inspection windows, and you can schedule multiple appointments.

But here's the thing: REO properties are sold "as-is" too. The bank won't fix anything. They've already done a basic inspection themselves, but they're not liable for hidden defects once you close. Get your full inspection done.

One advantage of REO: the bank's inspection report is often available upon request. Ask the listing agent or bank's loss mitigation team for it. It gives you an honest baseline about the property's condition.

Pricing Versus True Value: The Math Behind Discounts

Not every short sale or foreclosure is a deal. The discount exists, but you need to understand what you're actually buying.

Why Prices Are Lower (And What That Means)

In San Antonio's current market, short sales typically list 5–15% below market value depending on the neighborhood. North Star and Stone Oak properties move faster and hold value better, so discounts there are usually 5–8%. Older neighborhoods like Southtown or Beacon Hill may see 10–15% discounts.

That discount reflects risk. You're buying unknown condition. You're buying no warranty. You're buying a longer closing timeline. You're buying the possibility that your appraisal comes in low and your financing falls through.

Here's where a CMA (comparative market analysis) matters. If a home in Windcrest normally sells for $350,000 and you're looking at a short sale listed at $310,000, that sounds like a steal. But if that home needs $25,000 in foundation work and new HVAC (which you discovered on inspection), and comparable homes in good condition are moving at $345,000, your "deal" just became marginal.

When the Appraisal Problem Kills the Deal

Your lender orders an appraisal. The appraiser compares the property to recent sales of similar homes in the area. In San Antonio's fast-moving market, appraisals can come in LOWER than the short sale list price.

Example: You offer $300,000 on a short sale in Stone Oak. The seller accepts. Your lender's appraiser says the home is worth $285,000 based on recent comps. Your lender will only loan 80% of $285,000 = $228,000. Suddenly you need an extra $15,000 cash, or the deal falls apart.

Short sale lenders won't budge on this. Foreclosure banks sometimes negotiate, but rarely. Plan to have cash reserves ready.

Where to Find Short Sales and Foreclosures in San Antonio

Not all distressed properties are advertised equally. Some hide in plain sight.

MLS (Multiple Listing Service)

The easiest path: search the San Antonio MLS for properties with "short sale" or "REO" in the remarks. You can filter by neighborhood — search for Windcrest, Stone Oak, North Star, Terrell Hills, or any area you're interested in.

Use the "Property Type" filters to look for bank-owned or preforeclosure listings. Some agents tag these clearly; others bury them in description text. A good agent (like myself) can pull these reports for you automatically.

Bank Loss Mitigation Departments (Direct Contact)

Frost Bank, Cullen/Frost, and other San Antonio-based lenders sometimes list short sales directly through their loss mitigation teams before they hit the full MLS. Priority Bank historically held REO properties on an internal portal.

Call the loss mitigation department of major lenders in your area. You'd be surprised how responsive they are to serious buyers with pre-approval letters.

Online Foreclosure Databases

Zillow, Trulia, and Realtor.com let you filter for foreclosures specifically. These are accurate but often delayed by 2–3 weeks compared to MLS data.

Auction.com and HotBid list properties heading to courthouse auction (pre-foreclosure stage). Be cautious here — these require cash and close in days, not months.

Lessons From My Priority Bank REO Days: What We Learned About Bank-Owned Properties

I spent years managing Priority Bank REO properties across San Antonio, and it taught me things that still guide my advice today.

Banks Care About Clean Transactions, Not Relationship-Building

Priority Bank's REO team wasn't interested in creative negotiations or seller contributions. They wanted: clean title, solid offer price, clear appraisal, fast close. If you were inflexible, they moved to the next offer.

Lesson: When buying an REO property, present a strong initial offer. Weak offers followed by "let's see if they counter" don't work. Banks don't counter-offer like people do.

Hidden Defects Are Buyer Problems, Not Bank Problems

I watched multiple closings where buyers discovered foundation settling, outdated plumbing, or HVAC failures after closing. The bank had disclosed "sold as-is," and the buyer had no recourse.

Lesson: Your inspection is everything. Hire the best inspector you can afford. This is not the place to save $300 on the inspection fee.

Appraisals Are the Silent Deal-Killer

In 2021–2022, when San Antonio was appreciating fast, many REO properties were listed at yesterday's prices. The appraisal came in below the offer price in maybe 20% of the deals I managed. That killed more sales than any other single factor.

Lesson: Get pre-approved by a lender who knows REO transactions. Some lenders are stricter on appraisals than others. Frost Bank and Cullen/Frost have experience here. Know your lender's policy before you offer.

Foreclosures in San Antonio's Older Neighborhoods Are Mixed Bags

Properties in Southtown, Beacon Hill, or Alamo Heights sometimes came to foreclosure because the owner couldn't afford foundation repairs or roof replacement. Older homes = older systems failing at the same time. A great price didn't always mean a great value once the deferred maintenance tab came due.

Lesson: In older neighborhoods, assume you'll spend $15,000–$30,000 on repairs in the first two years. Price accordingly. Newer areas like Stone Oak had different issues — mostly cosmetic or minor systems needing attention.

Do You Need a Real Estate Agent to Buy a Short Sale or Foreclosure?

Technically, no. But realistically, yes — and here's why.

Short sales require navigating bank approval. Foreclosures often involve institutional complexity. A good agent familiar with distressed sales knows how to package your offer, what documentation the bank will request, and how to move things along when the process stalls.

I've also seen buyers discover that the property they want to offer on has title issues, environmental flags, or HOA liens that a routine title search misses. An experienced agent catches these before you fall in love with a property you can't actually close on.

The best agents on short sales and foreclosures in San Antonio understand the local lenders, local court processes, and neighborhood-specific risks. That's worth the commission.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I negotiate repairs or ask the bank to fix things before closing on a foreclosure?

No. Foreclosure REO properties are sold strictly "as-is" with no repairs, no credits, and no exceptions. Banks will not renegotiate the price based on inspection findings. What you see on inspection is what you're buying. Your only leverage is walking away before you're under contract, or requesting a price reduction before you submit your offer (which the bank will almost certainly decline). This is why a thorough pre-offer inspection is critical — not an inspection contingency where you discover problems after you've already committed to buying.

How much lower should I offer on a short sale compared to asking price?

That depends on the property's condition and current market. If it's priced realistically at 10% below market, offer 3–5% below asking. If it's priced aggressively low (15%+ discount), your offer at asking price might actually be accepted faster because the seller and bank are likely already expecting lower offers. Run a CMA in the specific neighborhood — Stone Oak comps move differently than Southtown comps. The bank is reviewing your offer against a CMA anyway, so come in with data, not hope. Strong offers with solid earnest money get reviewed faster than lowball offers that feel insulting.

What happens if my appraisal comes in lower than my offer price on a foreclosure?

Your lender will only loan a percentage (usually 80%) of the appraised value, not the purchase price. If you offered $300,000 and it appraises for $280,000, your lender will only fund $224,000 (80% of $280,000). You need to come up with the $76,000 difference in cash, or renegotiate the price with the bank. Most REO banks won't renegotiate — they'll move to the next offer instead. This is why having solid cash reserves or a pre-approval letter that accounts for appraisal variance is crucial. Some buyers request an appraisal waiver contingency upfront, but banks almost never grant these on REO properties.

Are short sales in San Antonio still happening, or is the market too hot?

San Antonio has fewer short sales than it did in 2010–2015, but they still exist — especially in neighborhoods with older housing stock (Southtown, Beacon Hill, Terrell Hills) where aging systems and deferred maintenance create underwater situations. In fast-appreciating areas like Stone Oak and North Star, short sales are rare because most owners have equity. You'll find the most short sale activity among owners who bought in 2018–2020 in appreciating neighborhoods but have since faced job loss or medical emergency. They exist if you know where to look — which is why working with an agent who understands market dynamics is valuable.

Can I use FHA or VA financing to buy a foreclosure or short sale in San Antonio?

Yes, but with caveats. FHA loans require the property to pass specific safety and habitability standards — meaning a house with deferred maintenance might fail FHA appraisal even if it's structurally sound. VA loans have similar requirements. Some lenders are more flexible than others, and some banks (like Frost Bank) have REO experience with FHA/VA buyers. Disclose your loan type upfront; banks may decline your offer if they think the property won't pass your lender's inspection. Getting pre-approved with a lender experienced in FHA/VA REO deals is essential if you're using government-backed financing.
