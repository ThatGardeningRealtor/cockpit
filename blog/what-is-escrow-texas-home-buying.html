What Is Escrow in a Texas Real Estate Transaction — And Why It Protects Everyone

By Chris Drawdy · November 24, 2025 · 9 min read

Escrow can feel like mysterious financial jargon, but it's actually one of the simplest protections in any home purchase. In this guide, I'll walk you through exactly how escrow works in Texas, why both the buyer and seller need it, and what happens to your money before, during, and after closing — so you can close confidently on your San Antonio home.

What Is Escrow? The Simple Version

Think of escrow like a garden contract between you and your neighbor — except instead of a handshake, a neutral third party (usually a title company or real estate attorney) holds onto the agreed-upon seeds, tools, or plants until both of you have done your part.

In a real estate transaction, that "neutral third party" is a licensed escrow officer or title company who holds your earnest money, earnest money deposits, and other funds. Neither the buyer nor the seller touches that money until every condition of the contract is met and the deal is officially closed.

This protects both sides: the seller knows the buyer is serious, and the buyer knows their down payment won't vanish if the seller backs out. It's the safety net that makes the whole process work.

Closing Escrow: What Happens to Your Money Before You Get the Keys

Closing escrow is the temporary holding period from the moment you make an offer until the day you officially close on the property. Your earnest money deposit — typically 1–3% of the purchase price in Texas — goes into this account.

Let me walk you through the timeline:

The Earnest Money Phase

When you write an offer on a home in Alamo Heights, Terrell Hills, or anywhere in the San Antonio area, you'll include an earnest money amount. If the seller accepts your offer, you typically have 3 days to deposit that money with the title company.

That earnest money sits in the escrow account, untouched, while inspections, appraisals, and title searches happen. If everything clears and the deal moves forward, that earnest money becomes part of your down payment at closing.

If you walk away from the deal for reasons NOT covered by the contract (like you simply changed your mind), you lose that earnest money. If the inspection reveals major foundation cracks or the appraisal comes in low — conditions covered in your contract — you can request your earnest money back.

The Title Company's Role in San Antonio Closings

In Texas, a title company (or sometimes a real estate attorney) is the escrow officer. They verify the seller's legal ownership, check for liens or claims against the property, and handle the actual release of funds at closing.

Companies like Fidelity National Title, Texas Title, and local San Antonio firms like North Star Title hold these funds in a trust account separate from their operating account. This separation is required by state law and ensures your money is protected even if the title company has financial trouble.

On closing day, the title company receives the buyer's loan funds from the lender, collects the earnest money from escrow, verifies all conditions are satisfied, and releases the funds to the seller's attorney or realtor. Once the deed is recorded with Bexar County, you officially own the home.

What Happens If the Deal Falls Apart

If the buyer and seller disagree about who keeps the earnest money, the escrow officer won't release it until both parties agree in writing or a court order is issued. This can take weeks or even months, but it protects both sides from having their money taken unfairly.

Most disputes resolve quickly in Texas because the contract clearly spells out who gets the earnest money if certain conditions aren't met. Your real estate agent or attorney will guide you through this.

Mortgage Escrow: The Long-Term Account That Manages Taxes and Insurance

After closing, a second type of escrow comes into play — and this one sticks around for years. Your mortgage lender sets up an escrow account (sometimes called an "impound account") to collect money for property taxes and homeowners insurance.

Here's why: Texas property taxes are significant, and homeowners insurance is mandatory if you're financing the purchase. Instead of asking you to pay these large bills in one lump sum once a year, your lender spreads the cost across your monthly mortgage payment.

How Your Monthly Escrow Works

Your mortgage payment is broken into four parts: principal, interest, taxes, and insurance (PITI). The "T" and "I" go into your escrow account with your lender.

In San Antonio and Bexar County, property tax rates average around 0.81–0.85% of home value annually. On a $350,000 home, that's roughly $2,835–$2,975 per year, or about $236–$248 per month — all rolled into your mortgage payment.

Homeowners insurance averages $1,000–$1,500 per year in Texas (depending on the home's age, location, and coverage level). That's another $83–$125 per month added to your payment.

Your lender sends these funds directly to Bexar County Tax Assessor-Collector and your insurance company when bills are due. You never have to write a separate check.

The Annual Escrow Analysis

Once a year, usually before tax season, your lender performs an escrow analysis. They check whether the amount they've been collecting is enough to cover your actual taxes and insurance when bills come due.

If they've collected too much, you get a refund. If they haven't collected enough, your monthly payment goes up slightly. Texas law requires lenders to give you 45 days' notice before raising your escrow payment.

It's like tending a garden through seasons — sometimes you need more water, sometimes less. The analysis keeps everything balanced.

Who Manages Escrow and How They're Regulated in Texas

In closing escrow, a licensed title company or real estate attorney holds the funds. In Texas, title companies are regulated by the Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR) and must maintain separate trust accounts for client funds.

For mortgage escrow, your loan servicer (the company you send your monthly payment to) manages the account. Companies like Rocket Mortgage, Better.com, local lenders like Broadway Bank, USAA (for military members), and others all manage escrow accounts the same way.

Both types of escrow are protected by law. Title companies carry errors and omissions insurance, and loan servicers are regulated by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC).

What Happens to Your Escrow Account After Closing

Once you close, your closing escrow account disappears. That earnest money has become part of your down payment, and the title company's job is done.

Your mortgage escrow account, however, stays active for the life of your loan — unless you pay off your mortgage early or refinance. Even if you refinance with a different lender in San Antonio, you'll likely have a new escrow account set up with your new loan servicer.

Can You Opt Out of Mortgage Escrow?

If you put down 20% or more and have excellent credit, some lenders will allow you to skip the escrow account and pay taxes and insurance yourself. However, most lenders require escrow if your down payment is less than 20%.

Even if you're allowed to opt out, many homeowners choose escrow anyway because it prevents the risk of forgetting to pay these bills and facing penalties or tax liens. It's a predictable way to budget.

Property Tax Appeals and Escrow in San Antonio

If you disagree with your property tax assessment from Bexar County Appraisal District, you can file a protest (there's even a free online tool at bcad.org). If your assessment is lowered, your escrow account will be adjusted, and your next payment may go down.

This is why it's worth checking your property tax appraisal every few years — small reductions add up over a 15- or 30-year mortgage.

Escrow and Your Rights as a Texas Homebuyer

Texas Property Code § 1955.001 spells out exactly how escrow agents must handle earnest money. The bottom line: they must follow the written instructions in your contract, and they can't release funds without written agreement from both buyer and seller (or a court order).

If a title company or attorney violates these rules, you have the right to file a complaint with TDLR or pursue legal action. This is rare because the industry is tightly regulated, but knowing your rights matters.

For mortgage escrow, you have the right to request an escrow analysis at any time, not just during the annual review. You can also request a detailed breakdown of how your escrow payment is calculated. If your lender makes errors, you can dispute them in writing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is my earnest money deposit refundable in Texas?

It depends on the reason you're backing out. If the inspection reveals serious defects (like termite damage or foundation issues), you can usually get your earnest money back if you use the inspection contingency in your contract. If you fail to get a mortgage approval despite a good-faith effort, you're typically entitled to a refund. However, if you simply change your mind or fail to meet a deadline through no fault of the seller, you'll likely lose your earnest money. Always review your contract carefully and work with your real estate agent to understand the specific contingencies that protect your deposit.

How much earnest money should I offer in San Antonio?

The standard in most San Antonio neighborhoods — from Northcliff to Southtown, Brackenridge to Leon Valley — is 1–3% of the purchase price. In competitive markets, offering closer to 3% signals you're serious and can strengthen your offer, especially if you're bidding against multiple buyers. However, 1–2% is perfectly acceptable in slower markets or if the home has minor issues. Your real estate agent can advise based on current conditions in your specific neighborhood and the property's appeal.

Can my lender force me into escrow for property taxes and insurance?

Yes, if your loan-to-value ratio is above 80% (meaning your down payment is less than 20%), your lender can require escrow. This is called an "escrow requirement" and protects the lender's investment because they know taxes and insurance will be paid on time. If you put down 20% or more, many lenders will allow you to pay these bills yourself, but some may still encourage escrow as a convenience feature. Always ask your lender about your options before closing.

What if my property tax or insurance bill is higher than expected?

If your escrow analysis shows that your actual taxes or insurance costs are higher than estimated, your lender will increase your monthly mortgage payment — but they must give you 45 days' written notice before doing so. You have the right to request a detailed explanation of the increase. If you think the estimate is wrong (for example, if you've filed a property tax protest with Bexar County Appraisal District), contact your lender and provide supporting documentation; they may adjust the escrow amount downward.

What happens to my mortgage escrow account if I pay off my loan early?

When you pay off your mortgage, your escrow account is closed. Any remaining balance (money you've overpaid into escrow) is refunded to you within 30 days. From that point forward, you're responsible for paying property taxes and insurance directly to Bexar County and your insurance company. Make sure you set up a payment system so you don't miss these bills — they're still required even without a mortgage.
