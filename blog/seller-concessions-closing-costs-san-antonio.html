How to Get the Seller to Pay Your Closing Costs in San Antonio

By Chris Drawdy · March 24, 2026 · 10 min read

Closing costs can quietly add thousands of dollars to an already stretched budget — but in San Antonio's current market, buyers have more room to ask for help than they might think. In this guide, you'll learn exactly what seller concessions are, how much is realistic to request in different SA neighborhoods, and how to structure an offer that actually gets a seller to say yes. Whether you're eyeing a starter home in Converse or a move-up property near Stone Oak, this is the practical playbook you need.

What Are Seller Concessions — and Why Do They Matter?

Seller concessions — sometimes called seller credits — are funds the seller agrees to contribute toward your closing costs as part of the purchase contract. Instead of receiving the full sale price at closing, the seller essentially gives a portion back to cover your lender fees, title insurance, prepaid taxes, homeowner's insurance escrow, and other line items on your closing disclosure.

Think of it like getting a head start in the garden. You're not asking the seller to lower the price of the home �� you're asking them to help you get planted without wiping out your savings on day one. The home's appraised value and sale price remain intact; the seller just redirects some of their proceeds to your side of the ledger at the closing table.

For buyers in San Antonio — especially first-timers using FHA loans or veterans using VA financing — this strategy can be the difference between being able to close at all versus having to delay for another six months of saving. Done correctly, it costs you nothing extra and gets you into your home with reserves still in your pocket.

What Closing Costs Can Seller Concessions Cover?

Seller credits can be applied to virtually any lender-approved closing cost: origination fees, discount points to buy down your interest rate, appraisal fees, title search and title insurance, prepaid homeowner's insurance, property tax escrow, and HOA transfer fees where applicable.

One of the smartest uses of seller concessions that many buyers overlook is using them to buy down your mortgage rate. If a seller contributes $6,000 toward a rate buydown on a $300,000 loan, you could lower your monthly payment by $80–$120 for the life of the loan — that's real money every single month in neighborhoods like Helotes or Schertz where property taxes are already a line item to respect.

What's Realistic to Ask For in the San Antonio Market Right Now?

San Antonio entered 2026 in a buyer-friendly posture compared to the peak frenzy of 2021–2022. Inventory has remained elevated across much of Bexar County, and homes in the $200,000–$350,000 range — the bread and butter of San Antonio's market — are sitting 35–55 days on average before going under contract. That breathing room gives buyers genuine negotiating leverage.

In neighborhoods like Universal City, Live Oak, and the far northeast side along the I-35 corridor toward Selma, concessions of 2%–3% are regularly being negotiated without much friction. In higher-demand pockets closer to the Loop 410 medical corridor or in Alamo Heights and Terrell Hills, sellers tend to have more pricing confidence and may push back harder on anything above 1.5%.

New construction is its own conversation. Builders in master-planned communities like Verano in Pearsall Road, Olympia Hills in Universal City, and Cibolo's Parklands at Wiederstein are actively offering closing cost incentives — sometimes $10,000–$15,000 — when you use their preferred lender. It's worth comparing those offers carefully, because the preferred lender's rate isn't always the sharpest pencil in the drawer.

Concession Limits by Loan Type

Lenders cap how much a seller can contribute based on your loan type and down payment, so it's important to know your ceiling before you write the offer. For conventional loans with less than 10% down, the limit is 3% of the purchase price. Put 10%–25% down and that ceiling rises to 6%.

FHA loans allow up to 6% in seller concessions regardless of down payment — which is why FHA buyers in San Antonio's affordable neighborhoods like Lackland Terrace, Calumet, or South Side are particularly well-positioned to ask. VA loans cap seller-paid concessions at 4% but allow the seller to pay all normal closing costs on top of that, making VA one of the most powerful tools a buyer can bring to the table in a city with one of the largest active-duty and veteran populations in the country.

How to Structure Your Offer to Actually Get the Concession

Here's where the gardening analogy really holds: timing, preparation, and the right conditions determine whether something takes root. A poorly structured offer asking for concessions will get rejected fast, even in a soft market. But a thoughtfully framed offer can get a seller to cheerfully cover your closing costs.

The most common and effective approach is to offer slightly above asking price and ask for the equivalent amount back in concessions. For example, if a home in Converse is listed at $265,000, you might offer $271,000 with $6,000 in seller concessions toward closing costs. The seller nets approximately the same amount, your loan amount goes up modestly, but your out-of-pocket cash at closing drops dramatically. This works as long as the home appraises at or above the offered price — which your agent should assess before writing the offer.

Pairing the concession request with a clean, strong offer matters enormously. A solid pre-approval from a reputable local lender like Frost Bank, Broadway Bank, or a well-regarded mortgage broker in the San Antonio area, a reasonable inspection period, and a flexible closing date all signal to the seller that you're a serious buyer — not someone fishing for freebies.

What to Include in the Contract Language

In Texas, seller concessions are written into the TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract under Paragraph 12, titled "Settlement and Other Expenses." Your agent will enter the specific dollar amount the seller agrees to contribute toward your closing costs in that section — it's not vague language, it's a firm dollar figure.

Be precise. Saying "seller to pay all closing costs" opens the door to disputes and lender complications. Instead, specify "Seller to contribute $X toward Buyer's closing costs, prepaid items, and loan discount points" — clean, lender-friendly, and enforceable.

When Sellers Say Yes — and When They Push Back

Sellers are most agreeable to concessions when their home has been sitting on the market for more than 30 days, when they've already had a prior deal fall through, or when they're motivated by a job relocation, divorce, or estate sale. In those situations, a well-packaged offer with a concession request can feel like relief, not an insult.

Sellers tend to push back hardest when they've received multiple offers, when the home is priced at or below market value and selling quickly, or when they're emotionally attached to a specific net number. In a neighborhood like Alamo Ranch on the northwest side or the Dominion area, where demand from families chasing Northside ISD schools remains strong, concessions above 1%–2% may be a harder sell.

It's also worth noting that estate sales — homes being sold by heirs or through probate in older neighborhoods like Woodlawn Hills, Beacon Hill, or along Fredericksburg Road — are frequently open to concessions because the sellers have no mortgage to pay off and are simply looking to close cleanly. These situations can be some of the best opportunities in the city for buyers using concessions strategically.

Red Flags That Could Kill Your Concession Request

If the home is priced below comparable sales in the area and you still ask for 3% back, a savvy listing agent will see right through it — and advise their client accordingly. Asking for concessions on a home that already needs price justification makes your whole offer look weak.

Similarly, if your pre-approval letter is from an online lender with no local track record, or if your earnest money is minimal for the price point — say $500 on a $300,000 home — your concession request may get dismissed before it's seriously considered. In San Antonio's market, earnest money of 1% of the purchase price is standard and signals real commitment.

Stacking Seller Concessions with San Antonio Down Payment Programs

One of the most powerful — and underused — strategies in San Antonio is combining seller concessions with local and state down payment assistance programs. The Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers down payment assistance grants of up to 5% for qualifying buyers, and those funds can be layered on top of seller-paid closing costs in many cases.

The City of San Antonio's Homeownership Incentive Program (HIP) also provides forgivable loans of up to $30,000 for eligible buyers purchasing within city limits — properties in districts like District 3 on the south side and District 2 on the east side often qualify. When you stack HIP assistance with seller concessions, you can genuinely arrive at the closing table with your down payment covered, your closing costs covered, and cash still in your savings account.

SAWS and CPS Energy rebate programs won't help at closing, but they're worth mentioning to new homeowners in the same conversation — energy-efficient homes in Bexar County can generate meaningful utility savings in those first critical years of homeownership when every dollar counts. A good agent will walk you through the full picture, not just the transaction.

Working With the Right Lender and Agent Makes All the Difference

Your lender needs to understand how to structure a concession properly so it clears underwriting without complications. Lenders like Cornerstone Home Lending, which has a strong San Antonio presence, and local credit unions like Generations Federal Credit Union or Security Service Federal Credit Union are experienced with Texas contract language and concession structuring. They won't be surprised or confused when you bring them a contract with a seller credit built in.

Your agent's job is to present the concession request in the most favorable light possible — contextualizing it with comparable sales data, framing your offer as strong overall, and communicating clearly with the listing agent before you submit. That agent-to-agent conversation often determines whether a concession gets accepted or killed before it's even discussed with the seller.

Like any garden, a real estate transaction grows best when the right people are tending it from the start. The right lender, the right agent, and the right strategy planted early can yield results that feel almost effortless at closing day — even when the negotiation behind the scenes was anything but simple.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a seller pay all of my closing costs in Texas?

In Texas, sellers can pay a significant portion of your closing costs, but the actual amount is capped by your loan type — not by state law. For FHA loans, the cap is 6% of the purchase price; for VA loans it's 4% in concessions plus normal closing costs; and for conventional loans with less than 10% down, it's 3%. In practice, total buyer closing costs in San Antonio typically run $5,500–$10,000 depending on loan size and which title company you use, so FHA and VA buyers especially can often get the entire bill covered. A lender familiar with Texas closings — like one based here in San Antonio — will be able to tell you exactly what's possible given your specific loan scenario before you write the offer.

Does asking for seller concessions hurt my offer in a competitive market?

It can, if the concession request isn't thoughtfully packaged — but it doesn't have to. The key is offsetting the concession with a higher offer price so the seller's net proceeds remain attractive, and pairing it with strong fundamentals like a well-qualified pre-approval, healthy earnest money, and a clean timeline. In slower neighborhoods or with motivated sellers, concessions rarely hurt at all. In genuinely competitive situations — like a well-priced listing in Northside ISD that draws multiple offers within 48 hours — you may need to choose between asking for concessions or maximizing your competitiveness. Your agent should advise you honestly on which situation you're in before you decide.

What's the difference between a seller concession and a price reduction?

A price reduction lowers the purchase price on paper, which also lowers your loan amount and monthly payment. A seller concession keeps the price intact but directs a portion of the seller's proceeds toward your closing costs — your loan amount is typically higher as a result. For buyers who are cash-constrained at closing but can handle a slightly higher monthly payment, concessions are often the better tool. For buyers focused on minimizing their long-term loan balance and monthly payment, a price reduction might be more valuable. Many experienced buyers and agents in San Antonio will weigh both options and sometimes negotiate a blend of both in the same contract.

Can I use seller concessions to buy down my mortgage rate in Texas?

Yes — and this is one of the most financially impactful uses of seller concessions available to buyers right now. A temporary or permanent rate buydown funded by seller credits can reduce your monthly mortgage payment meaningfully, especially when interest rates are elevated. For example, a 2-1 buydown on a $280,000 loan in San Antonio might cost around $5,600–$7,000 in seller-paid points but reduce your rate by 2% in year one and 1% in year two, giving you time to refinance or adjust financially. Ask your lender to run a side-by-side comparison of taking cash back at closing versus using those same seller funds for a buydown — the long-term math sometimes surprises people.

Do seller concessions affect the home appraisal in Texas?

Seller concessions do not directly reduce the appraised value of a home — the appraiser is assessing the property's market value, not restructuring your transaction. However, if you've offered above asking price specifically to fund concessions, the home must still appraise at or above your offer price or you'll face an appraisal gap. This is why agents in San Antonio typically run a careful comparative market analysis before recommending how high to go on an offer when concessions are part of the equation. If the home appraises below your offer price, you'd need to renegotiate the price, bring extra cash to cover the gap, or potentially walk away — all scenarios your agent should prepare you for in advance.
