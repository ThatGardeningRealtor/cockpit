Real Talk: What Nobody Tells You About Your First 3 Years as a Real Estate Agent

By Chris Drawdy · December 9, 2025 · 11 min read

The glossy recruitment videos show champagne and closed deals. Nobody shows you the lean months, the bad mentors, or the 2 a.m. doubt spirals. I'm breaking down what actually happens in your first three years as a real estate agent—and what separates the ones who make it from the ones who don't.

Year One: The Survival Phase (You're Going to Eat Dirt)

Let's be honest: Year one is brutal. You spent money on your license, joined an MLS, bought business cards, and maybe put up a small sign at your local office. Then silence.

You're competing against agents who've been doing this for 15 years. They know every lender from USAA in San Antonio to local players like Frost Bank. They have relationships. You have a phone and a commission structure that won't pay until you close something.

In gardening, you don't expect tomatoes the week you plant seeds. Real estate is the same—except you're paying rent on a home office and your confidence is wilting every time a lead goes cold.

The Real Income Picture

Most brokerages won't tell you this: you'll likely make under $30,000 in your first year, even if you work like mad. Some months you'll make $0. You might have a transaction fall apart at closing—happened to me in month 7—and suddenly that $4,000 commission evaporates.

Budget accordingly. Keep your day job if you can (I did for the first 8 months). If you can't, you need 12 months of personal expenses in savings before you start. Not 3 months. Twelve.

San Antonio's cost of living is reasonable compared to Austin or Dallas, but that doesn't mean you can afford to lose income for months at a time.

Why Your First Deal Takes So Long

Your first transaction will likely come from your sphere of influence—friends, family, former coworkers—not from cold leads or online marketing. And it'll move slowly because you don't know the loan timeline, the inspection contingencies, or how to handle a seller who's getting cold feet.

That's not failure. That's learning.

I closed my first deal at month 5—a home in Leon Valley, a modest 3-bed on a quiet street. The buyers were friends of a friend. I made $1,800 after broker split. It took me 6 weeks of paperwork, questions to my broker, and calls to the title company. But it gave me everything: real experience, proof that I could do it, and someone who referred me to their neighbor.

The Isolation Nobody Warns You About

Real estate can be lonely. You're self-employed, commission-based, and most of your "colleagues" are actually competitors. Your friends still have steady paychecks while you're checking your phone every 10 minutes hoping for a lead.

Find your people early. Join a local real estate investing group. Attend San Antonio Board of Realtors® meetings. Get involved in your community—I volunteer at local gardens and events, and it's made all the difference in staying sane.

If you're isolated and broke, you quit. If you're connected and supported, you push through.

The Mentor Trap (and How to Avoid It)

Someone at your brokerage will offer to mentor you. Maybe it's the top agent in the office. Maybe it's someone who seems to have it figured out. Don't automatically assume they're a good mentor just because they're successful.

Some mentors teach you systems. Some teach you shortcuts. Some teach you how to win at anyone's expense.

I had a mentor who was a closer—excellent at negotiation, great at reading people. He also told me to use high-pressure scripts on leads and misrepresent properties to make the deal work. He made $200k a year. He also got sued twice.

A good mentor will teach you the business and your market. A bad one will teach you survival at the cost of integrity. In San Antonio—where neighborhoods like Alamo Heights, Windcrest, and Terrell Hills are tight-knit and reputation matters—your name is everything.

Questions to Ask Before You Accept Mentorship

  • How do they handle difficult clients? (Red flag: if their answer involves abandoning someone)
  • How much time are they actually committing to you? (Not vague "whenever you need me"—actual hours per week)
  • How do they define success? (Commissions only, or client satisfaction too?)
  • Are they teaching you systems or just letting you watch them work? (Watching isn't learning)

The best mentors I've found in San Antonio are other agents who've built sustainable practices—people who close 10–15 deals a year consistently, not 40 deals a year with 20 unhappy clients.

Years Two and Three: The Mindset Shift From Surviving to Growing

If you make it past Year One, something changes. You've got one or two deals under your belt. You know the process. You're not completely terrified anymore.

Year Two is where most agents actually start building. You've stopped just surviving and you can finally think strategically. You know which neighborhoods you want to specialize in—maybe you love the character homes in Southtown, or you know the new developments in Schertz inside and out.

This is when you plant for harvest.

Specialization is Your Competitive Edge

Agents who try to be everything to everyone rarely excel. Agents who specialize dominate their niche. In San Antonio, this could mean: homes under $200k in South Side, luxury properties in Alamo Heights, investment properties in transition neighborhoods, or homes for first-time buyers in Windcrest and Terrell Hills.

I chose to specialize in properties with strong outdoor spaces and homes where people could build community gardens—not because it made me the most money, but because I actually cared about it. That specificity became my brand. That Gardening Realtor isn't a gimmick; it's a real filter for clients who want what I offer.

Your specialization should solve a real problem and reflect something authentic about you. Your clients will feel that.

Building a System (Not Just Grinding)

Year One, you're reactive. Year Two, you start being proactive. You create a lead-generation system that doesn't depend on you hustling 60 hours a week forever.

This could be: a consistent social media presence, a quarterly newsletter to your sphere, a referral partner network, a YouTube channel about neighborhoods, or (like me) a gardening blog that connects people to homes with land potential.

Systems take time to build but they compound. By Year Three, you're not scrambling for leads—they're coming to you.

The Money Gets Better (But Not Instantly)

Year Two income typically jumps to $40k–$80k if you're working smart and you closed deals in Year One. Year Three, if you've got a system, can be $70k–$150k+. These aren't guarantees, but they're realistic if you're consistent.

The key: don't inflate your lifestyle when money gets better in Year Two. Save that extra income. Build a cash cushion. Real estate is cyclical, and you'll need reserves for slow months.

I lived like I made $35k for my first two years even though Year Two was better. That decision meant I could weather a slow market in 2023 without panic.

Imposter Syndrome Is Normal (And Doesn't Mean You're Failing)

You will feel like a fraud. An experienced agent will know more about loan products than you. A client will ask about 1031 exchanges and you'll have to Google it in the car. You'll miss a contingency deadline and cost yourself a deal.

Every agent, even the ones making $500k a year, still has moments where they think someone's going to figure out they don't know everything.

The difference is: they stopped letting that feeling paralyze them.

How Imposter Syndrome Actually Serves You

Imposter syndrome keeps you humble. It keeps you learning. Agents who feel completely confident often get sloppy—they stop double-checking contracts, they stop asking questions, they stop growing.

Your doubt is useful if you channel it into study. Join continuing education courses. Read about Texas real estate law. Call your broker with questions. The agents who thrive aren't the ones who know everything—they're the ones who are comfortable not knowing and confident enough to find answers.

That's a strength, not a weakness.

The Real Things That Get You Through Year 3

Okay, strategy and mindset matter. But what actually keeps you moving forward when you're broke, isolated, and doubting yourself?

A Real Financial Cushion

I can't stress this enough: if you start real estate without 12 months of expenses saved, you're working scared. And scared agents make bad decisions—they over-promise, they cut commissions to close deals, they pressure clients.

A cushion lets you be selective. It lets you walk away from a bad client. It lets you invest in your business instead of just scraping by.

A Community (Not Just Contacts)

Having 300 people on your email list is contacts. Having 5 people you can text at midnight when you're panicking about a deal—that's community. Build the second one ruthlessly.

In San Antonio, show up. Volunteer. Go to coffee meetings. Join a church, a garden club, a running group—somewhere you see the same people repeatedly and they get to know you as a person, not a salesperson.

A Business You Actually Believe In

If you're just chasing commissions, Year Three will grind you down. If you actually believe in helping people find homes or build their lives or invest wisely, you'll push through the hard parts.

I stay in this business because I genuinely love helping people find properties where they can grow—where they can garden, where they can raise kids, where they can build community. When deals fall apart or months are slow, that belief keeps me going.

Find your actual why. Not the one that sounds good at the brokerage. The real one.

Permission to Adjust Your Path

Some people are built for transaction-heavy real estate. Some do better with investment properties. Some shine at commercial or property management or real estate investing.

If you're three years in and miserable, you don't have to push harder—you might need to pivot. Maybe real estate isn't your path, and that's okay. But maybe you're just in the wrong niche or the wrong brokerage or the wrong market focus.

Listen to that feedback. Course-correct. The agents who succeed aren't always the ones pushing hardest; they're the ones pushing in the right direction.

San Antonio-Specific Realities for New Agents

San Antonio's market is growing but it's not Austin or Dallas. We have steady demand, reasonable appreciation, and neighborhoods at every price point. That's good for new agents—less competition than coastal markets, real demand.

You'll Compete on Service, Not Hype

San Antonio people are loyal. They want someone they trust, someone who knows their neighborhood, someone who shows up. Come in promising hustle and flashy marketing and you'll struggle.

Build relationships. Know that Alamo Heights schools feed into a strong high school district. Understand that Schertz commute times to North Star Mall or the airport matter to families. Learn that Leon Valley is where professionals want space but don't want to be in the suburbs.

That knowledge, combined with genuine care, will beat aggressive marketing every time.

Local Lenders Matter

Understand the lender landscape: USAA (huge here), Frost Bank, major national players, and local mortgage brokers. Build relationships with at least 3 lenders you trust. When you can call someone you know and say "I've got a client who has $15k down and imperfect credit," that's valuable.

Your lender relationships will close deals that other agents lose.

Geography Is Your Friend or Enemy

San Antonio sprawls. Stone Oak to Southtown is 20 miles. If you specialize in one geographic area—say, everything south of 1604 or everything within the 78209 zip code—you become the expert. You're not trying to be everything everywhere.

When you specialize geographically, you actually know the schools, the commute patterns, the neighborhood character. Clients can feel that.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I have saved before becoming a real estate agent in San Antonio?

You should have 12 months of your personal living expenses saved—rent, food, utilities, insurance, everything. If you spend $3,000 a month to live, you need $36,000 saved minimum. This isn't paranoia; it's reality. San Antonio's cost of living is reasonable, but even here, 87% of new agents fail because they run out of money before they close enough deals. Having a full-year cushion means you're not desperate when money gets tight, and desperate agents make poor business decisions and terrible client experiences.

Should I join a big brokerage or a small one as a new agent?

There's a real trade-off. Big brokerages (Keller Williams, Century 21, eXp) offer more training, brand recognition, and support systems—helpful when you're learning. Small brokerages offer more attention, flexibility, and sometimes lower splits once you're producing. For your first year, a larger brokerage with a strong training program is usually worth the slightly lower commission split. You need the infrastructure. By Year Three, if you're consistent, you might negotiate better terms elsewhere.

What's the fastest way to get my first deal as a new agent?

Your sphere of influence—people who already know and trust you. Family, friends, former coworkers, neighbors. You're not good enough yet to cold-call or convert internet leads (those take skill you haven't built). But you are credible to people in your existing circle. Tell everyone you know that you're in real estate now. Call 50 people who know you and tell them directly. You'll be shocked how many have a friend who's thinking about selling or buying. Your first deal almost always comes from warm relationships, not from techniques or marketing. Master that before you worry about funnels.

What's the biggest mistake new agents make in San Antonio specifically?

Trying to serve every neighborhood and every price point. San Antonio is big. If you're new, you can't be the go-to agent for everything from $80k condos in Stone Oak to $1M homes in Alamo Heights. Pick a geography (maybe the 78209 or 78212 zip code) or a property type (first-time buyers, investment properties, homes with land) and become brilliant there. Generalists don't build real authority. Specialists do. San Antonio rewards expertise because relationships and reputation matter more here than in faster-moving markets.

Is it realistic to make a full-time income in real estate within 3 years?

Yes—but only if you're strategic and disciplined from Month One. Year One, probably not (average is $18k–$35k). Year Two, if you've been consistent and systematic, you can hit $50k–$80k. Year Three with a real system in place, you can earn $75k–$150k+. The catch: you need the financial cushion to get through Year One, you need a mentor who teaches you well, and you need to build a lead-generation system by end of Year Two. Just grinding and hoping doesn't work. You have to be intentional about business development from the start.
