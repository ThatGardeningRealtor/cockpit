PCS Move to San Antonio: How Military Families Buy a Home Fast Without Getting Burned

By Chris Drawdy · September 15, 2025 · 9 min read

A PCS move to San Antonio means tight timelines, unfamiliar territory, and high stakes. This guide walks you through VA loan approval, BAH-smart neighborhoods, and the contingencies that protect military families from rushed decisions—so you can plant roots confidently, not frantically.

Why San Antonio PCS Moves Feel Different (and Why You Need a Plan)

A PCS move is not a normal home search. You have a report date, orders in hand, and a handful of weeks to go from zero to homeowner in a city you may have never visited. The stress is real—and the mistakes are expensive.

San Antonio is a top military relocation hub because of JBSA (Joint Base San Antonio), which includes Lackland Air Force Base, Fort Sam Houston, and Randolph Air Force Base. That means San Antonio's real estate market knows military buyers, and lenders understand compressed timelines. But knowing where to look and avoiding the traps that catch PCS families in rush-mode purchases? That requires strategy.

I've helped dozens of military families navigate this transition. The ones who succeed are the ones who start early, use remote tools wisely, and build protective contingencies into their offers. Let me show you how.

VA Loan Approval on a PCS Timeline: Start Before You Know the Address

Your VA loan is your superpower in a fast market. It requires no down payment, has competitive rates, and sellers often welcome it because the VA appraisal is thorough (which protects everyone). But approval takes time, and that's where military families go sideways.

Pre-Approval Is Your Foundation

Get your Certificate of Eligibility (COE) immediately. You can request it online through the VA portal (va.gov) or ask a VA-savvy lender to pull it for you. This takes 2–5 business days, but do it before your PCS orders arrive if possible.

Once you have your COE, work with a lender who understands military timelines. San Antonio has excellent VA specialists like USAA Bank, Veterans United, and local lenders like Cornerstone Home Lending who close VA loans in 30–45 days when everything is aligned.

Your lender will pull your credit, verify income (use your current leave and earnings statement), and give you a pre-approval letter that names a specific loan amount. This letter is your hunting license—sellers trust it because VA loans rarely fall through at the last minute.

Entitlement and Funding Fee Reality

You'll hear about "VA entitlement" and "funding fee." Here's the plain English: your entitlement is how much the VA will guarantee (usually around $637K, but verify with your lender). The funding fee is a one-time cost the VA charges to guarantee your loan—typically 2.3% for first-time users, but it's zero if you're rated 10% disabled or higher by the VA.

Many military families roll the funding fee into their loan rather than paying it upfront. That's fine, but understand it adds to your total loan balance and monthly payment. With a $350K home purchase, a 2.3% funding fee ($8,050) adds roughly $40/month to your mortgage. Budget for it.

Pro tip: if you're close to a VA disability rating, get that processed *before* closing. I've seen families save thousands by timing their disability approval before loan closing.

BAH-Smart Neighborhoods: Where Your Housing Allowance Stretches Furthest

Your Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) is fixed by the Department of Defense based on your rank and San Antonio's local rates. As of 2025, an E-5's BAH is roughly $2,100/month. A junior officer might see $2,600–$2,800. The goal is simple: find a neighborhood where you can afford a home that fits your BAH without stretching.

The Neighborhoods That Fit Military Budgets

Windcrest (77041): Just north of Lackland, this is the workhorse neighborhood for junior enlisted and NCOs. Homes run $250K–$350K, schools are solid (Northside ISD), and your commute to Lackland is under 10 minutes. Windcrest has character—tree-lined streets, established yards, and an older but well-maintained housing stock. It's not trendy, but it's reliable and BAH-friendly.

Castle Hills (78213): This small, affluent enclave north of Fort Sam Houston appeals to officers and senior enlisted. Homes are pricier ($350K–$450K), but the school district (Northside ISD) is excellent, and you're 15 minutes from Fort Sam. If your BAH and VA loan approval are strong, this is where you buy quality.

Leon Valley (78238): West of the city, closer to Randolph Air Force Base. Homes run $280K–$380K, and the commute to Randolph is 20–25 minutes. Homes here tend to have larger lots and yard space—perfect if gardening or outdoor projects matter to you. Northside ISD schools.

Universal City / Cibolo (78130/78108): Further out but increasingly popular with military families because of affordability and newer construction. Homes can be found in the $260K–$350K range. Commute to Lackland is 25–30 minutes; to Randolph, 30–40 minutes. Trade a slightly longer drive for a newer home and more yard. Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD is well-regarded.

Northeast San Antonio (Terrell Hills, Alamo Heights—78209/78209): Premium neighborhoods with excellent schools (Alamo Heights ISD, one of Texas's top districts). Expect $400K+, and these fit officers or senior NCOs with higher BAH or significant down payment savings. Commute to all JBSA locations is 20–30 minutes, and you're buying into one of San Antonio's most stable, garden-friendly communities.

The BAH Math

A general rule: aim for a total mortgage payment (PITI + HOA, if any) that's 80–85% of your BAH. With $2,100 BAH, that's roughly $1,680–$1,785/month in housing costs. Using VA loan assumptions (no down payment, no PMI, ~7% current rates), that translates to a home price around $280K–$320K. If you have savings and can put down 5–10%, you stretch into the $340K–$380K range comfortably.

Don't get house-fever. I've seen military families buy homes that consume 95%+ of their BAH, leaving nothing for repairs, HOA increases, or life changes. Stick to the 80–85% rule, and you'll sleep better.

Remote Buying and Virtual Tours: The PCS Family's Secret Weapon

You might not be able to visit San Antonio in person until days before your report date. That's okay. Virtual touring and smart remote buying strategies can work beautifully—if you do them right.

How to Tour Homes Effectively Without Being There

Video walkthroughs are better than static photos, but they're not perfect. Ask your real estate agent to send you multi-angle video tours that linger on key areas: the kitchen condition, water stains on ceilings, foundation cracks, HVAC units, and yard drainage. A 10-minute video tells you more than 50 photos.

Use FaceTime or Zoom walkthroughs with your agent. Walk through the home in real time, ask questions, and have your agent zoom in on things like electrical outlets, cabinet hardware, and deck construction. This takes 20–30 minutes but gives you a real sense of the home's condition.

For neighborhoods, use Google Street View and satellite imagery to check lot sizes, tree coverage, and neighbor density. Then ask your agent or a trusted local contact to drive the neighborhood in person and report back. Send them a list of questions: "Are there young families here? What's the vibe at night? Are the yards well-maintained?" San Antonio Facebook groups (Windcrest Neighbors, Castle Hills Community, etc.) are goldmines for street-level intel.

The In-Person Inspection Visit

If you can arrange a long weekend 2–3 weeks before your report date, visit San Antonio and tour your top 3–4 homes in person. This is worth the cost if you're closing sight-unseen otherwise. Bring a home inspector with you and have them do a full inspection that day. Yes, you're paying for an inspection before you make an offer, but for a PCS buyer on a tight timeline, it's worth $400–$500 to avoid closing on a money pit.

While you're in town, meet your real estate agent in person, visit the neighborhoods at different times of day, and drive the commute to your duty location. You'll get a feel for traffic, the drive, and how you actually feel about the area—not just what the listing photos promised.

Protective Contingencies: Building in Safety When You're Buying Fast

Here's where military buyers often get burned: they waive contingencies to make their offer competitive. On a PCS timeline, you might feel pressure to offer "as-is, no inspection, no appraisal contingency." Don't do it. You need protection.

The Big Three: Inspection, Appraisal, and Financing

Inspection Contingency: Insist on a 7–10 day inspection period, even on a compressed timeline. If the inspector finds major issues (foundation damage, roof problems, HVAC failure), you have the right to renegotiate or walk away. Sellers in San Antonio are used to this—it's standard. Keep the inspection contingency unless you've personally inspected the home and trust it completely.

Appraisal Contingency: Your VA lender requires an appraisal anyway, so include an appraisal contingency in your offer. If the home appraises below your offer price, you can renegotiate. This protects you from overpaying in a competitive market. With VA loans, the appraisal is already thorough, so trust it.

Financing Contingency: Include a financing contingency with a clear deadline (usually 15–21 days for loan approval). Your pre-approval letter supports this. If your lender falls through (rare with VA loans, but it happens), you can exit. This is non-negotiable.

The POA and Final Walk-Through

If you can't be in San Antonio for closing, give your real estate agent a power of attorney (POA) to sign documents on your behalf. This is common and legal in Texas. But do a final walk-through via video 24 hours before closing. Have your agent take you through the home on FaceTime and confirm that agreed-upon repairs are complete and that nothing has changed. This final video walk-through has caught issues (like missing appliances or incomplete repairs) that would have been missed otherwise.

Timeline Compression: How to Close in 30–45 Days Without Disaster

Military lenders can move fast, but they're not magic. Here's the realistic 45-day sprint from offer to closing.

Days 1–7: Offer and Inspection

Make your offer with a 5–7 day inspection period. The lender orders the appraisal immediately. You arrange your inspection (in-person if possible, or inspector-on-demand service if remote). By day 7, you should have inspection results and know whether renegotiation is needed.

Days 8–21: Appraisal, Underwriting, and Title Search

The appraisal comes back (10–14 days typical). If it's at or above your offer price, you're clear. If it's below, you renegotiate or walk. Meanwhile, your lender's underwriter is reviewing your full file: employment, income, credit, assets. Respond to any underwriting requests *immediately*—don't wait a week. Military families often have VA pay stubs, Leave and Earnings Statements (LES), and orders in digital format, so document turnaround is fast.

Title company searches the property and begins title clearance. Address any title issues early (old liens, boundary disputes, etc.). Most Texas properties close cleanly, but don't discover title problems on day 40.

Days 22–35: Clear to Close

By day 22–25, you should be "clear to close"—meaning the lender has approved your loan and the title is clean. The title company prepares closing documents, and your lender funds the loan. Your real estate agent or attorney coordinates the closing appointment.

Days 36–45: Closing and Occupancy

Closing takes 1–2 hours in person or via remote notary (increasingly common). You sign documents, the lender funds, and the title is recorded. Your agent provides you keys, and you arrange utilities and move-in. Done.

This timeline works only if you're responsive and prepared. Delays happen when buyers are slow to respond to requests, when appraisals come in low and require renegotiation, or when employment verification takes longer than expected. Military income is usually straightforward to verify, so that's your advantage.

Neighborhoods Near Lackland and JBSA: Commute Reality and School Ratings

JBSA is huge—it's actually three bases (Lackland, Fort Sam Houston, and Randolph) spread across 300+ square miles. Where you live depends on which base you're assigned to.

For Lackland-Bound Families

Lackland is on San Antonio's southwest side, near the city limits. The closest neighborhoods are Windcrest (northeast, about 10 minutes against traffic), Leon Valley (west, 12 minutes), and Universal City (northeast, 25 minutes). Windcrest is your sweet spot for commute and BAH alignment.

Windcrest schools (Northside ISD) are adequate; they're not top-tier but stable. The North East ISD (northeast of Fort Sam) schools are better-rated if you're willing to drive 20–25 minutes from Lackland.

For Fort Sam Houston / Downtown-Bound Families

Fort Sam is roughly downtown, making Castle Hills (north), Alamo Heights (northeast), and Terrell Hills (north) your closest options. These are pricier but excellent schools and a 15–20 minute commute. If you're assigned to the Hospital or a downtown unit, these neighborhoods are worth the premium.

For Randolph-Bound Families

Randolph is northeast, about 20 miles from central San Antonio. Leon Valley (closer) and Schertz/Universal City (similar distance, often cheaper) are logical fits. Commute is 20–30 minutes. Schools in Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD are growing in reputation and are good alternatives to Northside ISD.

No matter where you're assigned, Google Maps your future duty location and test the commute from your top 2–3 neighborhoods during the times you'd actually drive. Morning traffic on I-37 from Windcrest toward downtown can surprise you—test it.

Final Checklist: Your PCS Home Buying Action Plan

Here's what to do, in order, starting today:

Weeks 1–2 (Immediately): Request your COE. Contact a VA lender and get pre-approved. Gather your LES, DD Form 1172 (dependent info), and any recent tax returns. Set a realistic budget based on your BAH and savings.

Weeks 2–3: Identify your top 3–4 neighborhoods based on your duty location and commute tolerance. Ask your lender and real estate agent to send you active listings in those areas. Request video walkthroughs and ask questions in local Facebook groups.

Weeks 3–4: If possible, visit San Antonio for a long weekend. Tour homes in person, meet your real estate agent, and do a full home inspection on your top choice. Drive the commute during your expected work hours.

Weeks 4–5: Make an offer with standard contingencies (inspection, appraisal, financing). Set clear expectations with your lender about your closing timeline. Request the appraisal order immediately.

Weeks 5–7: Complete the inspection, address any issues, and get clear to close. Be responsive to all lender requests. Schedule your closing appointment.

Week 7–8: Close, collect keys, and arrange utilities and a final walkthrough.

This timeline is aggressive but realistic for VA loans in San Antonio when you start early and stay organized.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a VA loan if I'm currently active duty and haven't yet completed my service contract?

Yes, absolutely. Your eligibility is based on your discharge status or current military service. Active-duty service members and the currently serving are fully eligible for VA loans. You don't need to wait until discharge. Your lender will use your current leave and earnings statement (LES) to verify income, and the VA will check your service record through DMDC (Defense Manpower Data Center). Many active-duty buyers purchase homes on a PCS move before they've completed their current contract.

What happens if the home appraises below my offer price on a VA loan?

The VA appraisal is final—it doesn't negotiate with market enthusiasm. If the home appraises at $340K but you offered $355K, the VA will only lend up to $340K. You have three options: ask the seller to reduce the price to match the appraisal, make up the difference with cash (if you have it), or walk away if the deal no longer makes sense. Most sellers in a military market understand VA appraisals and are willing to renegotiate. This is exactly why an appraisal contingency is critical to your offer.

Do I need to live in San Antonio for a certain amount of time before buying, or can I buy immediately upon PCS arrival?

Texas has no residency requirement for home purchase. You can buy before you even arrive—many PCS families do. Your military orders are your legal basis for relocation, and lenders accept them as proof of intent and ability to pay. You can close on a home while you're still stationed elsewhere; you just need a power of attorney or remote closing capability. Some families close 2–3 weeks before their report date, then move into the home as soon as they arrive in San Antonio.

Is it okay to waive the inspection contingency to make my offer more competitive?

I strongly advise against it, even in a competitive market. San Antonio's real estate market for military buyers is reasonably balanced—you don't usually need to waive inspections to be competitive. If you do waive inspection and the home has a major issue (foundation crack, roof leak, HVAC failure), you're liable for repairs that could cost thousands. Sellers expect inspection contingencies; it's standard. Keep it. If a seller insists you waive it, that's a red flag that something might be wrong.

What's the difference between Northside ISD and North East ISD, and which should I choose for my PCS neighborhood?

Northside ISD serves northwest San Antonio (Windcrest, Leon Valley, Castle Hills) and is larger and more established, with adequate but not exceptional schools. North East ISD serves northeast San Antonio (Alamo Heights, Terrell Hills) and is consistently rated higher academically and is smaller with more resources. If school quality is a top priority, North East ISD neighborhoods command a premium. If you're focused on commute and BAH, Northside ISD works fine. Both are significantly better than some Texas districts—neither is a poor choice.
