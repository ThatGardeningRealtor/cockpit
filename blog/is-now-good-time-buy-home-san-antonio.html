Is Now a Good Time to Buy a Home in San Antonio? An Honest 2026 Assessment

By Chris Drawdy · June 1, 2026 · 10 min read

If you've been watching San Antonio's housing market from the sidelines — refreshing Zillow, second-guessing the timing, wondering if you've already missed your window — this post is for you. I'm going to walk through what the numbers actually say in mid-2026: mortgage rates, inventory levels, median prices, and what all of it means for a real buyer trying to make a real decision. No hype, no pressure — just an honest look at the ground beneath our feet.

Where San Antonio Prices Actually Stand Right Now

San Antonio's median home price is hovering around $295,000 as of late spring 2026 — a number that tells an important story when you compare it to peer cities. Austin's median sits north of $480,000. Dallas is above $380,000. San Antonio remains one of the most affordable major metros in Texas, and that gap hasn't closed as quickly as many predicted.

Year-over-year appreciation has cooled to roughly 1.8%, which is a far cry from the 15–20% annual surges we saw in 2021–2022. That's not a bad thing. Slower, steadier appreciation is the kind of growth that actually builds long-term household wealth — more like a well-tended garden than a hothouse crop that burns out fast.

Entry-level inventory under $250,000 remains the tightest segment of the market. Neighborhoods like Westover Hills, Converse, and Live Oak still see competitive offers on move-in-ready homes in that price range. Above $350,000, buyers have noticeably more leverage — longer negotiation windows and more room to ask for repairs or concessions.

What Price Appreciation Looks Like by Area

The far Northwest corridor — think Helotes, Grey Forest, and Leon Springs — has held value particularly well, driven by Hill Country lifestyle appeal and limited land for new development. The I-35 growth corridor from Schertz down through Converse continues to attract new construction that keeps prices in check for buyers who are flexible on location.

Downtown-adjacent neighborhoods like Lavaca, Tobin Hill, and King William have appreciated modestly but remain niche markets where walkability and character command a premium. If your budget is under $320,000 and you want established character, areas like Woodlawn Hills, Beacon Hill, and parts of the Deco District offer genuine value that's still underappreciated by the broader market.

Mortgage Rates in 2026: The Real Math for SA Buyers

This is where most buyers get stuck ��� and honestly, where the honest conversation needs to happen. The 30-year fixed rate is averaging between 6.6% and 6.9% as of early June 2026. That's not the 3% environment of 2020–2021, and it's not going to feel like it. But it's also not historically unusual — the long-run average for 30-year mortgages since 1971 is right around 7.7%.

On a $295,000 home with 5% down ($14,750), your principal and interest payment at 6.75% comes to roughly $1,820/month. Add in property taxes (Bexar County averages around 2.1–2.3% depending on your MUD district and school zone) and homeowner's insurance, and your all-in payment is typically in the $2,400–$2,600 range. That's real money — and it deserves real planning.

Here's what changes the calculus significantly: rate buydowns. Several San Antonio builders — including D.R. Horton, Meritage Homes, and LGI — are currently offering 2/1 buydown programs on new construction that can bring your first-year rate to the mid-to-low 5% range. Local lenders like Synergy Federal Credit Union, USAA (for military families), and Frost Bank have also been active with competitive products tailored to San Antonio buyers.

The "Wait for Rates to Drop" Gamble

Many buyers are holding out for a return to 5% or below. That's understandable — but it carries real risk. If rates drop to 5.5%, San Antonio prices are likely to jump 8–12% as pent-up demand floods the market. You'd save on interest but pay significantly more for the home itself. Refinancing into a lower rate later is always an option; trying to time the market perfectly almost never works in anyone's favor.

The phrase I use with buyers: you marry the house, you date the rate. Buy the right home in the right neighborhood for your life, and refinance when the opportunity comes. That's not spin — that's the historical pattern of how most successful homeowners have actually built equity over time.

Inventory and Competition: Is This a Buyer's or Seller's Market?

San Antonio currently sits in a transitional market — not cleanly a buyer's market, not the seller's frenzy of 2022, but somewhere meaningful in between. With roughly 3.8–4.2 months of supply in Bexar County, buyers have more options and more time than at any point in the last four years. A balanced market is generally considered 5–6 months of supply, so sellers still hold a slight edge — but that edge has shrunk considerably.

Days on market have stretched to around 52 days on average, compared to single-digit timelines in 2021. That means you can actually schedule a second showing. You can get a home inspection without waiving it. You can ask for the seller to cover a portion of your closing costs — and in many cases, especially above $325,000, they'll say yes.

New construction is playing an especially important role right now. The New Braunfels, Cibolo, Seguin, and Boerne growth corridors have significant active communities from national and regional builders. That new inventory has taken pressure off resale homes and given buyers genuine alternatives, which is good for everyone's negotiating position.

Down Payment Help and Programs San Antonio Buyers Should Know About

One of the most underutilized advantages in San Antonio is the depth of down payment assistance available. The Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers programs like the Homes for Texas Heroes program — covering teachers, nurses, first responders, military, and corrections officers — with up to 5% down payment assistance paired with a competitive 30-year fixed rate. If you work in one of those fields and haven't looked into this, you're leaving real money on the table.

The City of San Antonio's Homeownership Incentive Program (HIP) provides forgivable loans of up to $30,000 for qualifying buyers purchasing within city limits. Income limits apply, but the program covers a wide range of moderate-income households, and the loan is forgiven after a five-year residency requirement. Properties in certain reinvestment zones may qualify for additional incentives layered on top.

For veterans and active-duty military — and San Antonio is home to Joint Base San Antonio, the largest military installation in the country — VA loans remain one of the most powerful homebuying tools available. No down payment, no PMI, competitive rates, and assumable loan features that could add real resale value down the road. If you're affiliated with JBSA (Lackland, Fort Sam Houston, Randolph), VA financing deserves to be your first conversation, not your last.

SAWS, CPS, and Property Tax Reality Checks

A detail many out-of-state buyers miss: San Antonio's property taxes vary significantly by school district and Municipal Utility District (MUD). Homes in NEISD (Northeast ISD) or NISD (Northside ISD) carry different rates than those in Judson, Southside, or East Central ISD. A $290,000 home in a MUD-heavy new development in Cibolo could carry $800–$1,000/month in property taxes and HOA fees combined — always pull the full tax history, not just the current owner's rate.

CPS Energy offers a free energy audit and rebate programs for efficiency improvements that can meaningfully reduce long-term ownership costs, especially in homes built before 2000. SAWS (San Antonio Water System) also has tiered billing that rewards efficient irrigation — relevant if you're buying a home with an established landscape or planning one. These details matter for the real monthly cost of ownership.

The Neighborhoods That Make Sense Right Now

For first-time buyers under $280,000, the best combination of value and livability right now includes areas like Converse (easy access to Randolph AFB and I-35), Universal City, and the Eastside near Kirby and St. Hedwig. These areas have improved infrastructure, reasonable commute times, and homes that offer genuine square footage for the dollar.

For move-up buyers in the $320,000–$420,000 range, Stone Oak, Sonterra, and the TPC Parkway corridor offer strong schools (NEISD), established tree cover, and proximity to the North Central employment hub. The Far West Side near SeaWorld and Westover Hills has strong appreciation fundamentals as Toyota's Tundra plant in South San Antonio continues to grow its regional supplier network and draw relocating employees.

Families prioritizing school districts should look at the pockets of NISD (Northside ISD) in Helotes and Leon Valley for elementary and middle school quality, while Comal ISD — covering much of New Braunfels and Bulverde — consistently outperforms on state ratings and is worth the slightly longer commute for some buyers. SAISD (San Antonio ISD) has several magnet school options that deserve more attention than they typically get from buyers focused purely on suburban ratings.

An Honest Take: Who Should Buy Now, and Who Should Wait

I'm a Realtor, and I'd be lying if I said I had no stake in you buying a home. But I've seen too many people rushed into purchases they regretted to pretend that everyone should buy right now. The honest answer is: it depends on your specific situation, and the answer isn't the same for everyone.

You're probably in a good position to buy now if you have stable income, plan to stay in the home at least 4–5 years, have a down payment and 2–3 months of reserves saved, and have shopped your mortgage options seriously. San Antonio's long-term economic fundamentals — military, healthcare, tourism, manufacturing — are genuinely solid. Buying with a 5+ year horizon in this city has historically been a reliable wealth-building strategy.

You should probably wait if your employment situation is uncertain, your debt-to-income ratio is already strained at current rates, or you're feeling rushed by external pressure rather than drawn by genuine readiness. Buying a home before you're financially grounded is like planting in drought conditions — you can do it, but the odds of something struggling are higher than they need to be. There's no shame in taking another 12 months to shore up your position.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will San Antonio home prices drop in 2026?

A significant price drop in San Antonio in 2026 is unlikely based on current market indicators. The metro continues to add jobs — particularly in cybersecurity, advanced manufacturing, and healthcare — which supports sustained housing demand. Inventory, while improved, is still below the 5–6 months that typically triggers meaningful price declines. The most realistic scenario is continued modest appreciation in the 1–3% annual range, with some softening possible in over-built new construction corridors like far South Bexar County if builder incentives dry up. Buyers waiting for a crash are likely to be disappointed; buyers negotiating strategically on individual homes can find genuine value right now.

What credit score do I need to buy a house in San Antonio in 2026?

For a conventional loan, most San Antonio lenders want a minimum 620 credit score, though you'll qualify for meaningfully better rates at 680 and above. FHA loans — which are popular with first-time buyers in San Antonio — are accessible with scores as low as 580 with 3.5% down, and some lenders work with scores in the 550–579 range with 10% down. VA loans through lenders like USAA or Navy Federal often have more flexible credit standards for qualifying veterans, sometimes accepting scores in the 580–600 range depending on overall loan profile. If your score needs work, San Antonio's nonprofit ClearPoint Credit Counseling offers HUD-certified homebuyer counseling that can help create a 6–12 month improvement plan. Local credit unions like Randolph-Brooks Federal Credit Union are also worth exploring for their in-house underwriting flexibility.

Is San Antonio a good place to buy investment property in 2026?

San Antonio's rental market remains strong, fueled by military rotation cycles, large university populations (UTSA, Trinity, UTSA's Health Science Center), and steady in-migration from higher-cost Texas metros. Single-family rental cap rates in most San Antonio submarkets currently run between 5.5% and 7.5% before expenses, which is competitive for a major Texas city. The South Side near Brooks City-Base and the East Side near Dignowity Hill have attracted investor interest as redevelopment momentum builds. Short-term rental regulations in San Antonio are relatively permissive compared to cities like Austin, though HOA restrictions in newer communities can limit Airbnb-style strategies. Investors should pay close attention to MUD district taxes, which can severely compress net returns on otherwise appealing new-construction properties in outlying areas.

How long does it take to buy a house in San Antonio right now?

From the moment you have an accepted offer, the typical San Antonio closing timeline runs 30–45 days for financed purchases in 2026, though VA loans sometimes stretch to 45–50 days due to appraisal scheduling with the VA's regional loan center. The pre-contract phase — getting pre-approved, working with an agent to identify homes, touring, and making offers — varies widely but often takes 4–10 weeks for serious buyers. In competitive price ranges under $260,000, expect to write 2–3 offers before one sticks; above $350,000, you may go under contract on your first or second offer. New construction timelines are entirely different — spec homes already under roof can close in 30 days, while to-be-built homes in communities like Kallison Ranch or Elan at Culebra can take 5–8 months from contract to close. Factor in your lease end date and give yourself honest runway.

Should I buy new construction or a resale home in San Antonio?

New construction in San Antonio offers real advantages right now: builder incentives (rate buydowns, closing cost credits), energy efficiency features that meaningfully lower utility bills in our hot climate, and warranty coverage on major systems for the first year. The tradeoffs are real too — longer timelines, communities without mature trees or established character, and the reality that some builder-heavy outer-ring neighborhoods (Applewhite, Far South, Von Ormy) are far from most employment centers, making daily commute times on Loop 410, US-281, or I-10 a genuine quality-of-life factor. Resale homes in established neighborhoods like Alamo Heights, Monte Vista, or Olmos Park offer irreplaceable landscaping, architectural character, and walkability that no new subdivision can replicate. The honest answer is that new construction wins on systems and efficiency; resale wins on soul, location, and often lot size. Knowing which matters most to you is the real starting point.
