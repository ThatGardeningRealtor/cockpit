How to Find a Real Mentor in Real Estate (And What to Do If You Got Stuck With the Wrong One)

By Chris Drawdy · January 30, 2026 · 8 min read

Starting in real estate without a good mentor is like planting a garden without knowing the soil. You might survive, but you'll waste years learning what someone could have taught you in months. This guide shows you exactly what separates a real mentor from someone just checking a box—and how to recover if you've already aligned with the wrong team.

Why Your Mentor Matters More Than Your Broker's Name

Your broker's logo doesn't teach you how to navigate the San Antonio market. Your mentor does.

A bad mentor—or worse, no real mentor—is the number one reason new agents flame out. You can have access to the best CRM on the market, the most luxurious office space on the Northside, and a broker with decades of experience, but if that experience isn't being actively transferred to you, you're going to struggle.

Think of real estate mentorship like cultivating a garden. The best gardening realtor doesn't just give you seeds and say "good luck." They show you how to read the soil, when to plant based on San Antonio's heat cycles, which neighborhoods drain well, and which ones need amendment. That's what a real mentor does in real estate—they teach you to read your market.

Red Flags: Signs Your Mentor Is Just Checking a Box

Before you invest months or years with someone, know what to look for.

They're Not Available (But Claim They Are)

A real mentor responds within 24 hours. They have scheduled times you can count on—maybe Thursday mornings or Tuesday afternoons—and they keep them like doctor appointments.

A checkbox mentor responds "whenever you reach out." Which usually means days later, or never. If you're texting your mentor at 10 p.m. about a contract question and getting replies at 8 a.m. three days later, that's not mentorship—that's access to a busy person.

Red flag: "You can call me anytime!" but they never actually pick up, or they say "I'm swamped this month, catch me next month."

Their Advice Is Generic or Outdated

San Antonio's real estate market has exploded in the last five years. Neighborhoods like Southtown and Government Canyon have completely transformed. Loan rates, appraisal processes, and buyer psychology have all shifted.

If your mentor is giving you the same playbook they used in 2015, they're not actively selling. Or worse, they're selling but not paying attention to what's actually working in 2026.

Red flag: They talk more about "the old days" than current market conditions. They can't tell you which neighborhoods are moving fastest or what's realistic for closing timelines in this market.

They Make Money Off You Staying Stuck

Some brokerages are structured so mentors profit when you fail. If your mentor makes a percentage of your commission (especially a large percentage), they have a financial incentive to keep you dependent and working under them rather than helping you grow into independence.

A real mentor wants to create another good agent. A checkbox mentor wants to create another revenue stream.

Red flag: Your mentor discourages you from pursuing independent opportunities, getting additional certifications, or building your own marketing systems.

They Don't Know Your Goals

Have you had a real conversation with your mentor about what you want to achieve? Not "make money"—that's obvious. But do you want to specialize in first-time homebuyers in the Stone Oak area? Build a team? Focus on luxury homes in the Alamo Heights school district? Help investors in the Pearl District?

If your mentor doesn't know your specific target market and goals, they can't mentor you. They're just managing you.

Red flag: When you ask them about your three-year plan, they deflect or give generic advice about "staying consistent."

What a Real Mentorship Relationship Actually Looks Like

So what should you be looking for?

They Meet With You Regularly, On Schedule

Real mentors commit to recurring meetings. Weekly check-ins, bi-weekly coaching calls, or monthly deep-dives into your numbers—whatever the rhythm, it's predictable and protected.

These meetings have agendas. You come with listings you've lost, contracts you're struggling with, or market questions. They come with frameworks and real feedback. You're not catching them between transactions; you're in their calendar as a priority.

They Help You Build Systems, Not Just Close Deals

A checkbox mentor teaches you how to close the deal in front of you. A real mentor teaches you how to build a system that generates deals consistently.

In San Antonio, this might mean: how to work the neighborhoods that matter most (North Central, Bulverde, Boerne), how to build referral relationships with loan officers at lenders like USAA Federal Savings Bank and local credit unions, how to prospect strategically in high-turnover areas like the Medical Center or near UTSA.

A real mentor helps you create your own lead generation machine, not just handhold you through one transaction.

They're Honest About What You're Doing Wrong

A real mentor gives you critical feedback. Not mean feedback—critical. They tell you when your listing presentation is weak, when you're pricing too high, when your follow-up is sloppy.

This is uncomfortable. But this discomfort is where growth lives. If your mentor only says nice things, they don't care enough to invest in your improvement.

They Know Your Local Market Inside and Out

Can your mentor tell you, off the top of their head, what homes in the $400k range in Windcrest have sold for in the last 90 days? Do they understand the difference between selling in the Medical Center versus near USAA's headquarters in Sea World?

A real mentor knows the minutiae of San Antonio's market—school district boundaries, HOA quirks, which neighborhoods have association restrictions, where investment properties cash flow best. They've sold in these neighborhoods recently enough to have current intel.

Red flag: They have to look things up or ask other agents. That's not mentorship; that's guessing together.

Questions to Ask Before You Commit to a Team or Brokerage

Ask these before you sign anything.

Who Will Actually Be My Day-to-Day Mentor, and How Available Are They?

Get a name, not a title. "The team leader will support you" is vague. "Sarah Martinez is your day-to-day mentor and she holds coaching calls every Tuesday at 10 a.m. and you can text her Monday–Friday before 5 p.m." is real.

Ask for their contact info right now and text them a question. See how they respond when you're not even signed up yet.

What's the Commission Split, and Does My Mentor Profit if I Fail?

Understand the structure. If you close a $400k home at 2.5% commission ($10k), how much goes to you, your broker, and your mentor?

Ask directly: "Do you make more money if I stay as an agent here, or if I grow and eventually start my own team?" A good mentor should want you to succeed even if it means you eventually leave.

Can I Meet With My Mentor Before I Join?

A good broker will facilitate this. Have a conversation. Ask them about their philosophy. See if they ask you questions about your goals or just talk about their success.

Red flag: The broker says you can't meet the mentor until you're "officially onboarded" or they're "too busy." That's not how real partnerships work.

How Do They Define Success for a New Agent?

If they say "$100k in the first year," that's possible for some people in some markets, but it's not realistic or healthy as a baseline. If they don't have a clear, thoughtful answer, they're not thinking about your success systematically.

A real answer sounds like: "We want you to close 6–8 transactions in year one, build a sustainable lead generation system, and create the foundation for long-term growth. That typically means $40k–$70k in commission in year one, and scaling from there."

What Training, Tools, and Resources Are Included?

Ask for specifics. Not "we have great training." Ask: "What courses do you provide? Who teaches them? Can I see the curriculum? What CRM are you using and why? Do you pay for continuing education or certifications?"

San Antonio has several strong real estate programs—many agents invest in designations like ABR (Accredited Buyer Representative) or SFR (Short Sale and Foreclosure Resource). Does your broker support that, or discourage it?

How to Course-Correct If You're Already With the Wrong Group

If you've already signed up and you're realizing something's off, here's what to do.

Have the Conversation (Directly and Early)

Don't ghost or seethe. Set up a time to talk to your broker or team leader and be honest: "I don't feel like I'm getting the mentorship I need. Here's what I'm missing—[specific examples]. Can we change this?"

They might surprise you. They might say "I didn't realize, let's get you paired with Sarah instead," or "Let's schedule weekly calls starting next week." Or they might get defensive, which tells you everything.

Give It a Real Window, But Not Forever

If they agree to change, give it 60–90 days. Track what happens. Are you actually getting scheduled mentorship? Is the advice improving? Does your mentor know your market and your goals?

But don't wait a year hoping things get better. You don't have that much time to waste in your first two years of real estate. This is when your foundation is being built.

Know Your Contract Terms Before You Leave

Before you jump ship, understand what you're contractually obligated to. Most brokerage agreements are relatively flexible, but some have non-competes or clawback clauses on commissions.

Talk to a real estate attorney in San Antonio—firms like Sprouse Shrader Smith have agents who understand brokerage agreements. Understand the cost of leaving before you leave.

Start Building Your Own Mentorship Network

If your official mentor isn't working out, you can supplement—or eventually replace—them. Find agents in your target niche who are succeeding, reach out respectfully, and ask for coffee.

In San Antonio, there's a real community of agents. People in the Southtown neighborhood market know each other. Investors near I-10 East have networks. New construction agents around The Dominion have their own crews. Access that community.

Consider Switching Brokerages

This is the nuclear option, but it's survivable. You won't lose your license. You won't lose your clients (they came to you, not to your broker). You'll just move your affiliation.

In San Antonio, there are boutique brokerages (like smaller teams at eXp Realty), traditional full-service brokerages, and independent brokers. Compare what you'd be walking into before you walk away from what you have.

You Can Survive This—And Thrive

A bad mentorship doesn't define your career. Plenty of successful agents had terrible starts.

But don't accept it as inevitable. Be clear-eyed about what you need: real availability, current market knowledge, genuine interest in your goals, and honest feedback. Hold your mentor and your broker accountable to that standard.

San Antonio's real estate market is strong and growing. Properties in every neighborhood—from the bustling Medical Center to the tranquil Texas Hill Country communities just 45 minutes north—need good agents. You can be one of them. But not if you're learning from someone who isn't actually invested in your growth.

Choose your mentor like you'd choose a business partner. Because that's what they are.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if I should leave my brokerage?

You should consider leaving if, after a genuine 90-day effort to improve the mentorship situation, you're still not getting scheduled, consistent support; if your mentor doesn't know your target market or goals; or if you feel your mentor is incentivized to keep you dependent rather than help you grow independent. Also pay attention to your gut—if you dread contact with your broker or team, that's real data. Life's too short and the stakes are too high to stay in a relationship where you're not being invested in. But before you leave, understand your contract and the financial implications.

What should I look for in a San Antonio real estate mentor specifically?

Look for someone who's actively selling in the San Antonio market right now (not someone who retired from selling five years ago). They should know neighborhood-specific details—which areas have the fastest-moving inventory, which have the highest investor activity, which are dominated by first-time homebuyers, and where cash buyers dominate. They should have relationships with local lenders (USAA, credit unions, local banks) and understand the unique challenges of the San Antonio market like our heat, the 1004MC appraisal requirements for Hill Country properties, and how to price competitively in neighborhoods that see lots of new construction. Finally, they should be able to explain why they focus on certain neighborhoods or buyer types—that shows intentional strategy, not just random activity.

Is it normal to feel lost as a new agent, or does that mean I have a bad mentor?

It's absolutely normal to feel lost—real estate is complex, the rules change, the market shifts. But you should feel "productively lost," not abandoned. A good mentor acknowledges the overwhelm and creates structure to address it—"Here's what we focus on weeks 1–4, here's what we tackle weeks 5–8." A bad mentor either tells you "everyone feels this way, you'll figure it out" or doesn't acknowledge it exists. The difference between a supportive learning curve and a bad mentorship is the presence of a roadmap and regular check-ins along the way. If you're five months in and your mentor still hasn't taught you their core system or shown you exactly how they generate leads, that's a red flag.

Can I have multiple mentors?

Yes, and you should. Your official mentor at your brokerage might teach you systems and daily operations, while a broker friend in a different niche teaches you investment property strategies, and a top agent in your target neighborhood teaches you local nuances. Multiple mentors actually accelerate your learning—you're seeing different approaches and can synthesize what works for your style. Just make sure your primary mentor is one person who knows your full picture and goals, and that your additional mentors aren't contradicting your core strategy. In a market like San Antonio with such diverse neighborhoods (Alamo Heights is a totally different game than Government Canyon), having specialist mentors in your specific target areas is actually smart.

What if I chose the wrong brokerage but have transactions in progress?

You can switch brokerages mid-transaction, and it doesn't affect your client or your commission. Your brokerage change is administrative—it doesn't interrupt the deal. The bigger question is whether staying long enough to close those deals and then leaving is worth it (it usually is, because the experience teaches you something) or whether the toxicity is so bad that you need to exit immediately. Most brokers understand this and allow transfers without penalty if you're leaving because of lack of support, rather than jumping brokerages every month. Talk to your broker about your timeline and needs; if they're not willing to work with you, that's confirmation you made the right choice leaving.
