San Antonio Housing Market: What Changed in 2025 and What to Expect in 2026

By Chris Drawdy · April 28, 2026 · 10 min read

The San Antonio housing market has been through a serious recalibration — and if you've been waiting on the sidelines wondering whether now is finally your moment, this breakdown is for you. We'll walk through exactly what shifted in 2025, where home prices and inventory stand today, and what the data suggests about buying or selling in San Antonio through the rest of 2026. No hype, no guesswork — just real local context to help you make a confident decision.

How 2025 Reshaped the San Antonio Housing Market

After a couple of frenzied years where homes in neighborhoods like Stone Oak, Alamo Ranch, and Converse were going under contract within days — sometimes hours — 2025 brought something many buyers had nearly forgotten: breathing room. The market didn't crash. It exhaled.

Rising mortgage rates that had been climbing since late 2022 finally settled into a sustained range in the mid-to-upper 6s through most of 2025. That single factor kept a meaningful portion of move-up buyers locked in place, which simultaneously reduced demand and kept existing inventory tighter than the raw numbers might suggest.

What we saw on the ground was a tale of two markets. Turnkey homes in desirable school districts — think NEISD, NISD, and Comal ISD zones — still moved with reasonable speed. But homes needing work, overpriced listings, and properties in less commuter-friendly corridors sat, accumulated days, and eventually saw price reductions. That's not a bad market. That's a normal market returning.

The Inventory Rebound Was Real — and Uneven

Active listings in Bexar County climbed from roughly 5,800 in early 2024 to over 8,500 by mid-2025, a significant increase that gave buyers more choices than they'd had in years. But that inventory wasn't evenly distributed across price points.

The $250,000–$350,000 range — historically San Antonio's sweet spot for first-time buyers — remained competitive. New construction from builders like D.R. Horton, Pulte, and LGI in areas like Converse, Von Ormy, and the Far West Side along Culebra Road helped add supply, but many of those homes came with builder incentives rather than price cuts.

Above $500,000, the picture changed dramatically. Luxury inventory in the Hill Country Village area, certain 78209 zip codes near Alamo Heights, and the booming TPC Parkway corridor stacked up. Sellers in that tier had to work harder — staging, pricing sharply, and offering concessions — to close deals.

San Antonio Home Price Trends: What the Numbers Actually Show

Let's be honest about what "prices dropped" means in context. San Antonio's median home price peaked around $315,000–$320,000 in 2022. By the end of 2025, that median had settled closer to $290,000–$298,000 depending on the data source. That's a pullback, not a collapse — and prices are still well above pre-pandemic levels from 2019 when the median hovered around $230,000.

Appreciation has essentially flatlined in most price bands, which is actually healthy for a market that was growing faster than wages could keep pace with. For buyers who sat out 2021 and 2022 feeling priced out, the combination of slightly lower prices and seller concessions has meaningfully improved affordability — even with rates still elevated.

Think of it like a garden that grew too fast in one season. The roots needed time to catch up with the canopy. San Antonio's market is doing exactly that — consolidating gains and finding stable ground before the next growth cycle.

Neighborhood-Level Price Shifts Worth Knowing

In Helotes and Leon Valley along the IH-10 West corridor, prices held relatively firm because demand from families prioritizing NISD schools remained steady. The commute into Loop 410 and Downtown is manageable, and those communities offer genuine neighborhood feel — big yards, mature trees, and neighbors who wave.

On the Northeast Side, communities around Schertz, Cibolo, and Universal City — popular with military families from JBSA-Randolph — saw modest softening, partly because a wave of new construction gave buyers options they hadn't had before. Meanwhile, Southside neighborhoods like Calaveras Lake area and east of IH-37 are quietly attracting attention for their lower price points and improving infrastructure.

In-town neighborhoods like Dignowity Hill, Lavaca, and Beacon Hill continued their long-running appreciation story, though at a slower pace than 2021–2022. Walkability, proximity to the Pearl District, and access to the San Antonio River Walk trails keep demand durable in those zip codes.

How Interest Rates Are Actually Affecting SA Buyers Right Now

At 6.4–6.7%, mortgage rates are no longer the shock they were when they jumped from 3% to 7% seemingly overnight in 2022. Buyers have adjusted psychologically, and many are using creative strategies to make the numbers work — including buying down points, exploring adjustable-rate mortgages, or leveraging builder incentives on new construction.

Local lenders like Synergy One Lending, Inspire Home Loans, and several credit unions including RBFCU and Generations Federal Credit Union have been competitive on rates and closing cost assistance, especially for first-time buyers. RBFCU in particular has been worth a call for members — their mortgage products often edge national lenders on fees.

Texas doesn't have state income tax, which is still a huge quality-of-life advantage compared to buyers fleeing California or Illinois. That trade-off continues to make San Antonio's effective cost of living genuinely compelling even with today's rates — and it's one reason relocation demand to SA hasn't dried up the way it has in some other Sun Belt markets.

Down Payment Assistance Programs Still Available in SA

The City of San Antonio's Homeownership Incentive Program (HIP) still provides down payment and closing cost assistance for income-qualifying buyers purchasing within city limits. Bexar County's AHFC programs offer similar options for buyers purchasing in unincorporated areas.

The Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) continues to offer the Homes for Texas Heroes program — a significant resource for teachers in SAISD, NEISD, or NISD, as well as veterans and first responders. Some buyers are combining TSAHC assistance with VA loans and coming to the table with very little out of pocket.

Is It a Buyer's Market or Seller's Market in San Antonio Right Now?

The honest answer: it depends on the price point and the neighborhood, which is why blanket statements about "the market" can be misleading. At roughly 4.8 months of supply overall, San Antonio sits in balanced-to-slightly-buyer-favoring territory. But that average masks real variation.

Below $300,000, you're still competing. Not the bidding-war chaos of 2021, but well-priced homes in good condition in Converse, Kirby, and far Northwest side developments still see multiple offers when they're priced right and show well. Sellers at this tier have real leverage — there are simply more buyers than available homes.

From $400,000 and up, the balance tips toward buyers. Days on market are longer, price reductions are common, and sellers are more willing to cover closing costs, buy down rates, or include appliances. If you've been waiting for a moment to get more for your money in the mid-to-upper tier, 2026 is giving you that window.

What to Expect From the San Antonio Real Estate Market in 2026

The broad consensus among economists and housing analysts watching the Texas market is cautious optimism for the remainder of 2026. Rates are expected to tick down modestly — potentially reaching the low 6s by Q3 or Q4 — which would unlock the "rate-locked" sellers who've been hesitant to trade their 3% mortgages for today's reality.

If that happens, San Antonio could see a meaningful inventory surge in the second half of 2026 as move-up buyers finally feel the spread is manageable. More supply at the mid-tier level would help affordability but could also moderate any significant price appreciation in the near term. Think steady, not spectacular.

San Antonio's economic fundamentals remain strong. The military presence across JBSA installations, continued growth in the healthcare sector through University Health and Methodist Healthcare, Toyota's manufacturing footprint on the South Side, and a growing tech and cybersecurity corridor near UTSA all underpin durable housing demand. This city has roots — and roots hold even when the weather gets rough.

What Sellers Should Be Doing Right Now to Prepare

If you're thinking about listing in late 2026, the preparation window is now. Homes that are updated, clean, and priced with current comps — not 2022 comps — are the ones moving. A fresh coat of exterior paint, landscaped curb appeal, and a pre-listing inspection go a long way in a market where buyers have choices.

Pricing strategy matters more than ever. Overpriced homes are sitting 70, 80, even 90+ days before seeing a reduction — and by then, buyers assume something is wrong. A sharp, realistic price from day one generates momentum that overpricing simply can't recover.

I always tell sellers it's like planting in the right season. You can't force a bloom by overwatering — and you can't force a sale by overpricing. The homes that sell well in 2026 will be the ones that make buyers feel they found something worth moving for.

Best Neighborhoods to Buy in San Antonio in 2026

For first-time buyers on a budget, the Northeast Side continues to offer the best value-to-quality ratio in the metro. Communities in Universal City and Converse offer homes in the $230,000–$270,000 range with decent lot sizes, easy access to IH-35 for commutes, and proximity to JBSA-Randolph for military buyers with VA benefits.

For families prioritizing schools, the Comal ISD zone — particularly newer developments in New Braunfels' 78132 and communities spilling into the northern reaches of Bexar County — continues to attract buyers willing to commute down IH-35 or TX-46. Comal ISD's consistent TEA ratings and the relative affordability compared to Alamo Heights ISD make it a compelling choice.

For buyers who want walkability, character, and investment upside, the older inner-loop neighborhoods deserve serious attention. Mahncke Park, Highland Hills, and the areas around San Pedro Springs Park offer bungalows and mid-century homes with real personality — the kind of places with established canopy trees, community gardens, and neighbors who actually know each other. They're not the cheapest option, but they're the kind of investment you feel good about for decades.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will San Antonio home prices drop further in 2026?

Significant further price declines look unlikely given San Antonio's employment diversity and continued population growth — the city consistently ranks among the fastest-growing metros in the U.S. What's more probable is price stability or modest appreciation in the 1–3% annual range as inventory and demand reach a new equilibrium. The biggest wildcard is what happens with mortgage rates: if they drop meaningfully into the high 5s, pent-up demand could push prices upward faster than expected. Buyers hoping for a dramatic price crash are likely to be disappointed, while those who act thoughtfully in the current window stand to benefit from today's relative softness. The 2026 market rewards preparation over speculation.

Is San Antonio a good place to buy a house as an investment or rental property in 2026?

San Antonio has historically been a solid long-term rental market, driven by its large military population (which creates steady, reliable tenant demand), major university presence through UTSA and UTSA Health, and a cost of living that keeps renters from easily converting to buyers in certain income brackets. Cap rates on single-family rentals in areas like the Southside, Southeast, and older Northeast Side neighborhoods have become more attractive now that prices have softened from peak levels. However, Bexar County property taxes — which can run 2.2–2.5% of assessed value annually — are a critical line item that investors need to underwrite carefully. The Texas Property Tax Code's homestead exemption doesn't apply to investment properties, so your carrying costs are higher than an owner-occupant would pay on the same home. Working with a CPA familiar with Texas investment property before closing is strongly recommended.

How long does it take to buy a house in San Antonio right now?

From the time you go under contract to closing, the standard timeline in San Antonio is 30–45 days for a financed purchase, assuming no major title complications or appraisal disputes. The front end — getting pre-approved, finding the right home, and negotiating a contract — varies widely but buyers who are pre-approved and have a clear sense of their priorities are typically under contract within 2–6 weeks in today's market. New construction timelines are different: production homes from large builders can close in 30–60 days if inventory is complete, but spec-build or custom options can stretch 6–12 months. San Antonio's title companies (Amrock, Independence Title, and Republic Title all have strong local operations) are generally efficient, but scheduling home inspections early in the option period — don't wait until day 7 of a 10-day option — helps avoid timeline crunches.

What are closing costs for buyers in San Antonio, Texas?

Texas buyers should budget 2–4% of the purchase price for closing costs, which include lender fees (origination, underwriting), title insurance, escrow deposits for property taxes and homeowner's insurance, and prepaid interest. On a $295,000 home, that means roughly $5,900–$11,800 in closing costs depending on your lender and loan type. One Texas-specific item to know: the Owner's Title Insurance policy is typically paid by the seller in Bexar County by custom — but this is negotiable, and in today's buyer-friendly conditions for mid-to-upper price tiers, sellers are often willing to cover it or offer a closing cost credit. VA loans have specific fee restrictions that can reduce out-of-pocket costs significantly for eligible veterans, making them one of the most powerful financing tools available in a metro with San Antonio's military population. Always get a Loan Estimate from your lender before going under contract so there are no surprises at the closing table.

What is the best time of year to sell a house in San Antonio?

Historically, March through June represents San Antonio's peak selling season — families want to be settled before the school year starts, and the weather is pleasant enough that curb appeal is easier to achieve before summer heat scorches the landscaping. Listings that go live in late February or early March tend to generate the highest traffic and fastest contract timelines. That said, San Antonio's year-round mild climate (relative to northern markets) means fall and early winter aren't as dead as sellers sometimes fear — military relocation orders, for instance, come through all year and generate motivated buyers regardless of season. The more important variables are pricing accuracy, home condition, and professional photography rather than the specific month. A well-prepared home in October will outperform an overpriced, poorly presented home in April every time.
