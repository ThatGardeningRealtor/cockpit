You Might Qualify as a First-Time Home Buyer Even If You Have Owned Before — The Rule Most People Miss

By Chris Drawdy · August 28, 2025 · 8 min read

You sold your home three years ago, got divorced, or downsized into a rental—and now you're thinking about buying again. Here's what lenders don't always advertise: you may still qualify for first-time home buyer programs and incentives. Learn how the 3-year rule opens doors (and saves money) for San Antonio buyers who thought those programs were behind them.

What the 3-Year Rule Actually Means

Most lenders and government programs define a "first-time home buyer" not as someone who has never owned, but as someone who hasn't owned a primary residence in the past three years. This is the rule that changes everything—and the one most people miss when they assume they're ineligible.

Think of it like tending a garden: if you've stepped away from your plot for three years, you're eligible to start fresh with a new growing space. The ownership clock resets. You're not permanently branded as an "experienced homeowner" the moment you sell.

The Federal Housing Administration (FHA), VA loans, and even state programs like the Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) use this three-year window as their baseline. If it's been three years or more since you last held the deed to your home, you're back in the game.

Who Qualifies Under This Rule: Four Common Scenarios

Let's walk through the real-life situations where this rule matters most in San Antonio.

Divorced Homeowners Coming Back to the Market

If you owned a home with your ex-spouse and the property was sold as part of your divorce settlement, that sale clock starts ticking now. Even if the divorce finalized just months ago, the three-year window begins from the sale date—not from when your marriage ended.

In San Antonio's booming market, many divorcing couples who owned in neighborhoods like Terrell Hills, Alamo Heights, or the North Star area are selling family homes. If you're the ex-spouse relocating or downsizing into rental, you may be first-time eligible again in three years.

This matters financially: divorce often means restarting with less equity and potentially lower credit scores temporarily. First-time buyer programs cushion that transition.

People Who Relocated and Rented for Three Years

Your company transferred you to San Antonio in 2022, and you rented in a neighborhood like Southtown or The Pearl while you figured out the market. If you never owned during those three years—just rented—you're classified as a first-time buyer regardless of previous ownership in another state.

This applies even if you owned a home in Austin, Dallas, or out of state before moving. The clock only counts if you were an owner in the past three years. Renters have a clean slate.

San Antonio attracts a lot of relocated professionals to UTSA, the medical district, and downtown tech jobs. If that's your story, you're in stronger position than you might think.

People Who Downsized From a Home into a Rental

Empty nesters and retirees often sell a larger family home and rent a condo or apartment to test-drive a new neighborhood before committing to a permanent purchase. If you sold your home three or more years ago and have been renting since, you qualify as first-time again.

This is powerful for someone who sold a 4-bedroom in Leon Valley in 2021 and has been renting a 2-bedroom in Southtown. You get first-time buyer access while bringing the experience and financial stability of someone who's already navigated homeownership once.

You're not starting over—you're making a calculated, more intentional choice with better information.

Property Owners (But Not Primary Residence Owners)

Here's a lesser-known nuance: if you've owned an investment property, a vacation home, or inherited land, that ownership doesn't disqualify you from first-time buyer status. The rule only applies to primary residences you've lived in.

If you've been a landlord or held a rental property while renting yourself, you can still access first-time buyer programs when purchasing a primary residence—as long as that primary residence ownership gap is three years or more.

What Programs and Benefits Unlock When You Qualify

Qualifying as first-time matters because it opens access to dozens of financial incentives and loan structures that aren't available to repeat buyers. These aren't small perks—they can save tens of thousands of dollars across a 30-year mortgage.

Down Payment Assistance Programs

Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers down payment assistance of 3–6% of your loan amount—money that doesn't need to be repaid if you stay in the home and meet income requirements. On a $350,000 home in the Stone Oak area, that's $10,500–$21,000 in free assistance.

San Antonio-based lenders like BBVA and FROST also run local first-time buyer down payment grant programs. These aren't widely advertised, but they exist.

Habitat for Humanity San Antonio also prioritizes first-time buyers in their homeownership programs, though homes are limited and competitive.

Lower Interest Rates

First-time buyer loan products often come with interest rates 0.25–0.5% lower than conventional loans for repeat buyers. On a $350,000 loan, that's a difference of $60–$150 per month—or $21,600–$54,000 over the life of the loan.

This benefit alone makes it worth confirming your status before you shop.

Reduced or Waived Mortgage Insurance Premiums

FHA loans (which first-time buyers favor) sometimes offer reduced mortgage insurance costs or, in some structures, waived mortgage insurance premiums if you put down 15% or more. For someone putting down $52,500 on that $350,000 home, that savings compounds significantly.

Tax Credits and Grants

Texas offers a first-time buyer tax credit up to $8,000 at the state level (though this varies by program). Some county programs in Bexar County add additional incentives for teachers, healthcare workers, and essential employees buying in underserved neighborhoods.

Why San Antonio's Market Makes This Rule Especially Valuable Right Now

San Antonio's housing market has heated up considerably. Median prices hovering around $385,000, and bidding wars in neighborhoods like Stone Oak, The Dominion, and Windcrest are common. Competition is fierce, and every financial advantage counts.

First-time buyer programs give you negotiating power. A seller sees you're FHA-approved with down payment assistance backing you—you're a serious, qualified buyer. In a competitive market, that matters.

Additionally, San Antonio still has pockets of relative affordability compared to Austin or Dallas. Combined with first-time buyer assistance, you can find 3–4 bedroom homes in family-friendly neighborhoods like Alamo Heights or Terrell Hills within reach for buyers earning $60,000–$90,000 annually.

The city's growth (driven by tech jobs at UTSA, healthcare expansion, and remote workers relocating) means consistent demand. Lenders are actively promoting first-time buyer products here because they know the market.

Steps to Confirm Your First-Time Status and Qualify

Don't assume you're ineligible—verify with a lender who specializes in first-time buyer loans. Here's the process:

1. Document Your Timeline

Gather the deed, closing statement, or sale document from your last home purchase. Write down the exact date you stopped owning a primary residence. If you sold in 2022, you're eligible now in 2025. If you sold in 2023, you'll be eligible in 2026.

If you're a renter and never owned, write down when your rental period began. That date becomes your "first-time" baseline.

2. Connect With a First-Time Buyer Lender

Don't just call any mortgage broker. Ask for someone with experience in FHA, VA, TSAHC, or local first-time buyer programs. In San Antonio, lenders like Frost Bank, BBVA, and Guaranteed Rate have dedicated first-time buyer teams.

Tell them upfront: "I owned a home before, but it's been more than three years since I sold. I want to confirm I qualify for first-time buyer programs." This speeds up the conversation.

3. Pre-Qualify and Get Loan Options in Writing

Before you look at homes, get pre-qualified. Ask the lender to specify which programs you qualify for and what the cost differences are between them. Seeing the numbers side-by-side (conventional vs. FHA vs. TSAHC) helps you make the right choice.

4. Work With a Realtor Who Knows These Programs

A good realtor—especially one who understands the San Antonio market and first-time buyer nuances—can help you navigate neighborhoods within your budget and point out homes that fit your timeline. They'll also flag if a home's condition makes it FHA-compatible (FHA has stricter property standards than conventional loans).

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Now that you understand the 3-year rule, here are mistakes people make when pursuing first-time buyer status:

Assuming You're Ineligible Without Checking

This is the biggest one. People exclude themselves based on the assumption that owning before disqualifies them forever. Check your timeline. It's free, takes five minutes, and could save you thousands.

Mixing Up "Primary Residence" With Other Property Types

If you own rental properties or investment land, that doesn't count against you. Only primary residence ownership in the past three years disqualifies you. Don't confuse the two.

Not Shopping Programs—Just Taking the First Loan Offer

A conventional lender might offer you a standard loan. But an FHA loan through a different lender might cost less, require less down, and offer better terms. Always get at least three offers, and specifically ask each lender about first-time buyer programs.

Waiting Too Long to Buy If You're At Year Two

If you sold in 2023 and it's now 2025, you're eligible. But if you sold in 2023 and it's still 2025 but only partway through the year, check the exact month. Don't leave money on the table by waiting unnecessarily—but also don't buy too early and disqualify yourself from programs.

Forgetting to Ask About Local Programs

San Antonio and Bexar County run niche first-time buyer programs for teachers (SAISD and NEISD employees), healthcare workers, and essential workers. Your mortgage broker or real estate agent should know these. If they don't, find someone who does.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the 3-year rule apply if I owned a home with my spouse but we're now divorced?

Yes, absolutely. The rule looks at when you stopped owning a primary residence, not your marital status. If you sold the marital home as part of your divorce settlement and it's been three years since that sale, you qualify as first-time again. The divorce decree and closing documents from that sale are all you need to verify the timeline. Each ex-spouse can independently qualify if they haven't owned another primary residence in the intervening three years.

Can I use FHA and TSAHC programs at the same time?

No, you typically choose one primary loan program. However, some down payment assistance grants (like certain TSAHC programs) can layer on top of an FHA loan. This means you could get an FHA loan (which has lower down payment requirements) plus a separate grant to help with that down payment. A specialized lender can structure this, but it requires planning. You can't double-dip into two major loan programs simultaneously—you pick the one that works best for your situation, usually based on the total cost (interest rate + fees + down payment required).

If I've owned investment property for 5 years, does that disqualify me from first-time buyer programs?

No. First-time buyer status only looks at primary residence ownership within the past three years. Investment properties, rental homes, land, and vacation homes don't count. You can be a long-time landlord and still qualify as a first-time buyer when purchasing a primary residence, as long as you haven't personally lived in and owned a primary residence in the past three years. This opens up programs for investor-landlords who are now ready to buy their own home.

What's the difference between FHA and conventional loans for first-time buyers in Texas?

FHA loans are government-backed and allow down payments as low as 3.5%, but they require mortgage insurance (FHA MIP) for the life of the loan. Conventional loans require larger down payments (usually 5–20%) but may have lower insurance costs if you put down 20%. For first-time buyers in San Antonio with limited down payment funds, FHA often wins on affordability. Conventional wins if you can save a larger down payment (15%+) and want to avoid permanent mortgage insurance. Your lender should run the math both ways so you can see the 30-year cost difference.

Can I buy in a San Antonio neighborhood like Stone Oak or Alamo Heights with first-time buyer programs?

Yes, you can use first-time buyer loans (FHA, VA, conventional) to buy anywhere in San Antonio, including Stone Oak, Alamo Heights, Terrell Hills, and The Dominion. The programs themselves have no geographic restrictions—they work on any home that meets their property standards. The only real constraint is your budget and whether the home passes the lender's inspection requirements. FHA does have stricter property standards than conventional loans, so an older home in Alamo Heights might need repairs to pass FHA inspection, but newer homes and well-maintained properties typically clear those hurdles without issue.
