Fig Trees in San Antonio: How to Grow Them, When They Produce, and Why Buyers Love Them

By Chris Drawdy · June 5, 2026 · 9 min read

A mature fig tree in a San Antonio backyard is not just fruit — it's a statement about what a home offers beyond square footage. Figs are one of the oldest cultivated fruits on earth, they love our heat, and they produce without much interference from the gardener. If you're planting for pleasure or selling and want something that moves buyers, here's everything you need to know.

Why San Antonio Is Fig Country

Fig trees (Ficus carica) evolved in the Mediterranean and Middle East — hot, dry summers, mild winters, alkaline soil. Sound familiar? San Antonio's climate is nearly a perfect match. Our heat that punishes tomatoes in August is exactly what figs want. Our caliche-heavy alkaline soil that frustrates so many gardeners is acceptable to figs, which tolerate pH levels that would starve other fruit trees. And our winters, while occasionally cold enough to kill the above-ground wood on figs, rarely kill the roots — meaning even a hard-frozen fig usually comes back strong from the base in spring.

Fig trees in San Antonio, once established, are about as close to a "plant it and walk away" fruit tree as you'll find. They don't need much fertilizer, they don't require pollination from another tree (common figs are parthenocarpic — they fruit without pollination), and they produce generously on relatively young wood. A two-year-old tree can give you a meaningful harvest. A five-year-old tree in good ground can produce more figs than a family can eat.

Understanding the Two Crops

Most San Antonio gardeners are surprised to learn that figs can produce twice a year. The first crop — called the breba crop — develops on the previous year's wood and ripens in late May through June in our climate. The breba figs are often larger but less sweet than the main crop, which develops on new wood from spring growth and ripens from late July through September depending on variety and heat load.

Not all varieties produce reliable breba crops. Brown Turkey and Celeste are the most consistent double-croppers in our zone. If you want maximum production, these are the trees to plant.

The Best Fig Varieties for San Antonio

The San Antonio nursery trade has standardized around a few proven varieties, and for good reason. These are the ones that reliably perform in our specific conditions.

Celeste (the SA Classic)

Celeste is the quintessential Texas fig. Small to medium-sized fruit with a honey-sweet flavor, closed eye (which reduces insect and disease entry), and a tight, dry interior that preserves well and resists souring. It handles our summer heat without skipping a beat, produces reliably in its first or second year, and the tree tends to be relatively compact — 8 to 10 feet in most SA conditions — making it well suited to smaller backyards. If you plant one fig tree in San Antonio and never look at another cultivar, plant Celeste.

Brown Turkey

Larger fruit than Celeste, with a mild, sweet flavor and reddish-brown skin. Brown Turkey is widely available at every major nursery in San Antonio, produces two crops reliably, and the tree is vigorous and fast-growing. It can reach 12 to 15 feet if unpruned. The fruit is excellent fresh and makes outstanding preserves. Brown Turkey's only weakness compared to Celeste is a more open eye, which can lead to souring if rainy weather coincides with ripening — but in our dry summers, this is rarely an issue.

LSU Purple

Developed at Louisiana State University specifically for Southern conditions. Deep purple skin, rich jammy flavor, excellent disease resistance, and strong heat tolerance. LSU Purple produces well in containers, which makes it useful for HOA situations where in-ground planting is restricted (see our HOA loophole guide). Less commonly available locally than Celeste or Brown Turkey, but worth tracking down at specialty nurseries.

Texas Everbearing

Despite the name, "everbearing" means two crops rather than continuous production, but those two crops are reliable and the tree is extremely adapted to Texas conditions. Medium-sized, brownish-purple fruit. Good for fresh eating and preserves. Available at many San Antonio nurseries and through Texas A&M Extension recommended plant lists.

Black Mission

The classic California fig — dark purple skin, rich flavor, beautiful. It can grow in San Antonio but requires a warmer, more sheltered microclimate than the other varieties. If your yard has a south-facing wall that radiates heat and protects from cold north winds, Black Mission can thrive. In more exposed settings, it's more vulnerable to our occasional hard freezes. Worth trying if you have the right microclimate, but not the first choice for a no-fuss planting.

How to Plant and Establish a Fig Tree in San Antonio

Figs are forgiving but benefit from a good start. The investment in proper planting pays back for decades.

Site Selection

Full sun, minimum six hours per day, is essential. South or west-facing exposures against a wall or fence are ideal — the reflected heat extends the growing season and protects from cold winter winds from the north. Figs tolerate our alkaline clay soil but perform significantly better with organic matter amendment and good drainage. Avoid low spots that collect water during heavy rains.

Planting

Dig a hole two to three times the width of the root ball and the same depth. Mix the native soil with a generous amount of compost — two to three cubic feet per planting hole. Place the tree at the same depth it was growing in the nursery container. Backfill, firm gently, and water deeply. Mulch four to six inches around the base, keeping mulch a few inches away from the trunk.

Plant in late winter to early spring — February through March in San Antonio — to give the tree a full growing season to establish before facing summer heat or winter cold. Fall planting works too with good irrigation, but spring gives you the most establishment time before the first summer test.

The First Two Years: Patience and Water

Newly planted figs need regular, deep watering for the first two summers — once or twice a week in peak heat, tapering off in fall and winter. This is the critical establishment window. A tree that struggles for water in its first summer may limp along for years before recovering. Once established (typically by year three), fig trees in San Antonio can survive on rainfall alone, though supplemental irrigation during dry spells will boost fruit production.

Resist the urge to over-fertilize in year one. A light application of balanced fertilizer in early spring is sufficient. Heavy nitrogen feeding of a young tree produces lush growth at the expense of root establishment and fruit development.

Pruning Figs in San Antonio

Fig trees produce fruit on both old wood (breba crop) and new growth (main crop), which means pruning strategy affects when and how much you harvest. For most home gardeners in SA, a light maintenance approach works best: remove crossing or dead branches in late winter before new growth emerges, thin the canopy to allow light penetration, and cut any frost-damaged wood back to live tissue in spring after the last freeze risk has passed.

If you want to keep a tree compact — useful for smaller yards or containers — summer pruning after the main crop harvest reduces vigor without sacrificing the following year's breba. Cut back the long whippy shoots by a third to half in late September.

Do not prune in fall before frost — that stimulates tender new growth that will be damaged by cold. Late winter is the window.

Why a Fig Tree Is a Real Estate Asset

Here's something I've observed from years of showing homes in San Antonio: a mature, productive fig tree in the backyard is a conversation-stopper for a certain kind of buyer — and that kind of buyer is becoming more common.

There's a growing segment of homebuyers — younger families, urban-to-suburban movers, people who have gotten into fermenting and preserving and food gardening — who specifically care about what a yard can produce. Not in a "I want a farm" way, but in an "I want to actually use my outdoor space" way. For these buyers, a five-year-old Celeste fig heavy with fruit in July is not a neutral feature. It's a reason to fall in love with a house.

I've seen it happen. The buyers come in, see the fig, and everything else about the yard looks better through that lens. The compost bins become intentional. The raised beds become evidence of a well-tended property. The existing irrigation becomes a bonus. The fig is the anchor that frames everything.

This isn't universal — not every buyer cares about edible landscaping. But for sellers in the $300,000 to $500,000 range targeting move-up buyers with families and garden interests, a mature fig tree is worth calling out in the listing description. Not in a precious way. Just: "established fig tree, two crops per year." Let the right buyer know it's there.

A fig tree doesn't ask for much. It asks for heat, a little water when it's young, and room to grow. In return, it gives you fruit, shade, and something to point to when someone asks what you've built here.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do fig trees need another tree for pollination in San Antonio?

No. The common fig varieties grown in Texas — Celeste, Brown Turkey, Texas Everbearing, LSU Purple — are parthenocarpic, meaning they produce fruit without pollination. They don't require a second tree, no bees are needed for fruit set, and there's no cross-pollination requirement. The Smyrna fig type, which produces some commercially dried figs, does require wasp pollination from a caprifig tree, but none of the backyard varieties commonly grown in San Antonio are in that category. You can plant one fig tree and get full production from it. If you plant two, you'll get more fruit — but the second tree is a pleasure, not a necessity.

Will a fig tree survive a San Antonio winter?

In most San Antonio winters, fig trees survive with minimal or no protection. Zone 8b and 9a winters are generally mild enough for the roots to survive even when the above-ground wood dies back. After a hard freeze (below 15°F), a fig tree may lose all of its above-ground wood and re-sprout from the roots in spring — this looks alarming but is not fatal. Celeste and Brown Turkey are the hardiest common varieties for our zone. If you've had a young tree die back after a cold snap, don't pull it out — wait until late April and look for green shoots emerging from the base. In most cases it comes back. To protect young trees in their first winter, a layer of heavy mulch around the base helps insulate the root zone during extreme cold events.

When do fig trees produce fruit in San Antonio?

San Antonio fig trees typically produce two harvests. The breba (first) crop ripens on last year's wood in late May through June — these are often larger figs but vary by variety in sweetness. The main crop, which is generally more abundant and sweeter, ripens from mid-July through September on current-season growth. The exact timing shifts by a week or two depending on variety and how hot the summer is running. You'll know fruit is ready when it softens noticeably, hangs down rather than pointing up, and the skin may begin to crack at the eye. A ripe fig also has a slight "nectar drop" at the eye in some varieties. Taste is the best test — underripe figs are starchy; ripe figs are sweet and jammy.

How much space does a fig tree need in a San Antonio backyard?

A standard in-ground fig tree in San Antonio will reach 10 to 15 feet tall and 8 to 12 feet wide at maturity if left unpruned. For most backyards, this means a single tree planted 8 to 10 feet from fences, structures, and other trees. If space is limited, figs respond very well to aggressive pruning — they can be kept at 6 to 8 feet in tree form or trained as an espalier against a south-facing wall, which also takes advantage of reflected heat. Container figs in large pots (25 gallons or more) can live productively for many years on a patio or deck, kept smaller through pruning and root management. The container approach is also the solution for HOA situations where in-ground fruit trees may be restricted.

Do fig trees attract pests or cause problems in San Antonio yards?

Fig trees are relatively pest-free compared to other fruit trees. The most common issues in San Antonio are fig beetles (the large green June beetles that show up in summer to eat ripe fruit), birds, and occasional spider mites in hot dry conditions. None of these typically require chemical intervention — fig beetles and birds can be managed by harvesting promptly when fruit ripens, and mites respond to a strong water spray. Fig trees do not produce significant litter problems beyond the fruit itself, which composts quickly. The roots can be aggressive in very old, large specimens, but a well-sited fig planted away from foundations and utilities is not a structural threat. Figs produce a milky sap that is a skin irritant for some people — wear gloves when pruning if you have sensitive skin.
