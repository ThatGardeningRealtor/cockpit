Earnest Money in Texas: What Happens to It If the Deal Falls Through

What Is Earnest Money and Why Does It Exist?

Earnest money — sometimes called an earnest money deposit or EMD — is a good-faith payment a buyer makes when submitting an offer on a home. It signals to the seller that you're serious, not just a tire-kicker who might disappear the week before closing. Think of it like planting a seed: it demonstrates genuine commitment before the full harvest of the transaction comes in.

In Texas, earnest money is governed by the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) contracts, which are the standardized forms your agent will use when writing your offer. The deposit doesn't go to the seller directly — it's held in escrow by a neutral third party, almost always the title company handling the closing.

The EMD is credited back to the buyer at closing, applied toward your down payment or closing costs. Its entire purpose is to put some financial skin in the game so both parties take the contract seriously from day one.

How Is Earnest Money Different From a Down Payment?

This is one of the most common points of confusion among first-time buyers. Your down payment is the large chunk of your own money going toward the purchase price — for a $350,000 home with 5% down, that's $17,500. Your earnest money deposit is typically a much smaller amount paid upfront when the contract is signed, weeks before closing.

At the closing table, your EMD gets absorbed into your total funds due — it's not paid twice. If you're putting 5% down on that $350,000 home, and your EMD was $3,500, you'd bring the remaining $14,000 (plus closing costs) to close.

How Much Earnest Money Is Standard in San Antonio?

In the San Antonio market, the typical earnest money deposit runs between 1% and 2% of the purchase price. On a $300,000 home in neighborhoods like Stone Oak, Helotes, or the Converse-Universal City corridor near Randolph AFB, that means $3,000 to $6,000. In hotter, higher-priced markets like Terrell Hills or 78209 zip codes, buyers sometimes offer more to stand out.

There's no hard legal minimum in Texas — technically, even $500 can be earnest money if both parties agree. But offering too little can hurt your offer's credibility, especially in a multiple-offer situation. Sellers and their agents absolutely notice.

During slower market periods, 1% is often perfectly acceptable. In a competitive scenario — say, a well-priced listing in the NEISD or NISD school districts that gets five offers over a weekend — bumping your EMD to 2% or even 3% can tip the scales in your favor without costing you anything extra at closing.

New Construction and Builder Earnest Money

If you're buying a new build from a builder like Lennar, Perry Homes, or Meritage in master-planned communities like Kallison Ranch, Alamo Ranch, or Teravista in Georgetown (for those commuting up I-35), the rules can be very different. Builders often require larger deposits — sometimes 2%–5% — and use their own contracts, not TREC forms.

Builder contracts are almost always more favorable to the builder, not the buyer. Having a buyer's agent review those terms before you sign is critical, and it costs you nothing since the builder pays that commission.

The Option Fee vs. Earnest Money: Two Very Different Animals

This is where Texas real estate gets unique, and where a lot of out-of-state buyers get tripped up. In Texas, buyers have the ability to purchase an "option period" — a negotiated window of time (typically 5 to 10 days) during which they can terminate the contract for ANY reason and receive their earnest money back in full.

To get that option period, the buyer pays a separate, non-refundable fee directly to the seller — this is the option fee. It's usually anywhere from $100 to $500 or more, depending on what's negotiated. That money goes straight to the seller, regardless of what happens. If you cancel during the option period, you keep your EMD but lose the option fee.

Think of the option fee as paying for the right to change your mind — like putting a hold on a rare plant at the nursery. The hold fee is gone whether you buy it or not, but it guarantees you the time to decide.

Why the Option Period Matters So Much

The option period is your due diligence window. This is when you schedule your home inspection, get your foundation evaluated (critical in San Antonio given our expansive clay soils), review HOA documents, and potentially negotiate repairs. A thorough inspector working in the 78230 or 78249 zip codes, for example, will often flag foundation movement, HVAC age, and roof wear — all things you need to know before your option period expires.

Once the option period ends without termination, you are contractually bound to proceed — and your earnest money becomes much harder to recover if you walk away.

When Do You Get Your Earnest Money Back in Texas?

The good news is that Texas's TREC contracts are written with multiple buyer protections that allow you to exit and recover your EMD under specific circumstances. Understanding those circumstances is the difference between a protected buyer and a frustrated one.

Situations Where You Typically Get It Back

Termination during the option period: As discussed above, if you terminate in writing before the option period deadline, your EMD is fully refundable. This is the cleanest and safest exit.

Financing contingency failure: If your loan is denied and you have a Third Party Financing Addendum in place (standard in most Texas purchase contracts), you can terminate and recover your EMD — provided you followed the process correctly and notified the seller within the required timeframe. Working with a reputable local lender like Frost Bank, USAA (widely used in San Antonio given the military community), or a trusted mortgage broker can help ensure your approval process is solid from the start.

Seller fails to disclose or remedy agreed repairs: If the seller agreed to make specific repairs and fails to complete them before closing, you have grounds to terminate and may recover your deposit. The specific language in your contract addenda matters enormously here.

Property doesn't appraise: If the home appraises below the purchase price and neither party can agree on a price adjustment, the buyer may be able to exit with their EMD intact — depending on how the appraisal contingency or financing addendum is structured.

Situations Where You May Lose Your Earnest Money

Once the option period expires, walking away from a contract without a valid contractual reason puts your EMD at serious risk. This is called a "default" situation, and Texas contracts give the seller the right to terminate the contract AND retain the earnest money as liquidated damages.

Common ways buyers lose their EMD: getting cold feet after the option period, failing to secure financing because of undisclosed debts or a job change, missing the closing date without extension, or simply deciding you want a different home. None of these are protected reasons under a standard TREC contract.

This is why I always tell my buyers: use your option period. Schedule that inspection within the first two days. Don't let the clock run out while you're still undecided.

Who Actually Holds the Earnest Money — and What Happens in a Dispute?

In virtually every San Antonio transaction, the earnest money is held by the title company — companies like Stewart Title, Fidelity National Title, Independence Title, or Alamo Title are all commonly used in our market. The title company acts as a neutral escrow agent, meaning they don't take sides; they follow the contract and the law.

The EMD must be deposited within the timeframe stated in the contract — typically 3 business days after the contract is executed (signed by both parties). Missing this deadline can technically put the buyer in default, so don't sit on that deposit once you're under contract.

If both parties agree to cancel the contract and sign a mutual release, the title company disburses the funds accordingly — usually back to the buyer. The problem arises when both parties claim the money. In that case, the title company is legally prohibited from releasing the funds without either a mutual written agreement or a court order. Disputes can drag on for months and may require mediation or legal action.

The Buyer's Agent's Role in Protecting Your Deposit

A good buyer's agent watches every deadline like a hawk. Option period expiration, financing approval deadlines, the closing date itself — missing any of these can cost you thousands. In my experience working with buyers across Bexar County and surrounding areas, the vast majority of EMD disputes happen when dates slip and nobody catches them in time.

That's not something that happens when you have an agent who's paying attention. Think of it like tending a garden — neglect it for a few days and things start going sideways fast.

Real Scenarios: What Actually Happens in San Antonio Transactions

Let me walk through a few real-world situations that come up in this market so you can see how these rules play out in practice.

Scenario 1 — The Cold Feet Buyer: A buyer goes under contract on a home in the Judson ISD area for $285,000 with a $2,850 EMD. They use their full option period to get an inspection but find no major issues. Two days after the option period expires, they decide they'd rather wait and look at more homes. The seller refuses to sign a mutual release. The buyer has no contractual exit at this point — the seller can claim the $2,850 and potentially sue for additional damages.

Scenario 2 — The Job Change: A buyer is under contract on a home in the Helotes area, under contract for $420,000. One week before closing, they accept a new job offer and their current employer pulls their verification of employment. The lender can no longer approve the loan. If the Third Party Financing Addendum is properly in place and the buyer provides written notice within the specified timeframe, they can likely recover their EMD — but it requires quick, careful action and documentation.

Scenario 3 — The Smart Option Period: A buyer is purchasing an older home in the Monte Vista Historic District. During the option period, the inspector flags significant foundation issues and an aging electrical panel. The buyer negotiates a $7,500 price reduction and an extended closing date to get contractor bids. Both parties agree. The buyer proceeds to close, fully informed, with their EMD protected the entire time. That's the system working exactly as it should.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a seller keep your earnest money in Texas if you back out?

Yes — but only under specific conditions. If a buyer terminates a contract outside of a protected contingency (like after the option period has expired, without a financing failure or other valid contractual reason), the seller has the legal right to claim the earnest money as liquidated damages under the TREC contract. However, the seller cannot simply take the money unilaterally — the title company requires either a mutual written release signed by both parties, or a court order directing disbursement. If the buyer refuses to sign a release, the dispute can end up in mediation or small claims court. This is precisely why understanding your deadlines and contingencies before signing is so important.

How long does it take to get earnest money back in Texas after a deal falls through?

If both parties agree to terminate and sign a mutual release, the title company can typically disburse the earnest money within a few business days — sometimes as quickly as 24–48 hours once the paperwork is received. The delay almost always comes from one party being uncooperative or slow to sign. In contested situations where neither party agrees, the funds can be tied up for weeks, months, or even longer depending on whether litigation is involved. Texas law does provide some remedies — a party who wrongfully refuses to sign a release can be liable for damages, attorney fees, and a statutory penalty — so there are consequences for bad-faith holdups. Your best protection is always having an agent who documents every communication and deadline meticulously throughout the transaction.

What is the difference between the option fee and earnest money in Texas?

These are two separate payments with entirely different rules, and confusing them is a costly mistake. The option fee is a small, non-refundable amount paid directly to the seller in exchange for the unrestricted right to terminate the contract during the negotiated option period — you lose it no matter what happens. The earnest money deposit, by contrast, is held in escrow by a neutral title company and is refundable under a variety of protected contingencies outlined in the TREC contract. The option fee is typically $100–$500+ while the EMD is usually 1%–2% of the purchase price. Both are credited toward closing costs if the transaction closes successfully, but only the EMD has meaningful refund protections built into the contract.

Does Texas require earnest money for a home purchase?

Technically, no — Texas law does not mandate earnest money as a legal requirement to form a valid real estate contract. A contract can be legally binding even with $0 in earnest money, as long as there is valid consideration (which is provided by the mutual promises in the contract itself). That said, in practice, virtually every offer in the San Antonio market includes earnest money because sellers and their agents view it as a measure of buyer seriousness and financial readiness. Submitting an offer without earnest money — especially in a competitive neighborhood like Boerne, Alamo Ranch, or near the STEM-focused schools in NEISD — would likely result in that offer being passed over. Even a modest deposit signals good faith and helps your offer compete.

What happens to earnest money if a home doesn't appraise in Texas?

This depends heavily on how your contract is written, particularly the Third Party Financing Addendum and any appraisal-related language. If your financing addendum states that your loan approval is contingent on the property appraising at or above the purchase price — which is standard language for most conventional and FHA loans — and the home appraises low, you may have grounds to terminate and recover your EMD if the seller won't reduce the price to meet the appraisal. However, if you agreed to an appraisal gap clause (where you committed to covering any gap between appraised value and purchase price), you could be on the hook to proceed at the contracted price or risk losing your deposit. In San Antonio's still-active market, appraisal gaps have come up in competitive offer situations, especially in zip codes like 78209, 78231, and 78230 — another reason having an experienced local agent reviewing every addendum is non-negotiable.
