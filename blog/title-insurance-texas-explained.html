Title Insurance in Texas: What You Are Actually Paying For and Why It Matters

By Chris Drawdy · May 13, 2026 · 9 min read

Title insurance is one of those closing costs that often gets glossed over—but it's quietly one of the most important protections you'll ever buy as a homeowner. In this guide, I'll break down what title insurance actually covers, the critical difference between owner and lender policies, and show you real scenarios where it's saved San Antonio buyers thousands of dollars.

What Title Insurance Actually Is (And Why It's Not Like Other Insurance)

Title insurance is a one-time payment that protects you against financial loss if someone challenges your legal ownership of a property. Unlike homeowners insurance, which protects against future damage, title insurance covers problems that existed before you closed—things nobody caught during the title search.

Think of it like soil testing before you plant a garden. You're not preventing problems; you're identifying and protecting against the invisible ones already in the ground. A title company does a deep historical search of the property, and if issues pop up after you own it, they'll defend your claim and cover losses.

In Texas, you pay this fee once at closing, and that single payment covers you for as long as you own the property. Whether you hold the home for five years or fifty, you don't renew, re-qualify, or pay additional premiums.

Owner Policy vs. Lender Policy: The Critical Difference

This is where most homebuyers get confused—and it's important, so let's be clear. Your lender will require a lender's policy, and you should absolutely get an owner's policy. They're different animals protecting different interests.

The Lender's Policy (Required)

Your mortgage lender requires a lender's policy to protect their financial interest in the property. If a title defect surfaces and someone has a valid claim against the home, the lender's policy ensures they don't lose their collateral.

Here's the critical part: the lender's policy only protects the lender, not you. It covers their remaining loan balance—so if you have $250,000 left on a mortgage for a $350,000 home, the lender's policy only protects $250,000 of your equity.

The lender's policy expires when your loan is paid off. Once you've eliminated your mortgage, you no longer have this protection.

The Owner's Policy (Your Real Protection)

An owner's policy is what actually protects *you*. It covers your entire equity in the home—today and forever. If a title defect is discovered five, ten, or twenty years after you buy, your owner's policy covers defending your ownership and compensating you for losses.

In Texas, an owner's policy costs roughly the same as a lender's policy, yet covers you completely while the lender's only covers their interest. This is a no-brainer—get the owner's policy. Many lenders will roll the cost into your loan, making it essentially interest-free.

Unlike the lender's policy, your owner's policy never expires. Your protection is as permanent as your ownership.

What Title Insurance Actually Covers

Title insurance protects against specific, documented title defects that existed before you closed. These include unpaid property taxes, mortgage liens, judgment liens, easement disputes, boundary issues, forged documents in the chain of title, and even homeowners association claims.

If someone shows up claiming they have a legal interest in your home—whether through a forgotten lien, a property line dispute with your neighbor, or a claim that a previous owner didn't have authority to sell—your title company steps in to defend you legally and cover the costs of resolution.

This coverage includes both the legal defense (attorneys, court costs) and any settlement or judgment awarded against you. In San Antonio, where properties sometimes have complex histories spanning decades, this protection is genuinely valuable.

Why It's a One-Time Cost (And Why That Matters)

You pay title insurance once at closing, and you're covered for life. This is radically different from homeowners insurance, property taxes, or mortgage payments—all things you pay repeatedly.

The reason it works this way is simple: title defects are historical. Either a problem existed before you owned the property, or it didn't. Once the title company has thoroughly searched the records and issued their policy, they're taking the risk for any hidden problems in that historical chain of ownership.

For a $350,000 home in Leon Valley or Alamo Heights, you might pay $2,000–$2,500 for both lender and owner policies combined—one payment that protects you indefinitely. Compare that to the ongoing cost of homeowners insurance or property taxes, and suddenly it looks like one of the smartest purchases you'll make.

Real San Antonio Stories: Where Title Insurance Saves the Day

I've seen title insurance prevent financial disaster more times than I can count. Let me share a few that matter.

The Forgotten Tax Lien

A buyer in Southtown purchased a beautifully renovated cottage they planned to flip. Three months after closing, the title company caught a property tax lien from 2015 that the previous owner's estate had overlooked during probate. Without the owner's policy, the buyer would have been personally responsible for that debt plus penalties and interest—over $18,000.

The title insurance company handled everything: they paid the lien, negotiated with the county, and the buyer never saw a bill. One-time payment; crisis averted.

The Boundary Line Surprise

A family bought a charming home in the North Star neighborhood with what they thought was a quarter-acre lot. When they decided to add a pool, a boundary survey revealed the previous owner had encroached 8 feet onto the neighbor's property decades ago. The neighbor suddenly demanded either removal of a portion of the deck or compensation.

The title policy covered the legal fight and ultimately negotiated a boundary adjustment agreement. The homeowners kept their deck; the title company ate the $12,000 in legal costs.

The Forged Deed

This one's rare, but it happens. A property in the Medical Center area had a chain of title going back to 1987. During a refinance, a thorough title search uncovered a forged deed from 1998 where someone had fraudulently transferred the property to cover a debt. The fraud had gone undetected for 25 years.

The owner's title policy covered the entire legal defense and settlement—protecting the homeowner from liability for a crime committed decades before they bought. That policy was worth everything.

What Happens If You Don't Have Title Insurance

Let me be direct: skipping title insurance is genuinely risky. You're betting that nobody will ever challenge your ownership, and in Texas real estate, that's a bet you might lose.

Without an owner's policy, any title defect discovered after closing becomes *your* problem entirely. You pay for your own attorney, your own settlement, or you lose the property. A judgment lien could wipe out your entire equity. A successful adverse possession claim could mean losing your home.

Even worse, most title defects take years to surface—long after you've poured money into the property, built memories there, and relied on it as part of your financial security. Without title insurance, that security collapses.

Your lender won't let you skip the lender's policy, but they also won't stop you from skipping the owner's policy. That's your choice—and it's one I've never seen a smart buyer regret protecting against.

How Title Insurance Works When You Actually Need It

If a title defect surfaces after you own the property, here's what happens. You contact your title insurance company and report the problem with documentation. They assign a claims adjuster who investigates your claim and determines whether it's covered under your policy.

If it's covered, the title company typically has two options: they can defend your ownership in court, paying all legal fees, or they can negotiate a settlement. Either way, you're protected. Most title companies have relationships with real estate attorneys and understand how to resolve these issues efficiently.

The process is confidential, usually fast, and handled by professionals. You're not managing a lawsuit; they are. You're not deciding whether to settle; they're advising you. It's their money on the line, not yours.

Title Insurance Costs in San Antonio and How Rates Work

In Texas, title insurance rates are state-regulated, meaning you can't shop around for a cheaper premium. The rate is essentially the same everywhere. For a $350,000 purchase, you'll pay approximately $1,400–$1,800 for an owner's policy and roughly the same for a lender's policy—or sometimes combined rates are slightly less.

That regulated rate structure actually *protects* you. It means the title company can't cut corners to undercut competitors. They're all meeting the same standard, backed by the same insurance reserves, following the same rules.

In San Antonio, major lenders like USAA Bank, Credit Union members, and conventional lenders like Chase or Loan Depot all require title insurance. The cost gets rolled into your closing costs or sometimes into the loan itself, so it's rarely a shock at the closing table.

Some title companies offer discounts if you bundle owner and lender policies, or if you're doing a cash transaction. Always ask—there's sometimes room to negotiate on title company fees beyond the insurance premium itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can title insurance be transferred if I sell my home?

No, your owner's policy doesn't transfer to the next buyer. When you sell, the new owner needs their own title insurance policy. However, the title company will research your policy when issuing the new one, and if your policy paid out on any claims, that's documented history that may affect the new owner's premium or coverage. The good news: the new owner's title company will build on all the work your original title search uncovered, making their policy cheaper and faster to issue.

What's the difference between a title search and title insurance?

A title search is an investigation—a title company digs through historical records to identify any defects, liens, or claims against the property. Title insurance is the protection that follows. The search finds problems; the insurance protects you if a problem wasn't found or if new claims emerge later. You always get a title search as part of the closing process. Title insurance is what covers you if something slips through the cracks.

Can I get title insurance for a property I'm buying with cash?

Absolutely, and you should. Even though a lender won't require it, you absolutely can—and should—purchase an owner's policy. In fact, cash buyers often benefit most from title insurance because there's no lender to require a thorough search. Some cash buyers skip the process entirely, which is risky. Get your own owner's policy and protect your investment.

Does title insurance cover my mortgage lender in San Antonio if I have a home equity line of credit?

Technically, no—your HELOC lender would need their own lien position protection, but title insurance itself doesn't directly protect against HELOC claims. However, your owner's title policy still covers you against title defects that could jeopardize your equity. The HELOC is a separate lien that sits in a specific position in the chain of title. If you're concerned about multiple lien holders, discuss this with your title company; they can sometimes issue endorsements that address specific situations.

What happens to my title insurance if I don't pay property taxes?

Title insurance doesn't cover your failure to pay taxes going forward—that's your responsibility. However, if someone *else* had unpaid taxes before you bought, and those were missed in the title search, your policy would cover that. Title insurance protects against past defects, not future obligations. Once you own the property, paying property taxes on time is entirely your responsibility (though many mortgage lenders pay them from escrow accounts, which simplifies things).
