Texas Property Tax Exemptions Most Homeowners Are Missing — Save Hundreds Every Year

By Chris Drawdy · June 1, 2026 · 9 min read

Most San Antonio homeowners are leaving hundreds—sometimes thousands—of dollars on the table every year by not claiming exemptions they're legally entitled to. Whether you're over 65, disabled, a veteran, or simply new to Texas homeownership, this guide walks you through every exemption available in Bexar County and exactly how to file for them.

The Homestead Exemption: Your Foundation for Tax Savings

If you own a home in Texas and live in it as your primary residence, you qualify for the homestead exemption—and this is the single most important exemption to claim. The homestead exemption reduces the taxable value of your home, which directly lowers your property tax bill, especially the school portion.

Here's how it works in plain terms: Texas appraises your home at, say, $350,000. Without a homestead exemption, that full $350,000 is subject to school taxes. With the exemption, the school district's taxable value drops to $280,000 (a 20% reduction is standard). That difference translates to real savings—often $400 to $800+ per year depending on your home's value and your school district.

The exemption also caps how much your appraised value can increase year-over-year for school tax purposes (capped at 10% annually), which is huge if you're in a neighborhood seeing rapid appreciation. If you bought a home in Stone Oak or Alamo Heights in the last few years, you know exactly what I mean.

How to File for Your Homestead Exemption in Bexar County

The process is straightforward, but timing matters. You'll file with the Bexar County Appraisal District (BCAD), not the county assessor's office—that's a common mix-up.

Step 1: Get the form. Visit the BCAD website (bexar.appraisal.org) or call their office at (210) 631-1700. You need Form 50-100, the Homestead Exemption Application. You can file online through their homeowner portal, by mail, or in person at their office near downtown.

Step 2: Gather documents. You'll need proof of ownership (deed or closing statement) and proof of residency (utility bill, driver's license, or lease). If you just bought your home, bring your closing documents. If you've been here a while, a recent property tax bill works fine.

Step 3: Submit before April 30. For a new home purchase, you have until April 30 of the year following your purchase to file for that year's exemption. If you're already a homeowner and somehow never claimed it, file immediately—you may be able to claim back years on your next appraisal review.

Step 4: Confirmation. BCAD will send you a letter confirming your exemption. Keep this with your tax records. Your exemption applies starting the next tax year, and it renews automatically each year as long as you own the home and it remains your primary residence.

I've seen homeowners in neighborhoods from Terrell Hills to Windcrest overlook this because they assumed it was automatic. It's not—you must actively claim it. If you're unsure whether you've filed, call BCAD with your address and they'll tell you in minutes.

The Over-65 Tax Freeze: Lock in Your Current Rate Forever

If you're 65 or older and own your home outright (or have a mortgage), Texas offers something truly valuable: the homestead exemption combined with a tax rate freeze. Once you file, your school district property tax amount is frozen at that year's level, even if your home's appraised value climbs dramatically.

This is different from the homestead exemption itself. The exemption reduces taxable value; the freeze locks your actual tax dollars. Imagine your tax bill is $3,200 one year. Even if your home appreciates and would normally trigger a higher tax bill, you'll still pay $3,200 (adjusted only for voter-approved tax rate increases, but not for home value increases).

For retirees on fixed incomes in established neighborhoods like Alamo Heights, Castle Hills, or Shavano Park, this freeze is pure peace of mind. You know exactly what your school taxes will be for the rest of your life.

Filing for the Over-65 Exemption and Freeze

You file this at the same time as your homestead exemption—same form (Form 50-100), same deadline. Simply note on the application that you're claiming the age 65 or over exemption. You'll need to provide proof of age (driver's license, passport, or birth certificate).

The freeze applies retroactively to the tax year in which you turned 65, so if you're 67 and just learning about this, contact BCAD immediately. They may be able to apply it to prior years and recover overpaid taxes.

One important note: the freeze applies only to school district taxes, not county or city taxes. But school taxes are typically 35–45% of your total property tax bill in most Bexar County jurisdictions, so the savings are still substantial.

Disability Exemptions: What They Cover and How to Claim

Texas provides property tax exemptions for individuals with disabilities, with one focused on homeowners and another for disabled veterans. If you have a disability that qualifies, you can exempt up to $12,000 of your home's appraised value from all property taxes (school, county, city, and any special districts).

To qualify, you must be determined to have a disability by Social Security Administration (SSA), the Railroad Retirement Board, or the Veterans Administration. You don't need to be a veteran; any qualifying disability works. The exemption is portable too—if you sell and buy another primary residence, you can transfer it.

This exemption stacks with your homestead exemption, which means you get both the 20% homestead reduction and the additional $12,000 disabled person exemption. For a homeowner with a $300,000 home, that's meaningful.

How to File for Disability Exemption in Bexar County

You'll file Form 50-100-D (Homestead Exemption Application for Disabled Persons) with BCAD. The deadline is the same: April 30 of the year following your application.

Required documents: proof of disability from SSA (your benefit letter works perfectly), proof of ownership, and proof of residency. If you've already filed a standard homestead exemption, let BCAD know—they'll add the disability portion to your existing exemption rather than requiring a whole new application.

Call BCAD at (210) 631-1700 if you're unsure whether your specific disability qualifies. They're helpful with these determinations, and the conversation is confidential.

Veteran Exemptions: Honor Your Service with Tax Relief

Texas honors military service with robust property tax exemptions for disabled veterans. If you're a veteran with a service-connected disability rated by the VA, you qualify for an exemption that scales with your disability rating.

Here's the structure: a 0% disability rating gets no exemption (though you might qualify under other programs). At 10-20% disability, you can exempt $5,000 of home value. At 30-40%, it jumps to $7,500. At 50-60%, you get $10,000. At 70% or higher, you can exempt up to $12,000. For 100% rated disabled veterans, the exemption is even more generous.

Additionally, if you're an unremarried surviving spouse of a veteran who died in military service or from a service-connected disability, you also qualify. These aren't "nice-to-have" benefits—they're substantial and often overlooked because veterans simply don't know they exist.

Filing Your Veteran Exemption

You'll use Form 50-100-V (Homestead Exemption Application for Disabled Veterans) with BCAD. You'll need your VA disability letter showing your rating percentage—this is the critical document. You can get a copy from VA.gov or by calling the VA directly.

The deadline is the same: April 30. If you're a surviving spouse, you'll need a copy of the veteran's death certificate and documentation of the service-connected nature of the death.

One clever point: if you're a disabled veteran age 65 or over, you might qualify for both the veteran exemption and the age 65 freeze. File for both. They work together to create maximum protection for your home's tax liability.

Protesting Your Property Appraisal: Win Fair Value for Your Home

Here's a truth that doesn't get enough attention: if you disagree with the Bexar County Appraisal District's valuation of your home, you have the right—and the power—to protest it. And homeowners win these protests regularly, especially in the current market when appraisals sometimes lag behind or overshoot reality.

Think of your home's appraised value like a garden that needs tending. If the BCAD appraised your home too high, you're paying taxes on inflated soil. A successful protest trims that back to reality.

You have 30 days from the date you receive the appraisal notice to file a formal protest. That notice typically arrives in April or May. Mark your calendar the moment you get it, because that 30-day window is fixed.

How to File a Property Tax Protest in Bexar County

Step 1: Gather comparable sales data. This is everything. You need recent sales of homes similar to yours in your neighborhood—same square footage, lot size, age, and condition. Look at properties that sold in the last 3–6 months. Real estate websites like Zillow and Redfin show recent sales; local MLS data (available through a realtor) is even better and more accurate.

Step 2: Document your case. Write a brief summary explaining why you believe the appraisal is too high. Reference your comparable sales. If your home has deferred maintenance, mention it. If the appraiser missed important details, note that. Keep it professional and fact-based.

Step 3: File your protest. You can do this online through BCAD's homeowner portal, by mail, or in person. The form is called the "Notice of Protest" and it's available on bexar.appraisal.org. Include your comparable sales data and your written explanation. There's no filing fee.

Step 4: Attend your hearing (if needed). BCAD will schedule an informal conference, typically within 30–45 days. Many protests are resolved at this stage—appraisers will review your data and sometimes agree to lower the valuation. If not, you can request a formal hearing before the Appraisal Review Board (ARB).

Step 5: ARB hearing (if necessary). If you go to the ARB, you can present your case in person or submit written evidence. The board is local and made up of community members, not BCAD staff. Bring your comparable sales data and be ready to explain why your home is worth less than the appraisal suggests.

A realistic success rate: homeowners win about 30–40% of appraisal protests, especially if they have solid comparable sales data. Even if you don't win a full reduction, you might get a partial adjustment—which still lowers your tax bill. The time investment is minimal; the savings can be real.

I've seen this work especially well for homes in transitional neighborhoods, homes with significant deferred maintenance, or homes that sold recently below the appraised value. If you bought your home six months ago and the appraisal is already 5��10% higher than what you paid, you have a strong protest case.

Bexar County Deadlines and Processes You Can't Miss

Timing is everything with property taxes. Miss a deadline by one day and you may lose an entire year of exemption benefits. Here's the Bexar County calendar you need to know:

April 30: Deadline to file new homestead exemptions, disability exemptions, veteran exemptions, and age 65 exemptions. This is non-negotiable. If you bought a home in 2025 and haven't filed yet, do it immediately.

April 30: Also the deadline to file a homestead exemption change notification if your circumstances changed (for example, if your home is no longer your primary residence, you must notify BCAD).

May–June: BCAD mails appraisal notices. This is when you receive your appraised value for the upcoming tax year. Read this notice carefully.

30 days from appraisal notice date: Deadline to file a property tax protest. Set a phone reminder the day you get the notice—don't wait.

June–August: Appraisal Review Board (ARB) hearing window. If you filed a protest, BCAD will schedule you during this period.

October–November: School districts and local entities set tax rates for the upcoming year. This affects your final tax bill calculation.

November–December: Tax bills are mailed. You'll see your final property tax amount.

January 31: Tax payment deadline (typically; always verify with Bexar County).

Pro tip: Set calendar reminders on April 15 and May 1 every single year. Tax deadlines creep up faster than weeds in a neglected garden, and the cost of missing one can be steep.

Special Exemptions You Might Qualify For

Beyond the big four (homestead, over-65 freeze, disability, veteran), Texas and Bexar County offer a handful of specialized exemptions worth knowing about.

Family Transfer Exemption (Agricultural/Biological Parent): If you inherited agricultural land from a parent and want to keep it in the family, you may qualify for agricultural exemption on the transfer. This is less common in urban Bexar County but relevant if you own land in the county's rural areas.

Charitable Exemption: If your property is owned by a charity and used for charitable purposes, it may qualify for exemption. This typically doesn't apply to individual homeowners, but if you own property held in trust for charitable purposes, investigate it.

Religious Exemption: Properties used exclusively for religious worship or education may qualify. Again, this is organizational rather than individual, but worth mentioning.

Exemptions for Historic Homes: Homes designated as historic landmarks in San Antonio may qualify for additional tax benefits. Contact the San Antonio Historic Preservation Office if your home is in a historic district (like Southtown, the Monte Vista Historic District, or King William).

For almost all homeowners, the homestead exemption is your starting point. The others listed above are icing.

Real-World Example: How These Exemptions Stack

Let's walk through a realistic scenario. Say you're 72 years old, a disabled veteran rated at 60% by the VA, and you own a home in the Northeast ISD area valued at $280,000. Here's what you'd file for:

Homestead exemption: Reduces your school taxable value from $280,000 to $224,000 (20% reduction). That saves you roughly $300–400/year on school taxes alone.

Age 65 freeze: Locks your school tax amount at the rate from the year you turned 65, preventing increases due to home appreciation.

Veteran exemption (60% rating): Exempts an additional $10,000 of home value from all taxes (school, county, city, special districts). That's another $150–250/year.

Total benefit: $450–650 per year in tax savings, plus the peace of mind that your tax bill won't increase as your neighborhood appreciates. Over 10 years, that's $4,500–6,500 you keep instead of sending to the tax collector.

And that's before any appraisal protests. If the original $280,000 appraisal was high and you successfully protest it down to $260,000, you're saving another $200–300 annually. This compounds.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I claim a homestead exemption if I have a mortgage on my home?

Yes, absolutely. You don't need to own your home outright. The homestead exemption is about your primary residence status, not your ownership type. Millions of Texans with mortgages claim homestead exemptions every year. Your lender (whether it's Chase, Frost Bank, USAA, or a local credit union) doesn't care—the exemption reduces what you owe in property taxes, not what you owe to them.

What happens to my exemptions if I sell my home and buy another one?

Your exemptions don't automatically transfer; you must file new exemptions for the new home. However, the process is quick—use the same forms (50-100, 50-100-D, 50-100-V) and submit them to BCAD at your new address before April 30 of the year after you close. If you have a veteran or disability exemption, those benefits stay with you personally, not the property—so reapplying is straightforward. You won't lose a year of benefits if you file promptly after closing.

How much does it cost to file a property tax protest, and do I need a lawyer?

Filing a protest costs nothing—there's no fee charged by BCAD. You don't need a lawyer, especially for the informal conference stage. You need comparable sales data, a clear explanation, and the willingness to show up for your hearing. Many homeowners successfully protest appraisals on their own. If you go to the formal ARB hearing, you can still represent yourself, though some people hire property tax consultants (not lawyers) who charge a percentage of the savings achieved—typically 20–40% of the first year's reduction. That might make sense for high-value homes with large appraisal overages.

If I live in an HOA community like Dominion or Stone Oak, does my homestead exemption still work the same way?

Yes, HOA status doesn't affect homestead exemptions one bit. Your homestead exemption applies to the school district and local taxing units regardless of whether you're in an HOA. The HOA fee is separate from property taxes—they're two different bills. File your exemptions the same way as anyone else. HOA homes often have higher appraised values due to amenities, which actually makes appraisal protests even more worthwhile if the appraisal seems inflated relative to recent sales.

Can my adult child who lives with me claim a homestead exemption on the same property?

No, only one homestead exemption can apply to any single property, and it goes to the person whose name is on the deed and who claims it as their primary residence. If the property is in your name and you live there, you claim it. If your adult child owns the property but you live there, your child claims it (assuming it's their primary residence too). You cannot claim separate exemptions on the same home. However, if your child owns a different property where they live as their primary residence, they can claim their own homestead exemption on that property.
