Growing Food in South Texas: The Best Vegetables, Herbs, and Fruit for San Antonio's Climate

By Chris Drawdy · December 9, 2025 · 10 min read

San Antonio's climate is a gift to food gardeners — if you know how to work with it instead of against it. In this guide, I'm sharing exactly what I grow in my own South Texas garden: which vegetables thrive in each season, which herbs laugh at our summer heat, and which fruit trees actually produce here. Whether you've got a sprawling backyard in Helotes or a raised bed on the east side, this is the practical, locally specific planting guide you've been looking for.

Understanding San Antonio's Growing Calendar (It's Not What You'd Expect)

The single biggest mistake new food gardeners make in San Antonio is treating summer like the main growing season. That works in Ohio. It does not work here.

Our summers are brutal — sustained weeks of 100°F+ heat that will cook most vegetable crops right in the ground. The sweet spot for food gardening in San Antonio is actually fall and spring, with a surprisingly productive winter window that most people completely overlook.

Think of our growing year in four distinct acts: a warm spring push from late February through May, a brief summer window for heat-lovers in June, a near-dead pause in July and August, and then our long, generous fall-into-winter season that runs from September all the way through January. Master that rhythm and you'll be harvesting food almost year-round.

The Two Planting Windows That Matter Most

Spring planting in San Antonio starts earlier than most gardeners expect — you can safely set out transplants of warm-season crops after February 15, and seed directly into the ground even earlier for cool-season crops. The Texas A&M AgriLife Extension office right here in Bexar County publishes a locally calibrated planting guide that I refer to every single season.

Fall planting — starting seeds or transplants in late August through October — is honestly my favorite time of year in the garden. The soil is still warm, the days are getting shorter, and crops like greens, broccoli, carrots, and beets absolutely explode with flavor once the first cool nights hit.

The Best Vegetables to Grow in San Antonio

Tomatoes: Our Most Rewarding (and Demanding) Crop

Tomatoes are the crown jewel of the San Antonio food garden, but they operate on a narrow window. You want to get transplants in the ground around Valentine's Day — no joke — so they can set fruit before the heat shuts them down in June.

Choose heat-tolerant varieties specifically. Celebrity, Solar Fire, Heatmaster, and the locally beloved Sweet 100 cherry tomato are all proven performers here. Heirloom varieties look gorgeous in catalogs but often sulk in our summer heat; if you want them, treat them as a spring-only crop and get them in early.

A fall tomato crop is also very much possible — start transplants in August for a September planting, and you can harvest well into November. I've pulled tomatoes off the vine in December in a mild year.

Peppers: The Perfect San Antonio Vegetable

If tomatoes are demanding, peppers are easy. They love our heat, they tolerate our soil, and they just keep producing from spring planting all the way through fall with almost no fuss.

Jalapeños, serranos, and poblanos are practically bulletproof here — plant them once and they'll reward you all season. Bell peppers are trickier because they need temperatures below 90°F to set fruit, so time them carefully for spring and fall windows.

I've had pepper plants in my garden survive mild San Antonio winters and come back as productive perennials in the second year. Throw some mulch around the base before our occasional cold snaps and you might be surprised what persists.

Greens, Brassicas, and Root Vegetables: Fall and Winter Stars

This is where San Antonio's mild winters truly shine. While our northern neighbors are buried in snow, we're out here harvesting kale, Swiss chard, collard greens, spinach, lettuce, and arugula from October straight through February.

Broccoli and cabbage both do exceptionally well as fall crops — start them from transplants in September and expect heads by November. Carrots and beets can be direct seeded in October and will sweeten considerably after a frost.

The only catch with winter greens is protecting them during our occasional hard freezes. A simple row cover or frost cloth (widely available at Rainbow Gardens on Bandera Road or Fanick's Nursery on St. Cloud) is all you need to protect most cool-season crops down to about 25°F.

Okra, Squash, and Southern Staples

Okra is essentially a native crop in South Texas — it needs absolutely no babying and will produce prolifically in the heat of June and July when almost nothing else will. Direct sow seeds in late April or May after soil temperatures are consistently warm.

Summer squash and zucchini produce fastest in spring, before squash vine borers arrive in force around June. Plant early, harvest enthusiastically, and compost what's left when the borers show up �� because they will show up.

Sweet potatoes deserve a special mention. They love San Antonio's long, hot summers and well-drained soil. Plant slips in May and harvest in October for one of the most rewarding and low-maintenance crops in the food garden.

Herbs That Thrive in San Antonio's Heat

San Antonio's heat is a liability for some crops and an absolute superpower for herbs. The same sun that fries lettuce in July concentrates the essential oils in herbs and makes them more flavorful than anything you'll ever buy at H-E-B.

Rosemary is practically a shrub here — plant it once in a sunny, well-drained spot and it will grow into a three-foot woody perennial that requires almost zero attention. I have a rosemary plant that's been in the same spot for six years. It just gets bigger.

Basil loves our summers but bolts quickly once temperatures drop — grow it from late April through September and preserve as much as you can going into fall. Mexican oregano (Lippia graveolens) is the locally adapted alternative to Italian oregano and handles our heat and alkaline soil with zero complaints. Thyme, sage, and lemon verbena are also reliable perennials in the San Antonio garden.

Herbs for the Fall and Winter Garden

Cilantro is the great San Antonio paradox: we put it on everything, but it absolutely refuses to grow in summer heat. The trick is to plant it in fall — September through November — and enjoy it through winter before it bolts in the warming spring.

Parsley, chives, and dill all perform beautifully as cool-season herbs in our climate. Plant them in fall and they'll often overwinter without any protection at all. I keep a pot of chives on my back porch practically year-round.

Fruit Trees That Actually Produce in San Antonio

Fruit trees are the long game of food gardening — the investment you make for your future self. The good news is San Antonio sits in a genuinely favorable zone for a wide variety of fruit production, as long as you choose varieties with the right chill hour requirements.

Chill hours are the number of hours below 45°F that a tree needs to break dormancy and produce fruit. San Antonio averages roughly 500–700 chill hours per year, though that number varies significantly from year to year — which is exactly why variety selection matters so much.

Stone Fruits: Peaches, Plums, and Figs

Peaches are San Antonio's most reliable stone fruit if you choose the right variety. Low-chill varieties like Tropic Beauty, Desert Gold, and the locally popular Sam Houston (developed specifically for Texas conditions) are your best bets. The Texas A&M Aggie Horticulture website maintains an updated list of recommended peach varieties for Bexar County.

Plums — particularly the Bruce and Methley varieties — are actually more forgiving than peaches in our climate and tend to produce more consistently in years when our chill hours run low. They're one of my top recommendations for first-time fruit tree growers in the San Antonio area.

Figs deserve a section of their own. Celeste and Brown Turkey figs are practically unkillable in San Antonio — they love our alkaline soil, tolerate our heat, and produce abundantly with almost no intervention. A mature fig tree in your backyard is one of the great pleasures of South Texas life.

Citrus: Yes, You Can Grow It Here (With Caveats)

Citrus is right at the edge of reliable production in San Antonio — we're in zone 8b, and most citrus wants zone 9. That said, cold-hardy varieties like satsuma mandarins (especially Owari), Meyer lemon, and kumquats will produce here with proper siting and some freeze protection.

Plant citrus on the south or southeast side of your home where they'll get reflected warmth from the structure and some protection from north winds. In hard freeze years like the February 2021 winter storm, even well-established citrus can suffer — but most will rebound from the roots if you protect the graft union.

Pomegranates are an underrated alternative for San Antonio gardeners who want fruit with a Mediterranean or Middle Eastern feel. Wonderful and Salavatski varieties thrive here, produce beautiful ornamental flowers in spring, and fruit abundantly in fall with absolutely no babying required.

Other Fruits Worth Growing in South Texas

Blackberries — specifically the Kiowa and Ouachita varieties — are extremely well-suited to Bexar County and surrounding areas. They need a trellis, minimal irrigation once established, and will reward you with pounds of fruit each spring.

Native persimmons (Diospyros texana) grow wild across the San Antonio Hill Country and make an excellent addition to any food-focused landscape. The Asian persimmon varieties Fuyu and Hachiya also do well here and are more palatable to people who haven't grown up eating wild persimmons.

Soil and Water: Working With What San Antonio Has

San Antonio's native soil is the biggest challenge food gardeners face here. Much of the city sits on caliche — a calcium carbonate-rich hardpan that drains poorly in some spots, bakes into concrete-hard layers in others, and tends toward high alkalinity throughout.

My honest recommendation after years of fighting caliche: build up, don't dig down. Raised beds filled with quality amended soil are transformative for food gardening in this area. A simple 8–12 inch raised bed with good compost, native soil, and expanded shale will grow vegetables better than almost any ground-level bed in our native caliche.

San Antonio Water System (SAWS) offers a rebate program for drip irrigation installation — a genuine gift for food gardeners who want to conserve water during our dry spells. Drip irrigation is also far superior to overhead watering for vegetable crops because it keeps foliage dry and dramatically reduces fungal disease pressure, which is a real issue in our humid summers.

Where to Buy Plants, Seeds, and Supplies in San Antonio

Supporting local nurseries is something I feel strongly about — they stock varieties specifically suited to our climate and the staff actually know what grows here. Rainbow Gardens on Bandera Road (north side) and Loop 410 (south side) are my first stops for transplants and specialty herbs.

Fanick's Nursery on St. Cloud Road has been a San Antonio institution since 1866 and has an exceptional selection of fruit trees. Milberger's Nursery on Broadway is another longtime local favorite with knowledgeable staff who understand South Texas conditions intimately.

For seeds, Barton Springs Nursery in Austin ships throughout Texas and carries an outstanding selection of heat-tolerant vegetable varieties curated for our climate. Southern Exposure Seed Exchange is another excellent mail-order source for heirloom varieties with strong heat performance. For supplies like drip tape, row covers, and soil amendments, the Bexar County Master Gardeners at the Agrilife Extension office on Nacogdoches Road host sales and workshops that are genuinely excellent resources.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I start my vegetable garden in San Antonio?

The answer depends entirely on what you want to grow. For cool-season crops like lettuce, spinach, broccoli, and carrots, the best times are September through October for a fall garden, or January through February for a brief spring window before it gets too hot. For warm-season crops like tomatoes and peppers, you want transplants in the ground around Valentine's Day (February 14–15) for spring, or in late August for a fall harvest. The Texas A&M AgriLife Extension office in Bexar County publishes a free, locally specific planting calendar that breaks this down month by month — it's one of the most useful free resources available to San Antonio gardeners.

What can I plant in my San Antonio garden right now in December?

December is actually a productive time in the San Antonio food garden if you started your fall crops in September and October — you may be harvesting greens, broccoli, carrots, beets, and herbs right now. For new plantings in December, focus on cold-hardy greens like kale, collards, and Swiss chard, which can be transplanted into the ground and will produce through winter with simple frost protection on our coldest nights. Garlic is also traditionally planted in December in South Texas — press individual cloves into the ground about two inches deep and you'll harvest bulbs the following June. Cilantro can still be seeded now in mild December weather and will establish before the coldest January nights arrive.

What fruit trees grow best in Bexar County, Texas?

The most consistently productive fruit trees in Bexar County are low-chill peach varieties (Tropic Beauty, Desert Gold, Sam Houston), Methley and Bruce plums, Celeste and Brown Turkey figs, and Owari satsuma mandarins planted on a south-facing wall for winter protection. Pomegranates and Kiowa blackberries are also exceptional performers that require very little input once established. When purchasing fruit trees locally, ask specifically about the chill hour requirement — Bexar County averages 500–700 chill hours per winter, so any tree requiring more than 800 chill hours will underperform here. The Bexar County Master Gardeners maintain a list of recommended varieties that's updated periodically and is available through the AgriLife Extension office.

How do I deal with San Antonio's clay and caliche soil for a food garden?

The most effective approach is to build raised beds rather than trying to amend native caliche soil in place, which is a losing battle for most food crops. Fill raised beds with a mix of quality compost (roughly 30%), native topsoil (30%), and expanded shale (40%) — expanded shale is a locally available soil amendment that dramatically improves drainage in heavy soils and is widely available at San Antonio landscape supply yards. For in-ground planting, till in generous amounts of compost each season and mulch heavily to prevent the surface from crusting into the hard cap that blocks water infiltration. SAWS also offers free compost through their program at the Dos Rios Water Recycling Center on Quintana Road, which is a genuinely valuable resource for any San Antonio food gardener working to build their soil over time.

Can I grow citrus trees in San Antonio without losing them to freezes?

Yes, with the right variety selection and site planning, citrus can be grown successfully in San Antonio — though it carries more risk than in the Rio Grande Valley or Houston. Satsuma mandarins (especially Owari), Meyer lemons, and kumquats are your best choices because they have the strongest cold tolerance among edible citrus. Site them on the south or east side of your home to benefit from reflected heat and wind protection from the north, and plan to cover them with frost cloth or bring container trees indoors during any forecast below 26°F.
