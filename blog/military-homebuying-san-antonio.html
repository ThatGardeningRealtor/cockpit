Military Home Buying in San Antonio: The Complete Guide for JBSA Families

By Chris Drawdy · April 28, 2026 · 11 min read

If you're PCS-ing to Joint Base San Antonio and trying to figure out VA loans, BAH, neighborhoods, and timelines — all while managing a move across the country — this guide was written for you. San Antonio is one of the most military-friendly real estate markets in the entire country, and with the right information, buying here can be one of the smartest financial moves you'll ever make. Let's walk through everything JBSA families need to know, from the moment you get your orders to the day you get your keys.

Why San Antonio Is One of the Best Markets for Military Homebuyers

San Antonio has the second-largest military presence in the United States, and that's not just a fun fact — it shapes the entire real estate ecosystem here. Lenders are deeply familiar with VA loans, sellers understand military timelines, and neighborhoods around JBSA have been absorbing PCS buyers for decades.

The market is also genuinely affordable by major-city standards. A solid three-bedroom home with a yard — the kind where you can actually put down roots, plant a garden, and let the kids roam — is still attainable on BAH in many parts of the city. That's increasingly rare in military markets like San Diego, D.C., or even Colorado Springs.

There's also a strong sense of community here that military families tend to appreciate. Neighborhoods near the bases are full of people who understand deployments, late-night duty calls, and the particular kind of resilience it takes to move every two or three years. San Antonio tends to feel like home faster than most duty stations.

Understanding Your BAH and What It Actually Buys You in 2026

Basic Allowance for Housing is calculated based on rank, dependent status, and the cost of living in your duty station's zip code. For San Antonio in 2026, BAH rates range from roughly $1,530/month for an E-4 without dependents up to $2,400/month or more for senior NCOs and officers with dependents. The key question is how that translates to a mortgage payment.

At today's VA loan rates — hovering in the 6.25–6.75% range as of spring 2026 — a $1,980/month BAH payment (E-5 with dependents) would cover principal, interest, taxes, and insurance on a home priced around $255,000–$270,000 with zero down. Bump up to O-3 with dependents at around $2,280/month and you're looking at homes in the $295,000–$315,000 range.

One thing many first-time VA buyers don't realize: your BAH is not taxable income, but lenders can count it toward qualifying income. A knowledgeable VA lender will know how to structure this properly so you qualify for the home you actually need — not a watered-down version of it.

BAH Pro Tip: Rate Protection Timing

If your BAH rate changes (promotion, PCS, dependent status), your housing payment stays the same — your fixed mortgage doesn't go up. That's a real financial stability advantage over renting, where landlords can adjust rent at every lease renewal. Many military families find that buying locks in a payment that actually gets easier to afford as their career advances.

How VA Loans Work in the San Antonio Market

The VA loan is the single most powerful home-buying tool available to service members, and San Antonio's real estate market is well-adapted to it. No down payment, no private mortgage insurance (PMI), competitive interest rates, and no prepayment penalties — those four features alone make it far superior to conventional financing for most military buyers.

The VA funding fee for first-time users putting 0% down is 2.15% of the loan amount. On a $280,000 home, that's $6,020 — but it can be rolled into the loan, meaning you truly can close with little to no money out of pocket. Veterans with a service-connected disability rating of 10% or higher are exempt from the funding fee entirely, which is a significant savings.

One legitimate challenge in the San Antonio market: some sellers remain hesitant about VA loans due to appraisal requirements and the (often overstated) assumption that they're slower or more complicated than conventional offers. Working with a realtor who has a track record with VA transactions — and who can communicate clearly with listing agents — makes a real difference in competitive situations.

VA-Savvy Lenders in San Antonio to Know

USAA, headquartered right here in San Antonio, remains a top choice for many service members and has deep familiarity with military financial situations. Security Service Federal Credit Union is another strong local option with competitive VA rates and in-house underwriting that can speed up the process. Veterans United and Navy Federal also have active loan officers working the San Antonio market who close VA loans routinely.

I always recommend getting pre-approved — not just pre-qualified — before you start touring homes. A full pre-approval with a verified Certificate of Eligibility (COE) tells sellers you're serious and ready to move, which matters especially when you're working against a hard PCS report date.

Neighborhoods Near Each JBSA Installation: Where Families Actually Live

San Antonio's four main JBSA installations each have their own surrounding neighborhoods, commute patterns, and price ranges. Knowing which one you're reporting to — and what your commute tolerance is — is the first step in narrowing down your search.

Near Lackland AFB (West San Antonio)

Lackland sits on the southwest side, and the neighborhoods closest to the gates include Westover Hills, Leon Valley, and Alamo Ranch. Alamo Ranch in particular has been one of the fastest-growing master-planned communities in the city, with homes in the $280,000–$380,000 range, good Northside ISD schools, and plenty of newer construction with warranties. The commute from Alamo Ranch to Lackland's main gate runs about 15–20 minutes via Loop 1604 and Highway 90, depending on traffic.

Leon Valley is a more established, slightly more affordable option — homes here often land in the $220,000–$290,000 range and have the benefit of being an independent municipality with its own city services. For families who want a larger yard and more mature trees (a gardener's delight), Leon Valley can offer character that newer subdivisions don't.

Near Fort Sam Houston (Northeast/Central)

Fort Sam sits near the Medical Center corridor and the northeast side. Popular neighborhoods for families stationed here include Converse, Universal City, and Schertz — all northeast of the installation with easy access via I-35 or Loop 410. These communities are squarely in the Judson ISD and Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City (SCUC) ISD footprints, both of which are well-regarded for families with school-age kids.

Schertz has become a particularly strong choice for VA buyers — it offers newer homes in the $270,000–$340,000 range, a small-town community feel, and a commute to Fort Sam of roughly 20–25 minutes. The city has invested heavily in parks and green spaces, which makes it feel less like generic suburban sprawl and more like an actual community.

Near Randolph AFB (Northeast, Universal City)

Randolph is located in Universal City, and its surrounding communities essentially overlap with the Fort Sam Houston corridor. Universal City, Converse, and Selma are the closest options. Selma in particular has seen significant new development along FM 78 and I-35, with home prices starting around $255,000 for newer three-bedroom builds.

Near Camp Bullis (Northwest)

Camp Bullis is in the far northwest Hill Country edge of San Antonio. Families stationed here often look at Stone Oak, Helotes, or even Boerne (just over the Bexar County line in Kendall County) for more acreage and a Hill Country lifestyle. Prices here trend higher — $330,000 to $500,000 and up — but the landscape is stunning and the Northside and Boerne ISDs are both excellent.

The PCS Timeline: When to Start and How to Move Fast

The military doesn't give you the luxury of a slow, leisurely home search. Most PCS buyers are working with 30–90 days from receiving orders to needing to be settled — and in that window, you need to find a home, go under contract, complete the VA appraisal, and close. It's entirely doable, but only if you start the right things immediately.

The moment your orders are confirmed, do three things: pull your Certificate of Eligibility (available through the VA's eBenefits portal or through your lender), contact a VA-experienced lender to start full pre-approval, and reach out to a local realtor who knows the military market. You can — and should — start this process before you ever step foot in San Antonio. Many of my military clients do their initial shortlist of neighborhoods and homes virtually, then make a final decision during a house-hunting trip.

Think of it like preparing a garden bed before the planting season actually arrives. You do the soil work, the research, the layout planning — so that when the window opens, you're ready to act decisively and nothing is wasted.

What to Expect Week by Week

Weeks 1–2: Finalize pre-approval, define your search criteria, begin virtual tours. Weeks 3–4: House-hunting trip to San Antonio, ideally 2–3 days of in-person showings. Week 5: Submit offer, negotiate, go under contract. Weeks 6–8: VA appraisal, inspections, underwriting. Week 9–10: Close, get keys. This timeline is tight but very achievable with the right team in place.

Texas-Specific Programs That Stack With Your VA Benefits

Texas has one of the strongest state-level veteran housing programs in the country: the Texas Veterans Land Board (VLB). The VLB offers its own home loan program with below-market interest rates specifically for Texas veterans, which can sometimes be used in conjunction with or as an alternative to a federal VA loan depending on your situation.

The VLB also offers a separate Land Loan program (helpful if you're interested in rural property or a larger lot) and a Home Improvement Loan program for veterans who want to buy a fixer-upper and build equity. These are underutilized programs that many out-of-state buyers don't even know exist until they're already settled.

Additionally, Texas offers a full property tax exemption for veterans with a 100% service-connected disability rating. For a $300,000 home with a 2.1% effective tax rate, that's roughly $6,300 per year back in your pocket — a number that should absolutely factor into your total cost-of-ownership calculation. Partial exemptions also apply for lower disability ratings and are worth verifying with the Bexar County Appraisal District.

What Military Buyers Get Wrong (And How to Avoid It)

The most common mistake I see military buyers make in San Antonio is rushing to new construction without fully understanding the timeline risk. Builders in popular military corridors like Alamo Ranch or Schertz can quote 6–8 month completion timelines that slip — and if your PCS date is fixed, that can leave you in a bind, paying rent while also paying a mortgage deposit.

Another frequent misstep is skipping the home inspection because they assume the VA appraisal covers it. It doesn't. The VA appraisal is a value assessment with Minimum Property Requirements — it is not a detailed inspection of the HVAC, roof, plumbing, or foundation. A $400–$500 inspection is cheap insurance on a $300,000 asset, and I always recommend it without exception.

Finally, some buyers underestimate Bexar County property taxes. At 2.0–2.3% effective rates, a $300,000 home can carry $6,000–$6,900 per year in taxes, which adds $500–$575/month to your PITI. This is especially important when matching your BAH to a payment — make sure your lender is using an accurate tax estimate, not a lowball figure that makes the number look prettier on paper.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a VA loan to buy a home in San Antonio if I haven't arrived at my duty station yet?

Yes — VA loans allow you to purchase a home before your PCS report date, provided you certify that you intend to occupy the property as your primary residence within a reasonable time (typically 60 days of closing). Lenders and VA underwriters understand military timelines and accept a signed PCS orders letter as documentation of intent to occupy. You can begin the pre-approval process, tour homes virtually, and even go under contract before your household goods have shipped. Many JBSA buyers successfully close on a home and move in during the same week they report for duty. Just be transparent with your lender about your timeline from day one.

What happens to my VA loan if I get PCS orders again in 2–3 years?

Your VA entitlement can be restored once you sell the home and pay off the VA loan, allowing you to use a VA loan again at your next duty station. Alternatively, if you have sufficient remaining entitlement (full entitlement was restored for most veterans under the 2020 Blue Water Navy Act), you may be able to carry a second VA loan simultaneously — commonly called "bonus entitlement." Many military families in San Antonio choose to convert their home to a rental property when they PCS out, which the VA permits after a period of owner-occupancy. San Antonio's strong military rental market — consistently in demand from incoming JBSA families — makes this a viable long-term wealth-building strategy. Speak with both your lender and a tax advisor before deciding between selling and renting.

Which San Antonio school districts are best for military families near JBSA?

Northside ISD (serving much of the Lackland and Camp Bullis corridors) is the largest district in San Antonio and offers strong academic programs, robust special education services, and a high degree of experience with military family transitions — including mid-year enrollment processes that are more flexible than many districts. SCUC ISD (Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City, near Fort Sam and Randolph) consistently earns high TEA accountability ratings and has a notably veteran-friendly culture given its community demographics. Boerne ISD and Comal ISD are both highly rated for families willing to live northwest or north of the city near Camp Bullis or Randolph respectively. The Military Child Education Coalition (MCEC), headquartered in Harker Heights, TX, publishes guides specifically on navigating Texas school transitions for PCS families. For families with children who have IEPs or 504 plans, the transition process between districts requires extra planning — give yourself at least a month of lead time.

Are there closing cost assistance programs for military buyers in San Antonio?

Beyond the seller-paid closing costs you can negotiate into a purchase contract (VA rules allow sellers to pay all of a veteran buyer's loan-related closing costs, up to 4% in concessions), several programs exist specifically for military buyers in Texas. The Texas Veterans Land Board offers reduced origination fees on its own loan products. Some San Antonio-area lenders — particularly credit unions like Security Service Federal Credit Union — offer lender credits or reduced fees for active-duty borrowers. The Homes for Heroes program provides cash-back rebates to active military and veterans through affiliated real estate agents and lenders, which can return $500–$2,000+ depending on purchase price. Additionally, certain nonprofit organizations like Operation FINALLY HOME and the Military Warriors Support Foundation provide grants and mortgage-free homes to combat-wounded veterans in the San Antonio region, though eligibility requirements are specific.

How competitive is the San Antonio housing market for VA buyers in 2026 — will sellers accept VA offers?

The San Antonio market in 2026 has cooled meaningfully from the frenzied 2021–2022 peak, and VA offers are being received much more favorably as a result. In most price ranges under $350,000, homes are sitting 15–30 days on average, giving buyers real negotiating room and eliminating the multi-offer chaos that once made VA appraisal contingencies a dealbreaker for sellers. The remaining friction points are typically around VA Minimum Property Requirements — older homes with deferred maintenance, peeling paint, broken windows, or roof issues may require repairs before the VA will fund the loan. One effective strategy is requesting a seller credit at closing to cover minor repairs rather than asking for them to be completed before closing, which gives sellers more flexibility. Working with an agent who can clearly explain VA appraisal requirements to listing agents — and frame your offer professionally — remains the single biggest factor in getting VA offers accepted.
