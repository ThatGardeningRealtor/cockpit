Buying a Home After Bankruptcy or Foreclosure in Texas — The Waiting Periods Nobody Tells You

By Chris Drawdy · August 11, 2025 · 9 min read

If you've walked through bankruptcy or foreclosure, you know that rebuilding takes time—much like waiting for a garden to recover after a harsh winter. This guide reveals the exact waiting periods lenders won't always explain upfront, the programs that might let you in sooner, and exactly what to do while you're waiting to own again in San Antonio.

Quick Facts
FHA Waiting Period (Chapter 7)2 years from discharge
FHA Waiting Period (Chapter 13)1 year from filing (with court approval)
Foreclosure Waiting (Conventional)7 years from sale date
VA Loan Waiting Period2 years from discharge (Chapter 7) or 12 months from filing (Chapter 13)
USDA Loan Waiting Period3 years from bankruptcy discharge
Texas-Specific BenefitTexas homestead exemption protects $420,000 in equity from creditors

Why These Waiting Periods Exist (And Why They Matter in Texas)

Lenders aren't being cruel by imposing waiting periods—they're managing risk. A bankruptcy or foreclosure signals financial upheaval, and lenders use time as proof that you've stabilized. Think of it like soil recovery: you wouldn't plant in scorched earth, but given time, nutrients return and growth becomes possible again.

In Texas specifically, the state's protections for homeowners are surprisingly strong. Texas has no personal income tax and strong homestead laws that protect up to $420,000 in primary residence equity from creditors. That means when you buy again in San Antonio, Austin, or elsewhere in Texas, you've got built-in protection that renters don't have—which is exactly why lenders eventually trust you again.

The waiting period is also the lender's way of seeing consistent paychecks, on-time rent payments, and responsible credit behavior. It's a test you pass by simply moving forward.

FHA Loans: Your Fastest Path After Bankruptcy

If you're filing Chapter 7 bankruptcy, the Federal Housing Administration allows you to apply for an FHA loan just 2 years after your discharge date. If you're in Chapter 13 (a repayment plan), you can sometimes apply after just 1 year from the filing date—with court approval and proof you're staying current on the plan.

Here's what lenders actually look for: that 2-year window doesn't mean "apply on day 731." It means lenders are watching how you rebuild during those two years. If you've missed rent once, late on a car payment, or run up credit card debt again, you'll be denied. If you've stayed squeaky clean, rebuilt credit to at least 580 (preferably 620+), and have steady income verified, you're in.

The clock starts ticking from your bankruptcy discharge date, not your filing date. That's the date you'll find on your discharge papers—the official moment the court released you from liability.

FHA Down Payment and Credit Score Requirements

FHA loans require just 3.5% down, which is huge if you're rebuilding. In San Antonio, where median home prices hover around $295,000, that's $10,325 down (plus closing costs). Your credit score needs to be at least 580, though 620+ gets you better rates and terms.

One detail many people miss: if you had a foreclosure on your record before the bankruptcy, the clock resets on the foreclosure waiting period separately. More on that in the next section.

Understanding Foreclosure Waiting Periods: Conventional vs. Government Loans

Foreclosure waiting periods are longer and stricter than bankruptcy periods—because from a lender's perspective, foreclosure looks like you walked away. Whether you lost Southtown, the Dominion, or a property in Boerne, the waiting period clock starts from the foreclosure sale date, not the beginning of the default.

Conventional loans require 7 years from the foreclosure sale date. FHA loans are gentler: 3 years from foreclosure sale if there were extenuating circumstances, or 7 years if it was just default. VA loans require 2 years after foreclosure with documented compensating factors (like increase in income), and USDA loans require 3 years.

If you had both a bankruptcy and a foreclosure, lenders will apply whichever waiting period is longest. So if your bankruptcy discharged in 2023 but your foreclosure was in 2021, you still can't apply conventionally until 2028 (7 years from sale).

What Counts as "Extenuating Circumstances" in Texas?

This is the loophole nobody advertises. If your foreclosure wasn't pure financial recklessness but rather triggered by job loss, medical emergency, death in the family, or other documented hardship, FHA might shorten the waiting period from 7 years to 3 years. Texas lenders know how to document these—medical bills, termination letters, obituaries—and it's worth exploring with an FHA-approved lender.

One San Antonio client I worked with had a foreclosure in 2021 after a spouse's cancer diagnosis and job loss. By 2024, with steady income in her new job and excellent payment history since, she qualified for an FHA loan with compensating factors. The extenuating circumstances clause saved her nearly 4 years of waiting.

VA and USDA Loans: Different Rules for Different Borrowers

If you're a veteran, VA loans offer a middle ground. After Chapter 7 discharge, you wait 2 years. After Chapter 13 filing (while still in the plan), you can apply after 1 year with VA approval. Foreclosure waiting is 2 years with documented compensating factors showing you've stabilized income and payment history.

USDA loans—available to rural and some suburban San Antonio properties—have stricter rules: 3 years from bankruptcy discharge, no exceptions. But USDA loans require no down payment and have low credit score minimums, making them valuable if you qualify geographically and have that 3-year window behind you.

The San Antonio metro area qualifies for USDA loans in areas like Boerne, New Braunfels, Blanco, and even parts of Schertz and Selma. If you're looking in those zones and can hit the 3-year mark, USDA is worth exploring with a local lender.

What to Actually Do During the Waiting Period

Waiting doesn't mean doing nothing. This is your soil preparation phase. The actions you take during the next 1–3 years directly determine whether you'll be approved at all—and at what interest rate.

Rebuild Your Credit Score Strategically

Don't ignore credit during this time; actively rebuild it. After bankruptcy or foreclosure, your score is likely in the 500s or 600s. Every month on-time with rent, utilities, and credit cards pushes it up. Consider becoming an authorized user on a family member's credit card with perfect payment history—this can add 50–100 points without hard inquiry.

Use a secured credit card ($300–$500 deposit) and use it monthly for small purchases you pay off immediately. This shows active, responsible credit use. Skip "credit repair" companies; they can't legally do anything you can't do yourself, and they often cost $100–$200/month for something that takes discipline, not magic.

Save for a Down Payment and Closing Costs

FHA lets you put down 3.5%, but you'll still need 5–7% of the purchase price when you factor in closing costs. In San Antonio, even a modest $250,000 home requires $10,000–$15,000 at closing. Start saving now, even $200/month adds up over 24 months.

Some lenders offer down payment assistance programs specific to San Antonio. The San Antonio Housing Trust Center and local nonprofits sometimes have grant programs for first-time buyers rebuilding after financial hardship. Check with the Bexar County area housing authority or local nonprofits—these grants don't need to be repaid.

Document Your Income and Employment Stability

Lenders want to see 2 years of continuous income history. If you're self-employed, keep detailed tax returns and business records. If you're W-2 employed, stay in the same field (changing jobs in the same industry is fine; moving fields raises red flags). If you've changed jobs, keep that gap explanation ready—"promoted within the same company" or "laid off for 4 weeks and immediately hired at competitor" is explainable; "quit to travel, then got hired 6 months later" is not.

Lenders pull your last 2 years of tax returns, 2 months of pay stubs, and verify employment directly with your employer. Make this bulletproof.

Keep Rental or Housing History Clean

If you're renting during the waiting period, pay on time. Lenders will verify rent payments with landlords. This is the most powerful proof that you've stabilized. One late rent payment during your waiting period can sink a loan application even if you meet every other requirement.

Conventional Loans: Why They're Harder, and When They Become Possible

Conventional loans (non-government-backed) require the longest waiting periods: 7 years from foreclosure sale date, and typically 4 years from bankruptcy discharge if you can show compensating factors. Some lenders will go down to 3 years if you have significant down payment (20%+) and near-perfect credit rebuilt since.

Why so long? Conventional loans don't have the safety net of FHA insurance backing them. If you default, the lender eats the loss. They're risk-averse and lean on time as proof of reformation.

The silver lining: once you hit the 7-year mark from a foreclosure, or 4 years from bankruptcy discharge with a 20% down payment and 700+ credit score, conventional loans offer better rates than FHA. You're no longer paying FHA mortgage insurance, and your monthly payment is lower. It's the long-game payoff.

Special Situations: Second Homes, Investment Properties, and Timing

The waiting periods above apply to primary residences. If you're considering a second home or investment property in the Texas Hill Country or Lake Travis area, the periods might be longer—sometimes 5–7 years even for FHA, depending on your equity position in the original property and lender appetite.

If your bankruptcy or foreclosure included an investment property alongside your primary home, lenders may view you as higher-risk. Be transparent about this early in the conversation with your loan officer.

One more timing note: if you're currently in Chapter 13 bankruptcy (still making plan payments), you cannot apply for any mortgage without court permission. Talk to your bankruptcy attorney before reaching out to any lender.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I buy a home while still in Chapter 13 bankruptcy?

Technically yes, but you need written permission from the bankruptcy court and your trustee. You'll file a motion to incur debt, and the judge must approve it. Most courts approve mortgages for primary residences if you demonstrate you can afford both the mortgage and your Chapter 13 plan payments. FHA and VA loans are more willing to work with Chapter 13 borrowers than conventional lenders. Contact both your bankruptcy attorney and a bankruptcy-experienced mortgage lender before assuming you're blocked.

Does filing for bankruptcy stop a foreclosure in San Antonio?

Yes—filing bankruptcy triggers an automatic stay that pauses all creditor actions, including foreclosure, immediately. However, the stay is temporary; lenders can request relief from the stay within days or weeks, and the foreclosure will resume unless you catch up payments or modify the loan through Chapter 13. In Texas, the foreclosure process is relatively fast (60–120 days), so bankruptcy delays but doesn't necessarily prevent loss without a solid plan to retain the home or reaffirm the debt.

Will I ever get a good interest rate after bankruptcy or foreclosure?

Yes, but not immediately. Right after your waiting period ends, expect 1–2% higher interest rates than borrowers with clean credit—if you're applying with a 640 credit score and FHA backing. However, after 4–5 years of perfect payment history (especially if you refinance during that time into a conventional loan), you'll eventually qualify for near-market rates. The key is proving you've learned and changed, which only time and consistent behavior can prove.

What if I had a bankruptcy discharged in one state but now live in Texas?

The waiting period follows you nationwide. A Chapter 7 discharge in California in 2023 means you can't apply for an FHA loan in Texas until 2025, regardless of where you are. However, Texas's strong homestead laws and lower cost of living compared to coastal states means once you qualify, you can build equity faster. Some lenders also view interstate moves positively—it shows you're building a new life, which is a compensating factor.

Can a co-borrower or co-signer help if I'm not ready yet?

Not in the traditional sense. If you're the one with the bankruptcy or foreclosure, you cannot use a co-borrower with clean credit to bypass your waiting period; the lender will require both borrowers' complete financial and credit history. However, a co-borrower can help once you're past your waiting period by strengthening your overall application (stronger income, better credit score, down payment contribution). A co-signer is different—they sign but don't appear on the deed—but most lenders won't allow co-signers on primary residence mortgages anymore.
