The $250,000 Tax Exclusion When You Sell Your Home — What Most Texas Sellers Do Not Know

By Chris Drawdy · June 18, 2025 · 8 min read

When you sell your home in San Antonio, you may owe federal capital gains tax on your profit — unless you qualify for the primary residence exclusion. This little-understood rule could save you tens of thousands of dollars, but only if you meet the IRS's 2-of-5-year requirement and understand what counts toward your gain. Let's walk through exactly how this works and what Texas sellers need to know right now.

Why the $250,000 Exclusion Matters to You

If you've lived in your San Antonio home for a few years and watched it appreciate — whether it's a charming bungalow in Southtown, a new construction in Boerne, or a family home near North East ISD — you likely have gained equity. The good news: the IRS lets you exclude up to $250,000 of that gain from federal taxes if you meet the right conditions.

This isn't a loophole; it's a deliberate policy designed to encourage home ownership and reward people who live in their primary homes long-term. Without this exclusion, a San Antonio couple selling a home they bought for $300,000 and selling for $600,000 would owe federal capital gains tax on the entire $300,000 profit — potentially $45,000 to $75,000 in federal taxes alone.

Instead, that same couple can exclude the entire gain because they're married filing jointly and eligible for the $500,000 exclusion. Understanding these rules now can make the difference between a life-changing windfall and an unpleasant tax surprise.

The 2-of-5-Year Rule: How Ownership and Use Work

The IRS requires you to own and live in your home for at least 2 of the 5 years before you sell it. That doesn't mean consecutive years — it means any 2 years within the 60-month window leading up to your sale date.

This is like tending a garden: you don't have to be there every single day, but you need to show consistent stewardship and presence. If you bought your home in North Central San Antonio in 2021 and plan to sell in 2025, you've easily met the 2-year threshold. But if you're considering selling a second property or an investment rental, the rules are much stricter — you cannot use this exclusion for investment properties.

The IRS also allows temporary absences, such as work assignments, medical care, or other unforeseen circumstances. If you moved for a job transfer out of state in 2024 but lived in your San Antonio home from 2021–2023, you still qualify. The key is that your primary residence must be the home you're selling, and you need that 2-of-5 ownership and use window in place.

Calculating Your 2-of-5 Window

To verify you're eligible, count back 5 years from your expected closing date and confirm you owned and lived in the home for at least 24 months during that period. If you're selling in June 2025, your 5-year window opened in June 2020.

Keep records of your utility bills, tax returns, voter registration, and driver's license address — the IRS may request proof of residence. Even if you rented out part of your home or had a tenant during part of your ownership, it doesn't disqualify you as long as you lived there yourself for the required time.

How Capital Gains Work: What Counts as Your "Gain"

Your gain isn't your selling price — it's your selling price minus what you paid for the house (your "basis") plus the cost of capital improvements you made during ownership.

Let's say you bought your New Braunfels-area home for $250,000 in 2019 and sell it today for $425,000. Your gross gain is $175,000. Since that's less than $250,000 and you're single, you owe zero federal capital gains tax.

But here's the critical part most sellers miss: you can reduce your gain by adding the cost of capital improvements to your basis. This is where record-keeping becomes invaluable.

What Counts as a Capital Improvement (vs. a Repair)

The IRS distinguishes between improvements and repairs. Repairs maintain your home's value; improvements add value or extend its life. A new roof, HVAC system, kitchen remodel, deck addition, or primary bathroom renovation all count as capital improvements.

Here's a practical example: if you spent $15,000 installing solar panels, a new fence, and upgrading your landscaping with native Texas plants and hardscaping, you can add that $15,000 to your basis, reducing your gain to $160,000. Repainting the interior, replacing broken windows, or fixing drywall typically do not qualify.

Paint touch-ups, small repairs, and routine maintenance never count. The test is whether the improvement adds substantial value, prolongs the home's life, or adapts it to a new use. When in doubt, ask your tax professional — but keep all receipts, contractor invoices, and before-and-after photos regardless.

Texas Homes and Improvement Documentation

In hot San Antonio summers, HVAC upgrades are common sense and count as improvements. Adding a pool, building a guest house, or installing a new fence in the Stone Oak or Helotes area qualifies. Even energy-efficient upgrades to windows, insulation, or doors — especially valuable in Texas heat — can be capitalized.

Keep a dedicated folder with all contractor agreements, permits, and paid invoices. Your title company or closing attorney can help organize these during the sale process, and your tax preparer will want them on file.

Why Texas Has No State Income Tax (But Federal Rules Apply)

One of San Antonio's greatest advantages is that Texas has no state income tax. This means you will never owe Texas capital gains tax on the sale of your home, regardless of how much you profit.

However — and this is crucial — federal capital gains taxes absolutely apply. When you sell your home and your gain exceeds the $250,000 (or $500,000 married) exclusion, the IRS will tax any excess gain at the long-term capital gains rate of 15% or 20%, depending on your total income for the year. Additionally, if your modified adjusted gross income exceeds certain thresholds, you may owe the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax.

Many Texas sellers mistakenly believe that no state income tax means no capital gains tax at all. This confusion has cost homeowners real money. Plan for federal taxes, work with a CPA before you list, and take advantage of every exclusion and deduction you're entitled to claim.

Real Examples: How This Works in San Antonio Markets

Scenario 1: The Young Couple in The Dominion. Sarah and Mark bought their home in The Dominion (North Central) for $425,000 in 2020. They've since renovated the primary bath ($8,000), upgraded the HVAC ($6,500), and added a stone patio ($5,000). Today it appraised at $625,000, and they're selling for $620,000. Their basis is now $444,500 (original $425,000 + $19,500 improvements). Gain: $175,500. They're married filing jointly, so they exclude $500,000 of gain. Result: zero federal capital gains tax.

Scenario 2: The Solo Seller in Southtown. James bought a charming home near Southtown in 2018 for $280,000. He installed a new roof ($12,000), replaced the HVAC ($7,500), and did substantial landscaping ($6,000). He's selling for $485,000. His basis is now $305,500. Gain: $179,500. As a single filer, he excludes $250,000 — but he's already below that threshold, so he owes zero federal capital gains tax.

Scenario 3: The Larger Gain in Boerne. Robert and Lisa bought their Boerne home for $380,000 and are now selling for $750,000. They added a guest house ($80,000) and renovated the kitchen and primary bath ($35,000). Their basis is now $495,000. Gain: $255,000. As a married couple, they exclude $500,000, so again: zero federal capital gains tax. Their improvement documentation saved them thousands by reducing their taxable gain in the first place.

Federal Tax Rates: What You Actually Owe on Gains Over the Exclusion

If your gain exceeds the exclusion amount, you'll pay federal long-term capital gains tax. For 2025, the rates are 0%, 15%, or 20%, depending on your filing status and total taxable income for the year.

A single filer earning under roughly $47,000 pays 0% on long-term gains. Between $47,000 and $518,900, the rate is 15%. Over $518,900, it's 20%. Married filing jointly has higher thresholds. Additionally, high-income earners may owe the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax on top of the capital gains rate.

This is why a CPA's guidance matters. If you're in a year with a lower income, or if you can stagger the timing of the sale, you might optimize your tax bracket and minimize your effective rate. Conversely, if you're already in the 20% bracket, minimizing your gain through accurate improvement documentation becomes even more valuable.

How to Prepare Now: Documentation and Planning Steps

Start gathering receipts and invoices for any capital improvements you've made — the more organized you are now, the easier your sale and tax filing will be. If you've lived in your San Antonio home for less than 2 years, understand that you'll forfeit the exclusion and owe capital gains tax on all your profit. Conversely, if you've been here longer, you're protected.

Meet with a CPA or tax professional 3–6 months before you plan to list. Walk through your basis, your expected sale price, and your improvements. Calculate your likely gain and your tax liability. Some sellers benefit from timing the sale in a specific tax year; others benefit from a 1031 exchange if they're buying investment property.

During the listing and sale process, provide your title company with copies of all improvement documentation. Work with an experienced real estate agent — someone who understands San Antonio neighborhoods and can help you price fairly so you maximize your equity without triggering unnecessary capital gains. Have a conversation with your lender or mortgage company too; if you have a large gain, understanding your after-tax proceeds helps with your next home purchase.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I claim the $250,000 exclusion if I only lived in my San Antonio home for 18 months?

No, you do not meet the 2-of-5-year requirement. You must own and live in the home for at least 24 months during the 5 years before sale. If you're short by 6 months, you'll owe capital gains tax on your entire gain (minus any valid deductions for repairs or selling costs). The IRS does allow waivers for unforeseen circumstances like a sudden job transfer, serious illness, or divorce, but these are exceptions, not the rule. Consult a tax professional immediately if your timeline is close.

If I rent out my San Antonio home for part of my ownership, does that disqualify me from the exclusion?

Not necessarily. You can still use the exclusion as long as you personally lived in the home for at least 2 of the 5 years before sale and it remained your primary residence for those years. However, if you converted it to a rental property (changed your primary residence elsewhere) for a significant portion of the 5-year period, the IRS may reduce your exclusion. Additionally, any depreciation you claimed while renting the property must be "recaptured" — meaning you'll owe a 25% capital gains tax on the depreciation portion, separate from the long-term gains rate. Consult a tax professional if your situation involves a rental period.

What happens if I'm married, but my spouse doesn't meet the 2-of-5-year ownership requirement?

If you file jointly, both spouses must meet the 2-of-5-year ownership requirement and the use requirement to claim the $500,000 married exclusion. If only one spouse meets it, you may only exclude $250,000 as a couple. If your spouse is a new homeowner (e.g., you married recently and added their name to the deed), they must have owned the home for 2 of the 5 years to qualify for the larger exclusion. This is a common issue when spouses have different ownership timelines; discuss it with a CPA before sale.

Do I have to pay federal capital gains tax on my San Antonio home sale if the gain is under $250,000 and I'm single?

No. If you meet the 2-of-5-year ownership and use requirement and your total gain is $250,000 or less, you exclude the entire gain and owe zero federal capital gains tax. The $250,000 is your full exclusion limit as a single filer; anything within that amount is tax-free. This assumes you haven't used the exclusion on another home sale in the past 2 years. Many San Antonio sellers in neighborhoods like Beacon Hill, Windcrest, and Castle Hills fall below the $250,000 gain threshold and owe no federal tax on their sale.

Can I use the primary residence exclusion more than once if I sell multiple homes?

You can use the exclusion once every 2 years per residence, but special rules apply if you own multiple properties. You can only claim the exclusion for one residence at a time — the one that qualifies as your primary residence. If you own a vacation home or rental property in addition to your primary residence in San Antonio, you cannot claim the exclusion on both. However, if you sell your San Antonio primary home in 2025 and later inherit a second property that becomes your new primary residence, you could potentially claim the exclusion on that sale after 2027. The rules are complex when multiple properties are involved; work with a tax professional to navigate timing and eligibility.
