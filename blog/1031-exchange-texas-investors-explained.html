What Is a 1031 Exchange and How Texas Investors Use It to Defer Capital Gains Tax

By Chris Drawdy · June 4, 2026 · 11 min read

If you're selling an investment property in San Antonio and facing a six-figure tax bill, a 1031 exchange might let you defer those capital gains entirely—and keep growing your portfolio. This guide walks you through exactly how it works, the critical 45 and 180-day deadlines, and real numbers showing how Texas investors build wealth tax-deferred.

The Foundation: What a 1031 Exchange Actually Is

A 1031 exchange is an IRS-approved strategy that lets you sell an investment property and buy another one without paying capital gains tax—as long as you follow very specific rules. Think of it like replanting a garden without losing the soil's nutrients: you're moving your wealth into new ground while keeping everything that's grown intact.

The name comes from Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code, which has allowed real estate investors since 1921 to defer indefinitely the federal income tax on investment property sales. In Texas, this is a game-changer because our state has no income tax, but the federal capital gains tax can still eat 15% to 20% of your profit depending on your bracket.

Here's the key: you cannot simply cash out and reinvest. The IRS requires a qualified intermediary to hold your sale proceeds, and you must identify and close on a replacement property within strict timeframes. Miss those deadlines by even one day, and the entire exchange fails.

The Two Critical Deadlines Every Texas Investor Must Know

This is where most people get tripped up, so I'm going to be crystal clear about how these timelines work.

The 45-Day Identification Deadline

From the moment your investment property closes, you have exactly 45 calendar days to identify the replacement property or properties in writing to your qualified intermediary. This is non-negotiable—weekends and holidays count.

You can identify up to three properties without any limit on value. Or, if you want to identify more than three properties, you can do so as long as their total value doesn't exceed 200% of the relinquished property's sale price. Some investors use this flexibility to hedge their bets.

For example: you sell a rental property in the Stone Oak area for $400,000. You can identify up to three replacement properties anywhere in the country, or up to $800,000 in total value across more than three properties. The clock starts ticking on your closing date.

The 180-Day Closing Deadline

You have 180 calendar days from your original sale closing to actually close on your replacement property. This is where the real timeline pressure comes in—you can't drag your feet on due diligence or financing.

The 180 days is your absolute outer limit, even if you identified the property on day 44. It all counts from your original closing date. In San Antonio's market, where properties move reasonably fast, this is usually workable, but it requires planning.

Both deadlines run simultaneously, and both are strictly enforced. If you close on day 181, or identify property on day 46, you lose the entire tax deferral.

How Capital Gains Tax Works (And Why 1031 Exchanges Matter)

Let me show you exactly what you're saving. Say you bought a rental property in San Antonio's Southside in 2015 for $250,000 and sold it today for $400,000. Your gain is $150,000.

If you took the cash and paid tax, you'd owe approximately $22,500 to $30,000 in federal capital gains tax (depending on your tax bracket: 15% or 20%, plus the 3.8% net investment income tax if you earn over $200,000 as a single filer). That money disappears—you can't reinvest it.

In a 1031 exchange, you defer that entire $22,500 to $30,000. You can reinvest all $400,000 into a new property. That's compounding power: the money that would have gone to taxes now stays in your portfolio, growing equity in real estate.

Over 10 years with modest 3% annual appreciation, that $30,000 in taxes you deferred could grow to $40,000+ in additional equity. That's the long-term wealth-building magic of 1031 exchanges.

Real Numbers: How a San Antonio Investor Builds a Portfolio

Let me walk through a realistic San Antonio example using actual market conditions.

Year 1: The First Property Purchase and Sale

You buy a duplex near The Pearl Brewery in 2018 for $380,000. You finance $304,000 at 4.5%, putting down $76,000. Over six years, you pay down $45,000 in principal and the property appreciates to $520,000. You sell it today.

Your gain: $520,000 (sale price) minus $380,000 (basis) = $140,000. Without a 1031 exchange, you'd owe roughly $21,000 in federal capital gains tax. You'd walk away with $195,000 in proceeds ($520,000 sale minus $304,000 mortgage payoff minus $21,000 tax).

With a 1031 exchange, you defer the $21,000 tax and have the full $216,000 in proceeds to reinvest ($520,000 minus $304,000 mortgage payoff).

Year 1-2: The 1031 Reinvestment

Within 180 days, you identify and close on a four-unit property in the Mahncke Park area for $480,000. You finance $384,000 (80%), putting down $96,000 of your own cash plus the full $216,000 from the exchange proceeds.

Your new basis is $480,000. The old $21,000 tax liability is deferred—it becomes part of your adjusted basis on the new property. You now own a larger, better cash-flowing property with zero tax hit on the transition.

Years 2-8: Rinse and Repeat

The four-unit property appreciates slowly (2% annually in this conservative scenario) to $585,000 by 2034. You decide to do another 1031 exchange into a small commercial property near Loop 410 with better growth potential. Again, zero federal capital gains tax on the transition.

By chaining 1031 exchanges together, you turn one duplex into a diversified portfolio—residential, small commercial, maybe a ground lease—all without paying tax on the gains between transactions. That's wealth compounding.

Which Properties Qualify? The "Like-Kind" Rule Explained

The IRS rules are actually more flexible than most investors realize. You can exchange a residential rental for a commercial property, a single-family house for an apartment complex, raw land for improved property—as long as it's real estate held for investment or business use.

What doesn't qualify: your primary residence, properties held primarily for resale (like a fix-and-flip), foreign property, or personal-use vacation homes. The property must be held "for investment" or "for productive use in a trade or business."

In San Antonio specifically, this opens doors: you could exchange a rental house in Alamo Heights for a commercial strip center near the Domain, or a duplex for acreage in the Hill Country if you're planning future development. The flexibility is one reason 1031 exchanges are so powerful for growing portfolios.

The Qualified Intermediary: Your Essential Partner

You cannot hold the sale proceeds yourself—not even for a day. The IRS requires a qualified intermediary (QI) to stand between you and the money. This person or company is not your realtor, not your accountant, and not your attorney. They're a neutral third party licensed specifically for 1031 exchanges.

The QI receives your sale proceeds at closing, holds them in a trust account, and releases them only when you've identified and are ready to close on replacement property. They're required to charge a fee, typically $500 to $1,200 depending on the complexity of your exchange.

In San Antonio, several qualified intermediaries specialize in local real estate exchanges. I recommend working with someone who understands Texas law and has experience with the types of properties you're exchanging. Your CPA or real estate attorney can often recommend a trusted QI they've worked with.

Common Mistakes That Kill 1031 Exchanges

After 11 years working with investors in San Antonio, I've seen every way a 1031 exchange can go sideways—and most of them are avoidable.

Missing the Deadlines by Not Planning Ahead

The biggest mistake is deciding to do a 1031 exchange after your property is already under contract to sell. You need to be thinking about this months in advance, not days before closing. When you're racing to identify a property in week six of the 45-day window, you're making rushed decisions.

Start conversations with a qualified intermediary before you list your investment property. Identify potential 1031-eligible replacements early. Have your financing pre-approved so when you find the right property, you can close quickly.

Not Reinvesting Enough of the Proceeds

Here's a sneaky trap: if you sell for $400,000 but only reinvest $350,000 in the replacement property, that $50,000 "boot" (leftover cash) becomes taxable. You pay capital gains tax on the lesser of your total gain or the boot received.

To defer 100% of your gain, you must reinvest at least 100% of your sale proceeds (after closing costs and realtor fees). The math has to work: sale price minus costs must equal or exceed your identified replacement property price.

Buying the Wrong Property Type or Location

Some investors get emotionally attached to an idea—a waterfront cabin, a property in another state where they're thinking about retiring—and realize too late it doesn't qualify as a 1031-eligible investment property. The IRS is clear: if it's not held for investment or business use, it's not like-kind.

Before you identify a replacement property, confirm with your CPA or tax attorney that it truly qualifies. A 45-day extension because you got this wrong will destroy your timeline.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I do a 1031 exchange with a property in San Antonio and buy one in another state or country?

Yes, you can exchange property across state lines—many Texas investors have done 1031 exchanges into Colorado mountain rental properties or Florida investment real estate. However, foreign property doesn't qualify. Your replacement property must be real property located in the United States to be 1031-eligible. If you're thinking about a second home overseas, that would disqualify the exchange. Stick to domestic investment property and you're fine.

What happens if I can't find a replacement property within 180 days?

The exchange fails, and you become liable for all deferred capital gains tax plus interest and penalties. This is why planning ahead and having a pre-identified list of potential 1031-eligible properties is so critical. If you're in month five and haven't closed, you're cutting it close. Some investors do a partial 1031 exchange—reinvesting what they can identify in time and accepting the tax bill on the portion they can't reinvest. It's not ideal, but it's better than missing the deadline entirely on everything.

Do I have to exchange into a more expensive property to avoid paying tax?

No—you can exchange into a property of equal or lesser value without triggering tax on the gain, as long as you reinvest all of your sale proceeds. However, if you sell for $400,000 and buy for $350,000, that $50,000 difference (the "boot") is taxable. You'll owe capital gains tax on the lesser of your total gain or the boot. In tax planning, sometimes a "down-exchange" makes sense if you're consolidating property or simplifying your portfolio, but work with your CPA first to understand the tax impact.

Can a spouse use my 1031 exchange to buy property, or do we both have to exchange?

Generally, each spouse must do their own separate exchange if both owned the property and both want to defer taxes. If you're a joint owner and one spouse wants to exchange while the other wants to cash out and pay tax, you'll need to split the property or use a TIC (tenancy-in-common) structure carefully. This gets complicated with community property rules in Texas, so definitely consult your real estate attorney and CPA before selling. They can structure your sale and exchange to minimize tax for both spouses.

If I've been doing 1031 exchanges for years, do I eventually have to pay the deferred tax?

The deferred tax follows your basis—it's built into the cost of the new property. If you keep doing 1031 exchanges, you keep deferring the tax indefinitely, which is perfectly legal and is exactly why these exchanges are so powerful for long-term wealth building. However, if you eventually sell a property and don't do a 1031 exchange, you'll owe all deferred taxes from all prior exchanges on that property's adjusted basis. If you hold the property until death, your heirs get a stepped-up basis and the deferred tax disappears entirely. This is one reason many long-term investors favor the 1031 strategy.
