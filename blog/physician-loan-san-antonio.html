Physician Loans in San Antonio: How Doctors, Dentists, and Vets Can Buy Without the Usual Rules

By Chris Drawdy · May 16, 2026 · 10 min read

You spent a decade in training, you have a six-figure income on the horizon, and somehow a conventional mortgage lender still looks at your student loan debt and says no — or worse, yes, but with a brutal PMI payment attached. Physician loan programs were built specifically to fix that problem, and San Antonio is one of the best cities in Texas to use one. In this guide, you'll learn exactly who qualifies, how student loans are treated, which local lenders offer these programs, and which San Antonio neighborhoods make the most sense for doctors buying their first home.

What Is a Physician Loan and Why Does It Exist?

A physician loan — sometimes called a doctor mortgage — is a specialized home loan product offered by select banks and credit unions that relaxes several of the rules that trip up high-earning professionals with heavy student debt. Standard conventional loans use your full monthly student loan payment in your debt-to-income ratio, which can disqualify borrowers even when their earning potential is exceptional. Physician loans were created because lenders recognized that a newly licensed MD with $300,000 in student loans is a fundamentally different credit risk than someone who simply overspent.

The core benefits usually bundle together in a way you won't find anywhere else in the mortgage market. No private mortgage insurance (PMI), reduced or zero down payment options, and a more flexible approach to student loan debt — all at competitive interest rates. Think of it like transplanting a well-rooted seedling instead of starting from bare seed: the foundational work is already done, the lender just needs to recognize it.

These programs are portfolio loans, meaning the lender keeps them on their own books rather than selling them to Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac. That's why banks can bend their own underwriting rules — they're not bound by government-sponsored enterprise guidelines for these products.

Who Qualifies for a Physician Loan in Texas?

Eligibility varies by lender, but most physician loan programs in San Antonio and across Texas cover a broader group of healthcare professionals than the name suggests. The baseline usually includes MDs (Medical Doctors) and DOs (Doctors of Osteopathy), but the list extends well beyond physicians in the traditional sense.

Degrees and Credentials Typically Accepted

Most programs accept the following credentials: MD, DO, DDS (dentist), DMD (dentist), DVM (veterinarian), DPM (podiatrist), OD (optometrist), PharmD (pharmacist), PA (Physician Assistant), and NP (Nurse Practitioner). Some lenders also extend eligibility to CRNAs, physical therapists with doctorate degrees, and even attorneys at certain institutions. If you hold a JD from St. Mary's University School of Law and your partner is a DVM at a Bandera Road animal hospital, you might each qualify for different specialized loan products.

Residents and fellows are specifically welcomed by many programs — this is a big deal because residency salaries at University Health or UT Health San Antonio typically run $60,000–$75,000 per year, which wouldn't normally support the purchase of a quality home in neighborhoods near the medical center. A signed employment contract showing your post-residency compensation is usually enough for lenders to underwrite against your future income.

What Lenders Are Looking For Beyond Your Degree

You'll still need a respectable credit score — most lenders want 700 or above, though some will work with scores in the 680s. You need to show you are currently licensed or within 90 days of starting a new position. Employment history gaps during training are completely acceptable because underwriters understand the residency and fellowship pipeline.

How Student Loans Are Handled — and Why It Changes Everything

This is the part that makes physician loans genuinely transformative, not just marginally better. On a conventional loan, your full student loan payment — or 1% of your total balance if you're in deferment — counts against your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio. A doctor with $350,000 in med school debt on an income-driven repayment plan might have a stated monthly payment of $200, but a conventional lender calculates it as $3,500. That kills deals.

Physician loan programs handle this in one of two ways. Some lenders exclude student loan debt entirely from the DTI calculation. Others use your actual income-driven repayment payment, even if it's very low or currently $0 during grace periods. Either approach dramatically improves your borrowing profile and can qualify you for a significantly higher purchase price.

To put real numbers on it: a physician earning $220,000 annually with $300,000 in student loans could be limited to purchasing a $380,000 home under conventional guidelines. That same borrower using a physician loan program might comfortably qualify for a $700,000–$800,000 home — a range that opens up entire neighborhoods in San Antonio that would otherwise be out of reach early in their career.

San Antonio Neighborhoods Worth Knowing If You're Using a Physician Loan

San Antonio's medical community is largely anchored in the South Texas Medical Center (STMC) corridor along I-10 and Loop 410 near Fredericksburg Road. University Health, Methodist Hospital System, Baptist Health System, and UT Health San Antonio all draw staff from surrounding zip codes. Where you buy depends on your commute tolerance, family situation, and honestly — how much outdoor space matters to you.

Close to the Medical Center

The neighborhoods of Leon Valley, Shavano Park, and the emerging areas around Braun Road put you within 10–15 minutes of the STMC without the highway congestion that plagues I-10 mornings. Homes in Shavano Park regularly list in the $550,000–$900,000 range — well within physician loan territory — and the lots are generous enough for serious garden beds, fruit trees, and the kind of outdoor space that actually restores you after a 12-hour shift. Northside ISD serves much of this corridor and consistently earns strong ratings.

If you prefer a more urban walkable feel, the Deco District near Fredericksburg Road and the Monticello Park area offer older charming homes with character-rich architecture. These neighborhoods are seeing renovation activity, prices still start in the $300,000s for updated homes, and the mature oak canopies alone are worth the commute savings.

Stone Oak, Alamo Ranch, and the Northern Suburbs

Stone Oak is the go-to for many physicians relocating to San Antonio, and for understandable reasons: North East ISD schools, newer construction, and a concentration of medical offices and specialty practices along Highway 281. The tradeoff is a 25–35 minute commute to the STMC depending on the time of day. Homes range from $450,000 to well over $1 million, and physician loans can comfortably cover most of that spectrum.

Alamo Ranch on the far northwest side near Loop 1604 and Highway 151 offers newer builds at slightly lower price points, typically $380,000–$650,000. It's popular with families in Northside ISD and has seen significant commercial development. For veterinarians working in the 78245 or Helotes area, this could be the sweet spot that eliminates a long commute entirely.

Boerne, Fair Oaks Ranch, and the Hill Country Edge

A growing number of San Antonio physicians are purchasing in Boerne or Fair Oaks Ranch — both accessible via I-10 West with commutes around 30–40 minutes to the STMC. The draw is acreage, privacy, and the kind of Hill Country landscape that genuinely feeds the soul. A half-acre to multi-acre property in the $600,000–$900,000 range is realistic here, and physician loans cover it cleanly. Boerne ISD has an excellent reputation and the town has its own small-town charm that's hard to replicate closer to the city.

Which Lenders Offer Physician Loans in San Antonio?

You won't find physician loan programs at every bank branch, and they're rarely advertised loudly. Part of my job as your real estate partner is connecting you with lenders who actually know these products, not just ones who've heard of them. That said, here are institutions with established physician loan programs that serve the San Antonio market.

Frost Bank, headquartered right here in San Antonio, offers physician and professional loan products and is known for local decision-making — a real advantage when your situation doesn't fit a standard algorithm. Flagstar Bank, TD Bank, and First National Bank of Texas (FNBT) all have physician loan products active in the Texas market. Laurel Road (now part of KeyBank) specifically markets to physicians and offers competitive digital-first experiences for busy residents who don't have time to sit in a branch for three hours.

Interest rates on physician loans typically run 0.125% to 0.375% higher than conventional rates for the same borrower — a modest premium for the flexibility you gain. On a $700,000 loan, that difference might be $75–$175 per month, which is far less than the PMI payment a conventional loan would require at a low down payment. It's worth running both scenarios with a lender who can show you the real numbers side by side.

The Buying Process: What's Different When You Use a Physician Loan

The actual homebuying process is largely the same as any other purchase — get pre-approved, find a home, make an offer, go under contract, close in 30–45 days. But there are a few nuances worth knowing when you're using a physician loan in a competitive San Antonio market.

Pre-approval letters from physician loan lenders carry the same weight as conventional pre-approvals with sellers, but you should make sure your lender can move quickly. San Antonio's housing market in desirable medical center-adjacent neighborhoods sees multiple-offer situations regularly, and a pre-approval that takes five days to issue can cost you the house. Ask your lender upfront about their timeline and whether they have experience with physician loan closings specifically.

One thing to watch: some physician loan programs have loan amount caps that vary by down payment tier. At 0% down, you might be capped at $750,000. At 5% down, the cap might extend to $1.25 million. At 10% down, some lenders go to $2 million. Know your target price range before you start shopping so we can match you with the right lender and loan structure from the beginning — like choosing the right seed for your soil before you ever break ground.

Texas-Specific Considerations for Physician Homebuyers

Texas has no state income tax, which meaningfully improves the effective value of a high medical income compared to states like California or New York. That advantage compounds when you're buying real estate — more of your gross income is available for housing costs. This is one of the reasons San Antonio and other Texas metros attract so many physicians relocating for residency, fellowship, or first attending positions.

Texas does have relatively high property taxes — Bexar County rates typically run 2.0%–2.5% of assessed value annually. On a $700,000 home, that's $14,000–$17,500 per year in property taxes, which is a real number to factor into your monthly budget. Texas does offer a homestead exemption (currently $100,000 of your assessed value) for your primary residence, which you should file immediately after closing. Over 65 homeowners and disabled veterans get additional exemptions that can dramatically reduce the tax burden.

The Texas Veterans Land Board (VLB) offers its own loan programs for veterans, including licensed veterinarians who served in the military. If you're a DVM who is also a Texas veteran, you may have access to stacked benefits — VLB loans combined with physician loan structures, or VA loans for the primary purchase with a physician loan used for a second property investment. It's a niche situation but worth exploring if it applies to you.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a physician loan in San Antonio if I'm still in residency or fellowship?

Yes — and this is one of the most valuable features of physician loan programs for San Antonio's large medical training community at UT Health and University Health. Most programs will accept a signed employment contract showing your post-training salary as proof of qualifying income, even if you haven't started the job yet. You'll typically need to close within 60–90 days of your start date, so timing the purchase to coincide with your final months of training is a common strategy. Some lenders will also use your current residency salary for qualifying and simply apply more generous DTI limits given your credential. Talk to at least two physician loan lenders before assuming you don't qualify based on your current income alone.

Is there a maximum home price for a physician loan in Texas?

Physician loans are not subject to conforming loan limits the way conventional mortgages are, which is one of their structural advantages. Because these are portfolio products that lenders keep on their own books, they set their own maximum loan amounts — which commonly range from $750,000 at 0% down to $2 million or more at 10% down depending on the institution. In San Antonio's current market, this flexibility matters most in premium neighborhoods like Shavano Park, Stone Oak estates, or Hill Country properties in the $900,000–$1.5 million range. Jumbo physician loans are available from select lenders for purchases above $2 million as well, though they typically require stronger documentation and reserves. Your best move is to get pre-approved at your realistic purchase range and verify the cap before falling in love with a specific property.

Do nurse practitioners and physician assistants qualify for doctor mortgage loans in Texas?

Many lenders have expanded their physician loan programs to include NPs and PAs, though coverage is less universal than it is for MDs and DOs — so you'll need to shop lenders specifically. In San Antonio's healthcare market, where advanced practice providers are in high demand across the Baptist, Methodist, and VA systems, this is a question that comes up frequently. When a lender does extend the program to NPs and PAs, the terms are usually identical: no PMI, student loan flexibility, and down payment options starting at 0–5%. Some lenders require NPs and PAs to have at least two years of experience in their field rather than accepting a future contract, so residency-equivalent flexibility may be slightly more limited. Institutions like Flagstar and certain credit unions serving healthcare workers have been among the more consistent options for advanced practice providers in the Texas market.

What credit score do I need for a physician home loan in San Antonio?

Most physician loan programs require a minimum FICO score of 700, though some lenders will consider scores as low as 680 with compensating factors like a strong employment contract and significant reserves. Unlike conventional loans where your rate improves significantly at each credit tier, physician loan pricing tends to be less sensitive to small score differences once you're above 720 — so obsessing over the last 20 points often matters less than people think. If your score is below 700 due to high student loan balances reporting as large individual debts, a credit counselor who understands the physician loan space can sometimes help you optimize quickly without requiring months of score-building. Hard inquiries from multiple mortgage applications within a 45-day window are typically treated as a single inquiry under FICO scoring models, so don't be afraid to shop multiple physician loan lenders at once. A strong letter of explanation for any unusual credit events during training — deferments, unusual payment patterns — is standard and expected by underwriters who work these programs regularly.

Can a veterinarian use a physician loan to buy a home with land in Texas?

This is a great question because it sits at the intersection of two important details: property type restrictions and credential eligibility. Most physician loan programs will accept DVM credentials, making veterinarians eligible on the credential side — though as noted, you should verify with each specific lender. On the property side, physician loans are designed for primary residences, and most lenders will finance a home on a modest acreage lot without issue, especially in suburban or semi-rural San Antonio zip codes. However, if the property includes working agricultural land, a barn classified as a primary structure, or income-producing livestock facilities, the lender may classify it as a farm or agricultural property, which requires a different loan type entirely. For veterinarians eyeing a home on 2–5 acres in the Helotes, Lytle, or Castroville area — which is very common — the key is ensuring the appraisal characterizes it as a residential property with land rather than agricultural use; your real estate agent should guide the process carefully from the initial offer stage.
