What "As-Is" Really Means in a Texas Real Estate Contract — It Is Not What Most Buyers Think

By Chris Drawdy · July 7, 2025 · 8 min read

Most buyers hear "as-is" and think they're buying blind—no inspection rights, no recourse, stuck with whatever's broken. That's not Texas law. Here's exactly what as-is really protects (and doesn't protect), why it's a negotiating position not a legal shield, and what you can still do during your option period to walk away clean.

The Myth: "As-Is Means I Can't Inspect"

When I'm walking a buyer through a property listing in the Alamo Heights school district or near the Pearl Brewery, they often ask: "Is this as-is? Does that mean I can't get an inspection?" The answer is almost always no—and this misconception costs buyers money and peace of mind every month in San Antonio.

Here's the legal truth in Texas: an as-is clause in your purchase contract does NOT remove your right to conduct a home inspection during the option period. The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) makes this crystal clear in their standard forms. Even if the seller marks the property "as-is," you still have your contractual window—usually 7 to 10 days—to hire a licensed inspector, dig into the roof, the HVAC, the foundation, the electrical panel, and everything else.

What as-is actually does is shift leverage after that option period closes. Once you sign off and your inspection deadline passes, you've waived future repair requests and your ability to walk away over newly discovered issues. But during those first 7–10 days? You own the right to look, learn, and decide.

What Sellers Are Really Saying When They Mark "As-Is"

In 20 years of working with buyers and sellers across San Antonio—from the North Star Mall area to the far North side near IH-35—I've learned that "as-is" is almost never about hiding problems. It's about position.

Sellers use as-is language to signal: "We're not going to negotiate repairs based on your inspection." That's legitimate. Many sellers have already priced the home to reflect its condition. They don't want to field demands for $8,000 in foundation work or a new AC unit after you've paid for an inspection.

But here's what sellers still have to do, even on an as-is home: they must complete the TREC Residential Property Condition addendum and any required lead-based paint disclosures. They must answer honestly about known defects—water damage, foundation issues, plumbing problems, structural concerns. In Texas, seller non-disclosure is a liability. If a seller checks "no" on a known roof leak and you find out post-closing, you have grounds to sue.

The key distinction: as-is doesn't mean "no disclosure." It means "we're not fixing it, but we're telling you about it."

Your Option Period Is Your Real Protection

Think of the option period like preparing soil before you plant. You wouldn't put seeds in ground you haven't tested, watered, or worked through—and you shouldn't buy a house without using those first 7–10 days to get a true picture of what you're inheriting.

During this window, you can:

  • Hire a licensed home inspector (typically $400–$700 in the San Antonio area)
  • Get specialized inspections: pest/termite, radon, pool (crucial in many North side neighborhoods), HVAC
  • Walk away from the contract for any reason—no explanation needed—and keep your earnest money
  • Renegotiate repairs or price based on findings (though on as-is homes, expect resistance)

This is non-negotiable. Even if a seller says as-is, never—and I mean never—waive your option period. If a seller refuses to grant an inspection period, that's a yellow flag worth listening to.

In competitive markets like we see every spring and fall near the Dominion or Boerne Hill Country areas, some buyers skip inspections to make their offer stronger. That's a gamble I don't recommend. An as-is house with a full inspection period still gives you an exit ramp. An as-is house with a waived inspection leaves you locked in with no recourse.

The Post-Option-Period Reality: When As-Is Becomes Binding

Once your option period ends and you've signed off on the inspection, that as-is clause locks in tight. You're now saying, "I've seen what I need to see, and I'm moving forward no matter what."

This matters because inspections aren't perfect. A good inspector might miss early termite damage in the crawlspace. They won't break into walls to see hidden plumbing. They can't predict when a 15-year-old HVAC system will fail—they just report its age. After the option period closes, these discoveries are on you.

In San Antonio's older neighborhoods—Southtown, Olmos Park, Alamo Heights, even some parts of the North Central area—you'll find houses built in the 1960s and 1970s with original systems. An as-is purchase on one of these homes after option period closes means you own full responsibility for the roof, the plumbing, the electrical panel, everything. There's no "surprise" repair clause.

That's why the inspection period is so critical. You need a thorough, professional inspection—and you need to hire a qualified inspector who will actually climb on the roof and test systems, not just walk through with a clipboard.

Disclosure vs. As-Is: Two Different Things

This is where many buyers get confused, and I've spent countless hours clarifying it over coffee or at the kitchen table of a home in Windcrest or Leon Valley. Disclosure and as-is are separate legal concepts.

Disclosure is what the seller must tell you about the property's condition. Texas law requires it, TREC forms demand it, and sellers who lie on disclosures face legal consequences.

As-is is what happens when the seller says, "I'm telling you the condition, but I'm not fixing anything." Those are two different statements.

Example: A seller in the Stone Oak area marks their property as-is, but they must still disclose that the foundation has visible cracks and that they've noticed water seepage in the basement. They don't have to repair it. But they do have to tell you it exists. If they don't disclose it, you have a claim even on an as-is home.

Conversely, if a seller discloses a problem and you proceed with the purchase anyway (or your option period expires without terminating), you've accepted the home in that condition. The as-is clause then protects the seller from post-closing repair demands.

Why Lenders Care About As-Is Purchases

Before you get too comfortable with an as-is offer, talk to your lender. Most conventional lenders in Texas—and mortgage brokers here in San Antonio like those with Guaranteed Rate, Chase, or USAA—will still require a full appraisal and sometimes a separate inspection for FHA or VA loans.

If the appraisal reveals major defects that affect the home's value, your lender might lower the appraised value or require repairs before closing. An as-is agreement with the seller doesn't override the lender's requirements. You could find yourself in a position where the seller refuses to fix anything, but the bank won't lend until certain items are addressed.

This is another reason to work with an experienced local real estate agent who understands both the seller's position and the lender's requirements. In my experience, communication between all three parties—buyer, seller, lender—early in the process prevents heartbreak at the closing table.

As-Is in Different San Antonio Neighborhoods: What to Expect

The prevalence and meaning of as-is varies significantly across San Antonio's diverse markets.

Older, In-Town Neighborhoods (Southtown, Olmos Park, Alamo Heights, Tobin Hill)

Here, as-is is common because many homes were built 50+ years ago and carry deferred maintenance. Buyers expect it and price accordingly. However, the inspection period becomes more critical—you're not just looking for cosmetic issues, you're assessing the integrity of old systems and structural components.

Newer Suburbs (Stone Oak, Boerne Hill Country, The Dominion, Castle Hills)

As-is is less common in newer developments because homes often come with builder warranties and are in better structural condition. When you do see as-is here, it's usually a red flag—something's driven the seller to that position, and your inspection should dig deeper.

Investor-Owned or REO Properties

Banks and institutional sellers often list properties as-is. They're legally obligated to disclose known conditions, but they're not interested in renegotiating repairs. These deals move fast, and your option period is precious.

The Negotiation: How Smart Buyers Handle As-Is Listings

If you find a home you love but it's listed as-is, you're not powerless. Here's how I approach it:

During the Offer Stage

Don't waive your inspection rights. Offer a competitive price, but keep your 7–10 day option period intact. If the seller demands a waived inspection, ask why—and consider walking away if the reason isn't clear.

During the Inspection

Get a thorough inspection. If significant issues emerge, you have two realistic paths: walk away (which is why you preserved the option period), or use the findings to renegotiate the price downward. Most as-is sellers won't repair, but they might accept a lower offer if the inspection reveals $15,000 in issues.

Closing the Deal

Make sure your title company clearly explains your post-closing position. Once you sign, as-is means as-is. There's no coming back.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I back out of an as-is home purchase before closing if I find major problems?

Only if your option period is still open. Once your inspection deadline passes and you've agreed to terminate option period, you're contractually bound even if problems emerge later. This is why using your full option period is critical—7 to 10 days gives you legal grounds to walk away penalty-free if the inspection reveals costly issues. After option period closes, your only remedy would be a lawsuit based on non-disclosure, which is expensive and difficult to win.

What does it mean if a home is listed as-is but the seller still has to make repairs?

It means your contract's terms override the listing status. If your offer includes a repair request and the seller agrees (perhaps to lower the price instead), they're obligated to complete it before closing, regardless of the "as-is" label. As-is is only binding if you accepted the home in that condition during option period. Always read the actual contract—the listing description is less important than what you and the seller signed.

Do FHA and VA loans allow as-is purchases in San Antonio?

FHA and VA loans are stricter than conventional loans about property condition. The government lender will require certain repairs—electrical hazards, structural defects, plumbing issues—before they'll fund the loan, even if the home is listed as-is. This means you could make an offer on an as-is home only to discover the VA appraiser requires $5,000 in repairs that the seller won't do. Always discuss as-is purchases with your lender first if you're using FHA or VA financing.

Is an as-is home a bad investment compared to a home with repairs included?

Not necessarily—it depends on the price, the home's actual condition, and your risk tolerance. Many as-is homes are priced lower to reflect their condition and the buyer's responsibility for repairs. If you inspect thoroughly and price accordingly, an as-is purchase can be a smart deal. The risk comes when buyers rush or skip inspection, or when they're emotionally attached to a home and ignore red flags. Knowledge and honesty about the home's true condition are what matter.

What happens if the seller lied on the disclosure form for an as-is home?

Non-disclosure is fraud, and the as-is clause doesn't protect a seller who lied. If a seller checked "no" on water damage but you find mold and water stains in the crawlspace post-closing, you can pursue a legal claim against the seller for misrepresentation. This is why hiring a thorough inspector during your option period is so important—you'll catch these issues while you still have the right to walk away. After the option period, proving fraud requires lawyers and is costly.
