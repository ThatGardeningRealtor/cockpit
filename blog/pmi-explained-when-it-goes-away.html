PMI: What It Costs, When It Starts, and Exactly How to Get Rid of It

By Chris Drawdy · May 27, 2026 · 9 min read

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) can feel like an invisible tax on your home purchase—but it doesn't have to be permanent. In this guide, I'll walk you through exactly what PMI is, what it costs here in San Antonio, when it automatically disappears, and the concrete steps you can take to eliminate it early.

What Is PMI and Why Does Your Lender Require It?

PMI—Private Mortgage Insurance—is a monthly or annual insurance premium that protects your lender, not you, if you default on your loan. Think of it like a safety net that lenders use when you haven't put down 20% of the home's purchase price.

The logic is straightforward: if you're borrowing more than 80% of a home's value, the lender carries greater risk. PMI compensates them for that risk, allowing them to offer mortgages to qualified buyers who haven't saved a massive down payment yet.

Here's the key insight: PMI protects the lender's investment, not your equity or your home. You're paying for insurance that benefits the bank, which is why eliminating it as soon as possible should be a priority in your mortgage strategy.

How Much Does PMI Cost in San Antonio?

PMI costs vary based on your credit score, loan type, down payment percentage, and loan amount—but there's a predictable range. Most San Antonio buyers pay between 0.5% and 1.5% of their annual loan balance in PMI, which breaks down to roughly $500–$1,500 per $100,000 borrowed each year.

Let's use a realistic San Antonio example. The median home price here hovers around $365,000 as of 2026. If you purchase a home at that price with a 10% down payment ($36,500), you're financing $328,500.

At 0.8% PMI annually (a reasonable middle estimate for a buyer with a 680+ credit score), you'd pay approximately $2,628 per year, or $219 per month. Over a decade before PMI drops off, that's nearly $26,000 in additional costs—money that's gone forever.

If your credit score is lower (say, 620–640), PMI climbs closer to 1.5%, pushing monthly costs to $410 and ten-year costs past $49,000. Conversely, buyers with excellent credit (760+) and a 15% down payment might see PMI as low as $150–$180 monthly.

Major San Antonio lenders like USAA, Frost Bank, and Comerica all price PMI competitively, but rates still swing 30–50 basis points depending on your profile. That difference of $50–$100 monthly compounds to thousands over time, so shopping around matters.

When Does PMI Get Added to Your Mortgage?

PMI isn't added the moment you sign papers—it's calculated into your loan at origination and typically appears in your first mortgage payment or is added to your loan balance.

Most lenders roll PMI into your monthly payment, so you'll see it itemized on your loan estimate and closing disclosure. Some loans allow upfront PMI (a one-time insurance premium paid at closing), but that's rarer in today's market and usually not the better choice since it increases your financed amount.

The monthly approach is standard: your payment includes principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and PMI all bundled together. When you receive your mortgage statement, you might not see PMI broken out separately—it's just part of your total payment—which is why many homeowners don't realize how much they're actually paying for it.

When Does PMI Automatically Disappear?

PMI is not a life sentence. Federal law requires your lender to automatically cancel PMI once you've built 20% equity in your home, which typically happens when your loan balance reaches 80% of the original home's purchase price (called 80% LTV, or loan-to-value ratio).

Here's the practical timeline: if you put 10% down on a $365,000 San Antonio home, you need to pay down your mortgage from $328,500 to $292,000—a reduction of about $36,500. Depending on your interest rate and loan term, that takes 8–12 years of regular payments.

However—and this is crucial—your lender doesn't automatically cancel PMI at 80% LTV unless you explicitly request it. They'll wait until you reach 78% LTV (meaning you've paid down 22% equity) before they're legally required to remove it without your request. But why wait? You can and should request cancellation at 80%.

One major caveat: the 20% equity threshold is based on the original purchase price, not current market value. So if your San Antonio home appreciates significantly (and many do, especially in neighborhoods like Castle Hills and the North Central corridor), you could reach 20% equity much faster through appreciation alone—though most buyers still need to rely on payments.

Timeline Factors That Speed Up PMI Removal

Your loan type matters. A 15-year mortgage reaches 80% LTV much faster than a 30-year loan because you're paying principal more aggressively. If you're putting down 10% on a 15-year loan, PMI could vanish in just 5–6 years.

Extra principal payments also accelerate the timeline. Even an extra $100–$200 per month can shave years off PMI and save you thousands in interest. Some San Antonio homeowners use tax refunds or bonuses to make annual lump-sum payments, which compounds the effect.

Home appreciation, while unpredictable, can help. If your $365,000 San Antonio home appreciates 5% per year to $400,000+, your equity grows faster even if your loan balance stays the same. However, you shouldn't count on appreciation alone—it's a bonus, not a plan.

How to Request Early Cancellation of PMI

The moment you've built 20% equity, contact your lender and request PMI cancellation in writing. Don't assume they'll tell you—it's on you to initiate.

Here's what you'll need to provide: proof of current home value (typically an appraisal, though some lenders accept comparable sales or automated valuation models), documentation that you're current on all payments (no late payments in the past 24 months), and verification of your original loan amount and down payment percentage.

The process usually takes 30–45 days. Your lender will order an appraisal or review comparable sales to confirm your home is worth what you claim. In hot San Antonio neighborhoods like Windcrest or The Dominion, homes often appraise above purchase price, which strengthens your case for early cancellation.

Pro tip: if you can demonstrate significant equity through an appraisal or recent assessment, some lenders will waive appraisal costs ($400–$600) or split them with you. Always ask—it's worth the conversation, and a $400 appraisal cost gets paid back within months if you eliminate PMI early.

Documents to Gather and Submit

You'll want your mortgage statement showing your current loan balance, the original loan documentation with your initial purchase price and down payment amount, and proof of residence (utility bill or property tax statement).

An appraisal or comparative market analysis (CMA) from a local San Antonio realtor is critical. Some lenders use automated valuation models (AVMs) instead, which are faster but sometimes less favorable than an appraisal. Always ask your lender which tool they prefer and request an appraisal if the AVM comes back low.

Keep copies of everything you submit. Follow up with your lender in writing (email preferred for a paper trail) at week three to confirm they received your request and appraisal. Don't let your cancellation request disappear into a file.

PMI vs. FHA Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP)—Why the Difference Matters

If you've looked at FHA loans, you've seen FHA Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP) mentioned instead of PMI. They sound similar, but MIP is often more expensive and much harder to remove—a critical distinction for San Antonio buyers.

With FHA loans (government-backed), you pay an upfront MIP at closing (typically 1.75% of your loan amount) plus an annual renewal MIP of 0.55% on down payments of 10% or less. On a $328,500 FHA loan, that's $5,749.75 upfront and roughly $180 monthly forever—or at least until you refinance.

Here's the crushing difference: FHA MIP never goes away automatically, even after you reach 20% equity. You're stuck with it for the life of the loan unless you refinance into a conventional mortgage (which costs time, money, and has its own qualification hurdles).

Conventional PMI, by contrast, vanishes automatically at 78% LTV and can be cancelled at your request at 80% LTV. Over a 30-year loan, the cost difference between FHA MIP and conventional PMI can be $30,000–$50,000, making conventional loans far superior for most San Antonio buyers.

FHA loans do serve a purpose: they're easier to qualify for with lower credit scores and allow down payments as low as 3.5%. But if you can qualify for conventional financing, PMI is almost always the smarter path because it's temporary.

Smart Strategies to Avoid or Minimize PMI

The 20% Down Payment Strategy

The surest way to avoid PMI entirely is to save 20% of your purchase price and put it down upfront. On a $365,000 San Antonio home, that's $73,000 saved and ready at closing.

I understand this feels daunting—most people don't have $70,000+ sitting in savings. But if you're in a position to wait 1–2 more years while you save aggressively, you'll sidestep years of PMI payments and build equity faster from day one.

Consider this: if you can save an extra $20,000 over the next 18 months, you'd drop your down payment from 10% to 15%, reducing PMI costs by roughly 25–30%. Even that incremental savings compounds to real money.

The Piggyback Loan Strategy (80/10/10)

A piggyback loan—also called an 80/10/10 structure—lets you avoid PMI by splitting your financing into two mortgages: an 80% first mortgage and a 10% second mortgage (home equity line of credit or HELOC), with you putting down 10% in cash.

The advantage: no PMI because the first lender is at exactly 80% LTV. The drawback: you're paying interest on two loans, and the second mortgage typically carries a higher interest rate (7–8% versus 6–6.5% on the first).

The math often breaks even or slightly favors piggyback loans for San Antonio buyers, especially those with strong credit. However, it adds complexity, and if interest rates rise sharply, it could become expensive. Run the numbers with your lender before committing.

The Lender Credit Strategy

Some lenders offer rate/credit tradeoffs: you accept a slightly higher interest rate (maybe 6.5% instead of 6.25%) in exchange for a lender credit that covers PMI costs for a set period (often 5–7 years).

This works if you plan to stay in your San Antonio home for at least 5–7 years and refinance thereafter. Over that period, you're paying more in interest but less in PMI. It's a calculation worth doing with your loan officer.

Accelerated Principal Paydown

Every extra dollar you put toward principal accelerates your path to 80% LTV and PMI cancellation. If you can commit to an extra $150–$200 monthly (or $1,800–$2,400 annually), you could eliminate PMI 2��3 years earlier, netting $3,000–$5,000 in savings.

The discipline required is real, but the payoff is concrete. Many San Antonio homeowners who receive annual bonuses or tax refunds dedicate those windfalls to principal payments—a smart, sustainable way to build equity faster without overstressing monthly cash flow.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I remove PMI before I have 20% equity if my home appreciates in value?

Yes, if your home appreciates enough that you can demonstrate 20% equity through a new appraisal, many lenders will allow early cancellation based on that equity, even if you haven't made enough principal payments to reach 80% LTV. This happens frequently in appreciating San Antonio neighborhoods. However, you must request this in writing and provide an updated appraisal at your cost ($400–$600). The lender is not obligated to accept the appraisal's value—they set their own standards—but it's worth attempting if you believe your home has appreciated significantly since purchase.

What happens to PMI if I refinance my mortgage?

When you refinance, your old PMI terminates, and a new PMI calculation applies based on your new loan amount and equity at that time. If you refinance when you've already built 15% equity, the new loan may not require PMI at all if you're refinancing to a lower amount. Conversely, if you refinance to pull cash out (a cash-out refinance), PMI might reappear if your new loan-to-value ratio exceeds 80%. Always calculate refinancing costs (appraisal, origination fees, title work) against PMI savings to ensure a refinance makes financial sense—sometimes it doesn't.

Is PMI tax-deductible in San Antonio or Texas?

PMI was tax-deductible for some taxpayers under the Mortgage Insurance Premium Deduction, but this provision has expired as of 2024 under current federal law, meaning most homeowners cannot deduct PMI payments. Your mortgage interest and property taxes remain deductible (if you itemize), but PMI itself does not. Always consult a tax professional, as tax law changes and individual circumstances vary. This is another reason to prioritize eliminating PMI: unlike mortgage interest, you get zero tax benefit from paying it.

How do I know if I'm currently paying PMI on my mortgage?

Review your mortgage statement or loan estimate—PMI should be listed as a line item or bundled into your total payment. You can also call your lender and ask directly about your current loan-to-value ratio and whether PMI is active. If your original down payment was less than 20%, PMI is almost certainly active. Some loans have PMI listed under a different name, such as "monthly mortgage insurance" or "lender's insurance," so ask your lender to clarify all insurance-related charges on your statement.

Should I wait to buy a San Antonio home until I have 20% down to avoid PMI?

This depends on your timeline, market conditions, and financial situation. If you can save 20% down in 12 months and you're in a stable housing market, waiting might be wise. However, if you're renting and you're paying $1,500–$2,000 monthly in rent, and your mortgage (including PMI) would be $1,800–$2,000, buying now with PMI and canceling it in 8–10 years often builds more wealth than waiting. The key is calculating your true break-even point: compare total PMI costs against rent savings, plus factoring in home appreciation. In many San Antonio neighborhoods, buying with PMI beats renting and waiting for years.
