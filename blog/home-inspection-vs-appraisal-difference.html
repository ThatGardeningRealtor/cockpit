Home Inspection vs. Appraisal: Most Buyers Confuse These and It Costs Them

By Chris Drawdy · June 2, 2026 · 9 min read

You've made an offer on that dream home in Alamo Heights or found a fixer-upper in Southtown that speaks to you. Now come two must-do steps that sound similar but serve completely different purposes—and mixing them up could delay your closing, tank your deal, or leave you with a money pit on your hands. Let's break down home inspections versus appraisals so you know exactly what each one does, who pays, and what to do when problems surface.

What Is a Home Inspection?

A home inspection is a detailed physical examination of a property's structure, systems, and condition. A licensed Texas home inspector walks through the house, tests the HVAC system, checks for water damage, examines the roof, reviews plumbing and electrical work, and documents everything they find.

Think of it like preparing garden soil before planting—you need to know what you're working with before you commit. The inspector gives you a comprehensive report that lists defects, safety issues, and deferred maintenance.

This inspection is entirely optional, but skipping it is one of the biggest mistakes I see buyers make in the San Antonio market. It protects you financially and legally.

What Is an Appraisal?

An appraisal is a lender's tool to determine whether a property's fair market value supports the loan amount you're requesting. A licensed appraiser visits the home, compares it to recently sold comparable properties in the area, and produces a report stating the property's estimated value.

Your lender orders and pays for this appraisal—it's not optional if you're financing. The appraiser isn't checking the condition of the home the way an inspector would; they're asking one question: "Is this property worth what the buyer is paying?"

If you're buying in neighborhoods like Northgate, the Medical Center corridor, or near Castle Hills, the appraiser will pull recent sales from those same school districts and price ranges to justify the valuation.

Key Differences: Inspection vs. Appraisal at a Glance

Purpose: An inspection assesses condition and safety. An appraisal establishes market value.

Who Orders It: You (the buyer) order the inspection. Your lender orders the appraisal.

Who Performs It: A licensed home inspector with technical training. A licensed appraiser with real estate market expertise.

What They're Looking For: The inspector finds broken water heaters, roof damage, code violations, and hidden problems. The appraiser compares your home to comps and verifies the asking price is reasonable.

Report Timeline: You get an inspection report within 24–48 hours. An appraisal report takes 3–5 business days.

Cost and Who Pays: You pay for the inspection ($300–$500 in San Antonio). The lender pays for the appraisal ($400–$700), though the cost is often rolled into your loan closing costs.

Who Pays for Each and Who Orders Them in Texas?

In Texas, the buyer almost always pays for and orders the home inspection. This is done early in the contract period—usually within the inspection contingency window (commonly 7–10 days after the offer is accepted).

The appraisal is ordered by your lender and is a condition of loan approval. Even though the lender pays for it, the cost is typically added to your closing costs or loan amount, so you're ultimately bearing the expense.

In a cash deal, no appraisal is required—but a home inspection is still strongly recommended, even for all-cash buyers. I've worked with plenty of investors in the San Antonio area who skip inspections on flips and regret it when foundation issues or electrical problems surface.

What a Home Inspector Looks For (And You Should Too)

A professional home inspector in Texas will examine:

  • Roof and gutters: Age, leaks, missing shingles, water damage inside the attic
  • Plumbing: Water pressure, leaks, galvanized pipes, drain functionality, water heater age and condition
  • Electrical: Panel condition, grounding, outlets, fire hazards, code compliance
  • HVAC: Furnace and AC age, functionality, maintenance history, ductwork condition
  • Foundation and structure: Cracks, settling, water intrusion, wood rot, pest damage
  • Interior: Windows, doors, flooring, walls, ceilings, insulation, ventilation
  • Exterior: Siding, paint, landscaping drainage, deck safety, garage door operation

In San Antonio's heat and occasional flooding, inspectors also pay special attention to water damage, AC efficiency, and proper grading around the foundation. Many older homes in neighborhoods like Beacon Hill or Dignowity Hill need scrutiny for foundation issues and outdated wiring.

You're welcome to walk alongside the inspector—in fact, I recommend it. Ask questions. Take photos. The inspection report is your property's baseline health document.

What an Appraiser Actually Determines

An appraiser compares your property to three to five recently sold "comparable" homes in the same area. They consider square footage, lot size, condition, age, amenities, and location. In San Antonio, they'll pull comps from neighborhoods with similar school districts and price ranges.

For example, if you're buying a home near Brackenridge Park or in the Alamo Heights school district, the appraiser will analyze recently closed sales in those same boundaries—not homes five miles away in a different school zone.

The appraiser also notes obvious condition issues, but they're not inspecting the way a home inspector does. They might note "roof appears aged" but they won't climb on it or test the HVAC system. Their job is to justify the price, not to diagnose every problem.

What Happens If the Home Inspection Reveals Problems?

This is where many buyers freeze up, so let me walk you through the real process. Once you receive the inspection report (usually within 48 hours), you have the inspection contingency period—typically 7–10 days—to request repairs, credits, or a price reduction from the seller.

Your options include:

  • Request repairs: Ask the seller to fix specific items before closing. Sellers often push back, especially in competitive San Antonio markets.
  • Request a credit: Ask the seller for a dollar amount to cover repairs yourself. This is often the middle ground.
  • Request a price reduction: Ask for a lower purchase price to account for repairs needed.
  • Walk away: If inspection issues are severe and you have an inspection contingency, you can back out and recover your earnest money.

I've seen buyers find $15,000 in deferred maintenance on a Southtown property and successfully negotiate a $12,000 credit. I've also seen buyers in competitive neighborhoods waive inspection contingencies and then be stuck with a roof replacement they didn't budget for.

The key is understanding what's a deal-breaker versus what's manageable maintenance. A 15-year-old roof might need replacement in 3–5 years; that's different from a roof actively leaking into the master bedroom.

What Happens If the Appraisal Comes in Low?

If the appraiser determines the property's value is lower than your purchase price, you have a problem. Let's say you agreed to pay $425,000 for a home in Stone Oak, but the appraisal comes in at $410,000. Your lender will only loan based on the appraised value, not your offer price.

Your options are:

  • Make up the difference in cash: Pay the extra $15,000 out of pocket at closing.
  • Renegotiate with the seller: Ask them to lower the price to match the appraisal.
  • Challenge the appraisal: Request a reappraisal if you believe the appraiser made an error or missed comparable sales. This is uncommon and rarely successful.
  • Walk away: If you have an appraisal contingency in your contract, you can back out without losing earnest money.

Low appraisals are less common in San Antonio's current market, but they do happen in neighborhoods with rapid price growth or when you're the first buyer willing to pay a premium price for a specific property.

Why You Need Both—They Serve Your Different Needs

Here's the practical truth: the inspection protects you, and the appraisal protects your lender. They don't overlap, so skipping either one costs you.

Without an inspection, you might discover after closing that the HVAC system is dying, the plumbing is corroded, or the foundation has settling cracks. These aren't appraisal issues—an appraiser won't catch them—but they cost thousands to fix.

Without an appraisal, your lender won't fund the loan, so you can't close. Even if you're paying cash, you have no independent verification that you're not overpaying in a neighborhood like Terrell Hills or the Castle Hills area.

Think of it this way: the inspection is your due diligence on the home's health. The appraisal is the market's validation of the price. You need both.

San Antonio-Specific Timing and Market Realities

In our market, inspection contingency periods typically run 7–10 days. During that window, you'll schedule and complete the inspection, review the report, and negotiate with the seller if needed. Don't rush this—it's the only chance you have to back out without losing earnest money if major issues surface.

The appraisal usually begins once your loan is submitted to the lender, which happens after your inspection contingency closes. Appraisal turnaround is 3–5 business days, so timing-wise, you'll usually have the appraisal report 10–14 days after your initial offer acceptance.

In fast-moving San Antonio neighborhoods—especially near Medical Center, North Star, or along the I-35 corridor—sellers sometimes push buyers to waive inspection contingencies. Do not do this without a compelling reason. A professional inspection costs $300–$500 and protects you from a six-figure mistake.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I waive the home inspection in Texas to make my offer stronger?

Technically, yes—you can waive the inspection contingency in your contract. But I strongly advise against it unless you're an experienced investor or you've had the home pre-inspected by your own inspector before making an offer. Waiving inspection removes your legal protection and locks you into the purchase even if serious defects are discovered. Many Texas buyers have done this in competitive markets and later discovered foundation issues, roof problems, or electrical hazards they're now responsible for fixing. If you want to strengthen your offer, consider other concessions like removing other contingencies, offering a higher earnest money deposit, or shortening the inspection period instead.

Will the appraiser catch the same problems as a home inspector?

No—appraisers and home inspectors have different roles and training. An appraiser is trained in real estate valuation and market analysis, not building diagnostics. They might note that a roof "appears to need replacement soon," but they won't climb on it, test the gutters, or examine the rafters for rot the way an inspector will. Appraisers also spend 30 minutes to an hour at a property, while inspectors typically spend 2–4 hours conducting a detailed examination. Don't rely on an appraisal to catch hidden plumbing, electrical, or structural issues—that's what the home inspection is for.

What if the inspection finds problems but the appraisal doesn't mention them—am I stuck?

No. The inspection and appraisal are independent reports, and a property can appraise fine while having significant repair needs. For example, a home might appraise at $400,000 even though the inspector finds a $25,000 roof issue. The inspection gives you leverage to negotiate repairs or credits with the seller during the contingency period. You're not automatically "stuck"—you have the option to request repairs, credits, a price reduction, or to walk away entirely (assuming you have an inspection contingency in your contract). The appraisal doesn't override your inspection findings; it's a separate valuation tool for your lender.

Who should I hire for a home inspection in San Antonio—and what questions should I ask?

Choose an inspector who's licensed by the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) and has experience in the San Antonio area—especially if you're buying an older home in neighborhoods like Dignowity Hill, Beacon Hill, or King William, where foundation and plumbing issues are more common. Ask if they're members of ASHI (American Society of Home Inspectors) and how many inspections they've completed. Request that they provide a detailed written report (not just photos) and ask what their cancellation policy is in case you need to reschedule. Many inspectors in San Antonio also offer thermal imaging to detect hidden moisture, which is valuable given our humidity and occasional flooding. You can also attend the inspection to ask questions in real-time.

Can a low appraisal kill my deal, and what can I do about it?

A low appraisal can definitely complicate your deal if you don't have cash reserves to make up the difference. If the appraisal comes in lower than your offer price, your lender will only finance based on the appraised value—you'll have to cover the gap in cash or renegotiate the price with the seller. You can request a reappraisal if you believe the appraiser made an error or missed comparable sales, but this is rarely approved. If you included an appraisal contingency in your contract (which I recommend), you have the right to walk away without losing earnest money if the appraisal comes in too low. This is another reason to work with a realtor familiar with your neighborhood's market trends—I can help you understand realistic appraisal ranges before you even make an offer.
