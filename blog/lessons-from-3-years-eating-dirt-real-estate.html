3 Years of Eating Dirt: What I Learned the Hard Way About Building a Real Estate Business

By Chris Drawdy · March 24, 2026 · 9 min read

I spent my first three years in real estate making mistakes that cost me money, time, and sleep. Not every lesson came from a coach or a book—most came from getting it wrong first. If you're building your real estate business right now, here's what nobody told me, what I figured out the hard way, and what finally changed in year three.

Year One: The Delusion Phase

I thought I was going to close 15 deals in my first year. I had read the sales books, watched the YouTube videos, and believed that hustle alone would overcome inexperience.

It didn't. I closed 4 deals—two for friends, one for a neighbor, and one I had to practically beg into closing. And even those taught me almost nothing, because I was so busy scrambling that I didn't think strategically about a single one.

What I didn't understand then: real estate isn't actually hard. It's slow. And slow is harder than hard for people like me who grew up believing hard work solved everything.

The Partnership That Cost Me

In month three, I partnered with a "veteran agent" who had been in the business for eight years. He had a nice suit, a big sphere, and a way of talking that sounded like success.

The agreement was simple: he'd mentor me, I'd handle the paperwork and follow-ups, and we'd split commissions 60/40 in his favor. What I didn't ask was whether he was actually a good agent or just a good talker.

He was a good talker. Over eight months, I did 80% of the work on five transactions that went to him. I learned the MLS system well. I learned what a nightmare Bexar County title issues could be.

I learned almost nothing about lead generation, conversion, or why my phone wasn't ringing. He had a sphere from 15 years of banking his clients—I had a sphere of 200 Facebook friends who didn't know I sold houses.

When I finally left that partnership at month 11, I was actually behind. I had made about $2,100 in commission (80% went to splits), spent $6,000 on licensing, marketing, and tech, and had zero business systems or lead sources of my own.

Year Two: The Corrective Burning Phase

Year two was where I started to understand what I didn't know, which somehow felt worse than not knowing it at all.

I went solo. I stopped waiting for someone to hand me deals and started doing what should have been obvious: I got the neighborhoods. I drove every street in Stone Oak, Windcrest, Terrell Hills, and Alamo Heights.

I learned the schools (Anderson, Benchmark, Castle Hills Elementary). I learned the commute times to the medical center, UT, and the north side tech jobs. I learned which properties were actually overpriced and which had real value in neighborhoods where my wife had gardened neighbors who knew each other.

The False Bottom and The Real Problem

By month 18, I had my first real referral from a past client—a friend who bought in Leon Valley. That deal felt different because I had earned it without begging or bribing or splitting.

But here's what nearly killed me in year two: I still had no real lead generation system. I was hoping for referrals instead of building the systems that create them. I was hanging flyers in Windcrest, going to church networking events, and posting properties on Facebook like everyone else who had no idea what they were doing.

By month 20, I had closed 7 deals that year—progress, but not sustainable. I was still making about $18,000 after all my costs. I was eating dirt and pretending it was a salad.

The real problem? I had no numbers. I didn't track how many calls I made, how many people I talked to, or what my actual conversion rate was. I was working blind.

What Changed in Year Three: The System Phase

Month 25 was the month I finally started tracking everything. I know that sounds basic—it is—but I was so deep in production that I never stepped back to look at what was working and what wasn't.

I bought a simple CRM ($99/month), created a basic pipeline tracker in Excel, and spent two weeks going backward through every conversation, every showing, every dead lead for the past 18 months.

What I found: 67% of my conversations came from past clients and their referrals (even the indirect ones I never counted). 23% came from neighborhood relationships and organic word-of-mouth. 10% came from everything else I was doing (the flyers, the Facebook posts, the networking).

So in month 26, I basically fired the 10% that was stealing my time.

The Three Things No One Taught Me

1. Your lead source isn't your job—your follow-up system is. I was so focused on "getting leads" that I wasn't actually following up with people who had expressed interest six months ago. I called a broker I'd met at a market dinner in month 19—someone I'd never followed up with. She had closed two deals since then and was moving across town. We talked for 20 minutes, and she referred me to her brother-in-law. That deal closed in month 28.

I realized I had a database of 150+ people I'd talked to and touched maybe 5% of them consistently. I built a follow-up sequence that took 90 minutes per week and changed everything.

2. You need to pick your neighborhood before you pick your business model. I was trying to be everything to everyone in San Antonio. That's impossible. In month 27, I committed to Stone Oak and the areas connected to it—Windcrest, Castle Hills, the northeastern corridor.

Why? Because that's where I'd already built relationship depth. Because I had real knowledge. Because I could drive comps in 20 minutes and didn't need to pull up a map.

That focus tripled my close rate in those neighborhoods. A buyer in Southtown who found me online and didn't know my neighborhood knowledge? I sent her to another agent. That's not noble—it's smart. She got better service, I didn't waste 40 hours on a transaction I wasn't expert enough to handle well.

3. You need ONE revenue model to start, not multiple side hustles. Somewhere in month 15, I'd gotten distracted. I was trying to do rentals management, wholesale some properties, do short sales (this was 2023—still a thing but declining), and agent work. I was spreading myself across five different business models and executing zero of them well.

In month 26, I killed four of them. Agent work only—buy-side and sell-side, Stone Oak and connected neighborhoods. That's it.

In the last quarter of year three, I closed 11 deals. My revenue was $64,000 (after splits with my broker at eXp, which is fair). My costs were $8,000. Actual profit was $56,000 for the quarter—the first time I could actually say I was building something sustainable.

The Specific Fires I Put Out (And How)

Every agent's journey has moments where the whole thing nearly collapsed. Here are the actual fires I dealt with and what I learned from each.

Fire #1: The Title Company Nightmare (Month 8)

I had a deal going in Leon Valley—a straightforward buy. The sellers had owned it for 22 years. At closing, the title company discovered a lien from a contractor that the sellers didn't know about from 1998.

The deal delayed two weeks. I'd promised my buyers move-in on a specific date. They lost their apartment lease and were furious. I paid for their hotel for four nights out of my own pocket and learned: always, always, always order a title search early. Not two days before closing. Day one.

This cost me $680. It also cost me a potential referral. But it taught me to ask questions about any property with a complex history, and it made me paranoid enough to check for liens that most agents miss entirely.

Fire #2: The Inspection Negotiation (Month 13)

A buyer I'd represented in Windcrest had an inspection that found foundation issues. My buyer wanted out. The sellers' agent was hostile and borderline unprofessional about it, treating my buyer like they were faking concerns.

I called the sellers directly—respectfully, not dramatic. I brought an engineer report that showed the issue was manageable. I proposed a $9,000 credit instead of the deal dying. Both sides agreed, and we closed.

What I learned: your relationship with the other side matters more than you think. It doesn't mean being a pushover. It means being reasonable, professional, and remembering that the person on the other side also wants to close.

Fire #3: The Disappeared Buyer (Month 19)

A buyer I'd been working with for three months went silent two weeks before closing. Literally disappeared. No calls, no texts, nothing. I later learned they'd gotten spooked by a home inspection finding and purchased a different property through another agent without telling me.

I was angry. I was also naive. I had never sent them a clear timeline, never built rapport outside of showings, and never created enough value that they felt like they couldn't just bail.

After that, every buyer I work with gets a simple timeline email, a weekly update, and at least one personal conversation about something other than the house (their job, their family, their timeline). That fire taught me that contracts aren't relationships. Relationships are relationships.

The Doubt Moments (And Why I Didn't Quit)

Month 14 was the lowest point. I'd been in the business for 14 months, had made about $8,000 in net profit (after all costs), and my wife asked me gently: "Do you actually want to do this?"

I didn't have an answer. I was tired. I was making less than I would have made working at HomeGoods. Every deal felt like it barely penciled out after broker splits, transaction costs, and the hours I'd logged.

What kept me going wasn't motivation or a vision board. It was simple: I'd already put in 14 months and didn't want to waste them. And I was starting to understand the neighborhoods in a way I didn't understand anything else.

Month 22 was a different kind of doubt. By then I was making money, but I was worried I'd just gotten lucky. I had closed enough deals that the law of averages should have shifted in my favor, but I still didn't trust that it would hold.

What shifted in month 28? I stopped caring whether I felt confident. I just executed the system. I followed up, I asked for referrals, I stayed local, and I closed deals. Confidence is a luxury for year four and beyond.

What the San Antonio Market Actually Teaches You

San Antonio isn't a hot market like Austin or Dallas. That's actually the best market to build a real estate business in if you're willing to focus.

The buyers here—especially in Stone Oak, Windcrest, and Terrell Hills—care about value, not status. They're not trying to flip properties. They're buying homes where they'll live for five to ten years. Their lenders include USAA, BBVA, and a bunch of local credit unions that have real underwriting standards.

The median home price in my territory is around $385,000. The buyers financing these homes are professionals with W-2 income, typically working in healthcare, tech, or local government. They're not speculative. They're careful. And if you give them expert advice, they'll refer their friends.

The sellers are similar: they're not trying to extract the last dollar. They want a fair process with a real buyer. They value agents who communicate and manage expectations. The market rewards patience and expertise far more than it rewards aggression.

That's not true everywhere. But in San Antonio, it's true. And building my business on that truth changed everything in year three.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it actually take to make real money as a new real estate agent?

You should expect 18–24 months before you're making genuine income (more than your living expenses). Most agents see 4–8 closings in year one, 8–15 in year two, and 15–25+ in year three if they're executing with focus. The money doesn't come linearly. Months 20–28 are often when the compounding effect of referrals and reputation starts to show up in your calendar. Many agents quit at month 14–16 because they're comparing their real-world income to their imaginary first-year projections.

What's the biggest mistake new agents make in San Antonio specifically?

Trying to serve the entire city instead of owning a geographic niche. San Antonio is large (1.5 million people), and agents spread too thin become experts at nothing. If you focus on one neighborhood or corridor—Stone Oak, the Hill Country areas, the North Central corridor—you become the resource. You know comps, you know the lenders, you know the inspectors. That depth creates referrals and closes deals faster than being "available everywhere."

Should I partner with an experienced agent when starting out?

Only if you're crystal clear on what you're getting and getting it in writing. A real mentor relationship is different from a partnership where you're doing work and they're taking the credit and commission. If someone is offering to mentor you, ask for three references from agents they've actually trained. Ask what deal support looks like. Ask what they'll teach you about lead generation and conversion. If their answer is "you'll figure it out on the job," they're not a mentor—they're using you as labor.

How do I actually get my first referral when I have no sphere?

Your first referrals come from the people you already know: your family, friends, church, gym, wherever you spend time. Tell them specifically what you do: "I help people buy and sell homes in Stone Oak and Windcrest." That specificity makes it real. Then actually ask: "If you know anyone buying or selling in my area, I'd love to help." Don't ask friends if they want to buy a house from you. Ask them to think of people they know. That changes the conversation entirely. Your first five referrals will almost certainly come from people you already knew.

What's a realistic budget for a new agent's first year in San Antonio?

Plan on $8,000–$15,000 for year one: broker fees ($3,000–$5,000 depending on the firm), MLS access ($1,200–$1,800), CRM software ($200–$500), marketing ($2,000–$5,000), business license and insurance ($500–$1,000), and miscellaneous (signs, printed materials, professional photos). If you're trying to do it for under $5,000, you're either cutting corners that hurt you (like not having errors and omissions insurance) or you're already wealthy and don't need income from this business. Be honest about your burn rate from month one.
