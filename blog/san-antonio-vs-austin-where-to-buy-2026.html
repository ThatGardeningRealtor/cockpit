San Antonio vs. Austin: Where Should You Buy a Home in 2026?

By Chris Drawdy · June 1, 2026 · 10 min read

If you've been weighing San Antonio against Austin for your next home purchase, you're asking exactly the right question — and the answer isn't as obvious as it used to be. In this honest, on-the-ground comparison, I'll break down home prices, value per dollar, lifestyle trade-offs, commute realities, and growth trajectories so you can make the decision that's right for your family. No hype, no sales pitch — just the real picture of two cities I know well.

The Price Gap Is Still Real — and Still Growing

Let's start with the number everyone wants to know: what does a home actually cost? In San Antonio, the median sale price as of early 2026 sits around $295,000, while Austin's median hovers closer to $515,000. That's a gap of roughly $220,000 for what are, in many cases, comparable square footages and lot sizes.

That price difference isn't just a headline — it translates directly into your monthly payment. At today's rate environment (roughly 6.5%–7% on a 30-year fixed), that gap means approximately $1,300–$1,500 more per month in Austin before you even factor in property taxes and insurance.

Austin did see a meaningful correction from its 2022 peak, dropping 15–18% from all-time highs. But it has since stabilized and begun creeping back upward, especially in neighborhoods like South Congress, Bouldin Creek, and East Austin. San Antonio, by contrast, never experienced the same speculative spike and has maintained steadier, more sustainable appreciation — more like a well-rooted perennial than a flashy annual that blooms and fades.

Value Per Dollar: What Your Budget Actually Buys

In San Antonio, $350,000 gets you a solid 3–4 bedroom home with a yard in established neighborhoods like Converse, Schertz, Helotes, or Leon Valley. In many of those areas you're looking at 1,800–2,400 square feet, a two-car garage, and mature trees — the kind of outdoor space where you can actually grow something.

That same $350,000 in Austin places you in a considerably more competitive position. You're likely looking at condos or townhomes in Rundberg, far north Pflugerville, or a heavily renovated fixer in a less desirable part of the metro. The days of finding a detached home with a yard inside Austin's city limits under $400,000 are largely gone.

San Antonio also offers standout value in its newer master-planned communities. Alamo Ranch on the northwest side, Cibolo Canyons near the JW Marriott, and Sonterra in Stone Oak all offer new construction and resort-style amenities at price points that would be almost unimaginable in comparable Austin-area developments. If land, space, and a sense of community matter to you, SA's value proposition is hard to beat.

First-Time Buyer Programs Worth Knowing

San Antonio has a legitimate advantage here that often gets overlooked. The City of San Antonio's Homebuyer Incentive Program (HIP) offers up to $30,000 in down payment and closing cost assistance for eligible buyers — one of the more generous municipal programs in Texas.

Bexar County also has its own assistance layer through the Housing Finance Corporation, and lenders like Generations Federal Credit Union, Broadway Bank, and Frost Bank are deeply embedded in the local market with products tailored for SA buyers. These relationships matter when you're trying to close in a competitive situation.

Austin does have some assistance programs through AHFC (Austin Housing Finance Corporation), but income limits are tighter given the city's higher cost of living, and the programs are frequently oversubscribed. For first-time buyers especially, San Antonio's support infrastructure is meaningfully stronger.

Growth, Jobs, and Economic Trajectory

Austin has long been the darling of the "Texas tech boom" narrative — and that reputation is earned. Apple, Tesla, Samsung, and Oracle have all planted significant flags there, and the University of Texas creates a constant pipeline of talent and economic activity. For buyers in tech or remote-friendly industries, Austin's employment ecosystem is a genuine draw.

But San Antonio's economic story has quietly gotten much more interesting. The expansion of Joint Base San Antonio (JBSA) — the largest military installation in the world by acreage — anchors tens of thousands of stable, recession-resistant jobs. Add in the growing cybersecurity corridor (SA ranks among the top five cybersecurity markets in the nation), Toyota's North American manufacturing hub, and CPS Energy's major infrastructure investments, and you have a diversified economy that doesn't hinge on any single industry's health.

USAA's continued downtown and northwest presence, Valero's headquarters, and a booming healthcare sector anchored by the South Texas Medical Center (the largest medical complex in the country outside of Houston's Texas Medical Center) give San Antonio a depth of employment opportunity that rarely gets the media attention it deserves. The soil here is fertile — it just doesn't always get the flashy headlines.

Lifestyle, Culture, and Community Feel

This is where it gets personal, and honestly where San Antonio tends to win the hearts of people who actually move here. SA has a strong, deeply rooted cultural identity — the River Walk, the Pearl District, the missions, Fiesta, Día de los Muertos. It feels like a city that knows who it is and isn't performing for anyone.

Austin's culture has shifted noticeably over the past decade. Long-time Austinites will tell you the "Keep Austin Weird" ethos has been squeezed by rapid growth, rising costs, and an influx of transplants. That's not a judgment — cities evolve — but it does mean what you're buying into culturally in 2026 is different from the Austin of 2012 or even 2018.

San Antonio's neighborhoods each carry their own distinct personality. Alamo Heights and Terrell Hills feel like quiet, established communities where people stay for generations. King William and Southtown have genuine artistic energy without the price tag of East Austin. The Hill Country Village and Hollywood Park areas offer near-rural peace within city limits. And for gardeners and outdoor lovers, the year-round growing season in SA's USDA Zone 8b/9a overlap is absolutely unmatched.

Schools: A Nuanced Picture

School district quality is neighborhood-specific in both cities, so broad generalizations aren't fair or helpful. In San Antonio, North East ISD, Northside ISD, and Comal ISD (which covers much of the New Braunfels and Garden Ridge area) consistently earn high marks and are worth prioritizing if you have school-age children.

NEISD in particular covers areas like Alamo Heights, Stone Oak, and Shavano Park and has a strong reputation for academics and extracurriculars. Comal ISD has grown rapidly but maintained quality standards — and the communities it serves offer exceptional value right now.

Austin ISD has faced some challenges with budget cuts and school consolidations in recent years, which has pushed many Austin-area families toward Round Rock ISD, Eanes ISD (which covers Westlake, one of the most expensive submarkets in Texas), or Leander ISD. Strong Austin-area school districts often come with a significant premium baked into home prices.

Commute Reality and the I-35 Corridor

One option that deserves serious consideration — especially if you work remotely part-time or need to access both cities — is buying in the corridor between San Antonio and Austin. Cities like New Braunfels, Kyle, Buda, and San Marcos sit along I-35 and have exploded in growth precisely because they offer more affordable housing than Austin while remaining drivable to either metro.

New Braunfels in particular has been one of the fastest-growing cities in the country for several consecutive years. Home prices there have risen significantly as a result, but you can still find solid family homes in the $350,000–$430,000 range with good Comal or Guadalupe-Hays access. The trade-off is that I-35 between San Antonio and Austin is legitimately congested — adding 20–40 minutes to what maps suggest during peak hours.

If you're fully remote and prioritizing value, San Antonio wins this calculus clearly. If you need to be in an Austin office three days a week, buying in San Antonio proper and commuting is possible but taxing — be honest with yourself about how often that drive would happen before making a $300,000+ decision around it.

Investment Potential and Long-Term Appreciation

Both cities have appreciated well over the past decade, but they've done so in very different ways. Austin saw explosive, speculative appreciation from 2020–2022 followed by a sharp correction. San Antonio's growth has been slower and steadier — roughly 4–7% annually in most submarkets — which actually makes it a more predictable environment for long-term investment.

For rental investors, San Antonio offers stronger cap rates. A home that costs $280,000 in San Antonio can often rent for $1,700–$1,950/month, depending on the area. A comparable Austin property at $500,000+ would need to rent for well over $3,000/month to achieve the same yield — a number that's difficult to hit outside of luxury or short-term rental markets.

The long-term growth thesis for San Antonio is also compelling. Population growth is sustained, infrastructure investment is ongoing (the I-10 and Loop 1604 expansions continue), and the city remains significantly more affordable than peer metros like Phoenix, Denver, or Nashville. That relative affordability acts as a constant pull for new residents, which is the engine of long-term appreciation. Like a slow-maturing fruit tree, San Antonio's returns are less dramatic but deeply reliable.

So — Which City Should You Choose?

The honest answer is: it depends on what you're optimizing for. If you need to be embedded in Austin's tech industry, value density and walkability, or want the cultural cachet of one of America's most talked-about cities — Austin may still be worth the premium for you.

But if you're prioritizing value per dollar, more space, a strong and diversified job market, genuine community roots, a more forgiving cost of living, and a city that's growing into its potential rather than correcting from over-exuberance — San Antonio deserves your serious attention in 2026.

I've helped buyers work through this exact decision, and what I've seen is that the people who move to San Antonio almost universally say they wish they'd done it sooner. The city gets under your skin in the best way. There's room here — room to grow, room to build something, room to put down real roots.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is San Antonio a good place to buy a home in 2026 compared to Austin?

For most buyers who aren't tied to Austin's job market, San Antonio offers a significantly better value proposition in 2026. Inventory has remained healthier in SA, bidding war frequency is lower, and the median price point leaves more room in your monthly budget for savings, renovations, or quality of life. San Antonio's market hasn't experienced the same post-pandemic volatility that Austin's did, meaning you're buying into a more stable price floor. For families, first-time buyers, and remote workers especially, SA deserves to be the first choice — not the consolation prize.

What neighborhoods in San Antonio are best for families relocating from Austin?

Families coming from Austin who want that same sense of community and good schools tend to land happily in Stone Oak, Shavano Park, or the Helotes area in northwest San Antonio — all served by strong school districts with active parent communities. For those who want a slightly smaller-town feel, the Cibolo/Schertz area offers newer construction, excellent Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD schools, and a tight-knit suburban character that Austin's sprawl has largely outgrown. The Dominion and Sonterra areas in north San Antonio attract buyers looking for resort-style amenities and luxury finishes at prices well below what comparable Austin addresses would command. Each of these areas has distinct personalities worth exploring in person before you decide.

How do property taxes compare between San Antonio and Austin?

Both cities carry relatively high property tax rates compared to national averages — a trade-off for Texas having no state income tax. In San Antonio, effective rates typically run 2.1%–2.4% depending on the specific taxing entities layered on a property (city, county, school district, MUD, etc.). Austin's rates are marginally lower on paper at 1.9%–2.2%, but because home values are so much higher, the absolute dollar amount you pay in Austin is dramatically more. A $500,000 Austin home at 2.1% generates $10,500/year in property taxes; a $295,000 SA home at the same rate generates $6,195. Texas does offer a Homestead Exemption that caps appraisal increases at 10% annually once you've claimed it — applying for this immediately after closing is one of the most important financial moves any Texas homeowner can make.

Can I live in San Antonio and work in Austin — is the commute realistic?

It's technically possible, and some people do make this commute regularly, but "realistic" depends heavily on your work schedule and tolerance for I-35. The drive from northeast San Antonio to downtown Austin runs 75–90 minutes on a good day, but can stretch to two hours or more during peak traffic or on rain days, when Texas drivers collectively forget how roads work. If you're working in Austin two days a week or less, many buyers find the commute manageable and the price savings worth it. For three or more days per week, most people burn out within a year and end up either moving closer to Austin or finding remote work. A practical middle-ground is buying in New Braunfels or San Marcos, which cuts the drive time meaningfully while still offering more value than Austin proper.

Are there down payment assistance programs for buying a home in San Antonio in 2026?

Yes, and San Antonio's assistance landscape is among the stronger ones in Texas. The City's Homebuyer Incentive Program (HIP) can provide up to $30,000 for income-qualifying buyers, covering down payment and closing costs — this alone can be the difference between renting and owning for many families. Bexar County's Housing Finance Corporation also offers additional programs layered on top of city assistance, and the Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) has statewide programs available to both first-time and repeat buyers in targeted zip codes. Local lenders like Generations Federal Credit Union and Broadway Bank have dedicated loan officers who specialize in navigating these programs together, which simplifies what can otherwise feel like a complicated process. It's worth having a conversation with a knowledgeable local lender before you assume assistance isn't available to you — many buyers are surprised by what they qualify for.
