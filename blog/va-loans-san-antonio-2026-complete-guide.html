VA Loans in San Antonio 2026: Everything Veterans Need to Know About Buying a Home

By Chris Drawdy · June 5, 2026 · 10 min read

San Antonio has always been a military city. With Joint Base San Antonio — the largest military installation complex in the United States — anchoring communities like Lackland, Randolph, and Fort Sam Houston, this city has deep, generational roots with the men and women who serve our country. And when it comes time to put down roots here permanently, one of the most powerful tools available to veterans is one that far too many people don't fully understand: the VA home loan.

Think of the VA loan like a well-established fruit tree in your backyard. It takes a little time to understand how it works, but once it's planted correctly, it produces incredible results year after year. Zero down payment. No private mortgage insurance. Competitive interest rates. And in a market like San Antonio — where home values have grown steadily even as interest rates have shifted — these benefits can mean tens of thousands of dollars in savings over the life of your loan.

Whether you're a first-time homebuyer fresh off active duty, a retired veteran looking to upgrade, or a surviving spouse navigating your options, this guide breaks down everything you need to know about using a VA loan to buy a home in San Antonio in 2026.

What Is a VA Loan and Who Qualifies?

A VA loan is a mortgage backed by the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs. It's not issued directly by the VA — instead, private lenders like banks and mortgage companies make the loan, and the VA guarantees a portion of it. That guarantee is what allows lenders to offer favorable terms without requiring a down payment or private mortgage insurance.

Basic Eligibility Requirements

To qualify for a VA loan, you generally need to meet one of the following service requirements:

  • Active duty service members who have served at least 90 continuous days
  • Veterans who served 90 days during wartime or 181 days during peacetime
  • National Guard and Reserve members with at least 6 years of service, or 90 days of active duty under Title 32 orders
  • Surviving spouses of veterans who died in service or from a service-connected disability, and who have not remarried (with some exceptions)

You'll also need a Certificate of Eligibility (COE) to prove your entitlement. The good news? Your lender can often pull this for you electronically in minutes. You can also obtain it through the VA's eBenefits portal or by submitting VA Form 26-1880.

Credit and Income Requirements

The VA doesn't set a minimum credit score, but most lenders in San Antonio look for a score of at least 580–620, with 640 or higher giving you access to the widest range of lenders and rates. The VA also uses a "residual income" test — meaning after all your monthly debts and housing expenses, you need enough income left over to cover basic living costs for your family size. This is actually a more veteran-friendly standard than the strict debt-to-income ratios used by conventional loans.

The Big Three Benefits: Zero Down, No PMI, and Competitive Rates

Let's talk about the benefits that make the VA loan a genuinely remarkable program — not just marketing language, but real financial advantages that matter in the San Antonio market.

Zero Down Payment

In San Antonio, the median home price as of mid-2026 hovers around $285,000–$310,000 depending on the area. On a conventional loan, a 5% down payment on a $295,000 home means coming to the table with nearly $15,000 — before closing costs. A 20% down payment to avoid PMI? That's $59,000. With a VA loan, eligible veterans can finance 100% of the purchase price with $0 down. That's not a teaser or a catch — it's the program working exactly as intended.

No Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

On a conventional loan with less than 20% down, lenders require PMI — typically 0.5%–1.5% of the loan amount per year. On a $295,000 loan, that could mean $1,475–$4,425 per year, or $123–$369 added to your monthly payment. VA loans have no PMI, period. The VA Funding Fee (more on that below) is a one-time cost, not an ongoing monthly drain.

Competitive Interest Rates

Because the VA guarantees a portion of each loan, lenders carry less risk — and that typically translates to interest rates that run 0.25%–0.75% lower than comparable conventional loans. Over a 30-year mortgage, even a quarter-point difference saves thousands of dollars. In 2026, VA loan rates in Texas are generally ranging between 6.25%–7.0% depending on the lender, your credit profile, and loan terms — often meaningfully below conventional rates for the same borrower.

Understanding the VA Funding Fee

Here's the one cost that catches some veterans off guard: the VA Funding Fee. This is a one-time fee paid to the VA that helps keep the program self-sustaining for future generations of veterans. It's not charged by your lender — it goes directly to the VA.

For first-time VA loan users making no down payment in 2026, the funding fee is 2.15% of the loan amount. For subsequent use, it rises to 3.3%. On a $295,000 loan, that's roughly $6,342 for first-time use — but importantly, this fee can be rolled into the loan amount rather than paid out of pocket at closing.

Several groups are exempt from the funding fee entirely, including:

  • Veterans receiving VA disability compensation
  • Surviving spouses of veterans who died in service or from a service-connected disability
  • Active duty service members who have received a Purple Heart

If you have a disability rating — even a partial one — make sure your lender knows before closing. This exemption can save you thousands of dollars.

VA Assumable Loans: A Hidden Gem in Today's Market

One of the most underappreciated features of VA loans is assumability. When you assume a VA loan, you take over the seller's existing mortgage — including their interest rate. In today's environment, where rates are significantly higher than they were in 2020–2021, a seller with a 2.75% or 3.25% VA loan has a genuinely valuable asset sitting in their mortgage.

Here's how it works: If a veteran homeowner in Schertz or Converse bought a home in 2021 with a VA loan at 3.0% and is now selling, a qualified buyer — veteran or civilian — can potentially assume that loan and lock in that rate for the remaining term. This can dramatically reduce monthly payments compared to taking out a new loan at current rates.

There are important caveats: the seller's VA entitlement remains tied up until the loan is paid off or the assumer refinances, and the buyer must qualify with the lender. But for the right transaction, assumption is a powerful strategy worth exploring. Talk to a knowledgeable VA lender about whether an assumable loan makes sense for your purchase.

Buying in San Antonio: Neighborhoods and Market Considerations for Veterans

San Antonio's military community tends to gravitate toward certain areas based on proximity to base, school quality, and value. Here's a practical look at some of the most popular areas for veteran homebuyers in 2026.

Near Lackland AFB / West San Antonio

Neighborhoods like Westover Hills, Helotes, and portions of Leon Valley offer relative proximity to Lackland with good value. Helotes in particular has that semi-rural, outdoor-living feel — big lots, Hill Country views, room for a garden — that resonates with veterans who want space after years of shared quarters. Median prices here range from $295,000–$380,000.

Northeast San Antonio / Near Randolph AFB

Communities like Schertz, Cibolo, Universal City, and Converse have long been popular with military families for good reason: excellent schools, newer construction, and reasonable commutes to Randolph and Fort Sam Houston. These areas have seen strong appreciation over the past several years. Expect median prices in the $280,000–$360,000 range, with new construction communities still actively building.

South San Antonio

For veterans who prefer more affordable entry points without sacrificing quality of life, south San Antonio neighborhoods along the 410 loop and beyond offer homes in the $210,000–$270,000 range. This is fertile ground — pun intended — for first-time buyers maximizing their VA benefit.

VA Loan Limits in Texas

Good news: since 2020, the VA eliminated loan limits for veterans with full entitlement. This means if you haven't used your VA benefit before (or have fully restored it), there's no cap on how much you can borrow with zero down — though lenders still apply their own underwriting standards. In San Antonio's market, this is rarely a limiting factor for most buyers.

Local VA Lenders and Resources in San Antonio

Choosing the right lender for a VA loan matters more than most people realize. Not all lenders are equally experienced with VA transactions, and a fumbled appraisal or a miscommunicated funding fee exemption can cause real problems. Here are some resources and lender types worth knowing about in San Antonio.

VA-Specialized Lenders

National lenders with strong VA track records — including Veterans United Home Loans, Navy Federal Credit Union, USAA, and PenFed Credit Union — all operate actively in the San Antonio market. These institutions know the VA process deeply and have dedicated teams for military borrowers. Local credit unions like Security Service Federal Credit Union and Randolph-Brooks Federal Credit Union (RBFCU) also offer competitive VA products with the added benefit of local customer service relationships.

Texas Veterans Land Board (VLB)

The Texas Veterans Land Board is a state program that deserves special attention. The VLB offers its own home loan program that can sometimes be used in combination with or as an alternative to a federal VA loan. VLB loans are available to Texas residents who qualify under VA eligibility guidelines and offer competitive rates with access to down payment assistance in some cases. Visit the VLB website at vlb.texas.gov to explore current rates and programs.

HUD-Approved Housing Counselors

For veterans who want independent guidance before choosing a lender, HUD-approved housing counselors are available at no cost. The San Antonio Housing Authority and Alamo Area Council of Governments both connect residents with counseling services that can help you understand your options without any sales pressure.

The VA Homebuying Process: Step by Step

The VA loan process isn't dramatically different from a conventional purchase, but there are a few key steps specific to VA transactions that are worth understanding before you start.

  1. Get your Certificate of Eligibility (COE). Your lender can often do this for you in minutes using the VA's automated system. Do this before you start seriously shopping.
  2. Get pre-approved with a VA-experienced lender. Pre-approval tells you exactly how much house you can buy and strengthens your offer when you find the right home.
  3. Work with a VA-savvy real estate agent. Not every agent understands how to write a VA offer or navigate VA appraisal requirements. Experience here matters.
  4. Find your home and make an offer. VA offers are competitive — don't let anyone tell you otherwise. Many sellers in San Antonio's military-heavy market understand and welcome VA financing.
  5. VA appraisal and inspection. The VA requires an appraisal by a VA-certified appraiser to confirm the home's value and ensure it meets Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs). This is about protecting you as the buyer, not creating obstacles.
  6. Underwriting and clear to close. Once the appraisal and your financial documents are reviewed, you'll receive your clear to close — typically within 30–45 days of going under contract.
  7. Close and get your keys. Welcome home. You've earned it.

One note on VA appraisals: sometimes sellers or listing agents express hesitation about VA offers because of appraisal concerns. A knowledgeable buyer's agent can address these concerns proactively and help structure offers that are competitive while protecting your VA benefits. Don't let outdated myths talk you out of using a benefit you've earned.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a VA loan more than once in San Antonio?

Yes — absolutely. VA loan benefits are not a one-time-use program. Once you sell your home and pay off your existing VA loan, your full entitlement is restored and you can use it again. You can also have two VA loans simultaneously in some situations — for example, if you're relocating due to a PCS move and need to purchase a new home before selling your previous one. This is called "bonus entitlement" or "second-tier entitlement." The rules are specific and depend on your remaining entitlement and the loan amounts involved, so it's worth having a detailed conversation with a VA-approved lender. The key point is that this is a lifetime benefit available to eligible veterans, not a one-time voucher.

How does a VA loan compare to a Texas VLB loan, and can I use both?

The federal VA loan and the Texas Veterans Land Board (VLB) home loan are two separate programs, and in some cases they can be used together — a strategy sometimes called "stacking." The VLB offers its own home loan program with competitive interest rates specifically for Texas veterans, and historically it has offered rates at or below market. The VLB also has a separate Home Improvement Loan program and a Land Loan program for veterans who want to purchase acreage. When used alongside a VA loan, the VLB loan can sometimes help cover costs the VA loan doesn't. Eligibility, current rates, and program specifics change regularly, so visit vlb.texas.gov or speak with a lender familiar with both programs to see what combination makes the most sense for your situation in 2026.

Do VA loans take longer to close than conventional loans in San Antonio?

VA loans have historically carried a reputation for slower closings, but that gap has narrowed considerably with experienced lenders. In San Antonio's active military market, many lenders process VA loans routinely and close within 30–45 days — comparable to conventional timelines. The VA appraisal process is the most common source of delays, particularly if the appraiser identifies property condition issues that need to be resolved before closing. Choosing a lender with a strong VA track record and a buyer's agent who knows how to prepare your offer and set realistic expectations with sellers will do more to keep your timeline on track than any other factor. Rushed timelines of 21–30 days are possible with the right team in place, though they require careful coordination.

What properties are eligible for VA financing in the San Antonio area?

VA loans can be used to purchase single-family homes, VA-approved condominiums, manufactured homes (with specific requirements), and multi-unit properties up to four units — as long as you intend to live in one of the units as your primary residence. The VA's Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs) exist to ensure the home is safe, structurally sound, and sanitary. Common issues that can trigger VA appraisal conditions in San Antonio include foundation concerns (particularly relevant in San Antonio's expansive clay soil), roof condition, exposed wiring, and non-functional HVAC systems. New construction in communities like Cibolo, New Braunfels, or Converse is generally VA-eligible, though you'll want to confirm that the builder and community have completed the VA registration process. Investment properties and vacation homes are not eligible for VA financing.

What are typical closing costs for a VA loan in San Antonio, and can the seller pay them?

VA loan closing costs in San Antonio typically run between 2%–5% of the purchase price, excluding the VA Funding Fee. These costs include lender fees (origination charges), the VA appraisal fee (typically $550–$750 in the San Antonio market), title insurance, escrow fees, prepaid property taxes, and homeowner's insurance. Importantly, the VA limits what veterans can be charged — for example, real estate commissions, certain lender fees above 1% of the loan amount, and attorney fees are non-allowable charges for VA buyers. Sellers are allowed to pay all of the buyer's closing costs, plus up to 4% in concessions toward other items like the funding fee, prepaid taxes, or paying down debt to help with qualification. Working with an experienced agent to negotiate seller concessions is a legitimate and common strategy that can significantly reduce your out-of-pocket expenses at closing.
