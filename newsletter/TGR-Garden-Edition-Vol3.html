Hey neighbors

We're deep into the brunt of summer and the front yard is still going strong. This is year three, and everything you're seeing right now traces back to the same place. The soil. This week I want to show you what that foundation actually built, and I need your help with what's coming next.
 
Three Years In

Everything You See Started Underground

People ask how the garden produces the way it does through a San Antonio summer and the honest answer is boring. It's not a trick. It's three years of building soil that can hold up to the heat. Compost, mulch, letting things break down instead of hauling them off. Found this guy while turning a bed this week, and that's exactly what you want to see. A healthy grub means healthy soil biology, and healthy soil biology is the entire reason anything here survives August.

Not glamorous, but this is what a healthy bed looks like underneath. Three years of building this.
 
Still Going Strong

Okra Doesn't Care How Hot It Gets

If your summer garden is struggling right now, okra is the plant that will save your confidence. It thrives in heat that shuts almost everything else down, and it's still pushing out pods every couple of days out front. Same soil foundation, different plant proving the point.

Picked fresh this week. Okra is the workhorse of a San Antonio summer garden.
 
More Than Plants

A Good Garden Attracts More Than You Expect

This week's harvest basket sat right next to a pot of cactus that had turned into a Hercules beetle gathering spot. Green tomatoes, okra, a nopal pad, and a container full of some of the biggest beetles in Texas, all within arm's reach of each other. Three years in and the garden isn't just growing food anymore. It's holding an entire ecosystem.

Harvest on one side, Hercules beetles on the other. This is what a mature garden looks like.
 
New Project

A Community Garden Bed Is Coming to LRH3

As we transition into fall, we're breaking new ground on the LRH side of the yard. Same concept that got this front yard to where it is, just opened up so more of the neighborhood can plant, harvest, and trade right alongside the stand. It only takes a little space and a little time to build something people gather around.

Part of what's already growing at LRH3. The new bed builds right off this same energy.

What would you want to see in a shared community bed? Reply and tell me. I'm reading every answer before we break ground this fall.
